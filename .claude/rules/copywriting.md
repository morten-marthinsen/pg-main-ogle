## Patterns extracted from draft → final edits

### Sentence structure
- **Split compound sentences.** If a line has a comma followed by a new idea, it's two lines. One thought per line. No exceptions in spoken copy.
- **Shorter = harder punch.** "Then, give you the smartest way to fix it — FAST." beats the longer version every time. Cut the connective tissue.
- **Kill the warm-up.** First drafts almost always open with a setup paragraph that gets deleted in the final. Lead with what the viewer is looking at, not why they should care yet.

### Spoken rhythm
- **Ellipses are pauses.** Dashes are beats. Italics are emphasis. These are delivery instructions, not decoration. Write the way a speaker breathes.
- **Natural transitions over headers.** "And here's the cool part" beats `**NEXT SECTION**`. The speaker's voice IS the structure.
- **Read it out loud.** If you stumble reading a line aloud, the talent will too. Rewrite until it flows in one breath per line.

### Viewer orientation
- **Name what they're seeing in the first 3 seconds.** Orientation before persuasion. Don't open with philosophy.
- **Branch for the viewer's state.** "If you've already done X... / If you need to do X..." — always address both situations. Tech-light audiences freeze when instructions don't match their screen.
- **Add a safety net after every action step.** One line that removes fear: "If you run into any issues, we'll get you sorted." Reducing anxiety is as important as the instruction itself.

### Stage directions and visual cues
- **Kill brackets.** `[Transition to screen share]` gets deleted every time. The copy should BE the transition: "Let me show you how this works."
- **Only include a visual note when referencing a specific asset** — a link, a file, a demo. Everything else is speakable copy, not a screenplay.

### Emotional close
- **End on feeling, not logistics.** "You're one step away from the best version of this experience" beats "Click submit and you'll receive a response within 48 hours." The last line is the emotion, not the feature.

### Complex ideas
- **Break into 3-5 tiny sequential beats.** Each line sets up the next. The listener absorbs one piece, then the next. Dense paragraphs don't land in spoken copy.
