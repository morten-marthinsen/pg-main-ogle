# Copywriting Principles — Persuasion Craft

These principles govern how to write copy that persuades. They are grounded in how humans process information, form beliefs, and make decisions. They apply to any persuasion deliverable — sales pages, e-comm, VSLs, emails, ads, scripts — and are not specific to any tool, LLM, or workflow.

This document has two tiers:
- **Tier 1: Foundational Principles** — rooted in human psychology. These apply everywhere.
- **Tier 2: Format-Specific Patterns** — how the foundational principles manifest in a particular deliverable type. These live in the skill directories for each format, not here. This document is Tier 1 only.

Principles are extracted from real draft-to-final editing sessions. Each one traces back to a psychological mechanism — not a surface-level rule about word choice or sentence structure.

---

## How Readers Process Information

### Every word the reader has to parse is a word they're not feeling

Ambiguous pronouns, unclear antecedents, and vague references create cognitive friction. In persuasion, friction kills momentum. When a reader has to backtrack to figure out what "it" refers to, they've left the emotional flow you built and entered an analytical mode. Name the thing. "The PG1 Smart System finds your root swing flaw" lands. "The PG1 Smart System finds it" makes the reader pause and scan backward. That pause is where you lose them.

This applies beyond pronouns. Any sentence that requires the reader to hold multiple unresolved ideas in working memory simultaneously is creating friction. Resolve one idea before introducing the next.

### Effort without payoff is a demand — effort with payoff is an invitation

When you describe what the reader will do ("the one thing to work on right now"), you're asking for their effort. People instinctively evaluate effort against reward. If you state the effort without the payoff, the reader's unconscious response is resistance — *why should I?* When you pair it with the payoff ("the one thing to work on right now to get better faster"), the effort becomes a path, not a cost. The payoff doesn't need to be elaborate. It needs to exist.

### The middle ground between system-level and component-level is a blur

When describing a system with multiple components, you have two clean choices: describe the system's purpose at a high level ("builds everything around fixing it"), or give each component its own dedicated space with enough room to land. The middle ground — cramming multiple components into a single clause ("matches you with a coach, and adapts the more you use it") — creates a blur where nothing lands. The reader processes it as a list they're skimming past, not as distinct value. Either zoom out or zoom in. Never split the difference.

## How Belief Forms

### The problem section must define the root cause, not describe the symptoms

The purpose of a problem section is to name the ONE thing the product addresses and make the reader feel it. Symptoms, patterns, and frameworks (like "the Symptom Spiral") are supporting evidence — they validate the reader's experience. But they are not the problem itself. If the reader walks away understanding the pattern of their frustration but not the root cause behind it, the problem section has failed. Define the root cause first. Then use symptoms and patterns to prove it's been operating in their life all along. The root cause is the star. Everything else serves it.

### Analogies earn their place by transferring emotional certainty

The best analogies don't just illustrate a concept — they transfer the emotional weight of something the reader already believes onto something they don't yet believe. "You would never let a doctor prescribe treatment without first running a diagnostic" works because it maps onto a conviction the reader already holds with near-certainty (doctors should diagnose first). That certainty then transfers onto the new claim (golf instruction should diagnose first). The reader doesn't need to be persuaded — they've already persuaded themselves in the familiar domain. You're just pointing out the parallel. Bad analogies illustrate. Good analogies transfer conviction.

But the analogy's power depends entirely on where it lands in the sequence. An analogy that arrives before the reader understands the problem it's illustrating is abstract. An analogy that arrives AFTER the reader has felt the problem — after the counter-sells have shown them exactly how every method they've tried has failed, after the bottom line has named the gap — becomes a gut punch. "That is the equivalent of a doctor prescribing treatment without ever running a diagnostic" hits differently after the reader has just watched YouTube, instructors, apps, and drills all get disqualified for the same reason. The analogy doesn't introduce the idea — it crystallizes what the reader has already been led to feel. Set up first, then analogize.

### A binary frame's length should match its structural weight

A binary frame presents two mutually exclusive outcomes: if you don't do X, bad outcome; when you do X, good outcome. This frame is powerful because it reduces a complex decision to a simple choice. When the binary is the foundational claim of the entire piece — the idea everything else builds on — it can afford to be expansive. Each "no matter how much you practice, no matter how new your clubs are, no matter how many lessons..." drives a nail deeper because the reader needs to feel the totality of the argument before moving on. When the binary is supporting a point that's already been made, compress it — two short contrasting paragraphs, let the structure do the work. The question isn't "shorter is better" — it's "how much weight does this idea need to carry from here forward?"

### In a pain-focused market, weight the frame toward consequences

Know the market's dominant emotional state. A market with more pain tallies than hope tallies in the research is a market that needs the problem to sit in the pain — the consequences of inaction, the money wasted on symptoms, the frustration of working hard with nothing to show for it. Hope comes after, and it should be shorter. If you lead with hope in a pain market, you're speaking a language the reader hasn't arrived at yet. Meet them where they are emotionally, THEN move them. The problem section is where they feel understood. The solution section is where they feel released.

## How Persuasion Sequences

### The reader can only feel a problem through the lens they're currently holding

If the reader's existing worldview is sufficient to feel the problem, you can go straight to it. But when the product addresses a root cause the reader doesn't yet know exists, the problem will land as ordinary frustration rather than devastating revelation — unless you give them the lens first.

A frame claim plants a new belief that transforms how the reader evaluates their own experience. "You have one root swing flaw, and until you fix it nothing else works" is a lens. Once the reader holds it, "instructors gave you different advice" stops being a frustrating anecdote and becomes proof that an entire industry has been treating symptoms of a root cause that was never identified. The same information hits completely differently depending on what the reader believes when they encounter it.

This is why frame claims and problems are different cognitive operations even when they feel related. The frame is educational — it introduces a new idea. The problem is experiential — it applies that idea to the reader's life. Combining them into one section rushes both. The frame hasn't had time to settle before you're asking the reader to feel its implications. When the idea is significant enough, it needs its own room to breathe before you put it to work.

### Give load-bearing ideas their own section

When an idea is important enough to carry structural weight in the argument — like a binary frame, a key proof point, or a paradigm-shifting claim — it deserves its own section with its own headline and its own visual treatment. Burying a load-bearing idea inside another section as a paragraph or blockquote diminishes it. The reader processes it as supporting detail rather than a foundational claim. If the piece would collapse without the idea, give it the real estate it deserves. A standalone section signals to the reader: this matters. Pay attention. Everything that follows depends on this.

### Each section's job is partially to set up the next section

Sections don't exist in isolation. Each one should make the NEXT section land harder. The ATF creates curiosity that the frame claim satisfies. The frame claim creates a worldview that makes the problem section devastating. The problem section creates tension that the solution section releases. When you're writing a section, ask: "What does the reader need to believe or feel BEFORE they hit the next section?" That's this section's job.

### Section headlines must connect to what came before

When each section's headline connects to the previous section's content, the reader feels forward motion — momentum. "The End of Traditional Instruction" followed by "Traditional Instruction Addresses Your Symptoms, NOT The Source" creates congruency. The reader feels the thread. Headlines that exist in isolation — that could appear in any order — signal that the sections are independent modules, not a sequential argument. In persuasion, you're building a case. Each headline should feel like the next logical step, not a new topic.

### Throat-clearing copy exists to make the writer comfortable, not the reader

Throat-clearing is what happens when the writer is transitioning between ideas and fills the gap with words that announce what's coming instead of delivering it. "What I am about to share with you is the reason nothing you have tried has worked" is the writer thinking out loud. The reader doesn't need to be told what's coming — they need to experience it arriving. If a section exists only to announce the next section, it has no function in the reader's experience. It's scaffolding the writer forgot to remove. Cut it. If the transition needs help, the problem is in the section endings and beginnings, not in the space between them.

### The solution section's job is clarity, not cleverness

By the time the reader reaches the solution, the problem section has created tension. The solution releases it. Clever language, poetic headers, and indirect descriptions delay the release and frustrate the reader who is now actively looking for the answer. State what the solution is. State what it does. State the outcome. Then show the steps. A strong line that crystallizes the system ("From knowing your name to knowing your game") belongs at the END of the solution — as a summation of something the reader now understands. At the beginning, before explanation, it's a riddle. Lines that crystallize belong after the explanation. Lines that orient belong before it.

### Don't reveal price before establishing value

Price without context is just a number. It triggers comparison shopping ("Is $399 a lot for a golf app?") rather than desire ("I need this — what does it cost?"). The reader must first understand what they'd be losing by NOT having the product. That understanding is built by the problem section, the solution section, and the proof. Only after those have done their work does price become meaningful. Showing price before value is established invites the reader to evaluate the number against their default frame of reference — which is almost certainly wrong and almost certainly unfavorable.

### Every section serves two audiences simultaneously

At any point on the page, two readers coexist: the one who already has enough information to act, and the one who needs more. The opening must let the ready buyer act immediately (clear CTA, easy path to purchase) while also being compelling enough that the unconvinced reader keeps scrolling. Neither audience should feel ignored. A CTA button without surrounding pricing details serves both — the ready buyer clicks through, the uncertain reader scrolls past without being jolted out of the emotional flow by numbers they can't yet evaluate.

### Headlines do structural work, not just attention work

A headline's job isn't just to grab attention — it's to orient the reader within the argument. "Here's A Fact About Your Swing Flaws" tells the reader: here's something you need to know before I explain the problem. "Traditional Instruction Addresses Your Symptoms, NOT The Source" tells the reader: here's what went wrong, framed through the lens you just accepted. These headlines are doing structural work — they're positioning the section within the sequence. A headline that's clever but doesn't orient ("From Knowing Your Name to Knowing Your Game") leaves the reader guessing what the section is about and where it fits in the argument. Orient first. Be clever second — and only if it doesn't cost clarity.

## How Copy Lands (Sentence and Paragraph Level)

### One thought per line

If a line has a comma followed by a new idea, it's two lines. Compound sentences split the reader's attention between two concepts and neither one lands with full force. In spoken copy this is non-negotiable — the speaker needs one idea to deliver per breath. In written copy it's nearly as important, because readers scan, and a compound sentence forces them to process two things in the space they budgeted for one.

### Shorter sentences carry more authority

"Then, give you the smartest way to fix it — FAST." Short declarative sentences land harder than their compound equivalents because they mimic the rhythm of conviction. People who are certain speak in short sentences. People who are hedging speak in long ones. The copy should sound certain.

### Kill the warm-up

First drafts almost always open with a setup paragraph that gets deleted in the final. The writer needed it to find their way into the piece. The reader doesn't need it at all. Lead with the thing itself, not the preamble to the thing.

### Punctuation is delivery instruction

Ellipses create pauses. Dashes create beats. Italics create emphasis. These aren't decorative — they tell the reader (or speaker) how to experience the rhythm of the sentence. Write the way a speaker breathes.

### End on feeling, not logistics

The last line of any section should be the emotion, not the feature. "You're one step away from the best version of this experience" beats "Click submit and you'll receive a response within 48 hours." The reader carries the last line forward. Make it worth carrying.

### Break complex ideas into tiny sequential beats

If a concept needs explanation, break it into 3-5 short lines where each one sets up the next. The reader absorbs one piece, then the next. Dense paragraphs that attempt to convey a complex idea in a single pass don't land — the reader either skims past or re-reads, and both outcomes break flow.

### Repetition is critical — redundancy is unacceptable

These are not the same thing. Repetition — the golden thread — is when a core message reappears across different sections, in different contexts, doing different work each time. "Root swing flaw" appears in the problem section as the thing you've never identified, in the solution section as the thing the system finds, in the proof section as the thing competitors ignore, and in the close as the thing that makes improvement inevitable. Same phrase, different job each time. That's the golden thread, and it's essential. Without it, the page feels fragmented — each section an island.

Redundancy is when two elements say the same thing the same way. A subhead that says "finds your root swing flaw and shows you how to fix it" followed by a value prop paragraph that says "finds your root swing flaw and gives you the one thing to work on to get better faster" — that's not threading. That's stuttering. The reader processes the second instance as "didn't I just read this?" It signals the writer couldn't commit to the stronger version, so they hedged by including both. Redundancy always dilutes. Pick the stronger version, kill the other, and let the survivor stand alone with full impact.

### Every element must earn its spot

The default is exclusion, not inclusion. Before including any beat — countersell, emotional close, proof stack, future pace, repetition — the piece must be worse without it. If removing it makes the copy tighter without losing anything, it didn't earn its place. This isn't about length. It's about whether each element is doing work that nothing else in the piece is already doing.

### Show, don't tell

Never announce what the reader should feel or conclude. Create the experience that makes them feel or conclude it on their own. "The first win comes fast" is telling. "Shots you couldn't hit last week start landing" is showing. Telling requires the reader to trust you. Showing lets them see it for themselves.

### Counter-sells create the gap — don't trash the competition, outposition it

When widening the gap between the existing solutions and yours, the goal is not to say the alternatives are bad. The goal is to show that they're incomplete in a specific, undeniable way that only your product addresses. "YouTube — great coaches giving great advice... that may or may not apply to your swing" doesn't trash YouTube. It acknowledges its value while making the gap self-evident. Each counter-sell should be one sentence that names the method, respects what it does, and reveals the one thing it can't do — the thing your product does. The reader should finish the list thinking "none of these are wrong, but none of them solve my actual problem." That's a fundamentally different feeling than "these are all scams" — and it's far more persuasive, because the reader knows the alternatives aren't scams and will resist being told they are.

### A sequence of points needs a final punch to land

When you build a case through a series of points — counter-sells, proof elements, examples — each one adds weight. But without a final line that drives the point home, the sequence is like a sentence that stops in the middle. The reader felt the buildup but never got the pop. The individual points created momentum, but nothing converted that momentum into impact. One sharp line after the sequence completes the thought and gives the reader something definitive to carry forward. Without it, the sequence dissipates. With it, the sequence lands.

### Make the reader the subject of the analogy's second half

"That is the equivalent of a doctor prescribing treatment without ever running a diagnostic" is the analogy. "When it comes to your health, you would NEVER do that" turns the reader into the protagonist. "But that's the approach you're taking every single day with your golf game" makes the contradiction personal and present-tense. The analogy alone creates understanding. Making the reader the subject of the second half creates discomfort — the productive kind that demands resolution. The reader can't sit comfortably with the contradiction, so they keep reading toward the solution. An analogy that stays abstract ("this is like that") informs. An analogy that turns personal ("and YOU are doing exactly that") motivates.

### Vague action language sounds like work, not value

"Builds everything around fixing it." "Works to address the problem." "Creates a comprehensive solution." These phrases describe what the SYSTEM does without telling the reader what THEY get. They sound like effort — and effort without a clear outcome triggers resistance, not excitement. The reader doesn't care what the system builds. They care what happens to their golf game. Replace vague action language with concrete outcome language. "Shows you step by step exactly how to fix it" tells the reader what their experience will be. "Builds everything around fixing it" tells them the system is busy. One sells. The other narrates.

### Kill cliches on sight

"Something clicks." "Everything changes." "It all comes together." "Take your game to the next level." These are dead phrases — they've been used so many times they create no image or feeling. Every sentence must do real work. If a phrase could appear in any ad for any product in any industry, it's a cliche. Replace it with language specific to the person, the moment, and the feeling — or delete it entirely.

### Complete the proof point — land the result

When you cite a credential, finish the story. Don't stop at the event — land the outcome. "The doctor who put together Tiger Woods' recovery plan after his spinal fusion" is a credential. "On his way to winning the 2019 Masters" is the proof. The credential earns attention. The result earns belief.
