Most people write project instructions like they're writing a grocery list.

That's exactly why their AI outputs sound bad. Here's the thing - I spent months figuring out how to make project instructions so detailed, so systematic, that they basically run themselves. And once you figure this out, you'll never write weak instructions again.

## **Your project instructions are probably missing 90% of what they need**

Think about the last time you tried to get AI to do something specific.

Maybe you wrote something like "Write a newsletter about productivity tips…" and wondered why it came back sounding like every other generic piece of content on the internet. The problem isn't the AI - it's that your instructions are basically asking someone to build a house with just "make it nice" as the blueprint.

Real project instructions need to be borderline obsessive in their detail.

### **Here's what effective project instructions actually contain**

I used to think project instructions were just task descriptions.

Then I realized they're actually complete operating systems. Every single project instruction document I write now includes:

  * One-line description (what it does in 10 words or less)

  * Important notes (what the user needs to provide)

  * Purpose & overview (why this approach matters)

  * Input requirements (exactly what information is needed)

  * Core guidelines (the actual step-by-step process)

  * Output standards (what success looks like)

  * Quality control process (how to verify it worked)




That's not overkill - that's the minimum.

## **The biggest mistake everyone makes with project instructions**

Many AI writers stuff project instructions with voice guidelines and audience profiles.

Wrong place, wrong time. Your project instructions should focus solely on the task at hand. They're not about who you are or who you're writing for - they're about HOW to execute perfectly every single time.

Think of it this way:

  * Project instructions = The recipe

  * Project knowledge = The ingredients and kitchen setup

  * Your prompt = Pressing start on the oven




When you mix your recipe with your ingredients list, you get a mess.

### **Why systematic beats creative every time**

Creative people hate this part, but here's the truth.

The more systematic your instructions, the more creative your outputs can be. It sounds backwards, right? But when AI knows exactly HOW to approach a task, it can focus all its power on making the content exceptional instead of guessing what you want.

I write my project instructions like I'm training a new employee who's smart but has zero context. Every. Single. Step. Gets. Documented.

## **The structure that makes instructions automatic**

Here's my exact framework for project instructions that run themselves:

  1. Start with the end result (one clear sentence)

  2. List every required input explicitly

  3. Break the process into numbered sections

  4. Include specific examples for each guideline

  5. Add decision trees for common scenarios

  6. Create quality checkpoints throughout

  7. Define exact output specifications




Most people do maybe two of these things.

### **Project knowledge is where your context lives**

This is where it gets interesting.

Your project instructions stay focused on the task, but your project knowledge becomes your context vault. That's where you store:

  * Detailed audience profiles

  * Voice DNA documents

  * Brand guidelines

  * Example content

  * Industry-specific knowledge

  * Your frameworks




The magic happens when clean instructions reference rich context.

## **Stop writing instructions like you're texting a friend**

I see people write project instructions like "make it engaging and use my voice."

That's not an instruction - that's a hope. Real instructions look more like "Each section must open with one of these six specific sentence types" followed by actual examples and when to use each one.

The difference between amateur and professional project instructions is specificity.

### **The test that reveals if your instructions actually work**

Want to know if your project instructions are actually good?

Delete your custom instructions completely. Load your project with just the project instructions and basic context. Run it. If the output isn't 90% of what you want, your instructions need work.

Most people's instructions fail this test spectacularly.

### **Building instructions becomes your competitive advantage**

Here's what nobody tells you about AI.

Everyone has access to the same models. Everyone can write prompts. But the people who build systematic, detailed project instructions? They're the ones producing content that doesn't sound like AI.

Your project instructions become intellectual property. They're the system that nobody else can replicate because they don't have your specific implementation details.

Start treating them that way.
