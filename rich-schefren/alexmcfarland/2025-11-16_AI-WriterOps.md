I’m staring at my Obsidian vault (entire content/context library) and I know something needs to change.

I have two brands running: AI Disruptor (my previous Substack newsletter) and AI WriterOps (my training program). They’re separate. They’re confusing. And honestly, it’s splitting my focus.

Every time I try to explain my value ladder to someone, I have to clarify which brand does what. Not ideal.

So I made a call: sunset AI Disruptor completely. Rebrand the Substack as AI WriterOps Newsletter. Create one unified brand with a clear progression—free content → paid newsletter → training program.

Two days later, the rebrand was live.

I used AI to do most of the heavy lifting. Not by throwing prompts at ChatGPT and hoping for the best. But by using systematic **context engineering** —building structured reference files that tell AI who I am, how I write, and what I’m building, then reusing them forever instead of crafting prompts from scratch each time.

This entire rebrand is proof that the system works.

### **Day 1: The decision and the build**

I started the day with a clear plan: create all the infrastructure I’d need to launch a professional newsletter under the AI WriterOps brand.

That meant writing:

  * Welcome emails for free and paid subscribers

  * A 5-day email course to convert free subscribers into paid ones

  * Landing page copy for people to opt into the course

  * Infrastructure copy like my About page, subscription benefits, and publication description

  * Plus updating my business documentation to reflect the consolidated brand




That’s 15+ pieces of professional content. Normally this would take weeks. Maybe months if you’re doing it carefully.

I gave myself two days.

### **The problem with most AI writing**

Here’s what most people don’t understand about writing with AI.

If you open ChatGPT or Claude with a blank slate and say “write me an email,” you’ll get generic garbage. It’ll sound like every other AI-generated email you’ve seen.

This is AI slop: generic, low-quality AI-generated content.

The reason this happens is simple: AI doesn’t know you. It doesn’t know your voice, your business, your audience, or what you’re trying to build.

So it guesses. And those guesses look like everyone else’s guesses.

### **The solution: context profiles**

Instead of prompting from scratch every time, I build context profiles—structured reference documents (saved as JSON files) that tell AI everything it needs to know about me, my business, and who I’m writing for.

I create these once, then load them at the start of every writing session. AI pulls from them automatically.

Think of it like this: instead of re-explaining who you are and how you write every single time you open ChatGPT, you document it once in a structured file. Then AI references that file whenever it writes for you.

**I use three core profiles:**

**1\. Voice DNA** \- This captures how I actually write. Not generic instructions like “be casual”—but specific patterns like how I open emails (vulnerable hooks, bold statements), how I structure sentences, what phrases I naturally use, my worldview (AI is powerful but 99% of people create garbage with it), and even my contradictions (casual but systematic, young but experienced).

**2\. Business Profile** \- This tells AI what I do, who I serve, what I’m selling, my methodology, my positioning. It includes my newsletter structure, my training program details, my value ladder, what makes me different. Everything AI needs to write about my business accurately.

**3\. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** \- This describes who I’m writing for. Not demographics like “35-year-old marketers,” but psychographics—their frustrations (”my AI content sounds robotic”), their fears (”damaging my reputation with bad AI content”), their aspirations (”build writing systems that scale without quality loss”).

With these three profiles loaded, AI doesn’t write generic content anymore. It writes content that sounds like me, positions my business correctly, and speaks directly to my audience’s pain points.

That’s context engineering.

### **Building the content**

I opened Claude Code (my preferred AI tool for writing) and loaded all three context profiles at the start of the session.

Then I asked it to help me create a 5-phase project plan in a markdown file. 22 distinct tasks. Clear deliverables. Checkbox tracking.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hj6M!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9ed462fc-89aa-41a4-9f54-69a8a366bc47_1502x1604.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hj6M!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9ed462fc-89aa-41a4-9f54-69a8a366bc47_1502x1604.png)The 5-phase project plan with 22 tasks—systematic, not rushed

And I started generating content.

The AI pulled from my voice DNA profile to match my writing style. It referenced my business profile to accurately describe my offerings. It used my ICP to address the exact frustrations my audience has with AI writing.

Within a few hours, I had first drafts of everything.

### **The voice DNA moment (and why this almost failed)**

Around midday, I read through the email course drafts.

They sucked.

Not terrible. But generic. A little cliche. Missing the anti-AI slop positioning I’m known for. They sounded... fine.

This is the moment that makes or breaks AI writing projects.

Most people would stop here. They’d tweak a few words, convince themselves it’s “good enough,” and ship it. And their audience would immediately sense something’s off.

I knew the problem: my voice DNA profile wasn’t capturing enough of what makes my writing distinct.

So I enhanced it.

I pulled techniques from effective emails I’d been studying—vulnerable opening hooks (”There is something really bothering me right now”), conversational validation phrases (”And don’t get me wrong...”), authority pattern observations (”All the professionals I know who write with AI...”), honest tension acknowledgment (”it’s working, but...”).

I updated my voice DNA file with these specific patterns and examples.

Then I regenerated everything from scratch.

### **The rewrites (and why this matters)**

All 5 email course emails. Completely rewritten with the enhanced voice profile.

The welcome emails too—I realized my initial strategy was wrong (automatic course enrollment felt pushy). I pivoted to an opt-in model with a stronger hook about AI slop being everywhere.

**Here’s why this matters:** When you have structured context profiles, regenerating content isn’t painful. It’s fast.

I didn’t have to manually rewrite 5 emails word by word. I improved the source (my voice DNA), hit regenerate, and got better output immediately.

That’s the compounding value of building systems instead of writing one-off prompts.

By the end of the day, I had:

  * 5 email course emails that sounded exactly like me

  * 2 welcome emails (free and paid)

  * Landing page copy with two different versions

  * 2 downloadable tools for subscribers

  * Updated business documentation

  * A published resources system for distributing free tools




Total time: 8-10 hours in a single focused session.

### **The human touch**

The AI-generated content was good. Like, 85-90% there.

But I went through everything and added my personal touches.

Simplified code examples. Tightened language. Added personality.

These small edits—maybe 15 minutes per email—made the content sound even more like me.

AI with good context gets you 85-90% there. The final 10-15% is your unique humanity. That’s the combination that works.

### **What I learned (and what you can steal)**

**Voice DNA is everything**.

When my first drafts felt generic, I didn’t ship them. I enhanced the voice profile and regenerated.

Don’t just accept.

If you’re getting generic output from AI, the problem isn’t the AI. It’s that you haven’t given it enough context about how you actually write.

**Context beats prompts every time**.

I loaded my three context profiles at the start of the session and referenced them throughout. Every piece of content pulled from the same voice DNA, business profile, and ICP.

That’s why 15+ pieces stayed consistent.

I wasn’t crafting individual prompts for each email. I was letting AI pull from structured context that already existed.

Most people spend 10 minutes crafting the perfect prompt for one email. I spend an hour building a context profile once, then reuse it forever.

**Systems let you move fast without breaking things**.

I could have rushed this (yes, even less than 2 days). Could have launched with first drafts and “figured it out later.”

But I built it systematically: 5 phases, clear deliverables, checkbox tracking, enhanced voice DNA when needed, regenerated when it wasn’t right.

That’s how you go fast sustainably. Not by cutting corners, but by building systems that make quality output repeatable.

**The methodology works (proof of concept)**.

This entire rebrand was executed using the methodology I teach:

  * Context engineering (structured profiles instead of random prompts)

  * Voice preservation (enhanced voice DNA when output felt generic)

  * Systematic implementation (project plan, phases, deliverables)

  * Professional output at scale (15+ pieces in 2 days)




I used the methodology to rebrand the business that teaches the methodology.

If it can handle a complete business rebrand in 2 days while maintaining voice quality and professional standards, it can handle your newsletter, your blog posts, your client deliverables.

### **The meta takeaway**

I didn’t get here by finding the “perfect prompt.”

I got here by building context systems that AI can reference.

A voice DNA profile that captures how I actually write.

A business profile that tells AI my positioning and what I’m building.

An ICP that describes who I’m writing for and what they’re struggling with.

When AI has that context, it doesn’t generate generic garbage. It generates content that sounds like me. At scale. Fast.

When the output isn’t perfect, I enhance the context and regenerate. When it needs personality, I add a final 10% of manual edits.

That’s the formula.
