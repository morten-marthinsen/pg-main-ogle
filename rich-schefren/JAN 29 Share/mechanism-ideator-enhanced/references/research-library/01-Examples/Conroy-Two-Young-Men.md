# Martin Conroy - Two Young Men (Wall Street Journal)

> **The $2 Billion Sales Letter**
> **Copywriter:** Martin Conroy
> **Product:** Wall Street Journal Subscription
> **Era:** 1975-2003 (28 years)
> **Revenue:** $2 billion in subscription sales

---

## The Mechanism

### The Story
Two young men graduated from the same college, both about equal in intelligence, ambition, and academic achievement. Twenty-five years later, at their class reunion, both were married and both had children. But there was a crucial difference:

**One was a manager in a small department of a company.**
**The other was the company's president.**

### The Mechanism Question
**What made the difference?**

### UMP (Unique Mechanism Problem)
**Root Cause:** Many intelligent, hardworking people fail to reach their potential - not due to ability, but due to what they KNOW.

**The Hidden Factor:**
- Equal starting points
- Equal effort
- Different outcomes
- The difference must be in KNOWLEDGE

### UMS (Unique Mechanism Solution)
**The Fix:** Stay informed about business trends, economic movements, and opportunities through the Wall Street Journal.

**The Insight:**
The successful man "made it a habit to keep abreast of developments in world affairs... to know when the stock market was right for making capital gains and how economic events would affect all his financial and business decisions."

### UMD (Unique Mechanism Delivery)
**The Vehicle:** Wall Street Journal subscription - "The daily diary of the American dream."

---

## The Copy

### Opening Lines (Legendary)
> "Dear Reader,
> On a beautiful late spring afternoon, twenty-five years ago, two young men graduated from the same college. They were very much alike, these two young men. Both had been better than average students, both were personable and both - as young college graduates are - were filled with ambitious dreams for the future."

### The Comparison
The letter continues to describe two parallel careers, building tension about what made the difference.

### The Reveal
The successful man read the Wall Street Journal. That's it.

### The Close
A straightforward subscription offer with low risk and high value.

---

## Why This Story Works

### 1. The Comparison Mechanism
Two identical starting points with different outcomes forces the question: "What was the difference?"

This is far more powerful than simply claiming "successful people read WSJ."

### 2. Self-Identification
The reader thinks:
- "I could be either of these men"
- "Which one am I more like?"
- "How do I make sure I become the president, not the manager?"

### 3. Story > Claim
A story engages emotions and bypasses skepticism. Claims trigger resistance.

### 4. The Answer Is Simple
The difference isn't luck, connections, or talent. It's something the reader can CONTROL - information.

### 5. Low-Key Presentation
The letter doesn't hype. It presents a simple observation and lets the reader draw conclusions.

---

## Psychological Hooks

### Primary Hook: Fear of Falling Behind
The story triggers status anxiety:
- "What if I end up like the manager?"
- "What am I missing that others know?"
- "Am I being left behind?"

### Secondary Hook: Hope for Success
The flip side of fear is hope:
- "If knowledge made the difference, I can get knowledge"
- "It's not too late for me"
- "This is actionable"

### Tertiary Hook: Self-Improvement Identity
Subscribing to WSJ becomes an identity statement:
- "I'm the kind of person who stays informed"
- "I'm investing in my success"
- "I'm serious about my career"

### Quaternary Hook: Social Proof
The implicit message: "Successful people read WSJ."

---

## The Copywriting Structure

### Setup (First Paragraph)
Paint the picture of two equal young men with equal dreams.

### Plot (Comparison)
Show their diverging paths without yet explaining why.

### Tension (The Question)
Make the reader ask: "What made the difference?"

### Reveal (The Mechanism)
The difference was knowledge, acquired through WSJ.

### Resolution (The Offer)
Subscribe and become the successful man.

## The Swipe File Debate

There's evidence the story structure was adapted from earlier ads:

> "There is strong evidence suggesting the narrative of the letter was swiped from older ads, specifically from ads for The Alexander Hamilton Institute (a popular correspondence school at the time). The ads Conroy was influenced by ran over 50 years earlier."

### Why This Matters
1. Great ideas often build on earlier great ideas
2. The STRUCTURE was swiped, not the exact copy
3. Conroy adapted the principle to a new product
4. This is legitimate creative inspiration

- Ambitious career-focused individuals

## Alternative Perspective

Gordon Grossman in his book noted:

> "What is not mentioned is that the Wall Street Journal had about the least-effective direct mail promotion in the industry."

The argument:
- WSJ had money to keep mailing
- The letter worked "just well enough"
- Success was due to persistence and budget, not just copy quality

### Counter-Argument
Even if true, the letter still:
- Ran profitably for 28 years
- Generated billions in revenue
- Became the most studied letter in history

## Lessons for Mechanism Creation

### 1. Comparison Stories Are Powerful
Instead of claiming your product works, show two people - one who used it, one who didn't.

### 2. Equal Starting Points
Make the subjects EQUAL at the start. This isolates the variable (your product/solution).

### 3. Let Readers Draw Conclusions
The letter doesn't say "you'll be a failure without WSJ." It lets readers conclude that themselves.

### 4. Simple Mechanism > Complex Mechanism
"Read this publication" is a simple mechanism. Simplicity wins.

### 5. Story-ify Your USP
"WSJ keeps you informed" becomes a story about two men's lives.

### 6. Long-Term Thinking
This letter was designed to run forever. It avoided trends and focused on timeless human desires.

## The Lesson of Longevity

The "Two Young Men" letter teaches:

### Timeless > Trendy
The letter avoided:
- Current events references
- Specific technologies
- Dated language
- Pop culture references

### Simplicity Wins
775 words. Simple story. Clear offer.

### Test, Then Scale
Once it worked, WSJ mailed it millions of times without major changes.
