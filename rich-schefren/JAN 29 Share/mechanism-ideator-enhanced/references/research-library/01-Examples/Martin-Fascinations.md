# Mel Martin - Fascinations

> **The Deadliest Bullet Copywriter**
> **Copywriter:** Mel Martin
> **Product:** Boardroom Publications (Books, Newsletters)
> **Era:** 1970s-1990s
> **Impact:** Turned Boardroom into $100M+ operation

---

## The Mechanism

### What Are Fascinations?
A "fascination" is a specialized type of bullet point that creates intense curiosity without revealing the answer.

> "Ten years before writing for Boardroom, Mel Martin pioneered a new and far more lethal brand of bullet called a 'fascination.'"

### UMP (Unique Mechanism Problem)
**Root Cause:** Traditional bullets list benefits. Benefits are nice, but they don't create ACTION. What creates action is CURIOSITY - the need to resolve an open loop.

### UMS (Unique Mechanism Solution)
**The Fix:** Write bullets that:
- Tease valuable information
- Create open loops
- Promise specific page numbers
- NEVER reveal the answer

### UMD (Unique Mechanism Delivery)
**The Vehicle:** Magalogs and sales letters filled with fascinations that drive book/newsletter sales.

---

## The Fascination Formula

### Basic Structure
**[Intriguing Hook] + [Implied Benefit] + [Location Tease]**

### Example Breakdown
**"The best time to give your lawn fertilizer. (Most gardeners do it wrong.)"**

Components:
- **Hook:** "Best time" (implies hidden knowledge)
- **Benefit:** Better lawn results
- **Tease:** "Most do it wrong" (implies you might too)
- **Location:** Page number in the book

## Types of Fascinations

### 1. The Mistake Warning
**"Tires on new cars are usually the wrong size."**

**Why it works:**
- Alerts reader to possible mistake
- Taps into fear emotion
- Creates need to know if they're affected
- Implies protection through knowledge

### 2. The Contrarian Reveal
**"Why painkillers may actually be worse for your health."**

**Why it works:**
- Contradicts common belief
- Creates cognitive dissonance
- Must resolve the dissonance
- Hints at hidden danger

### 3. The Specific Number
**"The 7 foods that burn fat while you sleep."**

**Why it works:**
- Specificity creates credibility
- Numbered lists imply completeness
- Benefit clearly stated
- Creates need to know all 7

### 4. The "What Doctors Know"
**"What your doctor won't tell you about [condition]."**

**Why it works:**
- Authority figure withholds information
- Implies conspiracy/insider knowledge
- Creates us-vs-them framing
- Positions book as access to secrets

### 5. The Common Item Twist
**"Former model gets rid of all her cellulite... with a coffee grinder!"**

**Why it works:**
- Unexpected item creates curiosity
- Simple household object implies easy solution
- Celebrity-adjacent proof
- Must know how this works

## Psychological Hooks

### Primary Hook: Curiosity/Open Loops
The human brain CANNOT tolerate unresolved loops. A fascination creates a loop that can only be closed by reading the content.

### Secondary Hook: Fear of Missing Out
"Most people do this wrong" implies YOU might be doing it wrong. Must know to protect yourself.

### Tertiary Hook: Insider Knowledge
Fascinations promise access to information that most people don't have.

### Quaternary Hook: Specificity
Numbers, page references, and specific details create the impression of real, actionable information.

---

## Why Fascinations Work

### 1. They Sell Information
Fascinations are perfect for selling:
- Books
- Newsletters
- Reports
- Courses

The product IS information, and fascinations tease that information.

### 2. They Accumulate
One fascination creates curiosity. Twenty fascinations create OVERWHELMING curiosity. The accumulation effect drives action.

### 3. They Pre-Qualify
People who respond to fascinations are information-seekers. These are the best customers for publishing.

### 4. They're Evergreen
A good fascination works indefinitely. No need to update with trends or news.

## How to Write Fascinations

### Step 1: Find the Fact
Read through the content and identify specific, useful, surprising facts.

### Step 2: Create the Tease
Write a headline that hints at the fact WITHOUT revealing it.

### Step 3: Add the Benefit
Imply why knowing this matters to the reader.

### Step 4: Include Location
Reference the page number to increase specificity.

### Step 5: Test for Curiosity
Ask: "Would I NEED to know this?" If no, rewrite.

## Lessons for Mechanism Creation

### 1. Curiosity Is a Mechanism
The fascination itself IS the mechanism - it creates an open loop that drives action.

### 2. Accumulation Matters
One fascination is good. A hundred fascinations is overwhelming. Stack them.

### 3. Specificity Creates Credibility
"7 foods" beats "some foods." Page numbers beat vague references.

### 4. Tease, Don't Tell
The moment you reveal the answer, the fascination dies.

### 5. Fear + Hope
Balance "what you're doing wrong" with "what you can do right."

### 6. Test Against "Need to Know"
If readers don't NEED to know, rewrite.

## Fascination Templates

### Template 1: The Warning
"The [common thing] that [negative consequence]. (Page [X])"

### Template 2: The Counter-Intuitive
"Why [thing everyone believes] is actually [opposite]. (Page [X])"

### Template 3: The Number
"The [number] [things] that [benefit]. (Page [X])"

### Template 4: The Secret
"What [authority figures] know about [topic] but won't tell you. (Page [X])"

### Template 5: The Simple Solution
"How to [big result] with [simple/unexpected thing]. (Page [X])"

### Template 6: The Mistake
"The [common action] that [negative result]. Are you making this mistake? (Page [X])"
