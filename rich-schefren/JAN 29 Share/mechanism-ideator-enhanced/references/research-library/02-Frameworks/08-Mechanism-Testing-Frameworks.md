# Mechanism Testing Frameworks

> **Sources:** Various A-list copywriters, Wes Kao (ELU Test), industry best practices
> **Purpose:** Validate mechanism strength before investing in full copy

---

## Overview

Testing your mechanism BEFORE writing saves time and increases success rate. These frameworks help you validate that your mechanism will resonate with prospects.

**Core Principle:** Physical and emotional reactions are more reliable than verbal feedback.

---

## The Eyes Light Up (ELU) Test

**Creator:** Wes Kao (Co-founder of Maven)

### How It Works:

When describing your mechanism to someone, watch for the "Eyes Light Up" moment - that instant when they become visibly excited.

### What to Watch For:

| Positive Signs | Negative Signs |
|---------------|----------------|
| Eyes widen | Polite nodding |
| Lean forward | "Hmm, interesting..." |
| Interrupt with questions | Glazed look |
| Ask for more details | Subject change |
| Want to tell others | Generic acknowledgment |

### The Testing Process:

1. Describe your mechanism to real people
2. Describe it slightly differently each time
3. Notice what makes eyes light up
4. Emphasize elements that resonated
5. Trim parts people didn't care about

### Key Insight:

> "Eyes lighting up is the ONLY reaction that matters. If you ask intellectual questions, you'll get intellectual responses. These are usually not helpful at all."

**The Ogilvy Warning:** "The problem with market research is that people don't think how they feel, they don't say what they think, and they don't do what they say."

### Watch for Patterns:

Do different people light up at the same moment? These patterns reveal your mechanism's true activation moment - the point where they first glimpse the transformation it promises.

## The Cocktail Party Test

### The Scenario:

Imagine you're at a cocktail party. Someone asks you about your product. You describe the mechanism.

### What You're Testing:

**Pass:**
- They ask follow-up questions
- They want to know more
- They seem genuinely interested
- They might mention it to someone else at the party

**Fail:**
- They smile politely and say "Oh, interesting..."
- They change the subject
- They look for someone else to talk to
- Generic acknowledgment

### The Standard:

If your mechanism wouldn't spark a conversation at a party, it won't spark interest in copy.

## The 12-Year-Old Test (Readability)

### The Question:

Could a 12-year-old understand your mechanism?

### Why It Matters:

- Average American adult reads at 7th-9th grade level
- Complex mechanisms create cognitive load
- Simple mechanisms are memorable and shareable
- If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough

### Testing Method:

1. Explain your mechanism in writing
2. Run through readability checker
3. Target Grade Level 6-8
4. Simplify until it passes

### Tools:

- Hemingway Editor
- Readable.com
- WebFX Readability Test
- Flesch-Kincaid calculator

## The 4-Word Test

### The Challenge:

Can you capture your mechanism in 4 words or less?

### Examples:

| Mechanism | 4-Word Version |
|-----------|----------------|
| Leptin resistance causes weight gain | "Leptin blocks weight loss" |
| Cortisol storage mechanism | "Stress makes you fat" |
| SEC filing pattern detection | "Track the smart money" |
| Gut bacteria imbalance | "Bad bugs make fat" |

### Why It Works:

- Forces ultimate simplicity
- Creates memorable soundbite
- Tests if you have a clear mechanism
- Makes it shareable

## The "Tell a Friend" Test

### The Question:

If your prospect understood your mechanism, would they:
1. Remember it tomorrow?
2. Be able to explain it to a friend?
3. Want to tell someone about it?

### The Shareability Standard:

Stefan Georgi: "A good mechanism is like a meme - something that the prospect can hear once, easily share with others, and then remember forever."

### Testing Method:

1. Explain mechanism to someone
2. Wait 24 hours
3. Ask them to explain it back
4. If they can't, simplify

## The Intuitive Proof Test

### The Question:

When they hear your mechanism, do they immediately think "Yeah, that makes sense"?

### What Creates Intuitive Truth:

| Element | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Connects to existing knowledge | "Of course stress affects weight" |
| Uses familiar concepts | "Like a traffic jam in your arteries" |
| Logical chain is obvious | "If A, then obviously B" |
| Matches lived experience | "I have noticed that..." |

### Testing Method:

1. State your mechanism
2. Ask: "Does that make sense?"
3. Watch for immediate nod vs. confused look
4. If they need extensive explanation, mechanism is too complex

## The Differentiation Test

### The Questions:

1. Is anyone else saying this?
2. Have prospects heard this mechanism before?
3. Does it feel NEW to them?

### Testing Matrix:

| Scenario | Verdict |
|----------|---------|
| No one else saying it + Makes sense | Strong mechanism |
| No one else saying it + Confusing | Needs simplification |
| Others saying it + You do it better | Need elaboration |
| Others saying it + Same level | Need new mechanism |

### Research Method:

1. Search for your mechanism terms
2. Review competitor messaging
3. Ask prospects what they've heard
4. Identify if it's truly fresh

## The Hope Test

### The Core Question:

Does your mechanism give them a reason to believe THIS TIME will be different?

### What Hope Looks Like:

- "I've never tried this approach before"
- "This explains why other things didn't work"
- "Maybe this is the missing piece"
- "I want to know more about this"

### Testing Method:

1. Describe mechanism to someone who's tried similar solutions
2. Watch for hope indicators
3. If they're skeptical, mechanism isn't novel enough
4. If they're excited, mechanism has hope potential

## The Proof Requirement Test

### The Questions:

1. Can you prove your mechanism is real?
2. Do you have evidence it actually works?
3. Is the proof credible to your audience?

### Proof Inventory Checklist:

- [ ] Scientific studies available
- [ ] Case studies documented
- [ ] Expert endorsements possible
- [ ] Statistical evidence exists
- [ ] Demonstrations possible
- [ ] Testimonials obtainable

### The Standard:

If you can't prove your mechanism, don't use it. Claims without evidence create skepticism.

## The Focus Group Method

### When to Use:

For high-stakes copy where mechanism must be validated before launch.

### The Process:

1. Gather 5-10 representatives of target market
2. Present mechanism in different ways
3. Observe reactions (not just feedback)
4. Focus on:
   - Readability
   - Emotional response
   - Storytelling style
   - Trust/believability

### What to Watch:

Physical reactions > Verbal responses

---

## The A/B Testing Framework

### For Digital Validation:

1. Create two versions with different mechanism presentations
2. Run to equal audiences
3. Measure:
   - Click-through rates
   - Time on page
   - Conversion rates
4. Winner indicates stronger mechanism resonance

### What to Test:

- Mechanism name
- Mechanism explanation length
- Mechanism placement in copy
- Mechanism proof elements

## Quick Validation Checklist

**Before Writing Full Copy:**

- [ ] Eyes Light Up Test - People get excited
- [ ] Cocktail Party Test - Would spark conversation
- [ ] 12-Year-Old Test - Simple enough to understand
- [ ] 4-Word Test - Can summarize briefly
- [ ] Tell a Friend Test - Memorable and shareable
- [ ] Intuitive Proof Test - Makes immediate sense
- [ ] Differentiation Test - Haven't heard it before
- [ ] Hope Test - Gives reason to try again
- [ ] Proof Requirement Test - Evidence available

**Scoring:**

- 8-9 checks: Strong mechanism, proceed to writing
- 6-7 checks: Good mechanism, minor refinement needed
- 4-5 checks: Weak mechanism, significant revision needed
- 0-3 checks: Start over with new mechanism
