# Terministic Screens and Reality Reframing

> **Core Insight:** Kenneth Burke's concept of "terministic screens" reveals that language doesn't just describe reality - it creates it. The words we use filter and shape perception. Mechanisms work by installing new terministic screens that make the old worldview impossible and the new worldview inevitable.

---

## Overview

Kenneth Burke (1897-1993) was one of the most influential rhetorical theorists of the 20th century. In *Language as Symbolic Action* (1966), he introduced the concept of terministic screens - the idea that all language acts as a screen that selects, reflects, and deflects reality.

**Key Principle:** Mechanisms don't just present new information - they install new ways of seeing that make the mechanism the obvious truth.

---

## The Core Concept

### Burke's Definition

> "Even if any given terminology is a reflection of reality, by its very nature as a terminology it must be a selection of reality; and to this extent it must function also as a deflection of reality."

**Three Functions of Terministic Screens:**

1. **REFLECT** - Language points to aspects of reality
2. **SELECT** - Language highlights some aspects while ignoring others
3. **DEFLECT** - Language actively hides or diminishes other aspects

### The Photograph Analogy

Burke uses color filters on photographs as an analogy:
- Same scene, different filters = different photographs
- The filter doesn't lie - it shows real aspects of the scene
- But each filter emphasizes some things while hiding others
- No unfiltered view is possible - you always see through a lens

**For Mechanisms:**
- The OLD worldview is one terministic screen
- The NEW worldview (mechanism) is another screen
- The mechanism doesn't reveal absolute truth
- It installs a more useful/compelling way of seeing

## Language Creates Reality

### Burke's Core Insight

> "'Reality' could not exist for us, were it not for our profound and inveterate involvement in symbol systems."

We don't perceive reality directly. We perceive reality through language and symbols. Change the language, change the reality.

### How This Works Psychologically

1. **We think in language** - Internal dialogue uses words
2. **Words have associations** - Each term carries connotations
3. **New words create new categories** - Novel terminology creates novel perception
4. **Old words limit perception** - Established vocabulary constrains thinking

### Example: The "Diet" Screen vs. "Metabolic Reset" Screen

**"Diet" Terministic Screen:**
- Reflects: Restriction, discipline, effort
- Selects: Calorie counting, willpower, sacrifice
- Deflects: Hormonal factors, metabolic adaptation, enjoyment

**"Metabolic Reset" Terministic Screen:**
- Reflects: Biology, systems, optimization
- Selects: Hormones, cellular health, efficiency
- Deflects: Willpower as factor, personal blame, restriction

Same underlying reality (weight management), completely different perceptions and emotional experiences.

## Application to Mechanism Design

### Mechanisms as Terministic Screen Installation

Every powerful mechanism installs a new terministic screen that:

1. **Selects** - Highlights the mechanism's focal points
2. **Reflects** - Creates a coherent worldview around the mechanism
3. **Deflects** - Hides or minimizes competing explanations

### The Mechanism Reframe Structure

```
OLD TERMINISTIC SCREEN
[How they currently see the problem]
          |
          v
SCREEN DISRUPTION
[Why the old screen is incomplete/wrong]
          |
          v
NEW TERMINISTIC SCREEN INSTALLATION
[The mechanism worldview]
          |
          v
PERCEPTION LOCKED
[Old screen now seems obviously wrong]
```

### Example: Gundry's Lectin Mechanism

**Old Screen (Conventional Nutrition):**
- "Whole grains are healthy"
- "Beans and legumes are superfoods"
- "Weight gain = too many calories"

**Screen Disruption:**
- "What if 'healthy' foods are actually toxic?"
- "What if your body is responding correctly to a threat?"

**New Screen Installation (Lectin Mechanism):**
- "Lectins are plant proteins designed to harm predators"
- "These proteins punch holes in your gut"
- "Your body gains weight as an inflammatory response"

**Perception Locked:**
- Now whole grains SEEM dangerous
- Now weight gain SEEMS like an immune response
- The old screen now appears naive

## Two Types of Terministic Screens

### Scientistic Screens

**Characteristics:**
- Descriptive focus ("What is it?")
- Black and white categories
- Definitions and classifications
- "This IS or IS NOT X"

**In Mechanisms:**
- Naming the villain (Lectins, Endotoxins, etc.)
- Categorizing the problem
- Scientific terminology that defines boundaries

**Example:**
> "Lectins are a category of plant proteins that bind to carbohydrates. They are NOT digestible by humans. They ARE capable of disrupting cellular membranes."

### Dramatistic Screens

**Characteristics:**
- Action-oriented ("What should we do?")
- Moral implications
- Motivation and agency
- "You should/should not do X"

**In Mechanisms:**
- The implications of the mechanism
- What the prospect should do
- Creating moral urgency

**Example:**
> "Every time you eat whole wheat bread, you're choosing to assault your own gut lining. The question isn't whether you can stop - it's whether you will."

### Optimal Mechanism Design Uses Both

```
SCIENTISTIC SCREEN
[Defines the mechanism reality]
"Lectins ARE..."
          |
          v
DRAMATISTIC SCREEN
[Demands action based on reality]
"Therefore, you MUST..."
```

## Creating Paradigm Shifts Through Terminology

### The Power of Novel Terminology

When you create new terms, you create new categories of thought. Novel terminology:
- Has no preexisting associations to overcome
- Allows you to define the meaning
- Creates proprietary intellectual property
- Signals insider knowledge

### Examples of Mechanism Terminology That Created New Screens

| Term | What It Replaced | Screen Shift |
|------|------------------|--------------|
| "Leaky Gut" | Intestinal permeability | Medical -> accessible |
| "Dirty Gene" | Genetic polymorphism | Technical -> personal |
| "Fat Switch" | Metabolic adaptation | Passive -> controllable |
| "Brain Fog" | Cognitive symptoms | Vague -> identifiable |
| "Erection Muscle" | Ischiocavernosus muscle | Clinical -> actionable |

### Creating Your Own Terministic Screen

**Steps:**
1. **Identify the current terminology** - What words do they use?
2. **Map the current screen** - What does this terminology select/deflect?
3. **Create new terminology** - What words would create your desired screen?
4. **Install the new screen** - Repeat new terminology consistently

## Burke's Definition of Man (Applied to Mechanisms)

Burke defined humans as "symbol-using animals" with several key characteristics. Each applies to mechanism design:

### 1. "The Symbol-Using Animal"

**Implication:** Humans live in a world of symbols, not just things. The mechanism isn't just about the thing (problem/solution) but about the symbols (language) used to represent it.

**Application:** Your mechanism IS the language. Change the language, change the experience.

### 2. "Inventor of the Negative"

**Implication:** Only humans can say "not." We define things by what they are NOT.

**Application:** Mechanisms often work through negation:
- "It's NOT about willpower"
- "It's NOT what you've been told"
- "The problem is NOT what you think"

### 3. "Separated from Natural Condition by Instruments of Own Making"

**Implication:** Our tools (including language tools) shape us as much as we shape them.

**Application:** Once the mechanism terministic screen is installed, it becomes the new lens through which everything is seen.

### 4. "Goaded by the Spirit of Hierarchy"

**Implication:** Humans are always ranking and ordering. We need to know what's better/worse, higher/lower.

**Application:** Mechanisms create hierarchies:
- Old understanding = inferior
- New understanding = superior
- Non-adopters = behind
- Adopters = ahead

## The Rhetorical Power of Reframing

### How Reframes Work

A reframe is a terministic screen shift. It takes the same facts and presents them through a different lens.

**Structure:**
```
SAME FACTS + DIFFERENT SCREEN = DIFFERENT MEANING
```

### Common Mechanism Reframes

| Old Frame | Reframe | Mechanism Effect |
|-----------|---------|------------------|
| "You lack willpower" | "Your biology was hijacked" | Blame externalized |
| "You need to exercise more" | "Your cells can't process energy" | Problem relocated |
| "You're getting older" | "A hidden switch got flipped" | Agency restored |
| "It's genetic" | "Genes can be turned on/off" | Hope created |
| "There's no solution" | "There's a hidden solution" | Action enabled |

### The Reframe Formula

```
OLD SCREEN: [Current belief]
DISRUPTION: [Why it's incomplete/wrong]
NEW SCREEN: [Mechanism-based reframe]
IMPLICATION: [What this means for them]
```

## Language as Persuasion

### Burke on Rhetoric

> "Rhetoric is the use of words by human agents to form attitudes or to induce actions in other human agents."

**Key Insight:** All language is inherently persuasive. You're always selling a worldview.

### For Mechanisms

You're not just describing a mechanism - you're persuading the prospect to adopt a new terministic screen. Every word choice either:
- Reinforces the old screen
- Installs the new screen
- Creates confusion (mixed screens)

### Consistency Is Critical

Mixed terminology creates cognitive dissonance:
- Don't use old-screen words when installing new screen
- Consistently use mechanism-specific terminology
- Create a complete vocabulary for the mechanism worldview

## Practical Implementation

### Terministic Screen Audit

For any mechanism, answer:

1. **What is the current terministic screen?**
   - What words do they use for the problem?
   - What aspects does current language select?
   - What aspects does current language deflect?

2. **What terministic screen does your mechanism install?**
   - What new terminology will you introduce?
   - What aspects will your language select?
   - What aspects will your language deflect?

3. **How will you execute the screen shift?**
   - What is the disruption moment?
   - How will you introduce new vocabulary?
   - How will you maintain screen consistency?

### The Screen Installation Sequence

**Step 1: Name the Current Screen**
> "You've probably been told that weight loss is about calories in, calories out..."

**Step 2: Expose Its Limitations**
> "But that screen hides something crucial - it completely ignores your hormonal response..."

**Step 3: Introduce the New Screen**
> "What if we looked through a different lens - what I call the 'Metabolic Switch' model..."

**Step 4: Make the New Screen Inevitable**
> "Once you see it this way, you can never unsee it. The old model now seems absurdly incomplete..."
