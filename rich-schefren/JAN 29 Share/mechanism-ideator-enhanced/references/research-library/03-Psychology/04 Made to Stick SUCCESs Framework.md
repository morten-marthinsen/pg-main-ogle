# Made to Stick: The SUCCESs Framework for Mechanism Stickiness

> **Core Insight:** Chip and Dan Heath's research reveals that sticky ideas share six common traits: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, and Stories (SUCCESs). Mechanisms that incorporate all six elements become memorable, shareable, and persuasive.

---

## Overview

*Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die* (2007) emerged from Chip Heath's research at Stanford Business School and Dan Heath's work at Duke University. Their central question: Why do some ideas stick while others die?

**Key Principle:** Stickiness isn't about luck or charisma - it's about following specific principles that can be learned and applied.

**Definition of a "Sticky Idea":**
- Understandable on first exposure
- Memorable long after exposure
- Changes perspectives or behaviors
- Shareable and retellable

## The SUCCESs Framework

### S - Simple

**Principle:** Find the core of the message and share it in compact form.

**The Psychology:**
- Cognitive load theory: Working memory holds only 4-7 chunks
- Decision paralysis increases with complexity
- The "Curse of Knowledge" makes experts overcomplicate
- Simple doesn't mean dumbed down - it means finding the essential core

**The Heath Brothers' Definition:**
> "Simplicity isn't about dumbing down - it's about prioritizing. It's not about making messages shorter; it's about making them more compact, like a proverb."

**Examples of Simple Messages:**
- Southwest Airlines: "We are THE low-fare airline"
- Clinton's campaign: "It's the economy, stupid"
- Apple: "Think different"

**Application to Mechanisms:**

Poor Mechanism:
> "Our proprietary supplement uses a combination of berberine, chromium, and alpha-lipoic acid to optimize cellular glucose uptake through GLUT4 transporter upregulation while simultaneously reducing hepatic gluconeogenesis."

Sticky Mechanism:
> "The Blood Sugar Reset Switch"

**How to Apply This to Mechanism Creation:**
1. **The 4-Word Test:** Can you state the mechanism in 4 words or less?
2. **The Proverb Test:** Could this become a saying people repeat?
3. **Find the core:** What's the ONE essential element?
4. **Resist the Curse of Knowledge:** Would a newcomer understand?

**Simple Mechanism Framework:**
```
CORE INSIGHT: [What's the essential truth?]
    |
    v
4-WORD NAME: [____ ____ ____ ____]
    |
    v
ONE SENTENCE: [If they remember nothing else, they remember...]
```

### U - Unexpected

**Principle:** Get attention through surprise and maintain attention through interest.

**The Psychology:**
- Pattern violation captures attention (novelty detection)
- The brain is a prediction machine - violated predictions demand attention
- Curiosity gap: Difference between what we know and want to know
- Surprise triggers dopamine and memory encoding

**Two Jobs of Unexpectedness:**
1. **GET attention** - Create surprise
2. **HOLD attention** - Create mystery/curiosity

**The Curiosity Gap:**
George Loewenstein's Information Gap Theory: Curiosity arises when attention is focused on a gap in knowledge.

To create curiosity:
- Highlight a knowledge gap
- Make them care about filling it
- Promise the information will satisfy

**Application to Mechanisms:**

Predictable (Not Sticky):
> "Eating too many calories causes weight gain."

Unexpected (Sticky):
> "The more you diet, the more your body fights back - there's a 'Starvation Switch' that gets flipped when you restrict calories."

**How to Apply This to Mechanism Creation:**
1. **Violate a common assumption:** What does everyone believe that's wrong?
2. **Create mystery:** What question will keep them reading?
3. **Use contrarian hooks:** "What everyone thinks... vs. what's actually true"
4. **The Flip:** Take a common belief and reverse it

**Unexpected Mechanism Framework:**
```
COMMON BELIEF: [What everyone assumes...]
    |
    v
PATTERN VIOLATION: [The surprising truth that contradicts...]
    |
    v
CURIOSITY GAP: [What question does this create?]
    |
    v
MECHANISM REVEAL: [The answer that satisfies curiosity]
```

### C - Concrete

**Principle:** Make abstract ideas tangible through sensory language and specific examples.

**The Psychology:**
- Abstract concepts overload working memory
- Concrete images are dual-coded (visual + verbal memory)
- The brain processes concrete information faster
- Stories are concrete; principles are abstract
- Sensory words activate sensory brain regions

**The Curse of Knowledge Problem:**
Experts see patterns and abstractions. Novices need concrete examples. The more you know, the harder it is to imagine not knowing.

**The Velcro Theory of Memory:**
> "The more hooks an idea has, the better it will cling to memory. Concrete ideas have more hooks than abstract ideas."

**Application to Mechanisms:**

Abstract (Not Sticky):
> "Inflammation causes cellular damage and metabolic dysfunction."

Concrete (Sticky):
> "Imagine your cells as boats with tiny holes. Every day, they're taking on water. You've been bailing - exercising, dieting - but the holes are still there. This mechanism patches the holes."

**How to Apply This to Mechanism Creation:**
1. **Use sensory language:** Sight, sound, touch, taste, smell
2. **Create mental pictures:** What does the mechanism LOOK like?
3. **Give specific examples:** Not "many people" but "127,543 people"
4. **Use analogies and metaphors:** Connect abstract to concrete
5. **The Tennis Court Test:** Size analogies make scale concrete

**Concrete Mechanism Framework:**
```
ABSTRACT CONCEPT: [The technical reality]
    |
    v
SENSORY TRANSLATION: [What does it look/feel/sound like?]
    |
    v
VISUAL METAPHOR: [Everyday object comparison]
    |
    v
SPECIFIC EXAMPLE: [Real numbers, real people]
```

### C - Credible

**Principle:** Help people believe through internal and external credibility.

**The Psychology:**
- Belief requires a reason to believe
- Authority can be borrowed or demonstrated
- Statistics work when made concrete
- Details signal truth (the Sinatra Test)
- Testable credentials let people verify for themselves

**Types of Credibility:**

1. **External Credibility:**
   - Expert authority
   - Celebrity/aspirational figures
   - Institutional backing
   - Anti-authority (unexpected source)

2. **Internal Credibility:**
   - Vivid details (suggest firsthand knowledge)
   - Statistics made concrete
   - Testable credentials ("see for yourself")
   - The Sinatra Test ("If I can make it there...")

**The "Human-Scale Statistics" Principle:**
Statistics become credible when made relatable:
- "37 grams of fat" = abstract
- "A day's worth of fat in one hamburger" = concrete
- "Your entire recommended daily fat in a single bite" = sticky

**Application to Mechanisms:**

Low Credibility:
> "Studies show this mechanism works."

High Credibility:
> "In a double-blind trial at Johns Hopkins, 847 participants using this mechanism lost an average of 23 pounds over 12 weeks - that's equivalent to 2 bowling balls of fat. You can see the study at pubmed.gov/8474532."

**How to Apply This to Mechanism Creation:**
1. **Stack external authorities:** Credentials, institutions, endorsements
2. **Add internal credibility:** Vivid details, specific numbers
3. **Make statistics human-scale:** Translate numbers into experience
4. **Create testable credentials:** Let them verify for themselves
5. **Use anti-authority when appropriate:** Unexpected sources

**Credible Mechanism Framework:**
```
EXTERNAL CREDIBILITY: [Who says so? What institution?]
    |
    v
INTERNAL CREDIBILITY: [What specific details signal truth?]
    |
    v
HUMAN-SCALE PROOF: [Statistics made concrete]
    |
    v
TESTABLE CREDENTIAL: [How can they verify?]
```

### E - Emotional

**Principle:** Make people care by appealing to identity and emotions.

**The Psychology:**
- People give more to one identified victim than to statistics
- We feel for individuals, not abstractions
- Self-interest is obvious, but identity can be more powerful
- "What's in it for my group?" can beat "What's in it for me?"
- The semantic stretch: Overused emotional appeals lose power

**The Mother Teresa Effect:**
> "If I look at the mass, I will never act. If I look at the one, I will."

Statistics can numb; individuals inspire action.

**Three Emotional Levers:**

1. **Self-Interest (WIIFM):**
   - Benefits for them personally
   - Maslow's hierarchy of needs
   - "Imagine yourself after using this..."

2. **Identity:**
   - What would someone like me do?
   - Group membership and belonging
   - "People like us do things like this..."

3. **Association:**
   - Connect to existing emotions
   - Link mechanism to things they already care about
   - Transfer emotional valence from known to unknown

**Application to Mechanisms:**

Low Emotional Appeal:
> "This mechanism improves metabolic markers."

High Emotional Appeal:
> "Imagine waking up energized instead of exhausted. Imagine your grandchildren asking to play and saying 'YES' instead of making excuses. Imagine finally fitting into those jeans that have been mocking you from the back of your closet."

**How to Apply This to Mechanism Creation:**
1. **Make it personal:** Individual stories, not statistics
2. **Tap identity:** "People like you are discovering..."
3. **Use aspirational imagery:** Future self visualization
4. **Connect to deeper motivations:** Beyond surface benefits
5. **Avoid semantic stretch:** Use fresh emotional language

**Emotional Mechanism Framework:**
```
SELF-INTEREST: [What do they personally get?]
    |
    v
IDENTITY: [What does this say about who they are?]
    |
    v
VISUALIZATION: [Help them see their future self]
    |
    v
DEEPER MOTIVATION: [What's the real emotional driver?]
```

### S - Stories

**Principle:** Stories are flights simulators for the brain. They teach and inspire through simulation.

**The Psychology:**
- Stories are Trojan horses for ideas
- Narrative transportation bypasses counterarguing
- Mental simulation improves performance
- Stories give both knowledge and motivation
- The "Springboard Story" creates action

**Three Story Patterns:**

1. **The Challenge Plot:**
   - Protagonist overcomes obstacles
   - David vs. Goliath, rags to riches
   - Inspires courage and perseverance

2. **The Connection Plot:**
   - Bridges gaps between people/groups
   - Love stories, Good Samaritan
   - Inspires empathy and collaboration

3. **The Creativity Plot:**
   - Mental breakthrough, new understanding
   - "Aha!" moments, paradigm shifts
   - Inspires new ways of thinking

**For Mechanisms, The Creativity Plot Is Key:**
The mechanism discovery story IS a creativity plot - someone had a breakthrough understanding that changes everything.

**Application to Mechanisms:**

Non-Story Mechanism:
> "Lectins cause gut permeability, which leads to inflammation and weight gain."

Story-Based Mechanism:
> "I was performing heart surgery when I noticed something strange in my patient's blood work. For the next five years, I searched for an answer. Then, in an obscure journal from 1973, I found it - a forgotten study about plant proteins that punch holes in the gut lining. That discovery changed everything I knew about medicine..."

**How to Apply This to Mechanism Creation:**
1. **Discovery narrative:** How was the mechanism found?
2. **Character arc:** Who discovered it and what did they overcome?
3. **Transformation stories:** Before/after from mechanism users
4. **Creativity plot:** The "aha moment" of realization
5. **Simulation:** Let them mentally rehearse mechanism success

**Story-Based Mechanism Framework:**
```
THE SEARCHER: [Who was looking for answers?]
    |
    v
THE STRUGGLE: [What obstacles did they face?]
    |
    v
THE BREAKTHROUGH: [How was the mechanism discovered?]
    |
    v
THE VALIDATION: [How was it proven?]
    |
    v
THE TRANSFORMATION: [What changed for others?]
```

## SUCCESs Integration Example

**Before SUCCESs (Technical, Forgettable):**
> "Chronic inflammation caused by lipopolysaccharide translocation through compromised intestinal tight junctions results in metabolic dysfunction and adipose tissue accumulation."

**After SUCCESs (Sticky):**

**Simple:** "Leaky Gut Syndrome"

**Unexpected:** "The 'healthy' foods you eat every day are punching microscopic holes in your gut."

**Concrete:** "Imagine your gut lining as a window screen keeping bugs out. Now imagine someone took a nail and punched hundreds of tiny holes in that screen. Those holes let 'bugs' - toxins - flood into your bloodstream."

**Credible:** "Dr. Steven Gundry, former head of cardiothoracic surgery at Loma Linda University Medical Center, discovered this mechanism after reviewing over 10,000 patient cases. The research has been validated by Yale, Harvard, and the National Institutes of Health."

**Emotional:** "Imagine finally understanding why nothing has worked - and having a clear path forward. Imagine the relief of knowing it was never about willpower. Imagine waking up in 30 days with energy you haven't felt in decades."

**Stories:** "I was a famous heart surgeon, saving lives every day, but I couldn't save my own patients from dying years later from preventable diseases. Then a patient changed everything. Big Ed weighed 450 pounds and was told he had six months to live. I put him on an experimental protocol based on this mechanism. Six months later, he'd lost 150 pounds and his heart disease markers had reversed. That's when I knew I had to share this with everyone..."
