## Source Material Summary

### What Is A Mechanism?

From our source materials, a mechanism is defined as:

**Fran Rengel's Definition:**
> "The logic justifying how your product delivers unique solutions"
> - Process, system, action, technique
> - The logical conclusion (big idea)
> - The vehicle

**Julien Reyes's Definition:**
> "How your product proves your promise"
> "How your problem proves your promise"

**Rich Schefren's Contributions:**
- Redefine problems as symptoms
- Revelation before explanation
- Self-esteem sale is the most important sale
- "Your solution to the problem is going to change the outward context of how people think about the problem"

**The Mechanism Formula:**
```
BIG IDEA = Mechanism + Intrigue Element

Mechanism Components:
├── Mechanism of the Problem (Root Cause) - "It's NOT your fault"
├── Mechanism of the Solution (How it works)
└── Delivery Mechanism (The vehicle)
```

### The UMP/UMS/UMD Framework

| Component | Definition | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **UMP** (Unique Mechanism Problem) | Root cause of failure | "Ball is NOT the bottom of the swing" |
| **UMS** (Unique Mechanism Solution) | The fix | "Perfect strike point" |
| **UMD** (Unique Mechanism Delivery) | The system/product | "Simple Strike Sequence" |

### Types of Mechanisms (by market awareness)

1. **Nudge** - New problem they didn't know about
2. **Affirm** - Known problem + extra piece (Yes... AND...)
3. **Flip** - Reverse the problem into solution (Upside Down World)

### Pleasure Markets vs Pain Markets

| Market Type | Focus | Example |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Pain Markets | Problem mechanism, nightmare story | Weight loss, survival, ED |
| Pleasure Markets | Solution mechanism, superpower | Dating, biz-op, sports |

## Mechanism Scorecard Categories (11 Dimensions - Original)

1. **Image Strength** - Does it evoke a strong mental picture?
2. **Simplicity** - Can a 5th grader understand it?
3. **Proof** - Is there demonstrative, credible proof?
4. **Virality** - Would they tell a friend? Is it shareable?
5. **Ease of Use** - Is it inherently effortless?
6. **Differentiation** - Does it explain past failures?
7. **Embedded Benefits** - Is the benefit in the mechanism name?
8. **Belief Compatibility** - Does it leverage existing beliefs?
9. **Central Thesis Cohesion** - Does it create binary thinking?
10. **Super Power** - Does it promise a tangible superpower?
11. **Emotional Response** - Does it trigger primal feelings?

## Three-Part Villain Structure

Every powerful mechanism needs a clear villain with:

1. **WHAT** - Name the villain explicitly
   - Examples: Lectins, Endotoxins, Dirty Gene, Erection Muscle weakness

2. **HOW** - Explain the attack mechanism
   - Examples: "Lectins break cells → create holes → flood body with toxins"

3. **WHY** - Origin story / evolutionary mismatch
   - Examples: "Introduced 1492 with nightshades, 1950s with modified wheat"

## Root Cause Architecture

### Reframe Structure
```
OLD BELIEF → REFRAME → NEW BELIEF

Example:
"You're overweight because you lack willpower"
→ "Actually, willpower has nothing to do with it"
→ "Your gut is leaky, flooding your body with toxins that force weight gain"
```

### Components
1. **False Causes Destroyed** - List everything they've tried that doesn't work
2. **Scapegoat Identification** - Who's responsible (Big Pharma, food industry, doctors)
3. **Countersells** - Proactive objection handling
4. **Self-Esteem Restoration** - "It's NOT your fault" positioning

## Notes for Research Agent

When conducting research, prioritize:

1. **Concrete Examples** - Actual mechanisms from real copy, not just theory
2. **Attribution** - Who created it, what product, what results
3. **Structure Analysis** - Break down each mechanism into components
4. **Naming Patterns** - Document exact terminology used
5. **Market Context** - What market/audience was this for
6. **Results Data** - Any available conversion/success data
