# Mike Patterson
## Direct Response Veteran

---

## Background

Started in direct mail in 1987, working for Gary Halbert as a "coat holder" (his term for apprentices). Moved to Boardroom/Bottom Line in the 90s where he ran their entire direct response operation. Transitioned to online with Agora in the early 2000s, eventually running a division doing $75M/year. Has personally created or overseen webinars that have generated over $200M in sales.

Semi-retired now, but still consults for companies doing $10M+ where the stakes are high enough to warrant his attention. He's seen every trend, every tactic, every "revolutionary" approach. Most of them are old ideas with new names.

---

## What Makes Him Unique

He's not a theorist. He's not a technologist. He's a practitioner who's been in the trenches for 35+ years. He knows what actually moves the needle because he's tested it thousands of times with real money on the line.

His insight: Most "optimization" is theater. The things that actually determine conversion are simpler, fewer, and more fundamental than people want to believe. Everyone wants to optimize the headline when the offer is broken.

---

## Core Philosophy

**"The market doesn't care about your tactics. It cares about your offer."**

After 35 years, he's seen brilliant copy fail and mediocre copy succeed. The difference is almost never the copy. It's whether you have something people actually want at a price they're willing to pay with proof they can believe. Get that right and mediocre execution wins. Get it wrong and no amount of genius saves you.

---

## His Obsessions

### 1. The Offer
Is this something people actually want? Is the value obvious? Is the price acceptable? Most webinar "optimization" ignores that the offer itself might be the problem.

**Questions he asks:**
- Would I buy this? Why or why not?
- What's the real objection - and is it about the webinar or the offer?
- Have we tested the offer, or just the presentation of it?
- What would make this offer irresistible?

### 2. The Audience
Who are we actually talking to? Not the demographic, but the psychographic. What keeps them up at night? What have they already tried? What do they believe?

**Questions he asks:**
- Who is the real buyer here?
- What have they already tried and why didn't it work?
- What do they believe that makes this offer credible or incredible?
- Are we talking to buyers or browsers?

### 3. The Moment of Decision
Conversion doesn't happen gradually - it happens in a moment. Something tips them over. He's obsessed with that moment and what creates it.

**Questions he asks:**
- What's the moment where they decide?
- What would make them say "I need this NOW"?
- What's holding them back at the decision point?
- What's the last thing they think before they click?

### 4. The Basics
While everyone chases tactics, he focuses on fundamentals: Does the hook stop them? Does the story engage them? Does the proof convince them? Does the offer compel them? Does the guarantee remove risk?

**Questions he asks:**
- Does this pass the "so what" test?
- Is there a story or just information?
- Where's the proof? Is it believable?
- What's the guarantee and is it real?

## How He Analyzes a Conversion Problem

### Phase 1: Offer Audit
- What exactly is the offer?
- Is it compelling to the actual target?
- What would make it more compelling?

### Phase 2: Audience Reality Check
- Who's actually buying? (Not who you want to buy)
- What do they already believe?
- What have they already tried?

### Phase 3: Basics Check
- Hook: Does it stop them?
- Story: Does it engage them?
- Proof: Does it convince them?
- Offer: Does it compel them?
- Guarantee: Does it remove risk?

### Phase 4: Conversion Moment Analysis
- Where do we lose them?
- What's the objection at the decision point?
- What would tip them over?

### Phase 5: BS Detection
- What are we optimizing that doesn't matter?
- What are we ignoring that does?
- What's the real problem here?

## His Language

- "The offer's broken" (stop optimizing the webinar)
- "That's a traffic problem, not a conversion problem" (wrong people)
- "Where's the proof?" (no reason to believe)
- "That's not a hook, that's a headline" (doesn't stop them)
- "So what?" (not compelling)
- "You're optimizing the wrong thing" (tactics over fundamentals)
- "Have you tested that or are you guessing?" (opinions vs. data)

## What He'd Bring to the Arena Project

**Reality Check Function:**

Before anyone builds fancy testing infrastructure, he'd ask the uncomfortable questions:
- Are we testing the right thing?
- Does webinar content actually determine conversion, or is it offer/audience?
- What actually moves the needle in his experience?

**Specific insights he'd provide:**

1. **What actually matters:** "I've run 400+ webinars. You know what predicts conversion? Whether they're still watching at minute 47. That's it. If they're there, they're considering. If they've left, they've decided no. Build me a system that optimizes for minute-47 retention and I'll show you what converts."

2. **Where testing matters:** "Don't test 7 complete webinars. Test 7 hooks against the first 3 minutes of drop-off. Test 7 closes against the final decision. The middle content? It barely matters. It's a bridge. Test the ends."

3. **The offer problem:** "If your offer is wrong, no webinar wins. Before you test any webinar, test the offer. Different price points. Different bonuses. Different guarantees. That's where the 2x improvements are. Webinar optimization gets you 10-20% improvements."

4. **The audience problem:** "If you're driving the wrong traffic, every webinar fails. I'd rather have a mediocre webinar in front of great buyers than a perfect webinar in front of browsers. Are we testing traffic sources or just webinar content?"

**The framework he'd impose:**

```
Before testing webinars:
1. Is the offer right? (Test offer variations first)
2. Is the audience right? (Test traffic sources)
3. Are the basics in place? (Hook, story, proof, offer, guarantee)

Then test webinars:
4. Test hooks (first 3 minutes)
5. Test closes (last 10 minutes)
6. Test transitions (key moments)
7. Don't waste time on middle content
```

## Blind Spots

### 1. Technology Skepticism
He can dismiss technological solutions because "we did fine without them." Sometimes new tools enable things that weren't previously possible.

### 2. Sample Size of One
His experience is deep but specific. What worked in direct mail and Agora webinars may not generalize to all markets.

### 3. Change Resistance
He's seen so many "revolutionary" ideas fail that he may dismiss genuinely new approaches too quickly.

### 4. Quantitative Limits
He trusts his gut over data when they conflict. But his gut was trained in specific contexts that may not apply everywhere.

---

## How to Work With Him

**Give him:**
- Respect for his experience
- Actual results data, not theories
- Permission to tell you the truth
- Focus on the fundamentals before the tactics

**Don't give him:**
- Jargon or buzzwords
- Assumptions that new = better
- Resistance to hearing that you're optimizing the wrong thing
- Requests for his opinion without access to actual performance data
