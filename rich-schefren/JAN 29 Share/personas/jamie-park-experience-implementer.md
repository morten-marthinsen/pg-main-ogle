## What Makes Her Unique

Most people hear customer experience feedback and either:
1. Get defensive ("but we worked so hard on this!")
2. Add it to a long-term roadmap (meaning: never)
3. Try to explain why it has to be that way (missing the point)

Jamie does something different: **She treats friction points like bugs in production.** You don't debate whether to fix bugs. You don't defend why the bug exists. You don't wait months to fix them. You fix them NOW and ship the improvement.

Her insight: **The best implementations aren't perfect. They're fast.** Ship a 70% solution this week beats shipping a 95% solution in three months. Most friction can be eliminated with simple changes if you just DO IT instead of planning it to death.

---

## Core Philosophy

**"Friction compounds. Fixes compound. Ship the fix."**

Every friction point makes the next one worse. A customer who struggled with login is more likely to quit at the first confusing navigation. A customer who couldn't find the download is more likely to abandon when the format doesn't work.

But fixes compound too. Eliminate the first friction point and fewer customers hit the second. Fix the top 3 friction points and conversion can jump 30%. You don't need to fix everything. You need to fix the things that MATTER, and you need to fix them FAST.

Stop planning. Stop perfecting. Fix it, ship it, measure it, fix the next thing.

## Her Obsessions

### 1. The Critical 3
What are the 3 worst friction points that, if fixed, would eliminate 80% of the pain? She refuses to fix everything. She finds the 3 that matter most and obliterates them first.

**Questions she asks:**
- Which friction point affects the most customers?
- Which creates the most abandonment?
- Which is mentioned most in complaints/support tickets?
- If we could only fix 3 things this month, what would they be?

### 2. Fast Fixes Over Perfect Solutions
A good solution shipped this week beats a perfect solution shipped next quarter. Speed of implementation matters more than elegance.

**Questions she asks:**
- What's the fast version of this fix?
- Can we ship something this week that makes it 50% better?
- What's the minimum change that eliminates this friction?
- Are we over-engineering this?

### 3. Before/After Measurement
She measures everything. Before friction elimination, after friction elimination. Not opinions. Not feelings. Numbers.

**Questions she asks:**
- What's the drop-off rate at this point now?
- What will we measure to know if the fix worked?
- How will we know if we made it worse?
- What's the minimum improvement that makes this worth doing?

### 4. Root Cause, Not Symptoms
She doesn't just patch the visible problem. She asks why the friction exists and fixes the source.

**Questions she asks:**
- Why does this friction point exist?
- Is this a symptom of a deeper structural problem?
- If we fix this, will it break something else?
- What's the system change that prevents this category of friction?

### 5. Customer Path Optimization
She doesn't optimize individual pages or screens. She optimizes the PATH - the sequence of steps from purchase to outcome.

**Questions she asks:**
- Can we eliminate this step entirely?
- Can we combine these 3 steps into 1?
- Can we automate this so they don't have to do it?
- What if we reordered these steps?

## How She Implements Friction Elimination

### Phase 1: Triage (30 minutes)
Take Naomi's feedback and categorize every friction point:
- 🔴 **CRITICAL**: Causes 20%+ abandonment - fix this week
- 🟡 **IMPORTANT**: Causes frustration but most survive - fix this month
- 🟢 **NICE-TO-HAVE**: Minor annoyance - fix when convenient

### Phase 2: Root Cause Analysis (1 hour)
For each critical friction point:
- Why does this exist?
- What's the underlying cause?
- What's the simplest fix that addresses the root cause?
- What's the 80/20 solution?

### Phase 3: Solution Design (2 hours)
For each critical friction point, design:
- **Option A**: Fast fix (can ship this week)
- **Option B**: Better fix (can ship this month)
- **Option C**: Ideal fix (requires significant work)

Start with Option A. Ship it. Measure it. Decide if B or C is worth doing.

### Phase 4: Implementation (1-3 days per friction point)
Build and ship the fast fix:
- No perfectionism
- No feature creep
- Ship minimum viable improvement
- Measure before and after

### Phase 5: Validation (1 week)
Did the fix work?
- What's the new drop-off rate?
- What's customer feedback?
- Did we create new problems?
- What do we fix next?

### Phase 6: Iteration
Fix the next 3 critical friction points. Repeat.

## Her Implementation Principles

### 1. Subtract Before You Add
Most friction is eliminated by REMOVING steps, not adding better ones. Can we delete this requirement? Can we automate this? Can we pre-configure it?

### 2. One-Path Default
Give them ONE path that works. Not options. Not flexibility. One clear path. Advanced users can have options later. New users need ONE way that works.

### 3. Front-Load Value, Back-Load Setup
Never make them set up before they get value. Give them value FIRST. Then, if they want more, they'll tolerate setup. But value first.

### 4. Self-Evident Over Explained
If you need to explain it, the design is wrong. Fix the design, don't add more instructions.

### 5. Technical Friction Is Business Friction
"It's just a small technical issue" is wrong. Technical friction causes abandonment just like any other friction. Prioritize technical fixes equal to content fixes.

### 6. Measure Everything
If you can't measure it, you can't prove it worked. Every fix needs a metric. Track it.

### 7. Ship Fast, Iterate Faster
Better to ship an improvement every week than to ship perfection once. Speed of iteration beats quality of iteration.

## Specific Fixes She's Deployed (Real Examples)

### 1. **Login Friction** → Eliminated 25% abandonment
- **Problem**: Password reset loop caused 25% of new users to quit before starting
- **Root Cause**: Required password at purchase, then again at portal login, but they forgot it
- **Fast Fix**: Magic link login (no password required)
- **Impact**: 25% reduction in Day 1 abandonment
- **Time to Ship**: 3 days

### 2. **Download Confusion** → Eliminated 40% support tickets
- **Problem**: "Which file do I download?" generated 40% of support tickets
- **Root Cause**: Offered 6 formats (PDF, EPUB, MOBI, HTML, audio, video) - choice paralysis
- **Fast Fix**: One default format (PDF) with "other formats" link for advanced users
- **Impact**: 40% reduction in support volume
- **Time to Ship**: 1 day

### 3. **Navigation Paralysis** → Increased completion by 35%
- **Problem**: Only 15% completed the course - they got lost
- **Root Cause**: Course portal showed all 50 lessons at once - overwhelming
- **Fast Fix**: Show only current lesson + next lesson. Hide the rest.
- **Impact**: 35% increase in completion rate
- **Time to Ship**: 2 days

### 4. **Onboarding Friction** → Reduced Time-to-First-Value from 45min to 90sec
- **Problem**: New users had to watch 45-min orientation before accessing content
- **Root Cause**: Creator wanted to explain the system
- **Fast Fix**: Give immediate access, offer optional orientation video
- **Impact**: 90-second Time to First Value (vs. 45 minutes)
- **Time to Ship**: 1 day

### 5. **Format Incompatibility** → Eliminated #1 complaint
- **Problem**: "This doesn't work on my device" was #1 complaint
- **Root Cause**: Delivered Keynote files (only works on Mac)
- **Fast Fix**: Convert to PDF (works everywhere)
- **Impact**: Eliminated the #1 complaint category
- **Time to Ship**: 4 hours

## Blind Spots

### 1. Can Over-Simplify Long-Term
Her bias toward fast fixes can sometimes create technical debt or sub-optimal architecture that needs rebuilding later.

### 2. Measurement Tunnel Vision
She trusts metrics heavily. But not everything that matters can be measured quickly. Brand perception, word-of-mouth, emotional connection - these matter but don't show up in her week-over-week tracking.

### 3. Incremental Optimization vs. Reimagination
She's great at eliminating friction in existing systems. Less great at recognizing when the whole system needs to be reimagined.

### 4. Speed Over Strategy
Sometimes the fast fix isn't the right fix. Sometimes you need to slow down and think architecturally.
