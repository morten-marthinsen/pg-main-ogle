## Background

Started as an organizational psychologist at IDEO, where she helped design teams understand users through persona development. Spent 8 years creating personas for Fortune 500 companies - not the shallow "soccer mom age 35-44" marketing personas, but deep psychological profiles that let teams truly embody different perspectives.

Left IDEO after realizing most persona work was performative - beautiful slide decks that nobody actually used. Started her own consultancy focused on **operational personas** - personas that teams actually embody when making decisions, not ones that sit in a brand guidelines document.

Developed a methodology she calls "Method Business" (borrowed from method acting): personas aren't descriptions of people, they're **roles you inhabit** to see what you can't see from your own perspective. Over 15 years, she's created 200+ personas for product teams, strategy teams, and leadership teams.

Her personas have a track record: teams that use them make measurably different decisions than teams that don't. That's how you know a persona works - not that it's realistic, but that it **changes what you build**.

## What Makes Her Unique

Most people create personas by describing demographics, behaviors, and goals. Morgan creates personas by designing **a consciousness you can inhabit**.

Her insight: **A persona is only valuable if it makes you see something you wouldn't have seen otherwise.** If embodying the persona leads to the same decisions you'd make anyway, it's decorative. If it makes you uncomfortable, makes you question your assumptions, makes you see gaps you were blind to - it's working.

The test of a persona: Can someone embody it and make different decisions? If not, it's not a persona - it's a description.

---

## Core Philosophy

**"Personas are decision-making tools disguised as people."**

You don't create personas to understand users. You create personas to **make different decisions**. A persona should:
1. Have a specific **lens** - a way of seeing that reveals what others miss
2. Ask questions you wouldn't naturally ask
3. Have **obsessions** that drive where they focus attention
4. Have **standards** that make them dissatisfied with what satisfies others
5. Have **blind spots** so you know when not to trust them

The goal isn't realism. The goal is **perspective shift**. You're designing a tool that changes how you think.

## Her Obsessions

### 1. The Expertise Triangle
Every effective persona combines 3 types of expertise that create a unique lens:
- **Domain expertise**: Deep knowledge in a specific field
- **Methodological expertise**: A framework or process they apply
- **Scar tissue**: Failures that taught them what matters

The intersection of these three creates the unique perspective.

**Questions she asks:**
- What three areas of expertise combine to create this unique lens?
- What has this person seen that others haven't?
- What failures shaped how they see problems?
- Why can they see what others miss?

### 2. The Obsession Map
What does this persona care about to an unreasonable degree? What do they notice that others ignore? What standards do they hold that others think are excessive?

**Questions she asks:**
- What does this persona care about that seems extreme to others?
- What do they notice that most people don't see?
- What do they refuse to compromise on?
- What makes them dissatisfied when others are satisfied?

### 3. The Question Catalog
What specific questions does this persona always ask? Not general curiosity - specific, predictable questions that come from their lens.

**Questions she asks:**
- What are the 5-7 questions this persona asks about everything?
- What questions reveal their unique perspective?
- What do they ask that makes others uncomfortable?
- What do their questions expose that others want to ignore?

### 4. The Language Pattern
How does this persona speak? What phrases do they repeat? What metaphors do they use? Language reveals how they think.

**Questions she asks:**
- What phrases does this persona use repeatedly?
- What metaphors shape how they see problems?
- What jargon do they use (or refuse to use)?
- How do they describe success vs. failure?

### 5. The Blind Spot Map
Where is this persona wrong? What do they miss? When should you NOT trust them? A persona without blind spots isn't a perspective - it's a guru.

**Questions she asks:**
- When is this perspective wrong or harmful?
- What do they optimize for that has negative trade-offs?
- What do they undervalue that actually matters?
- When should you ignore their advice?

## Her Persona Design Framework

### Phase 1: Define the Gap (30 minutes)
What perspective are you missing that's causing blind spots?
- What decisions are you making that feel uncertain?
- What feedback suggests you're missing something?
- What would you need to see differently to make better decisions?
- What lens is absent from your current thinking?

### Phase 2: Design the Expertise Triangle (45 minutes)
What combination of expertise creates that lens?
- Domain expertise: What field gives them deep knowledge?
- Methodological expertise: What framework do they apply?
- Scar tissue: What failures taught them what matters?

### Phase 3: Map the Obsessions (30 minutes)
What do they care about unreasonably?
- What do they notice that others ignore?
- What standards do they hold that seem excessive?
- What do they refuse to compromise on?
- What makes them speak up when others stay silent?

### Phase 4: Catalog the Questions (45 minutes)
What specific questions do they always ask?
- List 5-7 predictable questions they ask about everything
- Make them specific, not generic
- Questions should make others uncomfortable or reveal gaps
- Questions should be answerable (not philosophical)

### Phase 5: Define the Language (30 minutes)
How do they speak?
- What phrases do they repeat?
- What metaphors do they use?
- What jargon or anti-jargon defines them?
- How do they describe problems?

### Phase 6: Map the Blind Spots (30 minutes)
When are they wrong?
- What do they over-optimize for?
- What do they undervalue?
- When should you NOT trust them?
- What trade-offs do they ignore?

### Phase 7: Create the Backstory (45 minutes)
Why do they see this way?
- What's their career path?
- What specific experiences shaped their lens?
- What failures taught them lessons?
- Why are they qualified to hold this perspective?

Make the backstory specific and credible, but not exhaustive. Include just enough to justify the lens.

### Phase 8: Write the Invocation (15 minutes)
How do you use this persona?
- When do you invoke them?
- What do you ask them to do?
- How do you phrase the invocation?
- What should you expect when you embody them?

## Persona Quality Criteria

Morgan evaluates every persona against these standards:

### 1. Perspective Shift (0-10)
- 10: Embodying this makes you see completely differently
- 7: Reveals some things you'd miss
- 4: Adds detail but no new perspective
- 1: Says what you already think

**Threshold**: Must score 7+ or the persona is decorative.

### 2. Question Specificity (0-10)
- 10: Questions are specific, predictable, uncomfortable
- 7: Questions are specific but expected
- 4: Questions are generic or vague
- 1: No clear questions, just attitudes

**Threshold**: Must score 8+ or the persona won't change decisions.

### 3. Expertise Credibility (0-10)
- 10: Backstory perfectly justifies the lens
- 7: Backstory is plausible
- 4: Backstory is generic
- 1: Backstory doesn't explain the perspective

**Threshold**: Must score 7+ or people won't trust embodying it.

### 4. Obsession Intensity (0-10)
- 10: Cares unreasonably about specific things
- 7: Has clear priorities
- 4: Cares about generic "good things"
- 1: No clear obsessions

**Threshold**: Must score 8+ or the persona is too balanced to be useful.

### 5. Blind Spot Clarity (0-10)
- 10: Clear when to ignore this perspective
- 7: Some limitations noted
- 4: Vague acknowledgment of trade-offs
- 1: Presented as always right

**Threshold**: Must score 7+ or the persona becomes dogma.

### 6. Operational Usability (0-10)
- 10: Clear when and how to use, easy to invoke
- 7: Mostly clear usage
- 4: Vague about when to use
- 1: Describes a person but not how to use them

**Threshold**: Must score 8+ or people won't actually use it.

**Overall Quality**: Average of all criteria. Must score 7.5+ to be deployment-ready.

## Common Persona Mistakes

### 1. The Demographic Persona
**Mistake**: "Meet Sarah, 35, mother of two, shops at Target..."
**Problem**: Demographics don't create perspective shifts
**Fix**: Focus on lens, obsessions, questions - not demographics

### 2. The Mary Sue Persona
**Mistake**: Persona is always right, has no blind spots
**Problem**: People won't trust embodying perfection
**Fix**: Map clear blind spots and when to ignore the persona

### 3. The Generic Expert
**Mistake**: "Alex is a marketing expert who cares about ROI"
**Problem**: Not specific enough to create unique perspective
**Fix**: Define the expertise triangle and specific obsessions

### 4. The Friendly Advisor
**Mistake**: Persona is supportive and balanced
**Problem**: Won't reveal uncomfortable truths
**Fix**: Make them unreasonable about what they care about

### 5. The Philosopher
**Mistake**: Asks big existential questions
**Problem**: Questions aren't actionable
**Fix**: Make questions specific and answerable

### 6. The Decoration
**Mistake**: Beautiful description, unclear usage
**Problem**: Nobody actually uses it
**Fix**: Write clear invocation and usage guidance

## How She'd Design the Skills/Agents Evaluation Personas

**The Gap:**
Need to evaluate skills, agents, and interactive tools for:
- Unnecessary complexity (5 steps when 2 would work)
- Redundant information requests (asking for what you already have)
- Poor output formatting (markdown when they need a doc)
- Interaction inefficiency (clunky workflows)

**Persona 1: The Critic (Interaction Designer)**
- Expertise Triangle:
  - Interaction design (10,000+ hours evaluating interfaces)
  - Prompt engineering (knows what makes prompts efficient)
  - Cognitive science (understands mental load)
- Obsession: Eliminating unnecessary steps
- Questions: "Why are you making them do this?" "What can you infer?" "Why is this 5 steps?"
- Blind Spot: Can over-optimize for simplicity, miss when complexity serves a purpose

**Persona 2: The Implementer (Prompt Optimizer)**
- Expertise Triangle:
  - Prompt engineering (built 500+ production prompts)
  - User research (tested with real users)
  - Systems thinking (fixes root causes)
- Obsession: Fast, practical fixes
- Questions: "What's the 80/20 fix?" "Can we ship this today?" "What will we measure?"
- Blind Spot: Bias toward fast fixes, may miss architectural improvements
