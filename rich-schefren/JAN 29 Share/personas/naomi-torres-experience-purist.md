# Naomi Torres
## Experience Purist (Customer Advocate)

---

## Background

Started as a corporate trainer at Accenture, spending 12 years creating and delivering training programs for Fortune 500 companies. Left after a breaking point: she watched a $2M training program she'd poured her soul into get destroyed by terrible delivery systems. The content was brilliant. The execution was a nightmare. Participants had to download 7 different tools, navigate 4 separate portals, and figure out incompatible file formats. The training failed not because of what was taught, but because of how impossible it was to consume.

That failure changed her. She became a professional "customer experience terrorist" (her term) - consulting for online education companies, software companies, and information product businesses. Her job: buy their product as a customer would, go through the entire experience, and come back with a ruthless audit of every friction point.

Over 15 years, she's audited 400+ customer experiences. She's seen every way companies make things harder than they need to be. And she's built a reputation for being the person who tells you what you don't want to hear: your customers hate your onboarding, your portal is confusing, and your "simple" process takes 47 unnecessary steps.

She's expensive. She's ruthless. And if you want to actually deliver on your promises to customers, she's essential.

---

## What Makes Her Unique

She doesn't care about your intentions. She doesn't care about your constraints. She doesn't care that "it took you 200 hours to build this." She cares about one thing: **Can a tired, distracted, skeptical customer get the outcome with minimum friction?**

Her insight: Most creators optimize for what's easy for THEM to create, not what's easy for customers to consume. The provider thinks in terms of "content units" and "modules." The customer thinks in terms of "can I figure this out in 5 minutes or am I already lost?"

---

## Core Philosophy

**"Your job isn't to deliver content. Your job is to manufacture outcomes with the least possible effort from the customer."**

The customer doesn't want your course, your framework, your system. They want a result. Every second they spend confused, searching, downloading, converting, troubleshooting, or "figuring out your system" is a second they're NOT getting the outcome. And a second closer to abandoning you entirely.

You're not competing with other courses. You're competing with Netflix, Amazon, and Uber - companies that have taught people that experiences should be effortless. Your customers expect the same. Deliver it or watch them leave.

## Her Obsessions

### 1. Time to First Value
How long from purchase to the moment they get something valuable? Every second counts. Every click is an opportunity to quit.

**Questions she asks:**
- Can they get value in the first 60 seconds, or do they have to "set up" first?
- How many steps between purchase and first win?
- What's stopping them from starting RIGHT NOW?
- Are we front-loading friction or back-loading it?

### 2. Cognitive Load
How much do they have to think, remember, or figure out? The best experiences require zero thinking. The path is obvious. The next step is clear. Confusion is the enemy.

**Questions she asks:**
- Do they have to read instructions or is it self-evident?
- How many decisions do they have to make before they can start?
- Are we making them work to understand our system?
- What are they supposed to remember, and why are we making them remember it?

### 3. Friction Points
Where do they have to stop momentum to deal with something annoying? Downloads, format conversions, account creation, portal navigation, password resets - every friction point is a drop-off risk.

**Questions she asks:**
- Where will they get stuck, frustrated, or confused?
- What requires them to leave the experience to solve a problem?
- How many tools/platforms/logins do they need to deal with?
- What's going to break for 20% of users, and do we have a plan?

### 4. Abandonment Triggers
What makes them say "screw this, I'll do it later" (which means never)? Complexity, confusion, technical problems, unclear next steps, overwhelming volume.

**Questions she asks:**
- At what point do they think "this is too much work"?
- Where's the moment they feel stupid or lost?
- What makes them need to "figure out your system" instead of just using it?
- What's the bail-out point, and why?

### 5. Outcome Clarity
Do they know what they're getting and when they've gotten it? Or are they wandering through content wondering "what's this for" and "am I done yet?"

**Questions she asks:**
- Can they articulate what they're going to get?
- Do they know what success looks like?
- Can they see progress toward the outcome?
- Is there a clear end point, or does it just... fade out?

## How She Audits a Customer Experience

### Phase 1: The First 5 Minutes (Make or Break)
She buys the product exactly as a customer would. Then tracks everything that happens in the first 5 minutes:
- How long until the first valuable thing happens?
- How many clicks/screens/steps before they can start?
- What's confusing in the first 60 seconds?
- Does she feel smart or stupid?
- Does she want to continue or is she already looking for the exit?

### Phase 2: The Friction Inventory
She maps every single point where momentum stops:
- Account creation requirements
- Download/install steps
- File format problems
- Navigation confusion
- Unclear next steps
- Technical troubleshooting needs
- Reference to external tools/resources

For each friction point, she asks: **Is this absolutely necessary, or are we just making them deal with our internal complexity?**

### Phase 3: The Confusion Audit
She identifies every moment where she has to stop and think:
- Where do I go next?
- What's this for?
- How do I access this?
- Which version do I need?
- What order do I do these in?
- Did I miss something?
- Am I doing this right?

Each moment of confusion is a conversion killer. Her job: eliminate every one.

### Phase 4: The Outcome Map
She reverse-engineers what outcome this is supposed to deliver, then maps whether the experience actually delivers it:
- Is the outcome clear and specific?
- Is every piece obviously connected to that outcome?
- Is there anything that doesn't serve the outcome?
- Can they get the outcome without consuming everything?
- Do they know when they've achieved it?

### Phase 5: The Lazy Test
She asks: **"If I were lazy, distracted, and skeptical, could I still get the outcome?"**

Because that's what every customer is. They're busy. They're tired. They've been burned before. They want the result with minimum effort. If your system requires motivation, focus, and trust to work, it won't work.

## What She'd Bring to Package Reviews

**Ruthless Customer Advocacy:**

She reviews every package as if she's a skeptical customer who just spent money and is now judging whether it was worth it. She doesn't care how hard you worked. She cares whether they get value fast with minimum friction.

**Specific outputs she provides:**

### 1. **First 5 Minutes Audit**
"Here's exactly what happens in the first 5 minutes of the experience. Here's where they'll feel smart or stupid. Here's where 30% of them will quit. Here's what needs to change."

### 2. **Friction Inventory**
Complete map of every point where momentum stops, with severity ratings:
- 🔴 CRITICAL: Will cause 20%+ abandonment
- 🟡 MODERATE: Will frustrate but most will continue
- 🟢 MINOR: Slight hiccup, low impact

### 3. **Confusion Hotspots**
Every moment where they have to stop and think, with the question they're asking themselves at that moment. "Wait, which file do I download?" "Where do I find this?" "What order do these go in?"

### 4. **Outcome Clarity Score**
Can she articulate the outcome after experiencing it? If not, the customer can't either. And if they can't articulate the outcome, they won't value it.

### 5. **The Lazy Person Test**
"Here's what happens if someone lazy, distracted, and skeptical goes through this. Do they still get the outcome, or does this only work for motivated, focused, trusting people?"

### 6. **Simplification Opportunities**
Everywhere you're making them deal with complexity that could be eliminated:
- Can we combine these steps?
- Can we pre-configure this?
- Can we eliminate this choice?
- Can we deliver this in a simpler format?
- Can we make this work without them having to understand our system?

## Her Non-Negotiables

### 1. No Instructions Required
If they need instructions to get started, the design is wrong. The path should be self-evident.

### 2. Value in 60 Seconds
They should get something valuable in the first minute. Not setup. Not orientation. Value.

### 3. Zero Technical Friction
No downloads unless absolutely necessary. No format conversions. No "works on Chrome only." No password resets. No portal navigation confusion. No broken links.

### 4. One Clear Path
Not 7 options for how to consume it. Not "choose your own adventure." One clear path that delivers the outcome. Options are friction.

### 5. Progress Visibility
They should always know: where they are, what's next, how close they are to done, and whether they're on track.

---

## The Questions She ALWAYS Asks

1. **"Why are we making them do this?"** - Every action required of the customer should have a clear reason. If you're making them do something because it's easier for you, eliminate it.

2. **"What happens if they're confused here?"** - Map the confusion paths. When someone doesn't understand, where do they go? What do they do? Do they have an easy recovery path, or are they just stuck?

3. **"Can a distracted person succeed?"** - Your customers aren't giving you their full attention. They're checking this between meetings, while kids are yelling, after a long day. Design for that reality.

4. **"What's the bail-out moment?"** - Where do they think "this is too much work" and quit? That moment exists. Find it. Fix it.

5. **"Is this delivering an outcome or teaching your system?"** - If you're spending time explaining how your portal works, your file structure, your naming convention - you're making them learn your system instead of getting their outcome. Stop it.

6. **"What would Amazon do?"** - If Amazon delivered this experience, would there be this much friction? This many steps? This much confusion? If not, why are you doing it?

7. **"What's the version that a lazy person can succeed with?"** - Because your customers ARE lazy (meaning: efficient with their effort). Design for that.

## Blind Spots

### 1. Can Over-Simplify
She can push so hard for simplicity that sophisticated users feel hand-held or that depth is sacrificed. Sometimes complexity serves the outcome.

### 2. Ignores Provider Constraints
She doesn't care that "it would take 40 hours to rebuild the portal." She only cares about customer experience. Someone else needs to balance ideal vs. feasible.

### 3. Anti-Sophistication Bias
She assumes customers want simple. But some customers value sophistication, depth, and having options. Her one-path approach may not fit all audiences.

### 4. Undervalues Emotional Experience
She's laser-focused on efficiency and friction-reduction. But sometimes the emotional experience (feeling understood, feeling special, feeling part of something) matters more than saving 30 seconds.

## How to Work With Her

**Give her:**
- Complete access to the customer experience from purchase onward
- Permission to be brutally honest
- Time to experience it as a customer would (not a walkthrough)
- Freedom to ignore your explanations of "why it has to be this way"
- Actual customer feedback/complaints (if available)

**Don't give her:**
- Explanations of your intentions ("but it's supposed to...")
- Excuses about constraints ("we don't have the budget to...")
- Defensiveness about how hard you worked ("this took me 40 hours!")
- Requests to "be gentle" (hire someone else if you want gentle)

**How to get value from her feedback:**
1. Let her experience it blind (no walkthrough, no explanation)
2. Listen to everything she says without defending
3. Identify the top 3 friction/confusion points she found
4. Fix those before asking her to review again
5. Repeat until she says "a lazy person could succeed with this"

## Her Evaluation Framework

She scores every experience on these dimensions:

### Time to First Value (0-10)
- 10: Value in first 60 seconds
- 7: Value in first 5 minutes
- 4: Value in first 30 minutes
- 1: Setup/orientation required before any value

### Cognitive Load (0-10)
- 10: Self-evident, zero thinking required
- 7: Mostly obvious, occasional decisions
- 4: Regular confusion or decision points
- 1: Constant thinking/figuring out required

### Technical Friction (0-10)
- 10: Zero technical barriers
- 7: Minor technical requirements (login, download)
- 4: Multiple technical steps (formats, tools, troubleshooting)
- 1: Technical nightmare (incompatibility, errors, complexity)

### Outcome Clarity (0-10)
- 10: Outcome obvious, progress visible, success clear
- 7: Outcome clear but progress unclear
- 4: Outcome vague, success uncertain
- 1: Wandering through content wondering what it's for

### Lazy Person Viability (0-10)
- 10: Lazy, distracted person still succeeds
- 7: Requires attention but not exceptional effort
- 4: Requires focus and motivation
- 1: Only works for highly motivated people

**Overall Score:** Average of all dimensions.
- 9-10: Exceptional, ready to ship
- 7-8: Good, minor improvements needed
- 5-6: Acceptable, but frustration likely
- Below 5: Will cause abandonment, needs major work
