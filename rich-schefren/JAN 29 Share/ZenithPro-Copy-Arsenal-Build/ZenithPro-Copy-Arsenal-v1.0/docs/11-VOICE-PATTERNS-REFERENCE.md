# Voice Patterns Reference
## How Each Master Sounds—And When to Use Each Voice

---

## Why Voice Matters

The same message delivered in different voices produces dramatically different results. Each copywriter has a distinctive voice developed over decades of testing.

Match voice to audience. Mismatch kills conversions.

---

## Clayton Makepeace Voice

### The Sound
- **Intensity:** High emotional charge
- **Directness:** Says exactly what he means
- **Authority:** Speaks from proven expertise
- **Urgency:** Everything matters NOW

### Voice Characteristics
```
✓ Bold statements
✓ Confrontational when needed
✓ Proof-heavy assertions
✓ Emotional crescendos
✓ Strong imperatives
```

### Example
> "Listen to me carefully, because what I'm about to share could be the difference between struggling for another five years and finally breaking through to the income you deserve.
>
> I've seen too many smart, talented people stay broke because they believed the lies the gurus told them. That ends today."

### Best For
- Health/supplement markets
- Financial services
- High-emotion purchases
- Audiences in pain or fear
- Products requiring conviction

### Use When
- [ ] Audience is emotional, not analytical
- [ ] Product solves urgent problem
- [ ] Strong proof is available
- [ ] Competition is weak/generic
- [ ] Need to overcome skepticism with evidence

## John Carlton Voice

### The Sound
- **Conversational:** Like talking to a smart friend at a bar
- **Irreverent:** Breaks rules intentionally
- **Surprising:** Says the unexpected
- **Story-driven:** Everything's a narrative

### Voice Characteristics
```
✓ Casual, conversational tone
✓ Pattern interrupts
✓ Self-deprecating humor
✓ "A-Brain" triggers (primitive/visceral)
✓ Unexpected word choices
```

### Example
> "Look, I know you've probably read a hundred of these things. And 99 of them were pure garbage written by some kid who's never actually sold anything.
>
> So let me just tell you a quick story about a one-legged golfer I met in a bar in Reno. Yeah, I know how that sounds. Stick with me."

### Best For
- Golf/sports markets
- Self-improvement
- Markets tired of "marketing speak"
- Products needing differentiation
- Audiences who value authenticity

### Use When
- [ ] Audience is jaded, seen everything
- [ ] Need to stand out from competitors
- [ ] Product has an interesting story
- [ ] Market responds to personality
- [ ] Want memorability over formality

## David Deutsch Voice

### The Sound
- **Sophisticated:** Elevated but accessible
- **Thoughtful:** Takes time to build the case
- **Story-woven:** Narrative carries the logic
- **Trusted advisor:** Guidance, not selling

### Voice Characteristics
```
✓ Elegant sentence structure
✓ Parallel construction
✓ Story-logic flow
✓ Empathy before assertion
✓ "Show don't tell" approach
```

### Example
> "There's a moment in every successful person's journey—you may have experienced it yourself—when something shifts.
>
> Not a dramatic epiphany. More like a quiet recognition. You realize that everything you thought you knew about [topic] was slightly, but crucially, off.
>
> And in that recognition, everything changes."

### Best For
- B2B/professional services
- Sophisticated/educated audiences
- High-ticket offers
- Consulting/coaching
- Products requiring trust

### Use When
- [ ] Audience distrusts "marketing"
- [ ] Buyers are educated/sophisticated
- [ ] Decision has career/business implications
- [ ] Need to build trust first
- [ ] Product is premium/high-ticket

---

## Evaldo Albuquerque Voice

### The Sound
- **Methodical:** Builds belief brick by brick
- **Curious:** Opens loops, creates questions
- **Conversational:** Feels like explanation, not selling
- **Patient:** Takes time to establish each point

### Voice Characteristics
```
✓ Question-based structure
✓ Open loops throughout
✓ Mechanism revelation
✓ "Discovery" framing
✓ One belief focus
```

### Example
> "Have you ever wondered why some people seem to succeed effortlessly while others struggle year after year?
>
> I used to think it was luck. Or connections. Or some talent I didn't have.
>
> But then I discovered something that changed everything. A simple mechanism that explains why 97% of people fail—and how the other 3% quietly win."

### Best For
- Video Sales Letters
- Skeptical audiences
- Info products
- Health/wealth niches
- Products with a mechanism to explain

### Use When
- [ ] Audience is skeptical/burned before
- [ ] Need to build belief gradually
- [ ] Product has a "secret" or mechanism
- [ ] VSL is the format
- [ ] Price requires belief-building first

## Voice Selection Matrix

| Audience Type | Primary Voice | Why |
|---------------|---------------|-----|
| **In pain/desperate** | Clayton | Emotional intensity matches their state |
| **Jaded/cynical** | Carlton | Pattern interrupts break through |
| **Sophisticated/educated** | Deutsch | Respects their intelligence |
| **Skeptical/burned** | Evaldo | Gradual belief building works |
| **Action-oriented** | Clayton | Direct call to action |
| **Story-lovers** | Deutsch or Carlton | Narrative strength |
| **Analysis-paralysis** | Clayton | Forces decision |

## Mixing Voices (Advanced)

Sometimes the best approach blends voices:

### Carlton Opening → Evaldo Body
- Hook them with a pattern interrupt
- Then build belief methodically

### Deutsch Opening → Clayton Close
- Build trust with sophistication
- Then drive action with intensity

### Evaldo Structure → Clayton Proof
- Use belief-building architecture
- Supercharge with proof cascades

---

## Voice Diagnostic Questions

Ask yourself:

1. **How does my audience talk to themselves?**
   → Match that internal voice

2. **What have they heard before?**
   → Distinguish from what they're tired of

3. **What's their sophistication level?**
   → Don't talk up or down to them

4. **What state are they in emotionally?**
   → Match the energy they need

5. **What's the relationship we're building?**
   → Authority vs. peer vs. advisor

## Quick Voice Commands

**To write in Clayton's voice:**
```
/clayton-voice
[Your content]
Rewrite in Clayton's emotional, proof-heavy voice.
```

**To write in Carlton's voice:**
```
/carlton-voice
[Your content]
Rewrite in Carlton's conversational, irreverent voice.
```

**To write in Deutsch's voice:**
```
/deutsch-voice
[Your content]
Rewrite in Deutsch's sophisticated, story-driven voice.
```

**To diagnose which voice to use:**
```
Audience: [Description]
Product: [What you're selling]
Current copy: [Sample]

Which copywriter's voice would work best for this audience?
```
