Also informed by Luke Burgis's *Wanting* (2021), which translated Girard's core ideas into business language.

**Premise:** AI has access to more information about your external market than any human could process --- every competitor's public positioning, every influencer's content output, every conversation thread on social platforms, every review, every job listing, every podcast appearance, every pricing page change. Girard said desire is borrowed from models. AI can now map the entire model network your prospects swim in, track what desires are propagating through it, and show you exactly where your marketing sits inside that mimetic field --- or where it's invisible.

## Why Girard Is the Most Dangerous Thinker on This List

Every other thinker on the Deep Marketing Thinkers list describes a mechanism that operates *within* the individual --- how curiosity gets triggered (Loewenstein), how choices get framed (Thaler), how commitment shifts belief (Festinger). Girard is different. He describes the mechanism that operates *between* people. And that mechanism --- mimetic desire --- is the one that determines which products, which gurus, which strategies, and which business models get wanted in the first place.

Here is why this matters for AI: Girard's central insight is that desire is not about the object. It's about the model. People don't want a $10,000 business coaching program because of the curriculum. They want it because someone they admire, identify with, or compete with either has it, promotes it, or represents the kind of person who would buy it. The desire is *triangular* --- Subject looks at Model, Model appears to desire Object, Subject now desires Object. Remove the Model from the picture and the desire evaporates.

This was always theoretically interesting. It becomes operationally explosive when you combine it with AI's ability to research the entire internet. Because now, for the first time, you can actually *map the triangle*. You can identify who your prospects' models are. You can track what those models are publicly desiring, promoting, consuming, and signaling. You can detect when mimetic rivalries are forming between peers in your market. You can see which desires are propagating through professional networks and which are stalling. You can watch the scapegoat mechanism play out in real-time as markets unite against common enemies. None of this was possible when it required a human to manually monitor hundreds of sources. All of it is possible now.

The ideas below are not about "using social proof better." Social proof is the kindergarten version of Girard. These ideas are about reading the actual mimetic field your market operates in --- seeing the invisible desire architecture that determines what people want before they know they want it --- and positioning your marketing inside that architecture with precision.

## Idea 1: The Model Map

### AI-generated map of who your prospects are actually modeling themselves after --- the specific people whose desires are shaping your market's wants

**Girard Concept:** The triangular structure of desire. "Man is the creature who does not know what to desire, and he turns to others in order to make up his mind. We desire what others desire because we imitate their desires." The model is not a vague influence --- it is a specific person (or small set of people) whose visible desires become the prospect's desires.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

You give the tool a description of your target market (e.g., "online business owners doing $500K-$5M who are interested in AI and scaling"). The AI then conducts deep research across:

- Twitter/X, LinkedIn, YouTube, podcasts, Substack, and community platforms to identify who these people are *actually following, engaging with, quoting, and resharing* --- not who they say they admire, but whose content they interact with
- Podcast guest networks --- who appears on the same shows your prospects listen to, creating cross-pollination of desire models
- Amazon reviews and book discussions --- which authors and thought leaders are cited as influences by people in this market
- Conference speaker lineups and attendee discussions --- who gets the attention and imitation at events this market attends
- Course and program testimonial pages --- who are the visible "success stories" that function as aspirational models

The output is a **Model Map**: a ranked list of 15-30 specific individuals who function as desire mediators for your market, organized by:

1. **Reach** --- how many of your prospects are exposed to this model
2. **Mimetic Intensity** --- how much engagement (not just views) their content generates, indicating active desire imitation rather than passive consumption
3. **Desire Objects** --- what specific things each model is visibly desiring, acquiring, promoting, or signaling (tools they use, results they share, lifestyles they display, investments they make)
4. **Proximity** --- whether this model is an "external mediator" (distant celebrity/guru, safe to imitate) or "internal mediator" (peer/rival, triggers competitive desire)

For Rich's market specifically: the tool would surface that his prospects aren't just following "business influencers" generically. There's a specific cluster of 8-12 people --- some obvious (Hormozi, maybe), some non-obvious (a mid-tier operator whose threads get abnormal engagement from this exact cohort) --- and each one is propagating specific desires into the market. The tool shows you *which* desires are coming from *which* models.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

A human marketer might intuitively know 3-4 influencers in their space. They cannot systematically track engagement patterns across platforms for thousands of prospects to identify which specific individuals function as desire models vs. which are just visible. The cross-platform analysis --- seeing that the same person shows up in podcast guest lists, Amazon reviews, Twitter engagement, and conference lineups for a specific market segment --- requires processing volume no team could handle. Agencies do "competitive landscape" reports that list obvious players. This maps the *desire infrastructure* --- the actual influence network that shapes what your prospects will want next quarter.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 9/10 --- Seeing the specific list of people whose desires are literally becoming your prospects' desires, ranked by mimetic intensity, is a "the matrix just revealed itself" moment. Every marketer talks about "knowing your audience." This shows you who your audience is *trying to become*.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $50,000-$150,000 for a deep ethnographic market research study from a firm like Kantar or C Space, taking 3-6 months, and even then it would rely on self-reported influences (what people *say* they're influenced by) rather than behavioral evidence (who they *actually* engage with). The behavioral version was never available at any price.

## Idea 2: The Desire Propagation Tracker

### Real-time monitoring of what specific desires are currently spreading through your market --- which wants are gaining mimetic momentum and which are fading

**Girard Concept:** Mimetic contagion. "The mimetic desire for the object spreads just like a contagious virus. Unchecked, the shared desire exponentially infects the community." Desire is not static --- it propagates through networks, accelerating as more models adopt it and decelerating as it saturates or gets displaced by competing desires.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

The tool continuously monitors public discourse across your market's platforms and produces a monthly (or weekly) **Desire Velocity Report** showing:

1. **Rising Desires** --- things your market is increasingly wanting, talking about acquiring, or expressing aspiration toward. Not trending topics. Trending *wants*. Example for Rich's market: "Desire for AI agents that replace team members" might be rising at 3x the rate of "desire for better hiring systems" --- this tells you the market's mimetic energy has shifted from "build a team" to "replace the team with AI."

2. **Desire Sources** --- for each rising desire, which models introduced it and how it spread. Did it start with one person's viral thread? Did it emerge simultaneously from multiple models (indicating an organic shift)? Did a specific event trigger it?

3. **Desire Conflicts** --- where two incompatible desires are propagating simultaneously through the same market, creating the mimetic tension Girard predicted. Example: "desire for freedom/lifestyle business" vs. "desire for scale/empire building" --- both propagating through the entrepreneur market, creating internal conflict in prospects that makes them indecisive. Your marketing can resolve this tension explicitly.

4. **Fading Desires** --- things the market was intensely wanting 6 months ago that are losing mimetic momentum. These are the desires your competitors are still marketing to while the market has moved on.

5. **Desire Gaps** --- desires that are logically implied by the rising desires but that no model has yet articulated. If the market is increasingly desiring "AI-powered everything" but nobody is yet articulating the desire for "a framework to evaluate which AI tools are worth adopting vs. which are hype" --- that's a gap your content can fill. You become the model for a desire nobody else is mediating yet.

**Concrete Process:** The AI researches 50-100 sources weekly (social platforms, newsletters, podcast transcripts, community forums, book launches, conference recaps) and applies Girard's framework to categorize what it finds: What are people expressing desire *for*? Who are they pointing to as models? Is the desire accelerating or decelerating? Are rivalries forming around it?

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

Human marketers track trends. But trends are about what people are *talking about*. Desires are about what people are *wanting*. The difference is enormous. Someone can talk about AI without wanting AI tools for themselves. The signal is in the language of aspiration, acquisition, envy, and modeling --- "I need to get on this," "look what she built with this," "I can't afford to not have this" --- and that signal is distributed across hundreds of sources at low density. No human can read 100 newsletters, 500 social threads, 30 podcast episodes, and 20 forum discussions weekly to extract the desire signal from the noise. AI can.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 8/10 --- Knowing not just what's trending but what your market is *wanting* --- and watching those wants move in real-time --- gives you a lead time of 2-4 months over competitors who are still reading trend reports.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $30,000-$75,000/quarter for ongoing market research subscription (Gartner, Forrester, etc.), but those products track industry trends and technology adoption, not desire propagation. The desire-specific tracking at this granularity has never been a product.

## Idea 3: The Rivalry Detector

### Identifies where mimetic rivalry between peers is creating intensified desire in your market --- and shows you how to position your offer as the object of that rivalry

**Girard Concept:** Internal mediation and mimetic rivalry. When the model is not a distant celebrity but a *peer* --- someone in the same social sphere --- desire becomes competitive and intense. "Acquisitive desires, subject to mimetic mirroring, will inevitably attach themselves to a single object within the same field of play and generate hostility." The rivalry itself amplifies desire for the contested object far beyond what the object would generate on its own.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

The AI analyzes your market for *active mimetic rivalries* --- pairs or clusters of people who are competing for the same status, recognition, or outcomes --- and maps what objects (tools, credentials, achievements, affiliations) are being contested.

**Concrete inputs:** Your market definition, your product category, and optionally your existing customer list.

**Process:** The AI researches:
- Public disagreements, quote-tweets, response threads, and competitive positioning between figures in your market
- LinkedIn posts where people in your target segment are sharing wins in ways that are clearly aimed at peers (the "just had my best month ever" posts that are performing for an audience of rivals)
- Substack/newsletter wars where competing voices are positioning against each other
- Community forums where practitioners compare results, tools, and approaches in competitive frames
- Review sites and testimonial patterns where customers position their choice of vendor as superior to alternatives

**Output:** A **Rivalry Map** showing:

1. **Active rivalry clusters** --- groups of 3-10 people in your market who are in visible mimetic competition with each other, and what they're competing over
2. **Contested objects** --- the specific tools, credentials, results, and affiliations these rivals are fighting to acquire. These are objects whose perceived value is *inflated by rivalry* beyond their intrinsic value.
3. **Positioning opportunity** --- where your product could become one of the contested objects. If a cluster of mid-six-figure entrepreneurs are competing over who has the most sophisticated AI workflow, and your product teaches exactly that --- the rivalry is pre-generating desire for your product. Your marketing just needs to make the connection explicit.
4. **Rivalry-driven messaging angles** --- specific language patterns the AI identifies from rivalry discourse that you can incorporate: "While others are still doing X, clients in this program have already moved to Y" doesn't just claim superiority --- it activates the mimetic rivalry that's already happening.

**For Rich specifically:** His market of online entrepreneurs is *drenched* in mimetic rivalry. The "revenue screenshot" culture, the "I just did X with AI" one-upmanship, the competition to be seen as ahead of the curve --- this is pure internal mediation. The tool would show Rich exactly which rivalries are active and which objects are being contested, so his marketing can position Force Multiplier or ZenithPro as the thing these rivals are competing to have.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

Mimetic rivalry is socially invisible because nobody admits to it. People don't say "I'm buying this because my competitor has it." They say "I evaluated the options and this was the best fit." Detecting rivalry requires pattern-matching across hundreds of public interactions to see the competitive triangulation that individuals would deny if asked directly. No researcher can monitor the real-time competitive dynamics between dozens of peer clusters across multiple platforms. AI can process the signals --- the competitive framing, the side-by-side comparisons, the "look what I just did" posts --- and map the rivalry structure.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 9/10 --- Most marketers know rivalry exists in their market. None of them have ever seen a map of *which specific rivalries are active, what objects they're contesting, and how to position their product inside that contest*. This is marketing intelligence that feels like it shouldn't be legal.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** This has never existed as a service. Competitive intelligence firms ($20K-$50K/engagement) track what competitors are doing. Nobody tracks the mimetic rivalry dynamics *among prospects* and how those rivalries create desire for specific categories of product.

## Idea 4: The Scapegoat Radar

### Detects the "common enemies" your market is uniting against --- and shows you how to position your offer as the solution to the threat the scapegoat represents

**Girard Concept:** The scapegoat mechanism. "When violence is at the point of threatening the existence of the community, communal violence is all of a sudden projected upon a single individual... Former enemies now become friends, as they communally participate in the execution of violence against a specified enemy." In market terms: when anxiety and rivalry intensify beyond a threshold, the community resolves its tension by uniting against a shared villain.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

The AI monitors your market's discourse for the formation and escalation of scapegoat dynamics --- the process by which a diffuse anxiety coalesces around a specific target (a person, platform, methodology, trend, or institution) that the market unites against.

**Output: Monthly Scapegoat Report** containing:

1. **Active scapegoats** --- who or what your market is currently blaming for their problems. This is not "negative sentiment analysis." It's identifying the specific targets that are creating *community cohesion through shared opposition*. Example for Rich's market: "Big Tech companies that are going to replace everyone with AI" or "fake gurus selling recycled information" or "Elon Musk's platform changes destroying organic reach" --- whatever the market is uniting against *right now*.

2. **Scapegoat lifecycle stage** --- Girard showed this follows a pattern: (a) diffuse anxiety, (b) convergence on a target, (c) escalation of blame, (d) cathartic resolution (the target gets "destroyed" or the community moves on). Knowing where in the cycle the market is tells you how to time your messaging.

3. **Cohesion opportunities** --- the moment a market is uniting against a scapegoat is the moment community formation is easiest and desire for "the alternative" is strongest. If the market is scapegoating traditional business coaches as "outdated," and you position as the AI-forward alternative, the scapegoat dynamic is doing your differentiation work for free.

4. **Counter-positioning intelligence** --- which competitors are *becoming* scapegoats (and should be avoided or distanced from), and which are successfully *naming* scapegoats (and building community cohesion as a result).

**Practical example for Rich:** If the AI detects that the entrepreneur market is in the early stages of scapegoating "people who use AI to produce mass garbage content," Rich can position early as the antidote --- someone who teaches using AI for *quality and depth*, not volume. He's riding the scapegoat dynamic rather than falling victim to it.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

The scapegoat mechanism is invisible from inside the community experiencing it. Everyone participating feels they're making a legitimate critique, not performing a collective ritual. Seeing it *as* a mechanism requires observing the pattern across many voices simultaneously --- the convergence from diverse complaints to unified blame, the emotional escalation, the community bonding that happens around the shared opposition. No individual observer can process enough discourse across enough channels to see the mechanism forming before it's already obvious. AI can detect the convergence pattern weeks before it becomes a visible wave.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 8/10 --- "Your market is three weeks into a scapegoat cycle against X. Here's the language they're using and here's how to position as the alternative before your competitors notice" is genuinely actionable intelligence that feels like seeing the future.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** Brand reputation monitoring services ($5K-$15K/month) track *your* brand sentiment. Nobody provides a service that tracks the mimetic scapegoat dynamics of *your market as a community* and identifies positioning opportunities in real time.

## Idea 5: The Desire Autopsy

### Reverse-engineers why a specific product, launch, or campaign succeeded or failed by mapping the mimetic desire structure it activated (or failed to activate)

**Girard Concept:** The triangular structure of desire applied diagnostically. "The relationship between the subject and the object is not direct: it unrolls within a triangular relationship of subject, model, and object." If a product launch failed, it might not be because the product was bad or the copy was weak --- it might be because the *model structure* was wrong: the wrong model was used, or no model was present, or the model was an external mediator when internal mediation was needed.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

You give it a specific product, launch, campaign, or competitor effort to analyze. The AI then:

1. **Identifies the model structure** --- Who was (explicitly or implicitly) positioned as the model of desire? A celebrity endorser (external mediation)? A peer testimonial (internal mediation)? The founder's own success story? No model at all (product-feature-based marketing)?

2. **Assesses model-market fit** --- Was the model someone the target market actually imitates? Or was there a disconnect? Example: a $5,000 business program using testimonials from people making $50M is external mediation --- impressive but not imitation-triggering. The same program using testimonials from people at the prospect's level who just crossed $1M --- that's internal mediation, and it triggers rivalry-driven desire.

3. **Maps the desire triangle** --- What specific desire was activated? Not "they wanted the product" but "they wanted to be like [model] and the product appeared to be the path to that." Was this desire strong enough, specific enough, and mimetically active enough in the current market?

4. **Diagnoses failures** --- Common failure modes include: (a) No model present --- the marketing talked about the product but never showed anyone desiring or benefiting from it. (b) Wrong model type --- used external mediation (guru/celebrity) when internal mediation (peer/rival) would generate more intense desire. (c) Conflicting models --- testimonials from people in different camps that create mimetic confusion rather than mimetic convergence. (d) Model-obstacle collapse --- the founder positioned themselves as both the model to aspire to AND the gatekeeper, creating the skandalon dynamic where prospects feel simultaneously attracted and blocked.

5. **Produces rewrite recommendations** --- Specific changes to model structure, testimonial selection, positioning, and messaging sequence to align with how mimetic desire actually works.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

Marketers diagnose failures by looking at conversion metrics, copy quality, offer structure, traffic sources, and timing. They almost never examine the *model structure* --- the mimetic architecture of who is positioned as the desire mediator and whether that mediator is generating imitative desire in the target market. This requires analyzing the marketing alongside the market's actual mimetic landscape (who do these prospects really model themselves after?), which requires the external research capability from Idea 1. A copywriter can spot weak headlines. Nobody can spot a misaligned desire model without simultaneously understanding both the marketing and the market's mimetic field.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 9/10 --- "Your launch didn't fail because of the copy. It failed because you used external mediation with a celebrity-status testimonial when your market is in an intense internal-mediation rivalry phase and needed to see a *peer* succeed." That kind of diagnosis changes how a marketer thinks permanently.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $25,000-$75,000 for a post-mortem marketing analysis from a top agency --- but even the best agencies don't use mimetic desire as a diagnostic lens. They would analyze copy, funnel, timing, and audience targeting. The desire-structure diagnosis is a genuinely new category of analysis.

## Idea 6: The Mediator Positioning Audit

### Analyzes where YOU (the founder/brand) currently sit in your market's mimetic hierarchy --- external mediator, internal mediator, model-obstacle, or invisible --- and what that means for your marketing

**Girard Concept:** External vs. internal mediation, and the model-obstacle dynamic. "External mediation occurs where the model inhabits a different social sphere... so that mimesis remains a distant emulation rather than leading to direct competition. Internal mediation occurs where the desiring subject's object of desire and their model's object overlap and thereby becomes a pretext for rivalry." The skandalon: when the model becomes simultaneously the source of desire and the obstacle to fulfillment.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

The AI researches how your market *actually perceives* you (or your brand/founder) by analyzing:

- How your content gets discussed, reshared, and quoted by prospects vs. by peers vs. by the broader market
- Whether engagement patterns suggest admiration-from-distance (external mediation: people share your stuff with "this is brilliant" but don't see themselves competing with you) or rivalry-triggering (internal mediation: people see your success as something they could/should replicate)
- Whether you're being scapegoated by any segment (the model-obstacle dynamic where people resent your success while being drawn to your content)
- What language people use about you in forums, comment sections, reviews, and social discussions --- reverence vs. competition vs. resentment vs. indifference
- How your accessibility/pricing/positioning affects which mediation type you trigger

**Output:** A **Mediator Position Report** that tells you:

1. **Your current mediation type** for each market segment: Are you an external mediator (aspirational but distant) or an internal mediator (peer-level, rivalry-triggering)?

2. **The strategic implications:** External mediation makes you admired but doesn't generate intense purchase desire --- people admire from afar. Internal mediation generates intense desire but also rivalry and resentment. Neither is "better" --- but your marketing strategy needs to match your actual mediation type. If you're marketing as a peer when the market sees you as a distant guru, or vice versa, there's a disconnect.

3. **The skandalon risk:** Are you creating model-obstacle dynamics where prospects feel simultaneously drawn to you and blocked by you? Example: "Follow me and I'll teach you to succeed like me, but the program costs $13K and the application is competitive" can create a desire trap where the exclusivity amplifies wanting but also generates resentment. The AI identifies whether this is happening and how to manage it.

4. **Mediation migration strategy:** If you want to shift from external to internal mediation (or vice versa), the AI identifies specific changes in content strategy, positioning language, pricing visibility, and testimonial structure that would achieve the shift.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

You cannot assess your own mediation type from inside. It requires seeing how your market relates to you *as they express it to each other, not to you*. People don't tell gurus "I admire you from afar but don't see you as someone I'm trying to directly compete with" (external mediation). They don't say "I resent you because you represent what I want and I can't get it" (skandalon). These perceptions are embedded in how people discuss you in spaces you don't see --- forums, DMs, group chats, comment sections, podcast discussions about the market. Only AI can aggregate this distributed perception signal at scale.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 8/10 --- "Your market sees you as an external mediator in the guru category, which means they admire your content but aren't experiencing the kind of intense, rivalry-driven desire that makes people buy $5K programs. Here's what your competitors are doing differently to trigger internal mediation" --- that is a strategic insight most founders will never get from any other source.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $40,000-$100,000 for a brand perception study (focus groups, surveys, social listening) --- but none of these studies use mimetic theory as their lens. They'd tell you "sentiment is positive" without distinguishing between external-mediation admiration and internal-mediation desire, which are functionally opposite in terms of conversion potential.

## Idea 7: The Competitor Desire Theft Analyzer

### Maps exactly which desires each competitor in your market is capturing, which they're creating, and which are uncontested --- then identifies the highest-value desires nobody is mediating

**Girard Concept:** Acquisitive mimesis and the competition for desired objects. "If people imitate each other's desires, they may wind up desiring the very same things and easily become rivals as they reach for the same objects." In market terms: competitors are all mediating the same handful of desires while leaving others completely uncontested.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

The AI conducts a comprehensive analysis of 5-15 competitors in your market and maps the *desire landscape* they're collectively creating and contesting:

1. **Desire inventory by competitor** --- For each competitor, what specific desires does their marketing activate? Not "they sell coaching" but "their marketing activates the desire to be seen as a sophisticated operator who has systems for everything" or "their marketing activates the desire to escape the 9-to-5 and achieve time freedom." Each competitor mediates specific desires through specific model structures.

2. **Contested desire zones** --- Where multiple competitors are fighting to mediate the same desire. Example: if 8 out of 10 competitors in the AI business space are mediating "the desire to replace your team with AI agents," that desire zone is saturated. Not because the desire isn't real, but because the market is overwhelmed with models mediating it and cannot differentiate.

3. **Uncontested desire zones** --- Desires that the market demonstrably has (visible in forum discussions, search behavior, content engagement) but that no competitor is explicitly mediating. This is the Girardian equivalent of a "blue ocean" --- not an uncontested market, but an *uncontested desire*.

4. **Desire creation opportunities** --- Following Girard's logic that models *create* desire through their own visible wanting, the AI identifies what you could visibly desire/pursue/acquire that would generate *new* desire in your market that nobody else is triggering. This is the highest-leverage move: not competing for an existing desire, but becoming the model for a desire that doesn't yet exist in the market.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

Competitive analysis traditionally examines features, pricing, positioning, and messaging. Nobody maps the *desire structure* each competitor is activating. Two competitors might have identical feature sets and similar messaging but be mediating completely different desires --- one activating "desire to be ahead of everyone else" and the other activating "desire to finally get control of the chaos." Understanding this distinction requires reading not just the competitors' marketing but how their *customers* talk about them --- which desires their customers express having fulfilled. That requires cross-referencing marketing with reviews, testimonials, social proof posts, and community discussions for every competitor. At scale, this is only possible with AI.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 9/10 --- "Here are the 4 desires every competitor in your market is fighting over. Here are the 3 desires nobody is mediating. Here's the evidence they're real. You can own one of these unopposed." That changes a marketing strategy overnight.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $50,000-$100,000 for a traditional competitive positioning study --- but traditional studies map features, messaging, and market positioning. The desire-layer analysis has never been offered as a service because nobody uses mimetic theory as a competitive intelligence framework.

## Idea 8: The Testimonial Mimetic Optimizer

### Analyzes your existing testimonials, case studies, and social proof to determine which ones generate mimetic desire and which are mimetically inert --- then tells you exactly which new testimonials to collect

**Girard Concept:** The model must be someone the subject can imitate. "In external mediation, the model inhabits a different social sphere... so that mimesis remains a distant emulation." "In internal mediation, the model is near --- rivalry escalates." And critically: "Sometimes, consumers do not just desire a product for its inherent qualities, but rather, desire to be the celebrity that promotes such a product." The testimonial *person* matters more than the testimonial *content*.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

You feed the tool your current testimonials, case studies, success stories, and any social proof you use in marketing. The AI also researches your target market. Then it produces a **Mimetic Audit** of your social proof:

1. **Model-prospect distance scoring** --- For each testimonial person, how mimetically close or distant are they from your target prospect? A testimonial from someone making $50M when your prospect makes $500K = external mediation (admiration, not desire). A testimonial from someone who was making $500K and crossed $2M = internal mediation (intense, rivalrous desire). A testimonial from someone in a completely different industry = minimal mediation (no triangulation possible).

2. **Desire triangle analysis** --- For each testimonial, does the person function as a *model* (someone the prospect wants to be like), a *proof* (evidence the product works), or *neither* (just a nice quote from someone the prospect doesn't identify with)? These are very different mimetic functions. Most testimonial pages are 80% proof and 0% model.

3. **Missing model gaps** --- Based on your target market's actual mimetic models (from Idea 1), which models are missing from your testimonial portfolio? If your market's most powerful internal mediators are "solo operators using AI to outperform agencies," and none of your testimonials feature that archetype, you have a model gap that's costing you conversions.

4. **Testimonial collection priority list** --- A ranked list of the specific *types of people* you should be actively soliciting testimonials from, based on who your market is actually modeling themselves after. Not "get more testimonials" but "get a testimonial from a person who matches this specific profile because that profile is the most powerful internal mediator for your target segment."

5. **Placement optimization** --- Which testimonials should appear at which stage of the funnel, based on whether the prospect needs external mediation (aspiration/awareness stage) or internal mediation (consideration/decision stage). Girard's model predicts that external mediation works for initial attraction but internal mediation drives purchase.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

Marketers collect testimonials opportunistically and display them based on enthusiasm or production quality. Nobody evaluates testimonials through a mimetic lens --- asking "does this person function as a desire model for my specific prospect, and if so, is the mediation type right for this stage of the funnel?" Answering this question requires knowing who the prospect's actual models are (external market research), evaluating each testimonial person's mimetic proximity to the prospect (audience analysis), and mapping the desire triangle for each proof element. It's a multi-layer analysis that requires both internal data (your testimonials) and external data (your market's mimetic structure).

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 7/10 --- Less dramatic than the market-level tools but potentially the highest immediate ROI because it optimizes something you already have. "Your 24 testimonials include zero internal mediators for your primary target segment --- here are the 5 people to ask" is actionable today.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** Conversion optimization agencies ($10K-$30K/engagement) will A/B test testimonial placement and formats. None of them evaluate the mimetic model structure of the testimonial *people*. This is a genuinely new optimization axis.

## Idea 9: The Mimetic Content Strategist

### Tells you exactly what content to create based on which desires are currently propagating through your market and which model position you should occupy to mediate them

**Girard Concept:** Models create desire by being seen desiring. "Whenever a product is promoted, some celebrity is used to 'mediate' consumers' desires: in a sense, the celebrity is inviting people to imitate him in his desire of the product." But Girard's deeper point is that the model doesn't need to promote anything explicitly --- merely being seen *wanting, using, benefiting from, or competing for* something generates desire in those who watch.

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

This combines the outputs of Ideas 1, 2, and 6 into a tactical content plan. Each week or month, the AI produces:

1. **Desire-aligned content topics** --- Not "what's trending" or "what keywords have volume" but "what desires are currently propagating and what content would make you the model who mediates those desires." Example: If the desire "build AI systems that make my competitors irrelevant" is currently spreading through your market, the content isn't "How to Use AI in Your Business" (information). It's "How I Built an AI System That Made My Competitor's Approach Obsolete" (modeling). The first teaches. The second triggers mimetic desire.

2. **Model positioning for each piece** --- How to position yourself in each content piece as the right type of mediator. Should this piece establish external mediation (aspirational, "here's what's possible") or internal mediation (peer-level, "here's what I just did that you could do too")? The answer depends on the desire being mediated and the audience's current relationship to you.

3. **Rivalry activation content** --- Content specifically designed to activate the productive mimetic rivalries already present in your market. Not manufactured rivalry but content that makes existing rivalries visible. "Here's the divide forming in our industry between people who X and people who Y" --- this is not creating a false dichotomy. It's naming a real mimetic split and letting people self-select.

4. **Desire escalation sequences** --- Multi-piece content arcs that progressively intensify a desire. Girard showed that desire intensifies through repeated exposure to models desiring the same object. A content sequence that shows Model A getting results, then Model B (a peer of the prospect) getting results, then the prospect's own peer group starting to adopt --- this follows the natural escalation of mimetic desire.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

Content strategy today is driven by SEO, social algorithms, editorial calendars, and "what's our audience interested in." None of these frameworks address the mimetic question: "What should we show our market *wanting* in order to create desire for what we sell?" This requires simultaneous awareness of (a) what desires are propagating in the market, (b) who the active models are, (c) what mediation type our brand currently occupies, and (d) how desire escalation works sequentially. No content strategist --- no matter how good --- can hold all four of these in mind while also tracking them across the entire market in real-time.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 8/10 --- "Stop creating content about your product. Start creating content that makes you the model for the desires your market is already catching." This reframes content strategy from "information publishing" to "desire modeling" --- and the AI tells you specifically which desires to model in which order.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $15K-$40K/quarter for content strategy from a top content agency --- but all traditional content strategy is information-first or SEO-first, not desire-first. The mimetic content strategy is a genuinely different approach that has never been offered.

## Idea 10: The Anti-Mimetic Differentiator

### Identifies where you and your competitors have converged into mimetic sameness --- all copying each other's positioning, offers, and messaging --- and finds the path to genuine differentiation

**Girard Concept:** Mimetic doubling and the crisis of differences. "Conflictual mimesis is characterized by 'doubling,' which refers to the progressive and mutually reinforcing de-differentiation of subjects." As rivals imitate each other, they become increasingly similar while perceiving themselves as increasingly different. "Imitation eventually erases the differences among human beings."

**What It Does With Full AI Access:**

The AI conducts a *differentiation analysis* not at the feature or positioning level, but at the mimetic level --- finding where you and your competitors have unconsciously converged through mutual imitation:

1. **Messaging convergence map** --- The AI analyzes 5-15 competitors' websites, ads, social content, email sequences, and sales pages to identify where the market has converged on identical language, claims, structures, and approaches. Not "they all sell coaching" but "11 out of 12 competitors use the exact same narrative arc: pain point → failed solutions → breakthrough → offer. The language is interchangeable. The market cannot distinguish between any of you." This includes analyzing Rich's own marketing for where he has unknowingly converged with competitors.

2. **Offer structure mimicry** --- Where competitors' offer architectures (pricing tiers, bonuses, guarantees, delivery models) have converged through mutual imitation. If everyone offers "12 weeks + weekly calls + community access + bonuses," the structure itself has become mimetically saturated and no longer differentiates.

3. **Genuine differentiation axes** --- After mapping the convergence, the AI identifies what dimensions of difference are actually available. These aren't "what should we be different about" --- they're "where in the mimetic field is there actual open space that nobody is occupying because everyone has been too busy imitating each other to notice."

4. **Anti-mimetic positioning options** --- Specific ways to break out of the mimetic convergence. Girard's insight is that conscious awareness of mimetic patterns creates the possibility of *not* imitating. The AI identifies which competitor behaviors you're unconsciously copying and proposes alternatives that would create genuine distinctiveness.

**Why This Was Impossible Before:**

You cannot see your own mimetic convergence. That's the whole point of Girard's theory --- the people most trapped in mimetic imitation are the ones most convinced of their uniqueness. "We're different because we use AI" --- said by 10 of 10 competitors. The only way to see the convergence is to process *all* the competitors' marketing simultaneously and compare at scale. A human marketer can read 3-4 competitors' sales pages. They cannot systematically compare the messaging patterns, offer structures, narrative arcs, visual design, and social proof strategies of 15 competitors to identify where the market has collapsed into undifferentiated mimetic mush.

**Jaw-Drop Factor:** 9/10 --- "Here are the 12 things you think make you different from your competitors. 11 of them are identical to what your competitors say. Here is the 1 thing that's actually different, and here are 3 more things that could be different if you stopped unconsciously imitating." That is a genuinely humbling and valuable insight.

**Previous Cost to Approximate:** $30,000-$75,000 for a competitive brand audit from a branding agency --- and these audits typically compare at the positioning/messaging level without examining the *mimetic dynamics* that caused the convergence. They tell you "you sound similar" without explaining *why* (because you're all imitating each other's imitating each other) or how to break out.

## Closing Note on Girard and AI Marketing

The conventional wisdom in marketing is that you create desire through great copy, compelling offers, and persuasive arguments. Girard said something more radical: you don't create desire at all. You *mediate* it. Desire already exists in the mimetic field --- it's flowing between models and subjects through networks of imitation, rivalry, and aspiration. The marketer's job is not to manufacture want from nothing. It's to understand the desire that's already propagating and position the offer as the natural object of that desire.

AI makes this operationally possible for the first time. Not because AI is creative or persuasive, but because AI can *see the mimetic field* --- the web of models, desires, rivalries, and convergences that no individual human can perceive at scale. That vision is the unlock. Everything else is application.
