# Desire Autopsy — Force Multiplier Campaign
**Date:** 2026-03-04
**Product:** Force Multiplier by Rich Schefren / Strategic Profits
**Webinar:** "The Insane Leverage Window" — February 5, 2026
**Analyst Framework:** René Girard — Mimetic Desire Theory
**Sources:** Registration page, VIP upsell page, thank-you page, webinar brief v2, mechanism brief, competitive landscape synthesis, webinar transcript (Feb 5, 2026), Desire Profile (Feb 21, 2026), Desire Velocity Report (Mar 4, 2026), 42 real avatar applications

---

> **The Girardian lens for this autopsy:** Desire is not innate. It is mediated — triangulated through a model who shows the subject what to want. A product does not succeed because it is good. It succeeds because the buyer wants to become the person who has it. This autopsy asks: what was the model, what was the desire structure, and where did the triangle hold or break?

---

## SECTION 1: THE DESIRE ARCHITECTURE MAP

### Who Was the Model?

The Force Multiplier campaign deployed **three overlapping models**, each functioning at a different register:

**Model 1: Rich Schefren as External Mediator — The Founder-Strategist**
The primary model deployed throughout the campaign was Rich himself — presented as the person who not only sees the paradigm shift first, but who built the actual system and runs his business on it. The registration page states: "the strategist who took Agora from $220M to $1.25 billion, invented the automated webinar, and is known as 'Guru to the Gurus' for coaching Frank Kern, Russell Brunson, Ryan Deiss, and Jeff Walker."

This positions Rich as an **external mediator** — a figure of such superior achievement that he functions as an aspiration object rather than a rival. The prospect cannot be Rich. But they can learn to *think* the way Rich thinks.

**Model 2: The Implied Internal Mediator — The "6-Person Team That Outproduces 6,000"**
The campaign intermittently invoked an internal mediator structure through the "6 vs. 6,000 equation" — the idea that people like the buyer, with small teams, could use this architecture to outproduce massive competitors. This is a peer-level desire trigger: *someone in my position has already done this.*

However, this internal mediator was almost entirely **abstract**. No named peer was consistently visible. The only concrete success story cited in the registration copy was Matt Leitz: "I'm an AI expert and I've never seen anything like this. I'm blown away." — which is an expert endorsement (external mediation) rather than a peer transformation story (internal mediation).

The real avatar data reinforces this: 19% of buyers cited Rich's livestream demo as the conversion moment — they watched Rich build live and thought "I need to be able to do that." This is desire mediated by direct exposure to the model's capabilities, not by seeing a peer succeed.

**Model 3: The Abstract Outcome Model — "The Architect"**
The campaign's transformation promise pointed toward an identity destination: moving from "business owner as bottleneck" to "business owner as architect." The webinar brief states explicitly: "The goal isn't 'AI does what I do' — it's 'AI does what I do, better than I do it, and I become the architect instead of the laborer.'"

This is the object of desire: the Architect identity. But it was named in the brief more clearly than it was named in the live marketing. The aspiration was present; the label was not crystallized for the buyer to adopt.

---

### Mediation Type

**Dominant type: External mediation** — Rich as distant, elevated model.

The campaign's core credibility architecture is almost entirely external-mediator in structure:
- "$15B+ in client revenue generated"
- "Guru to the Gurus"
- "Agora: $220M to $1.25B"
- "Only consultant with a billion-dollar testimonial"
- "Invented the automated webinar"

These credentials create reverence and trust. Girard's analysis says external mediation produces admiration — the prospect admires the model. This is not the same as mimetic desire, which requires the prospect to feel they can *become* the model, or at least that they are competing with peers who already are.

**Where internal mediation appeared:**
The webinar itself was the most powerful internal mediation event in the entire campaign. Rich building live on screen — showing the actual workflow, the actual tools, the actual thinking — is the closest the campaign came to saying "you can do this too." The brief confirms this: "Avatar data from 42 real applicants shows that watching Rich build live was one of the top conversion drivers." The live build functioned as an *accessible demonstration* — the mediator showing their desire object in real time, close enough that the prospect can imagine reaching it.

This is the campaign's single most powerful mimetic moment: the webinar as an act of accessible modeling.

---

### What Was the Desire Object?

The desire object shifted across marketing layers, and this inconsistency is diagnostically important:

**In the registration page:** The object was LEVERAGE — specifically, "The 1,000x Multiplication Factor Behind The Biggest Wealth Transfer In A Generation." The desire here is for *magnitude of output* — to be someone who can do what 1,000 people could do.

**In the VIP upsell page:** The object shifted to ACCESS — being in a 50-person room with Rich, getting hot-seat coaching. The desire here is for *proximity to the model*, not the capability itself.

**In the webinar brief:** The object was stated most clearly as ARCHITECT STATUS — the transformation from labor to design. "You become the architect instead of the laborer."

**In the actual webinar (transcript):** The object surfaced as CERTAINTY IN AN UNCERTAIN MOMENT — Rich repeatedly framed the moment as historically pivotal ("I have chills now"), and the desire being activated was: *being on the right side of history's most important inflection point.* This is the "4 Moments of Maximum Profit" framing — the desire to be the person who recognized the moment and acted.

**Assessment:** The desire object was fragmented across touchpoints. A Girardian analysis of desire triangles requires the object to be *singular and consistent* across the Subject → Model → Object relationship. Force Multiplier had a strong, consistent Subject (the burned, skeptical, identity-wounded entrepreneur) and a strong Model (Rich), but the Object kept shifting depending on which page or moment in the funnel you were analyzing. The clearest formulation — "You become the architect" — never became the unifying label of the campaign.

---

### Desire Triangle Integrity

**Subject:** STRONG
The buyer profile was extraordinarily well-researched and accurately represented in the marketing. The "dominant latent narrative" — "I built this business with my brain, my guts, and my work ethic. Now everyone tells me that's not enough anymore" — is a precise identification of the Subject's identity wound. The avatar data from 42 real applicants confirms this. The mechanism brief's five emotional layers (Hungry, Burned, Ashamed, Skeptical, Urgent) are a forensic-grade map of who this person is.

**Model:** STRONG WITH A STRUCTURAL WEAKNESS
Rich as model is clear, credible, and differentiated. The weakness: the model is positioned primarily as *external* (aspirational, elevated, distant) when the market's specific moment called for *accessible demonstration* (internal, reachable, peer-adjacent). The webinar partially corrected this through the live build, but the marketing pages leaned almost entirely on the external-mediator register.

**Object:** FRAGMENTED
"Leverage," "access," "architect status," "certainty," "not being left behind," "multiplying your team" — these are not the same object. The campaign's desire triangle loses integrity at the Object vertex because no single, named identity destination was crystallized and repeated across all touchpoints.

**Triangle verdict:** The triangle was partially intact. The Subject-Model axis was coherent and powerful. The Subject-Object axis was present but fragmented. The result: strong enough to convert motivated buyers (the 71% who trust Rich specifically), but insufficiently powerful to generate new mimetic contagion — to make someone who had never heard of Rich urgently want what his buyers have.

## SECTION 2: THE FOUR DIAGNOSES

### Diagnosis 1: Model Type Assessment

**Rating: STRONG — with specific deployment gaps**

Rich as the primary model is the campaign's greatest structural asset. The "Guru to the Gurus" framing is a genuinely unique second-order model structure — the prospect does not want to BE Rich; they want the *thinking Rich produces*, which created the people they already admire. This is rare and powerful.

**Evidence it worked:**
- 71% of the 42-applicant avatar cohort cited "trust in Rich specifically" as the primary decision driver. They were not buying the topic; they were buying access to Rich's thinking.
- The live build was cited by 19% as the decisive conversion event — the model made visible in real time, close enough to activate desire.
- The Matt Leitz testimonial anchored expert-level credibility: "I'm an AI expert and I've never seen anything like this." An AI expert's validation neutralizes the "is this real?" objection for technically skeptical buyers.

**Evidence of deployment gaps:**
- The model functioned almost entirely as an external mediator in the marketing pages. "Guru to the Gurus," "$15B client results," "billion-dollar testimonial" — these create authority and distance simultaneously. The prospect admires from afar rather than being pulled toward mimetic identification.
- No peer-level internal mediators appeared in the marketing pages. The 42 real applicants presumably included success stories. None were featured as named, visible, story-driven examples of "someone like you who made this transformation."
- The model was inconsistently accessible across touchpoints. The webinar corrected this via live demonstration. The registration page and VIP upsell page did not.

---

### Diagnosis 2: Mediation Type Assessment

**Rating: MISALIGNED for this specific market moment**

The market's state in February 2026 was precisely diagnosed by the mechanism brief: "The Hype Hangover." Gartner's "Trough of Disillusionment" was hitting the small business AI market. The audience was burned, ashamed, and skeptical. They had already been sold by external mediators — the big AI voices, the $150M company founders, the LinkedIn thought leaders. Those models had failed them.

For a burned-and-skeptical audience, external mediation is *actively counterproductive*. The prospect's internal monologue upon seeing "I took Agora from $220M to $1.25B" is not "I want what he has." It is "You are too far from my world to be relevant to my problem." The very credibility that was meant to build trust can trigger the same defensive skepticism that was triggered by every previous AI pitch.

**What the market actually needed:** Internal mediation — peer models who were like the buyer *and had succeeded*. "Someone who had the same skepticism you have, the same team size you have, the same history of burned AI experiments you have — and built the system anyway, and it worked."

**Why the misalignment is partially mitigated:**
The webinar format partially solved this problem. Rich's live build was *not* the behavior of a distant guru — it was a practitioner showing his work. The statement "I'm training my team anyway; pull up a seat and learn alongside us" is an internal mediation move: Rich as peer-level practitioner, not elevated authority. This tone softened the external-mediator problem and likely accounts for much of the webinar's conversion success.

The registration page language also made one important internal-mediation attempt: "Someone in your niche is going to build an unkillable company. Someone is going to multiply their capability by 1,000x while their competition is still debating which AI tool to download." This invokes *rival peer mediation* — the most urgent form of internal mediation. Girard identifies rivalry-based desire as the most motivating: the desire activated not by the model's attractiveness, but by the intolerable prospect that *your peer* will have what you want and you will not.

This was the sharpest mimetic trigger in the campaign, but it appeared only at the bottom of the registration page and was not the lead or the dominant frame.

---

### Diagnosis 3: Model-Market Fit

**Rating: STRONG for warm/existing audience — WEAK for cold acquisition**

The avatar data provides the diagnostic directly: "Trust in Rich specifically (71%)" was the primary buying driver. These buyers had followed Rich for 10-20+ years. For them, the desire was fully formed before the webinar began. The campaign's job was not to CREATE desire — it was to provide a *trigger* and a *justification* for a purchase decision they were already emotionally predisposed to make.

This is not the same as Model-Market Fit at a market-generating level. Rich as model works powerfully for a warm audience of existing followers. It works less powerfully — and potentially counterproductively — for cold audiences encountering him for the first time.

**The structural evidence for this:** The "Guru to the Gurus" framing presupposes that the prospect already knows who Kern, Brunson, Deiss, and Walker are — and cares about their relationships with Rich. For an entrepreneur who does not follow the online marketing world, this framing is meaningless. For a 50-year-old consultant or agency owner whose reference points are not internet marketing, the model's desired signals simply do not activate.

**What this means for the MAJORITY who bought solo:** The finding that the majority of buyers purchased at the Solo tier ($5,000 for themselves alone) despite the campaign being designed for entrepreneur + team suggests that the buyer was primarily motivated by Rich as a model of *personal capability* — not team transformation. The desire activated was "I want to be able to do what Rich does," not "I want to turn my team into force multipliers." The product was designed for organizational transformation. The desire activated was for individual access to Rich's capability. These are not the same thing.

---

### Diagnosis 4: Desire Triangle Integrity

**Rating: BROKEN AT THE OBJECT VERTEX**

This is the central diagnosis of the campaign. The Subject (burned entrepreneur) and Model (Rich as second-order authority) were both well-constructed. The Object — the specific, named thing the buyer wants to BECOME — was never crystallized into a single, consistent, memorable identity label.

**Comparative analysis:**
- Brunson's campaign: Subject = frustrated marketer, Model = Brunson as 2-Comma-Club member, Object = **Funnel Hacker** (a named identity the buyer can adopt)
- Hormozi's campaign: Subject = business owner stuck at $1M, Model = Hormozi as ruthless operator, Object = **Acquisition Entrepreneur** (a named identity with a specific skill set)
- Force Multiplier: Subject = burned AI skeptic, Model = Rich as Guru to Gurus, Object = ??? ("Force Multiplier" was the program name but was never fully crystallized as an identity the buyer would inhabit after purchasing)

The name "Force Multiplier" was actually a strong candidate for a crystallized identity label — "I am a Force Multiplier" has the right ring. But the marketing did not consistently position enrollment as *becoming a Force Multiplier*. The program was positioned as something you buy, not something you become. The desire object remained functional (getting leverage, building systems) rather than identity-based (becoming the person who has that leverage built in).

## SECTION 3: FAILURE MODE CLASSIFICATION

### Primary Failure Mode: Object Fragmentation

**Definition:** The desire object is multiple things at different touchpoints, preventing the formation of a stable, imitable, crystallized desire in the buyer's mind.

**Evidence:**
- Registration page Object: "1,000x multiplication factor" (leverage/scale)
- VIP upsell Object: proximity to Rich (access/relationship)
- Thank-you page Object: preparation for a technical experience (capability building)
- Webinar Object: certainty in a historically pivotal moment (timing/positioning)
- Brief Object: becoming "the architect instead of the laborer" (identity transformation)
- Close Object: not falling behind competitors (rivalry/threat avoidance)

None of these are wrong. All of them are genuine desires present in the buyer. But Girardian desire theory holds that the object must be *singular* to generate maximum desire. When the buyer is simultaneously being offered leverage, access, capability, historical positioning, identity transformation, and threat avoidance, the desire becomes diffuse and the conversion depends entirely on which fragment resonates for that individual buyer. This is a conversion *lottery* rather than a conversion *system*.

**What it cost:** Buyers who trusted Rich already converted regardless — the desire was pre-formed from prior relationship. Buyers who did not already trust Rich had no single desire object to lock onto, meaning the campaign did minimal work to create new desire in non-existing-follower audiences.

---

### Secondary Failure Mode: Mediation-Model Mismatch for the Actual Buyer Behavior

**Definition:** The product was structured for a Team buyer (external mediation via organizational transformation) but the dominant desire activated was for Solo access to Rich's thinking (personal transformation via proximity to model). The mediation structure served a different buyer than the one who predominantly converted.

**Evidence:**
- Pricing structure: Team tiers ($7,997 for small team, $12,997 for mid-team) built into the offer architecture
- Actual buyer behavior: Majority purchased Solo tier ($5,000)
- Avatar data: 43% of ideal client profile are solo operators or 1-3 person teams
- Primary buying motivation: "Trust in Rich specifically" (71%) — this is a model-proximity drive, not a team-transformation drive
- Secondary motivation: "Fear of being left behind" (48%) — this is a personal rivalry drive, not a team-building drive

**What this tells us:** The product was designed for a Girardian desire structure centered on the Team — "your team becomes force multipliers" — but the desire actually activated was centered on the Founder — "I want to think and operate like Rich." These are different desire structures requiring different product positioning, different social proof, and different close language.

The team pricing tiers were not merely a packaging decision; they were a *desire declaration*. They signaled to the market: "This product is for people who have and want to multiply teams." But the market that responded most strongly was: "I want what Rich has, and I'll figure out the team part later." This mismatch is not a problem with the product — it is a problem with the desire target.

---

### Tertiary Failure Mode: The Skandalon (Model-Obstacle) at Scale

**Definition:** The "Guru to the Gurus" positioning, while powerful for a warm audience, created an aspirational distance for cold audiences that made the gap feel unbridgeable rather than inspiring.

**Girard's skandalon concept:** When the model is simultaneously the source of desire and the barrier to fulfillment — "I want to be like you, but your very existence makes me feel I never can" — desire collapses into resentment or resignation rather than motivated action.

**Evidence:**
- The campaign led with credentials that are essentially inimitable: $15B in client revenue, $1B testimonial (Agora), "invented the automated webinar," "Guru to the Gurus." These are not models the prospect can become. They are historical monuments.
- The "6 vs. 6,000" claim and "1,000x multiplication" language function at a scale that may have activated disbelief rather than desire in skeptical, burned prospects. The mechanism brief correctly identifies that "FOMO is now producing waste, not action" and that "traditional urgency tactics backfire with this audience." But the scale claims (1,000x, 6,000 without it) were of the same register as the urgency tactics they flagged as counterproductive.

**Mitigating factor:** The anti-hype positioning ("conviction not excitement") explicitly attempted to address this. The Two Lies framework deconstructed both inflated promises (Lie #1: SaaS efficiency hype) and inflated methods (Lie #2: prompt engineering theater). This was a smart countermeasure. But the campaign's headline — "The 1,000x Multiplication Factor Behind The Biggest Wealth Transfer In A Generation" — was itself hype-register language that contradicted the anti-hype positioning being delivered in the body copy.

## SECTION 4: COUNTER-FACTUAL RECOMMENDATIONS

### Recommendation 1: Crystallize and Name the Identity Object

**The problem:** No stable, memorable identity destination for the buyer.

**Specific change:** Adopt "Force Multiplier" as a buyer IDENTITY, not just a program NAME. The campaign should consistently answer: "After 10 days with Rich, you will BE a Force Multiplier — here is what a Force Multiplier does, thinks, and sees that other entrepreneurs do not."

**Implementation:**
- Registration page headline restructure: Lead with the identity destination, not the scale claim. Instead of "The 1,000x Multiplication Factor," try something in the register of: "The Moment You Become a Force Multiplier." (Scale as evidence; identity as destination.)
- Testimonials framed as identity adoptions: "Before Force Multiplier, I was a business owner. After, I became an architect. Here's what that actually looks like." (Real names required.)
- VIP upsell restructured around the identity: "You're already registered. Now decide if you want to enter the Force Multiplier cohort at the beginning — before you build, with Rich coaching your specific situation live." (Access as a stage of identity formation, not a separate product.)
- Program description: "Force Multiplier is not a course you take. It is a transformation you undergo. At the end of 10 days, you are not the same person who registered." (Identity language, not feature language.)

---

### Recommendation 2: Deploy Internal Mediators in Parallel with Rich

**The problem:** Rich functions as external mediator throughout the marketing pages, creating admiration but not the full activation of mimetic desire.

**Specific change:** Feature 3-5 peer-level success stories with full narrative depth — not testimonial quotes, but before/after stories from people who look like the buyer at enrollment. These should be placed on the registration page ABOVE Rich's credentials, not below them.

**Required story structure (Girardian):**
- "This is who I was when I enrolled" (the Subject — recognizably like the buyer)
- "This is what I built during the 10 days" (the transformation process — accessible, imitable)
- "This is who I am now" (the Object — the Force Multiplier identity)
- "This is the specific moment I knew it was working" (the desire-activation moment — the "if they can do it" trigger)

**Selection criteria for which students to feature:**
- Revenue range similar to buyer sweet spot ($500K-$2M, not outliers)
- Team size similar to actual buyer (1-5 people, not enterprise)
- Prior AI experience similar to buyer (burned skeptic who tried, not tech native)
- Industry where possible matches buyer industry cluster (marketing/coaching/agency)

**Where to place:** Registration page, between the "Two Lies" section and the "Why Listen to Me" section. Currently that space is empty — it is where the buyer's resistance peaks ("okay, he told me what's wrong, but can someone like ME actually do this?") and where internal mediation would do maximum work.

---

### Recommendation 3: Unify the Desire Object Across All Touchpoints

**The problem:** The desire object shifts across registration, VIP upsell, thank-you page, and webinar — preventing the formation of a coherent, stable desire.

**Specific change:** Choose ONE primary desire object and make it the through-line from first registration touch through program delivery. The strongest candidate is: **"The Architect" identity** — the business owner who no longer works in the system, only on it, because the system runs itself.

**Touchpoint-by-touchpoint unification:**

| Touchpoint | Current Object | Unified Object |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| Reg page headline | "1,000x Multiplication Factor" | "The moment you become the Architect" |
| Reg page body | Scale claims, leverage stats | What Architects can do that others cannot |
| VIP upsell | Access to Rich, 50-person room | "Architects get their blueprint reviewed before they build" |
| Thank-you page | Technical setup instructions | "Architects come prepared. Here is how you prepare." |
| Webinar opening | Historical moment framing | "Tonight you decide whether you become an Architect or stay a laborer" |
| Webinar close | Don't fall behind | "The Architects of your industry are deciding tonight" |

This does not require rewriting the marketing from scratch — it requires choosing the one thread that runs through all of it and making it explicit at every touch.

---

### Recommendation 4: Restructure the Registration Page to Lead with Rival Peer Mediation

**The problem:** The most powerful mimetic trigger in the campaign (rival peer mediation — "someone in your niche is going to do this") appears at the very bottom of the registration page after all the credentials and proof, where a significant portion of visitors have already decided.

**Specific change:** Move the rival peer mediation language to the opening sequence — before credentials, before proof, before the Two Lies. The page should open with the competitive threat:

Current bottom-of-page language: "Someone in your niche is going to build an unkillable company. Someone is going to multiply their capability by 1,000x while their competition is still debating which AI tool to download."

**Proposed page opening structure:**
1. Lead: The rival peer threat (mimetic rivalry trigger — urgent, immediate)
2. Bridge: "Here is why most people in your position will miss it" (the Three Camps framework as diagnosis)
3. Evidence: Why what they've tried didn't work (Two Lies + Hype Hangover validation)
4. Model: "I built the system that prevents this" (Rich as practitioner-model, not external guru)
5. Internal mediators: "Here is someone just like you who already did it" (peer transformation stories)
6. Close: Registration CTA with identity framing

**Rationale:** Girard identifies rival peer mediation as the most motivating form of desire. "Someone you know is going to figure this out and you will not" activates competitive desire more immediately than "this is the biggest wealth transfer in a generation," which is scale language that activated the Skeptical/Burned response instead.

---

### Recommendation 5: Separate the "Team Multiplier" Desire Structure from the "Personal Architect" Desire Structure

**The problem:** The product was designed for Team buyers, but the campaign successfully activated desire in Solo buyers. These two desire structures require different positioning, different social proof, and different close language.

**Specific change:** Run two distinct desire tracks from the same webinar, diverging at the offer presentation:

**Track 1: The Solo Force Multiplier** (founder without team, or founder who will use it personally first)
- Desire: "I want to think and operate like Rich — with AI as my personal capability amplifier"
- Model: Rich as sole practitioner who runs a $2.2M business with a small team
- Close: Solo tier at $5,000, framed as "giving yourself the tools Rich runs his own work on"
- Internal mediators: Solo founders who enrolled and transformed their personal workflow

**Track 2: The Team Force Multiplier** (founder with 3+ person team, wants organizational transformation)
- Desire: "I want my team to function the way Rich's team functions — everyone a force multiplier, not just me"
- Model: Rich as CEO-architect who multiplied his entire organization
- Close: Team tiers, framed as "the per-person cost of transforming your entire operation"
- Internal mediators: Founders who brought team members and describe the specific moment the team dynamic shifted

**Implementation options:**
- Segment by registration question ("How many people are on your team?") and deliver different email sequences to each segment
- Create two versions of the offer close in the webinar (same program, different close framing depending on audience skew)
- Most importantly: on future runs, let the actual buyer behavior (solo-dominant) shape the primary desire track rather than the originally designed track (team-dominant)

---

### Recommendation 6: Reduce the Skandalon — Bring the Scale Language Down

**The problem:** "1,000x multiplication," "6 vs. 6,000," and "Biggest Wealth Transfer In A Generation" function as hype language to a burned, skeptical audience, even when delivered with conviction rather than enthusiasm.

**Specific change:** Replace scale claims with *specificity claims*. Specificity signals authenticity to burned audiences; scale signals the hype they have already rejected.

| Current scale language | Specific replacement |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| "1,000x multiplication factor" | "A sales letter that used to take weeks now takes 45 minutes — not because I type faster, but because I'm no longer the laborer" |
| "6 vs. 6,000" | "D.F. runs a $150M company. He used the methodology. A fully AI-created sales letter brought in $1.3M in 7 days." |
| "Biggest wealth transfer in a generation" | "The window between now and when your largest competitor figures this out is 6-12 months. Rich knows this because he's seen three previous windows like it." |
| "Output of a hundred of me" | "My copywriting agents now find angles I wouldn't have found. They write drafts that beat my drafts. I became their editor, not their originator." |

The specificity does not reduce the claim — in some cases it makes it more impressive. It eliminates the hype register that triggers the Burned Skeptic's defensive response.

## SECTION 5: SUCCESS ELEMENTS

### Success Element 1: The Subject Was Forensically Accurate

The desire audit's highest praise goes to the buyer profiling. The Dominant Latent Narrative — "I built this business with my brain, my guts, and my work ethic. Now everyone tells me that's not enough anymore. I tried to learn the new thing. It didn't work. The courses were garbage. The tools broke..." — is Girardian subject-construction at its best. It names the identity wound without naming it as such. The buyer reads this and feels *recognized* — not sold to.

Girard's theory holds that desire is activated when the Subject sees themselves in the mirror the model holds up. This campaign held up an extraordinarily precise mirror to the Subject's current state. For buyers who were already in Rich's sphere, this recognition was the trigger that moved them from "interested" to "bought."

**Preserve and replicate:** The mechanism research that produced the five emotional layers (Hungry, Burned, Ashamed, Skeptical, Urgent) and the Identity Wound framework is genuinely rare. Most campaigns guess at buyer psychology. This campaign diagnosed it forensically. Every future campaign should begin with this level of subject-construction.

---

### Success Element 2: The Anti-Hype Positioning Was Correctly Identified and Largely Executed

The explicit decision to lead with "conviction, not excitement" and to validate the buyer's skepticism before selling was structurally correct for this market moment. The Two Lies framework was a clean deconstruction of the desire-killing options in the market.

In Girardian terms, this functioned as *desire clearance* — removing the other objects of desire that the market had already tried and rejected, so that the buyer's desire could flow toward the one remaining option (Rich's system). The campaign correctly identified that in a saturated, burned market, the path to activated desire runs through validation of prior failure — not through making a bigger, louder claim.

The phrase from the webinar opening — "I don't care if people have to leave and I miss the sale. If I can teach and share with you what I see coming here, it's that big of a deal" — was a live example of conviction-not-hype executing in real time. This registered as authentic to the audience because it violated the expected close-optimization behavior of a paid speaker.

**Preserve and replicate:** The anti-hype framing and the Two Lies structure. The specific language "conviction, not excitement" as a production principle.

---

### Success Element 3: The Live Build Was the Right Conversion Architecture

The brief states this correctly: "The live build IS the conversion event." And the avatar data confirms it: 19% of buyers cited watching Rich build live as the decisive moment.

In Girardian terms, the live build functioned as the most powerful form of desire activation available in a webinar context: *accessible modeling*. The model was not describing what he could do. He was doing it. The buyer was watching the desire object be created in real time by the model. This collapses the distance between model and subject — the most powerful mimetic move available.

No amount of testimonials, case studies, or scale claims can compete with watching the model demonstrate the capability live. This is why the draft mandate in the brief was correct: "Webinar creators should design the entire presentation to build toward and support this moment."

**Preserve and replicate:** The live build format, the "build toward the demo" webinar architecture, and the framing that the replay excludes the live build (creating urgency to attend live that is not artificial — it is genuinely true).

---

### Success Element 4: The Second-Order Model Structure Is a Genuine Competitive Moat

The "Guru to the Gurus" positioning — Rich as the person who shaped the market's most admired models — creates a mimetic structure that no competitor can replicate. In Girardian terms, Rich is a **model-of-models**: you don't just want to learn from him, you want access to the thinking that MADE the people you already want to be like.

This is the campaign's most durable structural advantage. Hormozi, Brunson, and Martell are direct models. Rich is a second-order model — the source that flows upstream of the direct models the market is already pursuing. This positioning will only become more valuable as the market continues to commoditize first-order AI training.

**Preserve and replicate:** Every campaign Rich runs should lean into this second-order model structure. Not as a credential (it often presents as one), but as a desire architecture: "You have been following the outputs of my thinking. Now you have access to the thinking itself."

---

### Success Element 5: The Pricing Architecture Correctly Reflected Team Structure — Even Though the Market Didn't Respond to It Yet

The tiered pricing (Solo: $5K, Small Team: $7,997, Mid Team: $12,997, Large Team: $19,997) was correct in principle even though the actual buyers predominantly took the Solo tier. The team structure created a *status ladder* within the product — a Girardian mechanism where the escalating investment signals escalating seriousness of intent.

More importantly: the team pricing architecture planted a seed for future desire. Buyers who enrolled Solo may return to Team pricing when they want to bring their team. The desire for organizational transformation was introduced even if it was not activated at enrollment. The product architecture is ahead of where the market's desire currently is — which is exactly where a market-defining program should be.

**Preserve and replicate:** The team pricing tiers. The framing of per-person cost as a price-anchoring mechanism. The planting of "bring your team next time" as a future desire that solo buyers carry out of the program.

## SUMMARY: THE CORE DIAGNOSTIC

The Force Multiplier campaign was a well-executed campaign operating on the correct diagnosis of buyer psychology with a partially intact desire triangle.

**What held:** The Subject mapping was forensic and accurate. The Model (Rich) had irreplaceable credibility and second-order model status. The anti-hype conviction positioning correctly read the market moment. The live build executed the most powerful mimetic conversion event available in a webinar format.

**What broke:** The Object — the desire destination — was fragmented across touchpoints and never crystallized into a single, adoptable identity label. The mediation type (predominantly external) was misaligned with the buyer's emotional state (burned, skeptical, needing peer-level accessibility). The campaign's designed desire target (Team buyer) did not match the desire that was actually activated (Solo buyer seeking personal proximity to Rich's capability).

**The one-sentence diagnostic:** The campaign successfully mediated the desire to *be near Rich's thinking* — but it was designed to mediate the desire to *transform your team*. Both are real desires. The one that converted was not the one the product was built for.

**The fix is not complex.** It requires choosing the desire object the campaign will primarily serve (Architect identity for Solo buyers OR Team Transformation for organizational buyers — not both simultaneously), crystallizing that object into a single, adoptable identity label, and deploying the internal mediators who have already made that transformation visible.

The raw material is the best in the market. The desire architecture needs one more turn of the dial.
