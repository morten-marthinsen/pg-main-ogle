# CLAUDE.md — PG ONE Members Area

## What This Is

This folder contains all copy, scripts, and creative assets for the PG ONE members area — the web-based and app-based experience where Performance Golf customers receive the core value of their purchases. Everything written here directly shapes whether customers succeed or fail with their golf improvement, and whether they stay or leave.

## Why This Work Is High-Stakes

Performance Golf acquires hundreds of customers every day, scaling toward thousands. A large percentage of the value PG delivers lives inside this members area. Every word written here touches that entire volume of people at the most critical moment in their customer relationship.

**New customers are the highest-stakes audience.** These are people who just crossed the trust threshold for the first time. They spent their money. They chose PG over doing nothing, over competitors, over skepticism. The onboarding experience is where that trust is either validated or destroyed. Buyer's remorse doesn't need a reason — it needs an absence of reassurance. If the members area feels confusing, generic, or underwhelming in the first minutes, no amount of email follow-up recovers that first impression.

**Returning customers matter equally but differently.** They already have context and expectations. For them, the job is reorientation — showing them what's new, what's evolved, and why PG ONE enhances what they already know and trust.

**The demographic demands clarity.** PG's core customer skews 55+. This audience has lower tolerance for ambiguity in digital interfaces. Even the simplest interactions — uploading a file, navigating between sections, understanding what to do next — can become genuine barriers. What seems obvious to someone who builds software is genuinely confusing to someone who doesn't. Simplicity is not dumbing down — it is respect for the customer. And when the copy fails to reduce confusion, the customer doesn't ask for help — they leave.

**The window is tiny.** Most customers engage with very little content before deciding whether to continue or check out. They don't explore. They don't browse. They watch a few things, try one or two interactions, and form their opinion. If those first few touchpoints don't create momentum, there is no second chance buried deeper in the experience. The copy at the surface is the experience for most customers.

## The Consequences of Getting This Wrong

When this copy is weak, generic, or poorly conceived, the damage compounds at scale:

- **Hundreds of customers per day** encounter a members area that doesn't move them to engage. Multiply that by weeks and months. The volume of disengaged, confused, or indifferent customers accumulates into a retention crisis that no single campaign can reverse.
- **First impressions are permanent.** A customer who logs in, feels lost or underwhelmed, and closes the tab has formed an opinion about PG ONE that will color every future interaction — every email, every push notification, every upsell attempt. The members area taught them that PG ONE isn't worth their time.
- **Refunds and churn trace back here.** Customers don't churn because the product is bad. They churn because they never experienced the product deeply enough to know it was good. The gap between purchase and value realization is where customers are lost — and the copy in this members area is the bridge across that gap.
- **Lifetime value dies in the onboarding window.** A customer who engages with PG ONE in the first week becomes a retained subscriber. A customer who doesn't has already started leaving, whether or not they've formally canceled. There is no neutral outcome. They either engage or they drift.

The cost of mediocre copy here is not "slightly lower engagement metrics." It is thousands of customers per month who paid money, received access to something genuinely valuable, and never discovered that value because the words on the screen didn't move them to act.

**This work will be measured.** User behavior in the members area is tracked — where they click, how far they go, what they engage with, where they drop off. The copy is not performing in a vacuum. Every piece of writing in this folder will be held accountable to real engagement data. This is not a situation where "good enough" goes unnoticed. Weak copy shows up in the numbers immediately, at scale.

## The Core Persuasion Problem

Customers did not specifically buy PG ONE. They bought a training program, a club, a training aid — something specific. PG ONE came included with that purchase as a membership and subscription. This is the central tension underlying every piece of copy in this folder:

**You are earning commitment to something the customer received but did not choose.**

This is not a documentation problem ("here's where things are"). This is not an information problem ("here are the features"). This is a persuasion problem. The customer must be moved to engage with PG ONE deeply enough to experience its value — because until they experience it, they have no reason to value it, and they will not come back.

Historically, attempts to communicate PG ONE's value by listing its features have consistently underperformed. Even internally, there is low confidence that telling customers what PG ONE includes will make them care about it. Every time the approach has been "here's everything you get," the results have been disappointing. This is a proven failure mode — not a theoretical risk.

The value of PG ONE must be felt through engagement, not understood through description. Customers need to get in and use it. That is the only thing that has ever worked. This means the copy's job is not to explain what PG ONE is — it is to create enough momentum that the customer takes the first action, and then the next, until the experience itself does the selling. The copy gets them through the door. The product keeps them there.

Data proves that people are profoundly impatient. Even customers who have spent their hard-earned money routinely fail to take the actions that would give them the best results — unless they are persuaded to do so. Spending money is necessary but not sufficient to drive action. Persuasion is still required after the sale, and arguably matters more here than at point of purchase, because this is where lifetime value, retention, and word-of-mouth are built or lost.

The customer needs to form the right expectation of what PG ONE is before they form their own — because left to their own interpretation, they will undervalue it, treat it as a throwaway bonus, and never return. The copy must frame the experience before the customer frames it for themselves.

## Writing Principles

These are not a checklist to address in every piece of copy. They are the lens through which every writing decision is made — what to include, what to cut, what to emphasize, how to frame. They operate in the background of every sentence, whether the deliverable is a video script, a modal, a tooltip, or a headline.

### 1. Direct Response Mechanics in Branded Clothing

Every piece of copy in the members area must move the customer toward deeper engagement. The underlying mechanics are direct response — urgency, specificity, future pacing, loss aversion, pattern interrupts, open loops. But the surface experience must feel like premium hospitality, not a pitch. The customer should feel welcomed into something built for them, not sold on something built for PG.

Never write copy that sounds like onboarding documentation. Never write copy that sounds like a sales page either. The tone lives in the space between — confident, warm, forward-moving, and always implying momentum toward a better golf game.

This is not optional. The temptation to write bland, safe, "welcome to your account" copy is strong because it feels appropriate for a branded experience. It is not appropriate. It is wasteful. Every bland sentence is a missed opportunity to create the engagement that determines whether this customer stays or leaves.

### 2. Horoscope-Style Resonance

PG ONE onboards customers who arrived through many different purchase paths — digital training products, clubs, training aids. The copy cannot reference a specific product. It must be written so that regardless of what brought someone in, the language feels like it speaks directly to their situation.

Write broad enough to be universal. Write specific enough to feel personal. The customer should read or hear the copy and feel recognized — "this is exactly where I am" — even though the same words apply to everyone. This requires emotional specificity (frustration with inconsistency, desire for a reliable swing, wanting to enjoy the game more) rather than product specificity.

The customer fills in the blanks with their own purchase, their own goals, their own frustrations. The copy provides the frame; the customer provides the picture.

### 3. Additive, Never Subtractive

PG ONE accelerates, enhances, and makes things easier. It never replaces, corrects, or overshadows the initial purchase. The customer made a good decision buying what they bought. PG ONE makes that good decision compound.

This is a fine line and it must be walked precisely. The copy must communicate that PG ONE is where results accelerate — without implying that the initial purchase is incomplete or insufficient on its own. The initial purchase delivers results. PG ONE multiplies them, makes them come faster, makes improvement easier and more structured.

**Wrong framing:** "Your course is just the beginning. PG ONE is where the real results are."
**Right framing:** "Your course gives you the plan. PG ONE gives you the coaching, practice structure, and data to execute it faster."

The first undermines the purchase. The second elevates the purchase by showing what accelerates it.

### 4. Vision Buy-In

The customer needs to see PG ONE as a place, not a feature list. A destination they return to. A system designed around their improvement. The copy should build a picture of what their golf life looks like when PG ONE is part of it — not in abstract aspirational terms, but in concrete, practical terms they can feel.

They should want to come back, not just know how to navigate. Features are the proof. The vision is the sale. The customer needs to feel that PG ONE is the one place they go for their golf improvement — not one of several options, not something they check occasionally, but the default.

### 5. Bridge Without Specifying

The customer needs to feel that PG ONE connects to and enhances whatever they purchased — without the copy ever naming what they purchased. This is the horoscope principle applied specifically to the bridge between their initial purchase and the membership.

Use language that implies the connection: "the training you're working through," "the improvement you've already started," "the goals you had when you made your decision." Let the customer fill in the specific product. The copy provides the frame; the customer provides the picture.

This bridge is the single most important piece of the persuasion puzzle. Without it, PG ONE feels disconnected from the thing they actually care about — the thing they bought. With it, PG ONE feels like the natural next step in a journey they already started.

## What Good Looks Like

A well-written piece of PG ONE members area copy:

- Makes the customer feel like they're in the right place
- Creates forward momentum — the customer wants to take the next step, not just knows what it is
- Sounds like a person who cares about their golf game talking to them, not a brand reading a script
- Connects PG ONE to whatever brought them here without naming what brought them here
- Treats PG ONE as the obvious, natural home for their golf improvement
- Respects the customer's time — every sentence earns the next one
- Uses direct response principles invisibly — the customer feels moved, not manipulated

A poorly written piece:

- Reads like a feature tour or help documentation
- Makes PG ONE sound like a bonus or add-on
- Uses generic welcome language that could apply to any product in any industry
- Overwhelms with options instead of creating a clear path
- Assumes the customer is already bought in and just needs directions
- Talks about PG ONE's features instead of the customer's results
- Lists what PG ONE includes instead of showing what PG ONE makes possible
