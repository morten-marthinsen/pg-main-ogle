## The Persuasion Problem

Customers did not buy PG1. They bought a course, a club, or a training aid. PG1 came included as a membership. The central tension: **you are earning commitment to something the customer received but did not choose.**

Listing PG1's features has failed consistently. The only thing that has ever worked is getting customers to engage deeply enough that the product sells itself. The copy's job is to create enough momentum for the customer to take the first action — then the next — until the experience does the selling.

**The window is tiny.** Most customers engage with very little content before deciding whether to come back. They don't explore. They don't browse. The first few touchpoints are the experience for most customers.

**The demographic is 55+.** Lower tolerance for digital ambiguity. Even uploading a file can be a genuine barrier. Simplicity is not dumbing down — it is respect.

## Core Positioning Concepts

These concepts come from PG1's broader product positioning and must be woven naturally into all three scripts — never listed, always narrated.

### Root Swing Flaw (The Lead Domino)
Every swing has one root flaw — the one thing that, when fixed, creates a domino effect that improves 3-4 other problems automatically. Most golfers stay stuck because they're working on the wrong thing. The coach's job is to identify the root flaw and build a plan around fixing it first. This is the thesis of why coaching matters.

### "The Right Thing, in the Right Order, at the Right Time"
This phrase captures what PG1 Pulse does differently from scattered self-improvement. The Smart Path ensures the customer is always focused correctly — not randomly picking drills or bouncing between tips. Use this framework across all scripts.

### Intelligence, Not Just Information
Golf has democratized information (YouTube, courses, data). PG1 adds the intelligence layer — the personalization that tells you what to do with the information. The Smart Path isn't more content. It's the system that makes content work for YOUR specific game.

### Practice Smarter, Not More
Respects the customer's time. They don't need to practice more — they need to practice the right things. The Smart Path ensures every session is targeted.

### Coach Sends Live Video Back
Not a text analysis. Not a generic tip. The coach records a real video breakdown showing the customer's swing with annotations, flaw identification, and targeted drills. This specificity is a major differentiator and should be stated explicitly.

### "No Guessing. No Overwhelm."
Clean contrast line. Captures the before/after in four words. Use sparingly — once per script max.

## Writing Principles (Non-Negotiable)

These principles must be present in every line of copy. They are not a checklist — they are the lens through which every sentence is written or cut.

### 1. Direct Response in Branded Clothing

Every sentence moves the customer toward deeper engagement. The underlying mechanics are direct response — urgency, specificity, future pacing, loss aversion, open loops. But the surface must feel like premium hospitality, not a pitch. The customer should feel welcomed into something built for them, not sold on something built for PG.

Never write copy that sounds like onboarding documentation. Never write copy that sounds like a sales page. The tone lives between — confident, warm, forward-moving, always implying momentum toward a better golf game.

### 2. Horoscope-Style Resonance

Customers arrive through many purchase paths — courses, clubs, training aids. The copy cannot reference a specific product. It must be written so that regardless of what brought them in, the language feels like it speaks directly to their situation.

Write broad enough to be universal. Write specific enough to feel personal. The customer fills in their own purchase, goals, and frustrations. The copy provides the frame; the customer provides the picture.

This requires **emotional specificity** (frustration with inconsistency, wanting a reliable swing, enjoying the game more) rather than product specificity.

### 3. Additive, Never Subtractive

PG1 accelerates, enhances, and makes things easier. It never replaces, corrects, or overshadows the initial purchase. The customer made a good decision. PG1 makes that decision compound.

**Wrong:** "Your course is just the beginning. PG1 is where the real results are."
**Right:** "Your course gives you the plan. PG1 gives you the coaching, practice structure, and data to execute it faster."

**The additive formula for returning users (Script 3 specifically):**
1. Validate what they've been doing ("you've been here, you know this")
2. Name the gap as something they didn't have access to — not something they were missing. "The rest was up to you" implies PG just got upgraded. "You were missing the key ingredient" implies PG was incomplete. The first is additive. The second is subtractive disguised as additive.
3. Show how the new thing fills that gap without replacing what they already use
4. Pattern: "Before, you had [good thing]. Now you have [good thing] PLUS [new thing]."

**Note:** The additive principle also applies to Script 1 (coaching). Coaching must be framed as the thing that makes the courses work faster — not as a separate, better product. The bridge line: "The courses teach you the concepts. Your coach makes sure those concepts work for YOUR swing." This keeps coaching additive to the course experience.

### 4. Vision Buy-In

The customer needs to see PG1 as a place — a destination they return to, a system designed around their improvement. Features are the proof. The vision is the sale.

### 5. Bridge Without Specifying

Connect PG1 to whatever the customer purchased without naming what they purchased. Use language like: "the training you're working through," "the improvement you've already started." The customer fills in the specific product.

## Copy Checklist (Apply to All Scripts)

1. **Name the transformation** — not "improvement," but the shift from frustration to confidence
2. **Name the specific emotion** — frustrated, embarrassed, confused, confident, fired up
3. **Include physical sensations** where appropriate — tight grip, tension in the arms
4. **Put them in a specific moment** — Saturday morning, first tee, the range
5. **Make progress feel attainable** — real gains, not perfection
6. **Emphasize personalization without tech jargon** — "built for your swing," not "AI-powered algorithm"
7. **Lead with feeling, then show data** — emotion first, proof second
8. **Name The Scattered Playbook as enemy** — random tips, conflicting advice, no clear path. **Audience-specific:** Use explicitly for new users (Script 2) — they just lived it. Do NOT use for returning users (Script 3) — they already chose PG, and naming the enemy implies their previous PG experience was the problem. For returning users, the contrast is "great courses alone" vs. "great courses plus a system," NOT "scattered mess" vs. "PG1."
9. **Position PG1 as weapon against confusion** — one connected system vs. scattered fragments. Same audience caveat as #8.
10. **Use Brixton's voice** — encouraging + real (see voice rules above)
11. **Focus on loving the game** — the ultimate goal is joy, confidence, looking forward to the next round

### Forbidden Patterns

- Overhype ("CRUSH 50 YARDS!!!")
- Tech jargon ("AI-powered ML algorithms")
- Stern/dismissive tone
- Golf cliches ("Take your game to the next level")
- Promises of perfection
- Robotic/clinical voice
- Data before feeling
- Focus on pros (this is for everyday golfers)

## Tone Guardrails (Learned From Drafting)

These are judgment calls that surfaced during writing and need to be codified:

### Loss Aversion vs. Future Pacing
In modal-length scripts (60–90s), **future pacing is the preferred urgency mechanism** — not loss aversion. "Within your first week, you'll have a coach reviewing your swing" creates urgency by painting an attainable, concrete outcome that's close enough to feel real. Loss aversion ("the ones who skip it don't come back") is optional and should be used at most as a single sentence — if at all. In testing, the scripts land cleaner without it. If used: frame as aspiration missed, not punishment received.

### The "One of Many" vs. "The Face" Problem
Martin Chuck is not "one of the coaches helping you." He's the credibility anchor for the entire coaching system. He introduces the coaching experience as the expert who designed it. The user's assigned coach may be someone different, but Martin is the reason they trust the system. Use: "this is how coaching works inside Performance Golf" — not "I'm one of the people here."

### Scattered Playbook — Audience Split
- **New users (Script 2):** Name the enemy directly. They just lived it. "Random tips, conflicting advice, 12 swing thoughts" — this is their last 6 months.
- **Returning users (Script 3):** Do NOT name The Scattered Playbook. They already chose PG. Calling out the scattered approach implies their previous PG experience was part of the problem. For them, the contrast is "courses alone" vs. "courses plus a complete system."

### 55+ Upload Language
Don't use golf jargon for camera positions. Use physical placement instructions: "Set your phone behind you, pointed at the target" beats "record down the line." Describe where the phone goes, not what the angle is called.

### Feature Listing vs. Vision Painting
When describing what the Smart Path includes, weave features into a narrative of what happens to the customer — don't list them. "You'll learn from our top coaches, connect with a real coach who reviews your swing, practice with structured drills, and track your rounds" reads as a journey. The same items in bullet-point form read as a feature comparison chart.

### Sentence-Level Pacing for Modal Scripts
Every sentence must earn the next one. In a 60–90 second modal, the customer is one click away from closing. The test: if you removed this sentence, would the script lose momentum? If not, cut it. Modal scripts should feel tight and propulsive — not comprehensive. Leave the comprehensive details for the experience itself.

### The "Everything Moves Together" Line
In the new user script, the single most important differentiator to land is that PG1 Pulse is a connected system — not a collection of separate tools. The line "everything moves together" (or equivalent) is the thesis of the Smart Path. It's what separates PG1 from every other golf app that has courses, has coaching, has drills — but doesn't connect them into one path. This line should appear once, clearly, and not be buried.

## Script Format Spec

Each script is written as flowing copy — **one line per paragraph** (one thought, one beat). Visual/screen direction appears on its own line in brackets:

```
[Screen shows PG1 Pulse Smart Path dashboard]

Hey, I'm Brixton — and what you're looking at right now is the single biggest upgrade we've ever made to Performance Golf.

[Click into the first lesson]

This is your path. Built for your swing, your tendencies, your game.
```

Rules:
- Visual notes in `[brackets]` only where needed — not every line
- On-camera vs. voiceover noted for Martin Chuck script
- No music/sound effect cues — voiceover + visual direction only
- Estimated total duration per script noted at the top
