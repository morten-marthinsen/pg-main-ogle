# THE PROBLEM

---

## The universal truth

Everyone agrees on one thing:

**Retention comes from progress.**

Golfers stay when they feel they're getting better.
They leave when they don't.

The problem isn't *whether* progress matters.
The problem is **how progress is experienced and remembered**.

---

## The enemy we're fighting: The Scattered Playbook

Right now, most golfers are trapped in what we call **The Scattered Playbook**.

It looks like this:
- Random YouTube tips that contradict each other
- Magazine drills with no context
- Buddy's advice from his lesson last week
- Training aids collecting dust in the garage
- Standing over the ball with 12 swing thoughts

**What The Scattered Playbook creates:**
- Paralysis and confusion
- No clear path forward
- Information collected but never connected
- More effort = more frustration

This is the pattern we're fighting against. Not instructors. Not other companies. The *system* that keeps golfers confused.

---

## Problem 1: Progress is invisible

Most golf products measure progress in ways that are:

- **Internal** (felt, not seen)
- **Abstract** (confidence, control, stability)
- **Fragmented** (many metrics, no anchor)
- **Private** (nothing shareable)

When progress is only *felt*, it's fragile.

A chunked wedge can undo a month of momentum.

That frustrated walk back to the cart? That's not a bad shot.
That's The Scattered Playbook winning.

Even when improvement is real, golfers keep asking:

- "Am I actually getting better?"
- "Is this really working?"
- "Would anyone else even notice?"

If the answer is unclear, motivation drops.

Progress that can't be **recognized** is hard to protect.
Progress that isn't protected doesn't drive long-term retention.

## Problem 3: Too many metrics = no shared focus

Today, progress is spread across:
- Swing mechanics
- Practice behavior
- Mental state
- App engagement
- Multiple dashboards

This creates confusion:
- Users don't know what "success" looks like
- Product teams don't know what to optimize toward
- LTV becomes impossible to model predictably

**When everything matters, nothing anchors.**

Users don't build habits around dashboards.
They build habits around one thing they care about protecting.

Even when improvement *does* happen, it usually stays private.

Most progress:
- Can't be shown clearly
- Can't be explained quickly
- Isn't visually obvious to others

As a result:

This forces growth to rely on paid acquisition rather than the product itself creating moments worth sharing.

Progress that drives retention must be:

# THE SINGLE METRIC

**How Swing Match % Works (Without Exposing Complexity)**

---

## Swing Match % is an identity signal, not a swing stat

This is critical to understand:

Swing Match % is not raw data.
It's the **final expression** of progress—experienced as a single, clear number.

Under the hood, it's built from objective, angle-based comparisons.
On the surface, it's experienced as: *"Here's how close you are to becoming the golfer you want to be."*

**Users never see mechanics.**
**They see progress.**

---

## Rules for Swing Match % (non-negotiable)

This metric must:

- **Update weekly** — consistent rhythm creates habit
- **Improve with effort, not perfection** — progress compounds
- **Be impossible to fake** — fed by real behavior
- **Be emotionally meaningful** — connected to identity
- **Feel like something worth protecting** — stopping = loss

If a user stops using the app, this is what they feel they're abandoning.

---

## What feeds Swing Match %

Everything rolls up into one number:

- **Practice behavior** — showing up matters
- **Swing Scan validation** — real measurement
- **App engagement** — consistent usage
- **Magical Shot frequency** — capturing the wins
- **Product usage** — training aids and gear that accelerate

Everything else is secondary.

One metric. One focus. One story.

---

## The five swing phases (how it's measured)

Swing Match % is evaluated across **five universal swing phases**:

1. Setup
2. Backswing
3. Downswing
4. Impact
5. Finish

These phases are:
- Familiar to golfers
- Visually recognizable
- Consistent across skill levels

This creates a stable framework for comparison.

---

## Checkpoints within each phase

Each phase contains **3–5 specific checkpoints**, selected for visual and biomechanical relevance:

- Rotation degrees
- Tilt angles
- Bend amounts
- Shaft angles
- Clubface orientation

Each checkpoint is:
- Angle-based
- Objectively measurable
- Comparable to a reference swing

The system does **not** evaluate "good" or "bad."
It evaluates **distance from the target identity**.

---

## Identity-based comparison

Every user swing is compared against a **target swing model**, defined by their selected identity:

- A professional golfer
- An influencer
- A modeled archetype

For each checkpoint:
- The system measures the angular difference
- That difference maps to a proximity score
- Proximity scores roll up within each phase

This creates a **relative match**, not an absolute judgment.

**The user never sees this math.**

---

## From checkpoints to Swing Match %

Checkpoint proximity scores are combined through a weighted equation to produce:

**One Swing Match %**

Weighting allows the system to:
- Prioritize high-leverage positions
- Reduce noise from low-impact differences
- Maintain stability week to week

This ensures the metric:
- Rewards meaningful effort
- Avoids volatility
- Reflects true directional change

---

## What the user experiences (by design)

The user does **not** see:
- Angles
- Degrees
- Equations
- Phase breakdowns
- Multi-metric dashboards

They see:
- Their Swing Match % after upload
- One recommended focus area
- Clear visual before/after comparisons

The experience is:

*"Here's where you are."*
*"Here's what moves you forward fastest."*
*"Here's proof that it's working."*

That's it. That's the whole experience.

---

## Summary

Swing Match % is:
- Grounded in precise measurement
- Expressed as simple identity progress
- Reinforced through visible change

Complexity lives under the hood.
Meaning lives on the surface.

**When progress feels possible and personal, you don't just play better. You start to love your game in a whole new way.**

# THE REFLECT STEP

**The Emotional Hinge of the Loop**

---

## Why the Reflect step matters most

This is where most platforms fail.

Reflection that shows data without meaning creates awareness.
**Reflection that shows identity creates attachment.**

The Reflect step is the emotional hinge of the entire loop.

It's the moment where belief either locks in—or leaks out.

---

## The Reflect step must answer one question

Before showing any data, the Reflect step answers:

**"What does this say about me as a golfer right now?"**

Before charts.
Before metrics.
Before analysis.

Identity first. Data second.

---

## Reflect step structure (the standard)

### 1) Identity statement (always first)

One sentence. Calm. Affirming. Directional.

Examples:
- "You're becoming more consistent week to week."
- "Your great shots are happening more often."
- "Your misses are tighter than last week."
- "You're showing more control under pressure."

This sentence should feel like **a coach speaking**, not software reporting.

This is Brixton's voice: unwavering, constructive, uplifting.

---

### 2) Supporting signal (optional, minimal)

Only show data that supports the identity statement.

Examples:
- "You captured 3 more quality shots than last week."
- "Your Swing Match improved by 2% for your focus area."
- "Your consistency stayed within range for most of the session."

**No dashboards.**
**No multi-metric panels.**

One identity statement. One supporting signal. That's it.

---

### 3) Emotional anchor

Tie the reflection back to effort.

Examples:
- "This is the result of showing up."
- "Consistency like this compounds."
- "Keep doing what you're doing."
- "Make progress permanent."

The user should leave the screen feeling **steady and motivated**, not analytical.

---

## What the Reflect step should NEVER do

- Dump mechanics
- Show 10+ metrics
- Create fixation
- Increase self-criticism
- Make the golfer more analytical than confident

**The one filter for every Reflect output:**

Before showing any insight, ask:

*"Does this make the golfer more confident or more analytical?"*

If confident → show it.
If analytical → hide it.

---

## Why this structure works

When identity is stated first:
- Progress feels real
- Effort feels validated
- Motivation stays stable
- Bad sessions don't spiral

The golfer leaves thinking:

*"I'm someone who's getting better."*

Not:

*"I need to analyze what went wrong."*

**That's the difference between retention and churn.**
