**Headline:** How to make your sales promotions more profitable

**Byline:** by Ogilvy & Mather

Ogilvy & Mather has been a partner with its clients in creating over $1,500,000,000 worth of sales promotion. Here are 23 of the lessons we've learned.

**Couponing**

**1.** If you can afford to go the whole hog, use a combination of coupons delivered in newspapers, magazines, on the package, and by direct mail.

On the average, you can expect 75 percent of coupons redeemed within 3 to 6 months.

**2.** If a coupon has poor results, try to find out why. So far no one can predict whether a coupon will do well or poorly.

**3.** Add an ad to your coupon promotion — the dollar allocation to the coupon plus a small amount for the ad makes the promotion far more effective.

**4.** If you are running a national promotion, be sure to get state-by-state or regional breakouts of redemption, so you can fine-tune future activity.

**5.** Adding coupons to your advertising can improve your promotion and simultaneously generate better ad readership.

**6.** Consider adding coupons to your advertising promotions to deliver a powerful message in a coupon-cluttered environment.

**7.** Add a second promotion device to some couponed promotions — such as sweepstakes.

**8.** Look for the use of in-store when you tie the coupon in to an in-store promotion.

**Premiums**

As an advertiser, Ogilvy & Mather goes through a formal screening of all premiums to be sure of quality. We use these guidelines:

**9.** When you have to stretch your dollars, use a self-liquidating premium. If it is something the consumer can't buy elsewhere — the perceived value is even higher. And if you can add the premium to the core pack, then the premium of value to the consumer creates additional demand.

**10.** Consider using "continuity" or "collector" promotions — a series of items — to build regular repurchase. They help build traffic over a long period.

**11.** You can sell yours at full retail premium and still get promotional value. With direct mail, you can easily do retail, with a quarter to one-third price there are a number of excellent premium items that can yield full retail price in promotional values.

**12.** Use the smallest possible allowances for trade promotions rather than bigger allowances which are easily combined. Generally, enough dollars to reach the equivalent of 10 to 15 percent off invoice are enough.

**Price-Offs**

**13.** Put your price-off reductions in the 15 to 20 percent range. More could cheapen your brand and make it difficult to go back to normal prices. Less — 5 percent, even 10 percent — does not provide enough incentive.

**14.** The best price-off promotion is one that does something for the customer beyond price. Getting more of your product, getting it in a variety of attractive sizes or offers for free.

**Refund offers**

**15.** A cash refund, one that exceeds the price of the product, has proven itself a very good promotion. The refund must be enough to motivate purchase. You will find that retail coupons for trade redemption are more effective.

Few experts have had experienced positive results with refund offers.

**Contests and Sweepstakes**

**16.** Whenever possible, tie your contest or sweepstakes to your product's advertising theme.

**17.** Every sweepstakes, in Ogilvy & Mather's experience, tends to be a trade-oriented promotion — primary usage to sell in for a certain period of time.

Don't expect your contest or promotion to produce a permanent sales increase. And be wary of the effect of using premiums to promote trial. Consider this approach: build your entry requirements around proof-of-purchase.

You get pretty sophisticated if your contest plus all the advertising and promotion around the contest don't break even from the profits of incremental sales.

**"Seed-for" Premiums**

**18.** Self-Liquidating Premiums — which are paid for by the consumer — can provide trade interest and off-shelf display. Self-liquidating premiums are less costly to the manufacturer and provide the consumer with a better premium.

For the retailer, enough to sell for off-shelf display.

Close the loop — as in stamps with the coupons combined. "Magic Number" is a term used for a combination of coupons, sweepstakes, and premiums on the back of your package. It's difficult to explain in 25 words or less but it works.

**19.** Play it straight with coupon advertising. Advertisers have "fooled" consumers into mistaking their advertising for editorial matter. Don't sacrifice your permanent good will for a few more coupon redemptions.

**Premium Packs**

**20.** Select your premium with great care. A good premium pack accomplishes several objectives simultaneously: it builds display, it's off-shelf, it builds brand trial or reuse, it identifies your brand. Find premiums that accomplish all four.

And try to find a premium at a price that will let you give the consumer good value.

**21.** The most effective shelf-building. Advertising for premium packs is the most effective shelf-building advertising you can do. But don't pay more for the premium than you can possibly afford.

Buy bulk before you leap. A hedging fund for buying premiums — a reserve against the possibility that the premium may do less well than you hoped — will pay for itself many times over in peace of mind. Ogilvy & Mather will simply reject your promotion if you have not been able to test it first.

**22.** Apply on-line per share premiums to build trial, not loyalty. Or use them as accessories that add extra value to your product, such as recipe books, garden tools, food tongs, toothbrush holders, and similar items.

It will tell you the maximum profitable promotional expenditure for almost any promotional plan that's practical.
