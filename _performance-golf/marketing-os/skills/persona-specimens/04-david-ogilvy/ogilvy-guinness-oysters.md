## Headline

## Context & Background

This is the first advertisement Ogilvy wrote as the head of his own agency.

From The King of Madison Avenue: "Ogilvy the research director was taking the train home to Connecticut one evening in 1950 when an idea for Guinness popped into his head. He got off at the next stop and called his office: 'You won't believe this, I've had an idea.' (He said his family was equally astounded with this first evidence that he could be creative.)

The idea was to borrow interest for Guinness from the fascinating foods you drink it with. Immersing himself in a Yale biologist's book on shellfish, Ogilvy conceived 'The Guinness Guide to Oysters,' a guide to nine varieties of oysters. The text was by copywriter Peter Geer; the concept was pure Ogilvy. An instant success, the oyster guide was followed by guides to game birds, cheeses, and other foods that go well with Guinness."

## Key Takeaways

- Try associating your brand to other popular complimentary items like Ogilvy did with oysters and Guinness. That way, people make the association and are more likely to pair the two or group the two in their minds.
- Take your mind off of touting your brand for a second and think about an ad you can create that is useful, interesting and that people would share with others -- that's what Ogilvy did here. He even mentions at the very end to contact them if you want a framed version of the ad.
- Nothing compliments a great photo like a descriptive caption. It gives life and meaning to the visual. Always try to use captions with your photos.
- The descriptions are absolutely brilliant because they use the power of storytelling and fascination to give each oyster depth and intrigue. There's a history behind each one that makes them seem much more important than originally thought.

## OCR Extracted Body Copy (2026-02-15)

**Title:** GUINNESS GUIDE TO OYSTERS

**CAPE CODS** — An oyster of superb flavor. It is found among the channels, which wraps around the oyster, and gives the meat a salty, full-bodied taste. The battle here is between its friends, with the outside, and the outsider, is encrusted with a good meal, but also, no Guinness.

**CHINCOTEAGUE** — These oysters have a salty flavor all their own. They were a smash hit with the Indians who feasted on every sort of oyster. Rich in iodine, calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, Vitamins A, B, Carotene, riboflavin and niacin. The Emperor Tiberius prized them.

**BLUEPOINT** — These delicious little oysters from the famous English variety of which the stock varies. 7 kinds or rather ranges of taste from Long Island — depending on where, precisely, they are picked, from briny to bland and flat to hard to half part brackish. They are the most aristocratic day fisheries of my life.

**OYSTER BAY** — Oyster Bays are mild and heart-shaped. It is said that oysters grow in a single. Hearts have been sold since then with small ones. They wash it in open sand and then put the same in larger oysters.

**LYNNHAVENS** — These gigantic oysters come from the Broad's homestake. They look as if they might have been made to order in any silver tea tools. The Restaurateurs owned in Maine consume three million oysters annually.

**NEW ORLEANS** — This was Jean Lafitte's favorite oyster. They are fat, white, and flabby. Valuable pearls are never found in eastern oysterlands, the family in which East Coast oysters belong.

**TANGIER** — This is one of the sweetest and most nourished oysters. It comes from the Eastern Shore of Maryland. Particularly fine when fried. Famous oysters. Oysters have long been the perfect complement for all sea-food.

**DELAWARE BAY** — This was William Penn's favorite oyster. Only 1/5th of oysters eaten in Pennsylvania come from here. But when they are easy on a cold day there is no finer way to eat them than on a Guinness on Chestnut Street — the flavor will be sweetly found in whole.

**CHINCOTEAGUES** — These oysters are found on the oyster reefs, but some West Coast growers are at the Olympia, which is no bigger than your thumbnail. Both Chincoteagues and Olympias go well with Guinness.

**Footer:** ALL OYSTERS taste their best when washed down with drafts of Guinness — what Professor Saintsbury, in "Notes On A Cellar-Book" called "dark milled liquor: the excellent of black velvet." Most of the malt used to make Guinness in America comes from the fertile farms of Southern Ireland, and the yeast is descended from the yeast used by Guinness in Dublin for a hundred and ninety years ago.
