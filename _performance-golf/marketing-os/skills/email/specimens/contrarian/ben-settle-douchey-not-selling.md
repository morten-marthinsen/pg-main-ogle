## Full Email Text

<!-- [OPENING -- Disturbing trend observation targeting his own subscribers] -->
I've been noticing a disturbing trend lately -- even amongst some of my "Email Players" subscribers.

(Who should know better).

And that trend is...

Not selling in every email.

<!-- [BODY -- Moral inversion: the contrarian reframe] -->
Why is this disturbing?

Because, for one thing, in most (not all -- there are exceptions to this, but generally speaking...) cases if you don't plug a product or service in your emails (or something that will directly and clearly lead to the sale of one of your products or services), you just completely wasted your time writing the email.

And secondly:

There are people who think they are being "nice guys" by not selling.

They're not.

They're being complete and utter douche bags.

Listen, I've said it before, I'll say it again:

(And I'll KEEP saying this forever...)

If you have something to sell that can benefit peoples' lives, then it's your moral and ethical duty to let them at least know about its existence in every email.

Frankly, it's selfish NOT to let them know.

After all...

People are going to spend money on something if they have a problem they want to fix... so isn't it better they buy your righteous product or service rather than your inferior competition's?

How does it help either of you to keep your product a secret?

<!-- [BRIDGE -- Moral of the story + daily cadence instruction] -->
Moral of the story?

Sell your list something.

Every day.

And, do it without apology or regret.

Only then a douche bag won't you be...

Ben Settle

## Structural Analysis
- **Conventional Wisdom Challenged:** "It's polite and respectful to not sell in every email" -- the widely-held belief that constant selling annoys your list and that good marketers provide value without always pitching.
- **Reframe Device:** Moral inversion through logic -- Settle flips the moral polarity. Not selling is reframed as selfish and "douchey" because (a) you're withholding a solution from people who need it, and (b) they'll spend the money anyway on an inferior competitor's product. The reframe uses the reader's own moral framework against them.
- **Lesson Extraction:** If your product genuinely helps people, not telling them about it in every email is a disservice to them, not a kindness. Selling is a moral and ethical duty, not an imposition.
- **Bridge Type:** Imperative bridge -- "Sell your list something. Every day. And, do it without apology or regret." The instruction IS the bridge because the product (Email Players) teaches exactly how to execute the instruction.
- **Key Structural Move:** Meta-coherence -- the email itself sells a product about selling in emails. The message and the execution are the same thing. The reader experiences the proof of the argument while reading the argument. This self-demonstrating structure is the most elegant form of contrarian proof: the email IS the evidence.
