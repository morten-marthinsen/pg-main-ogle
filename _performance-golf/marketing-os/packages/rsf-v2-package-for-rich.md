## CONDITION 5: NARRATIVE REORGANIZATION (NR)

### Definition

The degree to which a Big Idea reorganizes the audience's **domain-specific biographical narrative** — making past struggles/experiences suddenly make sense and installing a new self-concept within that domain.

### Critical: Domain-Specific, Not Whole-Life

NR applies to the audience's story **within the domain the product addresses**, not their entire life:

| Domain | What NR Reorganizes |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Financial (End of America)** | Their feelings about the country, economics, retirement — why investments haven't worked, why they feel uneasy about the future |
| **Golf (Slice fix)** | Their feelings about their swing, their history with the slice, why lessons/tips haven't worked, their identity as a golfer |
| **Health (Diet)** | Their health journey, why diets failed, why they've struggled with weight, their relationship with food |
| **Relationships** | Their relationship history, why past relationships failed, their patterns in love |

### What High-NR Big Ideas Do

From your call: **"confirms, amplifies, names, and connects"**

1. **CONFIRMS** — "Your experiences are true/valid"
2. **AMPLIFIES** — "It's even more significant than you thought"
3. **NAMES** — "Here's what it's called / here's the pattern"
4. **CONNECTS** — "All these disparate experiences are actually one story"

### NR Scoring Scale

| NR Score | Description | Audience Response |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1-3 | No biographical relevance | "This could be anyone's problem" |
| 4-5 | Some past-explaining power | "I guess that could explain some things" |
| 6-7 | Strong reorganization | "This explains my whole history with [domain]" |
| 8-10 | Complete narrative transformation | "I always knew this but couldn't put my finger on it — now my entire story makes sense" |

### The Missing Chapter Effect

From your testimonials:
- "something I have always known. But could not put my finger on"
- "making the solution seem as though we knew it all along even though we didn't"

This is the NR signal — when the audience feels like the Big Idea explains something they already knew but couldn't articulate.

## HOW NR INTEGRATES: FSSIT-FIRST (Option B)

Rather than just scoring Big Ideas for NR at the end, NR is now **foundational** through FSSIT-first generation:

### The Integration Flow

```
STEP 1: FSSIT CANDIDATES SCORED FOR NR
         ↓
         latent_resonance_field.json now includes:
         - narrative_reorganization_potential (1-10)
         - past_explaining_power (description)
         ↓
STEP 2: BIG IDEA GENERATION STARTS FROM HIGH-NR FSSITs
         ↓
         Top FSSIT selection prioritizes:
         PRIMARY: narrative_reorganization_potential ≥ 7
         SECONDARY: momentum_adjusted_strength
         ↓
STEP 3: BIG IDEAS INHERIT NR AS FOUNDATIONAL
         ↓
         Each Big Idea angle must:
         - Preserve the FSSIT's past-explaining power
         - Explicitly explain WHY past didn't work in this domain
         - Connect root cause + mechanism + promise to complete the narrative
         ↓
STEP 4: NR VERIFIED AT SCORING (NOT FIRST INTRODUCED)
         ↓
         Tournament selection includes Biographer perspective:
         "Does this explain their past and change how they see their story?"
```

### Why Option B (FSSIT-First NR) Is More Powerful

**Option A (scoring only):** Generate Big Ideas → Check NR score → Flag low NR
**Option B (FSSIT-first):** Start from high-NR FSSITs → NR is foundational → Verify preservation

Option B produces higher-quality NR because the past-explaining power is the STARTING POINT, not a validation check.

## ADDITIONAL UPGRADES FROM RICH'S PATTERNS (v4.2)

After analyzing Rich's three packages (Dispatcher-Manager-Agent, Mechanism Ideator Enhanced, ZenithPro Webinar Arena), we identified two needle-moving upgrades that address previously unknown gaps:

### Upgrade 1: Marketplace Reality Check (Dual Evaluation Layer)

**Source:** ZenithPro Webinar Arena's separation of "Expert Critics" from "Marketplace Judge"

**The Gap:** CopywritingEngine's tournament evaluation conflated two questions:
1. "Does this follow the methodology correctly?" (RSF dimensions)
2. "Would this actually sell?"

**Solution:** Added a **Marketplace Reality Check** as a final gate after tournament selection:

```
FOR top 5 tournament finalists:
  EVALUATE from cold-read perspective (no methodology context)
  ANSWER:
    1. "Would I actually stop scrolling for this?"
    2. "Does this feel like something I'd share or remember?"
    3. "Or does it feel like 'marketing'?"

  FLAG: Any candidate with high RSF (>7.5) that fails cold-read check
```

This catches Big Ideas that "look right" to the methodology but wouldn't actually work in market.

### Upgrade 2: Learning Ledger (Calibration Loop)

**Source:** ZenithPro Webinar Arena's `learning_ledger` with judge calibration

**The Gap:** CopywritingEngine evaluates and scores outputs but never learns whether those evaluations were accurate. Without feedback, the system drifts toward what "looks right" rather than what IS right.

**Solution:** Added a **calibration_record** to every Big Idea output:

```yaml
calibration_record:
  big_idea_id: string
  predicted_winner: string
  rsf_scores: {SD, RD, RA, TF, NR}
  marketplace_check_passed: boolean
  actual_outcome: null  # Updated when performance data available
  calibration: null     # Calculated post-outcome
```

Over time, this enables answering:
- "Are our RSF scores actually predictive?"
- "Which judge perspectives are most accurate?"
- "Where do we systematically over/under-estimate?"

### Why These Two (and Not Others)

| Considered | Selected? | Reason |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| **Marketplace Reality Check** | ✅ | Addresses methodology/effectiveness gap |
| **Learning Ledger** | ✅ | Creates feedback loop for system learning |
| Research Library as Foundation | ❌ | Different architecture — already have Deep Research layer |
| Hard Quality Thresholds | ❌ | Already present in constraints |

Both upgrades share a theme: **ensuring evaluations correlate with reality**, not just methodology correctness.
