Fran Rendulic was some­
what apprehensive when
Scott Baron, M.D., recom­
mended that she have licr total
knee replacement surgery at
Monongahela Valley Hospi­
tal (MVH). A resident of the
Mon Valley, she had never
been to MVH. Trusting her
orthopedic surgeon’s advice,
she had the surgery at the
Orthopedic Institute at
Monongahela Valley Hospi­
tal and was pleasantly sur­
prised with the program and
the hospital.
“I was impressed with the
pre-op class that I attended
prior to my surgery,” said
Ms. Rendulic. “The pro­
gram was very informa­
tive and I thought a nice
touch in helping me to
prepare for the surgery
and understand expecta­
tions post-surgery.
“My stay in the hospital
was very pleasant. From the
admittance through the sur­
gery, everything was on time
and I never felt abandoned as
a patient. On the day of my
surgery, I felt like I was their
only patient for the day. 1
remember being wheeled into
the operating room and
having eveiyone introduced
to me and telling me what
they would be doing.
“Once in my room after
surgery, I received exemplary
care. When I needed assis­
tance, my call button was
quickly answered. Everyone,
and I mean everyone, that
entered my room was friend­
ly and professional. This
extended from the nursing
staff, physical and occupa­
tional therapy, housekeeping
and food services.
“The entire program that’s
been created for knee replace­
ments has been well thought
out and well executed. I can­
not express how satisfied I am
with my stay at MVH and I
would not hesitate to recom­
mend M VH to others.”
Ms. Rendulic was one of
the first people to have total
joint replacement at the
Orthopedic Institute at
Monongahela Valley Hospi­
tal. The program, which offi­
cially launched on Jan. 21, is
a comprehensive program
that is based on a proven
national model for hip and
knee replacements.
The Orthopedic institute
incorporates advanced mini­
mally invasive and rapid-
recovery surgical techniques
— with an environment that
maximizes patient recovery
through education, a culture
of early mobility, family
involvement, and group inter­
action. The result of this new
approach to joint replacement
recovery, and superior out­
comes according to national
best practice studies.
Thomas F. Brockmeyer,
M.D., Ph.D., medical director
of the Institute, is one of three
orthopedic surgeons who,
along with the administrative
and clinical team at MVH,
developed this program to
provide a superior experience
for individuals having hip or
knee replacement surgery.
Scott L. Baron, M.D., who
has an outstanding reputation
for total hip and knee
replacements, and Ari E.
Pressman, M.D., whose areas
of expertise include total hip
and knee replacements as well
as revision joint replacements,
and Richard Mitchell, M.D.,
whose area of expertise is in
the diagnosis and treatment of
injuries to the shoulder,
elbow, knee, and ankle, part­
ner with Dr. Brockmeyer at
the Orthopedic Institute.
“We see our patients as
healthy individuals who are
coming to the hospital
because they have hip or knee
pain, and want to live a better
quality of life by having joint
replacement surgery,” said Dr.
Brockmeyer.
The Orthopedic Institute
experience actually begins in
the physician’s office. Once
someone decides to have joint
replacement surgery, they are
provided with a comprehen­
sive guidebook explaining
their upcoming surgery and a
list of activities, including pre-
operative strengthening exer­
cises that they need to begin
prior to surgery.
This guidebook also serves
as a clinical diary that is
brought to pre-operative class,
to all appointments, and to
surgery is less pain, quicker
the hospital.
Key components of the pro­
gram include:
• Each person having total
hip or knee replacement sur­
gery chooses a coach usu­
ally a family member or close
friend — who is an active
participant in their loved
one’s care before, during and
after discharge from the hos­
pital. Both the patient and
that includes a pre-operative
class where they meet their
team from the Orthopedic
Institute in addition to learn­
ing in detail about the proce­
dure, what to expect in the
hospital and care necessary'
after discharge. Coaches, who
wear a special coach’s button,
learn the program right along
with their loved ones, and
provide encouragement and
support every step of the way.
• A designated care coordi­
nator oversees the patient’s
entire stay and is their
resource before, during and
after discharge.
• Patients dress in com­
fortable clothes — not hos­
pital gowns that they
wear during the day.
• Patients follow an
aggressive daily schedule
coach are required to partici­
pate in Surgical Prehab,M
that begins at 6 o’clock
the morning after surgery
on a unit devoted only to
individuals having joint
replacement surgery.
Two group therapy
sessions are held daily —
one in the morning, and a
second in the afternoon —
in a large room located on
the unit. Group therapy is
supplemented by individual
in-room sessions.
Group lunches with fellow
students and staff also take
place on the unit
Patients compete with each
other on distance walked each
day. All progress is tracked on
a large exercise board named
“Steps to Success” on the wall
in a common area. This exer­
cise board provides visual
reinforcement of goals met
each day. People who have
had the surgery find the
camaraderie both challenging
and rewarding.
• Care continues after
discharge. Patients receive
calls from the Orthopedic
Institute staff within 72 hours
of discharge, and again
one to two weeks later.
• Reunions for patients and
their coaches arc held quar­
terly with surgeons and their
entire team.
“We evaluate each patient on
multiple clinical, functional,
and patient satisfaction
criteria,” explained Dr. Brock­
meyer. “These outcomes are
then analyzed during monthly
performance improvement
team meetings so that we
have data to back up our
reported outcomes.'
As far as superior out­
comes, MVH has the data to
prove it and Ms. Rendulic
has the new knee and
pleasant memories of her
experience at The Orthopedic
Institute at Monongahela
Valley Hospital.
