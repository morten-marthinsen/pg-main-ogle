■ Preliminary results
show levy defeated by
just 91 votes.
By BRAD KANE
br acikane@sanduskyregister.com
HURON
Despite passing at the halfway
point, Huron City Schools’ 4.9-
mill emergency operating levy
was defeated on Tuesday’s ballot.
In preliminary results, the
measure lost by just 91 votes,
with 51.3 percent voting against
the levy. The levy was supposed
to bring $ 1.3 million a year to the
district over the next five years. It
would have cost the owner of a
$100,000 home an additional
$ 150 per year in property taxes.
“Fiscal emergency and state
controlling of our schools is a
very real concern," said Sally
Sprunk, president of the Huron
Board of Education.
Sprunk said Huron schools
will put the issue back on the
ballot in May to overcome the
close margin.
“There are so many variables
in an election,” she said.
The state auditor’s office is
now going to require the devel­
opment of a financial plan to lead
the district out of debt. It had
been borrowing money to help
pay for its functions, and the
emergency levy would have
erased that deficit. The deficit
was anticipated to be $1.3 mil­
lion, the exact amount the levy
was supposed to generate.
“This would have allowed us
to break even,” Treasurer Christy
Moore said. “We would still have
had to continue to be frugal.”
Even if the levy passes on the
May ballot, Moore said the loss
in today’s election was particu­
larly damaging.
“If we didn’t pass it today, we
lose a year’s worth of money,"
Moore said.
