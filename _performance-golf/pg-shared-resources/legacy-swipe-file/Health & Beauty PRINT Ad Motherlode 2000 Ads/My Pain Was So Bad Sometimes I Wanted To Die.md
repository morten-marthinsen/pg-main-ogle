# Right to sue HMOs takes effect

RALEIGH (AP) — North
Carolinians who feel they've
been wrongly denied cover-
age from a health mainte-
nance organization now have
extra avenues in which to
find a solution, including
going to court.
A new law, one of several
that took effect Monday, pro-
vides managed-care patients
with an additional review of
their case by an independent
panel to determine whether
an insurer should have paid
for a procedure.
If all appeals have been
exhausted, a patient unhappy
with the panel's decision
could then sue for damages,
claiming an HMO failed to
exercise proper care.
While Congress is still at
an impasse over a nationwide
"right to sue" law, North
Carolina becomes the latest
among 10 states to allow an
unhappy patient to sue man-
aged-care companies in state
court. The Department of
Insurance and the group rep-
resenting HMOs in the state
say they're unsure how many
patients will use the new
independent review and ulti-
mately file litigation. More
than a million North
Carolina residents partici-
pate in managed-care plans
in the state.
State law already has
allowed for a two-tiered
review and grievance process
for patients to challenge cov-
erage decisions, but those
were performed internally by
the insurer. If coverage is still
denied, a patient will now be
able to seek a third opinion
through an external, inde-
pendent review organization.
Among the other laws
that took effect Monday:
• Locksmiths now must be
licensed to perform services
in North Carolina under a
bill approved last year.
Locksmiths working for at
least two consecutive years
prior to Monday will receive

indexed for the cost of living.

# Higher death rate


# from cancer in


# rural Appalachia

Death rates from cancer
are higher in Appalachia
than in the rest of the
country, largely because of
higher smoking rates
resulting in lung cancer
deaths, researchers con-
cluded.
For every 100,000 people
living in Appalachia, there
were 173.1 cancer deaths
during the study period,
from 1994-98, compared to
166.7 cancer ^eaths per
100,000 nationally during
the same period.
In rural sections of
Appalachia, that number
rose to 176.3 cancer deaths,
according to the research
done at the University of
Kentucky and Penn State
in collaboration with the
Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention.
But while death rates
from breast, cervical and
colorectal cancers varied
in Appalachian states, only
lung cancer death rates
were almost universally
higher in Appalachia than
in the rest of the country.
