Wouldn't it be great if the title of The
Last Dinosaur Book, written by WJ.T.
Mitchell, a professor of English and Art
History (not Paleontology), could be
taken literally? But don't count on it.
His book (University of Chicago Press,
S35) is a stylish account—sometimes
rueful, often witty, always thoughtful—
of how dinosaurs have seized the hu­
man imagination. Just about every imag­
inary dinosaur
you'd never
want to meet is
included in
these lavishly
and provoca­
tively illustrat­
ed pages, from
Barney, "the
saccharine
saurian," to the
"post-modem"
velociraptors of Jurassic Park.
There are, Mitchell observes a bit
mournfully, more dinosaur images to­
day than there ever were dinosaurs, and
most of them (always excepting God­
zilla) emanate from the United States.
Nor is this fascination entirely new:
Thomas Jefferson, a budding paleon­
tologist, was so intrigued by "big bones"
(probably from mastodons) that he be­
came known as "the Mammoth Presi­
dent." One legend Mitchell disputes is
that all children love dinosaurs. Some
don't, he insists, saying that he himself
considered them a "crashing bore" com­
pared to medieval dragons. Maybe so,
but he has written anything but a bor­
ing book about them.
For more serious "dinophiles" (Mitch­
ell's word), there's Night Comes to
the Cretaceous, by James Lawrence
Powell (Freeman, $22.95). This cogent,
popularly written book validates the the­
ory that the extinction of the beasts was
caused by the collision in prehistoric
times of a huge asteroid with the earth.
