# I can’t turn it off!

by Jeff Carroll, PhD
Most people don’t talk about it; many
live with it and it really frustrates them; some
can’t sleep or relax because of it. That’s tinni-
tus, which sounds like a ringing, whooshing or
roaring in the ears, and it can become a con-
stant, 24/7 torment. If you’re an aging baby
boomer who has grown up listening to loud
music at concerts, in your car, or on your ste-
reo that may be the cause of your tinnitus.
Think back to your teenage years, lis-
tening to your favorite Who album (Peter Town-
shend has tinnitus) with the volume cranked up
high, and your mother yelling, “Turn that down!”
It turns out she was right. The main cause of
tinnitus is damage to the auditory system from
noise exposure, and the average age of suffer-
ers is around 55. Tinnitus can result from years
spent playing amplified music without any ear
protection. But being subjected to constant
noise of any kind above 85 decibels (the level of
busy city traffic sounds) can cause hearing dam-
age and tinnitus.
While many people experience tinnitus
occasionally, some suffer constantly and cannot
get relief. Tinnitus can affect their sleep, or abil-
ity to concentrate; cause anxiety, depression,
or irritability; and generally hurt their quality of
life. And when they seek medical attention tin-
nitus patients are often told that “nothing can be
done,” or “you just need to relax.”
The good news is that treatments are
available, and many do find relief. One of the
most successful ways to change the perception
of tinnitus is through sound therapy, which uses
external sound to interfere with the tinnitus, po-
tentially enabling the patient to find relief. This
approach has been used for over 30 years and
has brought remarkable success.
