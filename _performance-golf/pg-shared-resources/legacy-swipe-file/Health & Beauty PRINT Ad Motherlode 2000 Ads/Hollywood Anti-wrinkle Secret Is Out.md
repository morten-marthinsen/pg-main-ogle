# Florida's poor struggle


# to make ends meet

FLORIDA CITY (AP) —
Severe arthritis ended Ida
Brown's ability to earn a living
cleaning houses, a job she took
pride in for more than 20 years.
Now, the 62-year-old great-
grandmother from Miami
depends on food stamps and dis-
ability assistance to make ends
meet, not an easy task consider-
ing she is raising two of her
grandchildren.
Brown lives on just over
$500 a month. She is one of near-
ly two million people in the state
living below the poverty level.
"You get by the best you can,"
she said.
Over the past decade, poverty
numbers have remained rela-
tively stable in Florida. In 1990,
1.6 million Floridians, or 12.7 per-
cent, lived below the poverty
level. According to U.S. Census
figures released Thursday,
12.5 percent, or 1.9 million were
living in poverty in 1999 — the
latest year for which statistics
are available.
The percentage of families
living in poverty is unchanged at
9 percent from 1990 to 1999,
while the poverty rate for fami-
lies with children dropped to
14.2 percent in 1999, down from
14.7 percent in 1990.
Elizabeth Hirst, a spokes-
woman for Gov. Jeb Bush, said
Florida's poverty rate was signif-
icantly below that of other south-
eastern states and 1 percent
lower than the national average.
In Miami-Dade County, the
most populous of the state's
67 counties, 18 percent, or
396,995 residents, live in poverty.
In 1990, that number was
341,261,or 17.9 percent.
The numbers don't mean a
thing to Brown. All she knows is
she has to pinch pennies, and
find ways to make supplement
her meager monthly amount
from SSI.
On a recent morning, she
stood in line at Farm Share, a
Florida City nonprofit organiza-
tion that distributes food and
vegetables donated by the USDA
and farmers for the poor.

TALLAHASSEE (AP) — A
proposed constitutional amend-
ment to create a statewide
board to oversee management
of Florida's public universities
can go on the ballot, the state
Supreme Court said Thursday.
U.S. Sen. Bob Graham has
spearheaded the petition drive,
which must collect about half a
million signatures to go before
voters in November.
Elections officials have veri-
fied some 154,000 signatures,
according to the state Division
of Elections; the campaign has
said it has another 300,000 in
the pipeline.
Florida's high court reviews
citizen initiatives for compli-
ance on two requirements: The
ballot question can deal with
only one subject, and it must be
fairly and clearly explained by
its ballot title and summary.
The "Education Excellence
for Florida" campaign that
Graham kicked off in November
would restore a Regents-like
board to oversee public univer-
sities but retain local boards of
trustees created two years ago
by state lawmakers.
Graham, one of the state's
top elected Democrats, wants
to create a 17-member
statewide Board of Governors
similar to the old regents, which
was abolished by the
Republican-led Legislature last
summer.
Oversight of the state uni-
versity system is now the
responsibility of the Florida
Board of Education, a seven-
member appointed panel that
came into existence last sum-
mer.
Beginning in January, the
Florida Board of Education will
be responsible for education
from kindergarten through
postgraduate studies, creating
what supporters call a "seam-
less K-20" governance struc-
ture.
