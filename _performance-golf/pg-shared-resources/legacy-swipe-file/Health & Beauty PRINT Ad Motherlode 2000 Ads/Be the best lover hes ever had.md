"When my brother Miles was past eighty, I asked him if he believed in

"Some of these recipes tell what to put in but not how to do it. Mama

other words, sometimes itdoesn't tell what you are making."

Unlike other types of dancing,
the challenge for Irish dancers is
to not dance with the upper body.
All movement is done with the
lower half of the body. Dancers
wear either soft or hard shoes, de-
pending on the dance.
Josie is now learning her first
hard shoe dance, which Josie said
"is easy and fun. I like the hard
shoes because I like the noise."
The hard shoes enable the
dancer to tap out the rhythm of
the song with the shoes.
"You sing the song with your
feet," Lori said.
Lori said the competition is
good for her daughter, not only be-
cause she gains confidence but
also because she's meeting new
people all the time. This year, Josie
will go to 10 competitions, and the
family will travel around the re-
gion. The longest trip so far has
been to Nashville, Tenn. Often she
meets dancers from other coun-
tries, and she's learning to accept
people from different cultures.
Advancing to different levels is
exciting to Josie, who is in the first
level.
At various levels, the dancers
wear different outfits. Those start-
ing out, like Josie, wear a begin-
ning outfit, which is the blouse
and kilt and costs about $65. The
next level is the school dress,
which Josie will get next year, and
that's about $600.
The advanced female dancers
wear the solo dress, which is about
$1,000, but it can be more expen-
sive. Used dresses also can be pur-
chased for about $500. The solo
dresses are all done in embroidery
in Ireland, and the pattern is in-
spired from Ireland's Book of Kells.
The sport can get expensive.
Group lessons for Josie are $36 a
month, which includes a one-hour
lesson each week. Lori has to drive
Josie to Indianapolis two times a
week, once for the group lesson
and another time for the private
lesson. The shoes also are expen-
sive, with hard shoes costing
about $110-and soft shoes costing
about $35 to $60 in Josie's size
range.
"If I didn't feel Josie wasn't fo-

want her to have fun, first and
foremost I recognize that she has
what it takes, even what it takes to
go to worlds especially under Liz,
so whatever she aspires to be...."
Lori's son Dalton, age 9, also is
learning Irish dance. He began
taking lessons in October and jug-
gles lessons with soccer, baseball
and basketball.
"It helps me with my sports,"
Dalton said. "It's almost as tough
as football. I have to do a lot of
dances, kicks and teamwork."
He has to do stretches to im-
