It’s True.
Dentists want people to have healthier smiles. It’s also true that a healthy
smile is usually a beautiful smile. April has been designated to draw attention
to a dangerous, “silent” disease that affects more and more people each year.
April is Oral Cancer Awareness Month.
The Facts.
The majority of the approximately 37,000 people who will be diagnosed with
oral cancer in the U.S. this year will already have an advanced form of the dis-
ease. According to the Oral Cancer Foundation, late diagnoses don’t occur
because the cancer is difficult to find, but rather because of the lack of aware-
ness and failure to screen for the disease. The good news is that those who
visit their dentist a minimum of twice per year are probably already being
monitored for oral cancer – even if they may not know it. If you are curious
about what your dentist’s oral cancer screening protocol is – just ask!
Oral Cancer Screenings.
The American Association of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgeons informs us that
90% of oral cancer is found in the soft tissue that lines the inside of the mouth,
as well as the sides of the tongue and the floor of the mouth. An oral cancer
screening begins with a visual inspection, as well as a gentle tactile inspection
of the tissues in the mouth and the lymph nodes in the neck. This is often
followed by a VELscope® scan. The VELscope® is an FDA-approved device
that uses auto florescence – a specialized light – to indicate areas that may
be problematic. Healthy tissues fluoresce in distinctive patterns, any disruption
to this pattern is readily apparent. If an area is suspicious, your dentist may
recommend a biopsy, which can be done by laser in the office or surgically,
which usually requires a visit to an oral surgeon.
What YOU can do.
You have the power to protect yourself from oral cancer. Alcohol and tobacco
use, especially in combination, makes you 37 times more likely to develop oral
cancer. Additionally, sun exposure can also lead to cancer developing on your
lips, so wear sunscreen and a wide-brimmed hat when outdoors. You should
also be aware that cancerous lesions often look like a typical mouth sore in
the beginning, and are usually white or red in color. If you have a sore that
doesn’t heal within three weeks, you should make an appointment to have it
checked without delay.
Remember…when found early, oral cancer is treatable. Stay aware and you
will enjoy your healthy smile for life!
