What could possibly have been so impor-

# The best bets


# for fall travel


# M

ost people think of fall as a
time to get back to school
and down to business.
That's why it's a thrifty time to travel.
Some hotels cut their rates. Cities
stage special events to keep visitors
coming. But the biggest attraction: a
vacation free from the hordes of sum-
mer and holiday travelers.
Here are four of the American
Automobile Association's top choices
for rail travel destinations along with
special fall happenings.
Boston: The weather is crisp and the
foliage throngs of early October arc
thinning. Mainstay attractions that are
always inexpensive: the Museum of
Fine Arts, the Museum of Science and
the New England Aquarium. Fall
highlights: the Head of the Charles re-
gatta Oct. 22, when more than 100
universities from the USA, England
and Germany compete in crew races;
and in Salem, 17 miles north, Haunted
Happenings, Oct. 21-31, is the ulti-
mate way to get spooked for Hal-
loween. Call (508) 744-0004.
Cincinnati: The tourist season slacks
off, but things to do don't. Visit the
Cincinnati Zoo or the city's two muse-
ums — the Taft Museum and Cincin-
nati Art Museum — or take a riverboat
cruise along the Ohio River. Special
events: the International Folk Festival,
Nov. 17-19, at the Convention Cen-
ter, where 30 ethnic groups provide
eats and entertainment. Or sneak a
peek of winter at Kings Island's Win-
ter Fest, Nov. 18-19 and Nov. 24-26.
Grand Canyon National Park, South
Rim, in northern Arizona: Tempera-
tures range from 60s during the day to
30s at night, crowds have dwindled
(200,000 visitors in November vs.
540,000 in July), and some lodging
prices are reduced (check out the Mo-
qui Lodge, just outside the park en-
trance; doubles drop Nov. 1 from S62
to $50 a night, call (602) 638-2401).
Just one warning: Late November
brings snow.
Washington, D.C.: Fall brings fabulous
weather without spring's mobs. After
you've seen the monuments and the
museums, check out the White House
Fall Garden tour in mid-October (call
(202) 485-9666 for details), and the
Veterans Day memorial ceremony at
Arlington National Cemetery.
By Carol Clurman
