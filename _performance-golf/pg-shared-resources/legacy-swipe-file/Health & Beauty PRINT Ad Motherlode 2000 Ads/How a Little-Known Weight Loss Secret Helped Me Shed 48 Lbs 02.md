# Before Regis, there was Monty Hall

If there was one person not sur­
prised at the prime-time success of
“Who Wants to Be a Millionaire,” it
was Monty Hall. That’s because
Hall has been there himself as host
of “Let’s Make a Deal.”

“When ‘Let’s Make a Deal’ was
the hottest show on television, NBC
put us on at night, and we cleaned
up,” Hall says, sitting down for a
chat in a Northwest Side restau­
rant. “We beat Ed Sullivan, ‘Get
Smart,’ Red Skelton — all of them.”
That was in the summer of 1967,
when “Let’s Make a Deal” was
thrown onto Sunday nights almost
as a sacrifice only to thrash reruns
in the ratings. “So now comes the
fall, and we say, ‘Are we on the
schedule?’ And they say, ‘Oh no, we
don’t put shows like yours on in
prime time.’ And for more than 25
years nobodv ever put a show like
that on.
“We’ve always been treated, even
when they put us on in night time,
as, Oh, let’s throw them a bone,’ ”
Hall adds. “Now ‘Who Wants to Be
a Millionaire’ comes along, and all
of a sudden it has the respect of the
nation. Why? Take a look at those
ratings.”
“Millionaire” occupied the top
three spots in the Nielsen ratings
this season thanks to its three dif­
ferent weekly air times. Yet, ironi­
cally, one of the few people “Million­
aire” does not have the respect of is
Hall. He may not have been sur­
prised at its success, but he still
doesn’t think much of it as a quiz
show, dismissing a typical question
as, “What color is a brown cow?”
That said, he doesn’t grudge any­
one success.
“I have only two things to say
about it,” Hall says. “One, it is too
easy to win a million dollars, grant­
ed. But the other side of the coin is
let Regis Philbin be successful. Let
him earn it, because this business
is so tough and eats up people.
“I know my struggle. All my kids
are in the business,” he adds. “So
let him have it. It’s his turn.”
That’s remarkably charitable con­
sidering that one of Hall’s kids,
daughter Joanna Gleason, will co­
star this fall in Bette Midler’s new
Monty Hall
sitcom opposite, of all things, the
new Wednesday edition of “Million­
aire.’’ Just don’t get him started on
which is a better game show or a
more demanding job for a host:
“Millionaire” or “Deal.”
For those youngsters out there
who might never have seen it,
“Let’s Make a Deal” found Hall
roaming the studio audience bar­
tering with various people. He
might give a woman $5 if she could
remember her driver’s license
number, then offer to trade the $5
for what was either behind a door
or inside a box. Behind the door
might be a new car. Inside the box
might be a billy goat. Adding to the
hysteria, the members of the audi­
ence typically dressed in costume,
held up comic banners or simply
acted crazy in an attempt to get
Hall to call on them. All the while,
Hall had to act as director, ringmas­
ter and comic ad-libber.
“It’s not just sitting back and say­
ing, ‘Is that your final answer?”’
Hall says with a smile.
Nevertheless, the weekday game
show was huge in its day. It
debuted in 1963 on NBC, then
jumped to ABC in 1968, where it ran
for seven and a half years more. It
has been back in various incarna­
tions at regular intervals ever
since, usually with Hall as host, and
the format remains popular in for-
eign-language editions in France,
Germany and Israel, to name just
three countries with their own indi­
vidual versions of the show.

# Roker loving ‘adventure’ of fatherhood

Associated Press
NEW YORK — Few things
seem to cloud Al Roker’s sunny
mood. But last November the
jovial weathercaster was feeling a
bit sad. His daughter Leila was
marking another birthday; his lit­
tle girl was growing up.
Leila had turned I.
Any viewer watching Roker on
NBC’s “Today” can tell that the
portly prophet loves his job. He’s
also nuts tor his wife of five years,
ABC News correspondent Debo­
rah Roberts. But when it comes
to Leila and her big sister, Court­
ney — well, in Roker’s heavens,
still made time for each young­
ster.
At 46, he ponders the difference
between being a kid in his day
and now, and the difference
between his dual roles as dad of a
teen and a toddler.
And he marvels at the law of na­
ture dictating that all of us, as
parents, turn into our own par­
ents. He recounts being at the
wheel on a family outing when
Courtney and her cousins made a
little too much racket from the
back seat. He was helpless.
“Those six little words came out
involuntarily, the ones that I had
heard my father use with us some
they hung the moon.
For evidence, read Roker’s
memoir “Don’t Make Me Stop
This Car! (Adventures in Father­
hood),” which arrives in stores
just in time for Father’s Day
(more than coincidence?). It is full
of feelings and observations that
will ring true for most any parent.
Or anybody who had one.
In his book, Roker tells of the
nail-biting time he shared with his
former wife Alice in adopting
Courtney, now 13.
He recounts the not-just-mak-
ing-whoopee campaign he and
Deborah waged to become preg­
nant.
Roker recalls his happy child­
hood in Queens. He had five
younger siblings, three of them
adopted, and a “really good guy”
dad (Al, Sr.) who put in back-to-
back shifts driving a city bus, yet
30 years ago: DON’T MAKE ME
STOP THIS CAR!”
Roker insists he didn’t set out to
write a book on parenting. “But I
had always kept a journal. And I
write the daily journal for my Web
site.”
Roker contends that parenting
is the real national pastime.
“I think most of us are prewired
to be parents,” he says. “For
some of us, the switch is hidden
behind some sheetrock. But once
you have the baby, that sheetrock
comes off and you flip the switch,
and you go, ‘Oooh, I love this!”’
When Roker is asked to explain
why fatherhood is so great, his
response is swift.
“It’s an adventure,” he says,
smiling brightly, no clouds in his
forecast. “They can drive me
crazy. But I look in Courtney’s
eyes, or Leila’s eyes, and I feel
like I’m the king of the world.”
