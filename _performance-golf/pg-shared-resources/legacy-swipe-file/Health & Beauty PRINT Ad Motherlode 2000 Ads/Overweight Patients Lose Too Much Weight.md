# Civil rights


# cases near


# resolution

Two civil rights cases pending
against Thornton township officials
are nearing resolution, special coun­
sel Jerry Lambert told the township
board of trustees this week.
Lambert reported to the board in
a closed session Tuesday evening on
the cases that were filed during the
summer of 1987.
When the board returned to reg­
ular session. Supervisor Fred Redell
told him to make the report in public.
All discrimination charges filed by
Catherine Laddner, a counselor in the
Harvey youth office, have been dis­
missed, Lambert said. But allega­
tions that the board retaliated
against her during a work evaluation
shortly after the lawsuit was filed,
are still active.
The attorney said he expects a rul­
ing on this allegation in the near fu­
ture.
The other case involves a former
employee, Lavonna Duff. She alleged
that she was illegally forced to resign
and there was a pattern of discrim­
ination in her employment with the
township.
“The court has ruled there was no
basis in fact,” on the charges sur­
rounding her dismissal, Lambert
said. Still to be decided is the dis­
crimination charge, but Lambert
said the charges that were dismissed
were “the essence of her case.”
He said, “Our goal has been to
prove non-discrimination. We've
been successful.”
In other news, the township board
announced it will conduct a public
hearing this evening on the disposi­
tion of general revenue sharing
funds. The township must dispose of
the remaining funds by March I, and
has been reviewing applications from
nonprofit groups. The fund still con­
tains about $60,000.
Also, plans are under way to field
a team at this spring’s Special Olym­
pics May 4 at Bloom high school in
Chicago Heights. The special recre­
ation department hopes to have IO
athletes, township officials said.

# Home repair


# fraud charges


# filed by Daley

Criminal charges have been filed
against a Markham man for home
repair fraud, according to a spokes­
man Terry Levin of the state’s attor­
ney’s office.
Mike Baker of Baker Home Im­
provement and Installation, 16730
South Richmond, is being charged
with taking a $1,750 down payment
from a northwest suburban Wheeling
homeowner to replace windows and
perform other improvement work
but allegedly failed to either perform
the job or refund the money.
Levin said this was the only in­
cidence of home repair fraud being
leveled against Baker. An arrest war­
rant has been issued for Baker, Levin
said.
If convicted, Baker could face up
to one year in jail and fines up to
$1,000, and full restitution will also
be sought.
Levin said that the state’s attorney
office has a 95 percent conviction
rate and that the charges against Ba­
ker is part of Daley’s ongoing
crackdown on home repair fraud.
Levin said once Baker is arrested,
bond will be set and a court date
will be scheduled.
Baker’s phone has been discon­
nected and could not be reached for
comment.

By THOM GIBBONS
Oral arguments were heard Tuesday and a decision was expected
yesterday on a lawsuit to put a referendum on the April 4 ballot to
dissolve the Dixmoor park district.
Circuit court Judge Stephen Yates worked with attorneys from
both sides to work out an agreement on parts of the case However,
they couldn’t agree on whether the petitions had been properly served,
the designation of the election and whether there is a compelling
reason to force the park board to call for the referendum.
Attorney Dirk Vanbeek, representing the petitioners, said he left
the petition at the listed office of the park district in the Rosa Parks
school, which was not open at the time. He left them with a boy and
the petitions eventually found their way to the park district.
But board attorney Paul Russo argued the petitioners should have
come to a board meeting and presented the petitions to the board
secretary, Charles Walker, in person. He said the petitioners had
ample opprtunity to do so.
Vanbeek said, “One of the (commissioners) duties is not to make
themselves as scarce as possible." Russo countered, “The issue is it
was too inconvenient to show up.”
The second point concerns at what election to hold such a referen­
dum. There is an election for park commissioner April 4, but no
candidates have filed nominating petitions.
Russo argues that if the petitions are valid, then they have been
filed too soon for elections that could be held in 1991.
He also argued on the third point that the board is under no
obligation to hold an election this year, because it is the duty of a
board to designate the election.
Outside of the hearing, board President Bobby Jackson questioned
the validity and motives of the petitioners. There were about 225
signatures, and about 80 percent of them were from the trailer parks
along Western avenue.
Russo and Jackson said those petitioners could have asked to
deannex from the district and join an adjoining district.
He also complained that no one attends park board meetings and
that the village has not cooperated with the park board. “They always
tell us they’re going to get back to us, but they don’t.”

counselors meeting.

Holiday Inn in Matteson.

the Well-Being of American Youth.”

school counselor and president of the
200-member organization, said the
speaker also will highlight some of
the “unexpected findings of the
study.”

ered demographics and family com­
position; health, behavior and atti­
tudes; family income; education, and
employment and finances.
