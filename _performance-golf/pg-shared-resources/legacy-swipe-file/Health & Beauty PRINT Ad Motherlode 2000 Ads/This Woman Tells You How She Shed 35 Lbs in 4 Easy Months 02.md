BY JOHN PATTERSON
Daily Herald State Government Editor
SPRINGFIELD — Lingering
publicity from former Gov.
George Ryan's sweeping death
penalty action combined with
stricter scrutiny of would-be
workers is producing record
numbers of requests for par-
dons and clemency
Already'Gov. Rod Blagojevich
has received more than 600
requests from people who want
their records cleared of past
wrongs. If that pace continues,
and state officials think it will,
, the numbers,will easily eclipse
those from Ryan's four years.
And Ryan fielded and granted
more requests in one term than
any previous governor.
The chairman of the state's
Prisoner Review Board said the
reason is twofold: more people
know there's a process for clear-
ing their record due to extensive
publicity about the controver-
sial death penalty hearings last
year, and more people have
reason to do so as prospective
employers delve further into
applicants' backgrounds
because of tighter security
prompted by terrorism.
"The numbers of persons
seeking executive clemency has
never been higher, and it is
increasing," board Chairman
Craig Findley said. The board is
the state agency that reviews
such requests and makes confi-
dential recommendations to the
governor, who makes the final
decision.
The process for clearing one's
criminal history moved into the
spotlight, last year when Ryan
announced he would review
every death row case before
leaving office in January. Soon
the airwaves and newspapers
were awash with coverage of
how to go about asking the gov-
ernor for mercy.
Ultimately, Ryan used the
powers granted him by the
state's constitution to change
the death sentences for the 164
people on death row to life in
prison without parole. In five
cases, he freed death row
inmates.
In his four years, Ryan
received 1,734 requests for
clemency, including people
wanting to be freed from death
row and senior citizens seeking
.to erase ugly chapters from their
lives years ago. He granted some
form of relief in 620 cases,
which includes the death row
actions.
As people have become aware
that this clemency process
exists, more are choosing to use
it. The vast majority are not
death row inmates. In fact,
Findley said, there's been an
influx of people convicted of rel-
atively minor crimes who are
unable to get or keep jobs.
"hi these days of heightened
security, all people are much
more careful in the hiring prac-
tice. And the result of that is
many people who might have
been employable before Sept.
11 now find themselves unem-
ployable," Findley said. "The
only remedy is to petition the
governor for clemency."
The reasons people seek
clemency range from wanting
to coach Little League to trying
to keep a job hauling hazardous
waste. Illinois law prohibits past
felons from coaching youth
teams while truck drivers carry-
ing hazardous waste fall under
tighter scrutiny because of new
anti-terrorism regulations.
Just as the governor can free-
someone from prison or reduce
a prison term, the state consti-
tution also gives the governor
the power to erase criminal his-
tories. Once the records are
expunged, a future employer
would find no trace of the past
conviction. Neither would a
police officer who stopped
someone.
And as mass petitioning for
clemency continues, some offi-
cials worry both about the
process and the potential result
of so many people emerging
with clean records.

Carol Stream Republican with a
30-year record in Jaw enforce-

# Walkers brave cold


# to help fight disease

BY BETH SNELLER
Daily Herald Staff Writer
. Walkers bundled up in winter
coats, gloves, scarves and ear-
muffs Sunday for a walk to raise
money for the fight against mul-
tiple sclerosis.
Despite the chilly tempera-
tures, about 1,500 people
participated in the 2003 MS
Walk along the Naperville River-
walk, sponsored by the Chicago
chapter of the National Multiple
Sclerosis Society.
The six-mile walk was one of
18 taking place across Illinois
Sunday. There also were walks
in Chicago, St. Charles,
Schaumburg, Oak Forest and
Highland Park.
Organizers of the Naperville
event said they were expecting a
few more walkers to show up,
but figured the smaller turnout
was due to the unusually cold
temperatures. Some of those
who showed up gave up after a
nippy mile or two and some left
after handing in their pledges.
Others, however, did the walk
in record time. Donna
Makowsky of Oswego and her
relatives, walked the route in
one hour and 20 minutes.
"I guess when you talk and
walk, it goes fast," she said.
The walk was established in
1998 as the National MS Soci-
ety's only simultaneous national
fund-raising event. Last year,
Napervilie's walk raised more
than $218,000 and organizers
hope this year will be even more
successful.
Some of the participants said
they make the fund-raiser part
of their annual routine. Gary
Klock of Grayslake has been
walking with his adult children
and other family members for
more man seven years. His wife
suffers from MS.
"We do it as a family group,"
he said. "It's kind of a morale
booster."
