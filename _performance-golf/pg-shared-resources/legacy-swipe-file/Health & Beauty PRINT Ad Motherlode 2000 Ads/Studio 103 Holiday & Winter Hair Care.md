By Liz Katynski
SERVICE dogs are trained to work with
the blind, but they can also work with
autistic children and lend a helping paw
to parents.
Eight-year-old Fort Rouge twins Quinn
and BrodyThomsonhave been approved
for their own service dog, to be provid-
ed by National Service Dogs of Cam-
bridge, Ont. The dog will cost $12,000,
and there are additional travel-related
expenses for their mom, Joan Leslie-
Thomson, to travel to Cambridge for five
days of training prior to the dog’s arrival
in their home.
“Safety is our biggest issue,” says Joan
Leslie-Thomson. “The dog can warn us,
or prevent an unsafe act. That will give
us some peace of mind. Our kids have
already run out in front of cars. The cars
slam on the brakes and these kids are
not phased by it. You can’t explain the
danger to them. The dog will bond with
them and help them to stay focused.”
Friends are hosting a benefit social for
the Thomson family this Friday from 8
p.m. to 1 a.m. at Crescentwood Commu-
nity Centre. Money raised will help the
family cover the additional expenses and
pay for private speech and occupational
therapy services for the twins. The
evening will feature entertainment by
Big Bob’s Big Band and a silent auction.
Leslie-Thomson applied for the dog in
February 2003, after seeing a program
on TV that showed what dogs can do for
autistic kids. The family was approved in
July, and they expect to welcome a dog
into their home in the next year or so.
On the twins’ birthday on Sept. 15,
Leslie-Thomson and husband Bob Thom-
songave Quinn and Brody a five-foot tall
The twins were diagnosed with autism
just before their fourth birthday. They
have a profound communication disor-
der and speak few words but understand
some simple language.
“Autism is an invisible disability,” says
Leslie-Thomson. “People often judge our
kids for their behaviour without under-
standing what’s up. When we attach
them to a service dog, they will realize
there is a disability.”
Thomson and Leslie-Thomson are both
teachers. Thomson continues to teach at
Stanley Knowles School, but Leslie-
Thomson quit her job to stay at home
with their four sons. The other two are
Caelan, 9, and Riley, 6.
“It is a full-time job to stay at home,
take the kids to appointments like speech
therapy, occupational therapy, medical
appointments,” she says. “We take one
day at a time. We rarely do anything as
an entire family. It’s just too tough.”
The service dog will be another pair
of hands — or paws — for them.
“It will give us some peace of mind,”
says Leslie-Thomson. “It will help us
cope with everyday life, things that are
an extra struggle for us. It will become
a part of our family. We are really excit-
ed. It seems like a dream.”
National Service Dogs places about 20
dogs with autistic children per year, says
Leslie-Thomson. This will be their first
with two children.
