SACRAMENTO, CA - By Mark Moore
- Some 38 million Americans suffer from
hearing loss. However, for many senior
citizens, hearing loss has just become
an accepted part of growing older and
something that must be dealt with.
But there are extreme consequences,
much bigger than simply “not hearing.”
Older adults with hearing loss appear more
likely to develop dementia, and their risk
increases as hearing loss becomes more
severe, according to a report in the February
issue of Archives of Neurology, one of the
JAMA/Archives journals.
The study observed association
between hearing loss and incident
dementia, was conducted at John Hopkins
Hospital and reported that the risk of
dementia was increased among those with
hearing loss of greater than 25 decibels, with
further increases in risk observed among
those with moderate or severe hearing
loss as compared with mild hearing loss.
For participants’ age 60 and older, more
than one-third (36.4 percent) of the risk
of dementia was associated with hearing
loss. Furthermore, the risk of developing
Alzheimer’s disease which accounts for
50 to 80 percent of dementia, specifically
also increased with hearing loss. For every
10 decibels of hearing loss, the extra risk
increased by 20 percent.
The risk of dementia appears to
rise as hearing declines. Older people with
mild hearing impairment—those who have
difficulty following a conversation in a
crowded restaurant, say—were nearly twice
as likely as those with normal hearing to
develop dementia, the study found. Severe
hearing loss nearly quintupled the risk of
dementia.
Recent studies have also shown
that 94% of those diagnosed with memory
loss also had uncorrected hearing loss. In
order for something to be remembered, it
must first be heard. If you didn’t hear it, you
can’t remember it. Memory and hearing
affects the processing abilities in the higher
areas of the brain, which account for our
ability to understand. Understanding the
information we hear and receive is key
in slowing down the progression of these
horrible diseases.
Untreated hearing loss is often
mistaken for and attributed to dementia.
For example, the symptoms of hearing loss
can almost mirror those of Alzheimer’s
disease. In fact, Alzheimer’s disease is 25
times more likely in people with a history
of depression. Untreated hearing loss can
lead to isolation which often leads to
depression and a disconnect from the rest
of the world.
Untreated hearing loss can
induce social isolation, which can lead to
confusion and loss of orientation. Most
often hearing loss leads to some form of
depression, and this could be a form of
pseudo dementia.
In some isolated cases, patients
were misdiagnosed as having some form
of memory-related disease when in fact
they simply had hearing loss. We know
how hearing loss affects the ability to
“receive” the sounds we hear, but we are
just beginning to understand how it can
affect our ability to then process what
we heard and understood. Because of
this, all seniors should have their hearing
routinely tested at the onset of symptoms
of hearing loss or any sign of memory-
related diseases.
