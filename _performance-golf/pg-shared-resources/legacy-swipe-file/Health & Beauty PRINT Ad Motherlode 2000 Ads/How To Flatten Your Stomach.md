# Nickerson offers


# to tutor readers

RED DEER, Alta. (CP) — War-
ren Nickerson has spent the sum-
mer trying to persuade illiterate
railway workers that after pound-
ing spikes in the hot sun for 12
hours, they should hit the books at
night.
The 20-year-old Brandon Uni-
versity student has been working
since May as a laborer-teacher
with a CP Rail gang in central
Alberta.
Working alongside the rest of
the crew, Nickerson has quietly
tried to encourage men who read
poorly, if at all, to take advange
of the tutoring program he is of-
fering through Toronto's Frontier
College.
Their lack of interest has frus-
trated him.
"I walked into this one kind of
blind. It seemed like kind of an
adventure," said Nickerson, who
plans to teach full-time one day.
"I get things like 'Why do you
have westerns and those kinds of
things in the library? Why don't
you buy porno books? They'd be
used a lot more.' "
Nickerson has grown ac-
customed to railway life —
pounding spikes all day, sleeping
in a bunk, the cursing and crude
jokes.
"You just have to prove your-
self on the track to fit in. You
have to carry your fair share of
the work, that's all," he said.
But the rail workers are a
proud lot, and their lack of read-
ing and writing skills is a fiercely
guarded secret.
Illiteracy carries with it a
stigma — to the laborers it im-
plies stupidity. Even the mention
of the word is a deep insult to the
street-smart crews, said
Nickerson.
"I can identify the signs of
people who can't read, but I don't
know how to approach them with-
out deeply offending them.
"Lots of times you are working
side by side with people who just
hate their jobs and they wouldn't
mind doing something else, but
they don't know how to do it," he
said.
Frank Stewart was one of the
few laborers to take advantage of
Nickerson's program.
For 17 years the Newfoundland
native has spent nine months
each year away from his family,
working on a rail gang. He wants
off the railway.
"I don't want to die here. I
started yakking one night about
starting a gas station."
Nickerson has helped him put
his dream into action by finding
contacts, developing a financial
blueprint and even drawing up a
site plan.
Stewart hopes to follow the plan
through this winter.
"Warren, he goes a long way. I
just hope it works out."
Gang supervisor Neil Faulkner
said apathy is common because
the crew resists hitting the books
after working 12 hours a day in
the hot sun.
"These guys work hard all day.
The last thing they want to do is
use their heads a little at the end
of the day. They just want to clear
their heads and relax," said
Faulkner.
Assistant bull cook Bob Bhatia
said men might be more willing
to take advantage of the program
if the railway paid them for the
hours they spent studying.
"If the company says to go
spend one hour (learning) Eng-
lish, they might do it."
But CP Rail spokesman Earl
Olson said it is unreasonable to
ask the railway to pay men for
the time they spend learning.
The Frontier College program
began at the turn of the century
when reading tents were estab-
lished in lumber camps, mining
towns and railroad camps. The
college is Canada's oldest adult
education school.
Program co-ordinator Brent
Boulton said the 24 per cent of
Canadians who are functionally
illiterate gravitate toward jobs
like laboring on the railway.
"Having been out there myself,
there are numerous people who
have a Grade 9 education or
less."
As advances in technology and
the downsizing of railways
eliminate laborers' jobs, literacy
programs will grow in import-
ance, Boulton said.
