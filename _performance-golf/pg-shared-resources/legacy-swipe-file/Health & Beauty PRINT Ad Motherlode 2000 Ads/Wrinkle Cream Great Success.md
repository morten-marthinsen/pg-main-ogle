HARRISBURG — With the
Florida presidential election fresh
in mind, a Republican state sena­
tor said he will introduce legisla­
tion next year to standardize
Pennsylvania’s system of voting.
However, creating a uniform,
computer-based system in all of
the state’s 67 counties may prove
difficult because counties exercise
their own control over voting
methods and some have already
invested in new technology, a
Democratic counterpart said.
Pennsylvania counties currently
record the will of the people with
three forms of electronic voting, as
well as voting machines and, in
some locations, the old-fashioned
paper ballot.
“It’s a real hodge-podge of voting
methods,” said Sen. Stewart J.
Greenleaf, the Republican law­
maker from Montgomery County
who said he hopes to introduce the
bill for the next session even before
a new General Assembly begins its
work in the new year.
Currently, 39 counties use some
form of electronic voting, includ­
ing by computer, paper ballots that
are scanned with an optical device
and punch cards that are read by a
device. Twenty-one counties use
voting machines.
Another seven counties use man­
ually counted paper ballots in
some areas in combination with
either electronic devices or
machines, according to the state
Bureau of Commissions, Elections
and Legislation.
“I think it’s important for us to
do this now, before Pennsylvania
finds itself in the situation that
Florida finds itself in,” Greenleaf
said.
