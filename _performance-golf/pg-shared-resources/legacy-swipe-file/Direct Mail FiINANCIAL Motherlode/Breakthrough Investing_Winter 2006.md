What do you do when a stock you own drops in price? If
you're like many investors, you buy more to lower your average
cost. This is referred to as "averaging down." Sometimes it's a
smart move, but sometimes it's throwing good money after bad.
VectorVest can help ensure you don't outsmart yourself by
letting you know whether a price dip is a buying opportunity
or a trap. For starters, look at your stock's Relative Timing
(RT). If it's below 1.00 that's a sign the stock is in a short-term
downturn. The lower the number, the more pronounced the trend. If the stock is a
good deal today, wouldn't it be an even better deal at a lower price tomorrow?
But is it a good deal? To find out, look at the Relative Value (RV). Is it above
1.00? If not, you may want to avoid buying more since our studies show that stocks
with an RV less than 1.00 almost invariably lag the market.
Next, compare the stock's current price with its value. If it's selling for less than
its value, you have a potential bargain on your hands. If it's selling for more than its
value, you're banking on future growth to push the price higher. Check its growth
rate (GRT) to see if its prospects justify the price. You might also want to check the
Relative Safety (RS) to determine how steady and predictable earnings are likely to be.

If the stock price rises above the strike price, the other investor
can require you to sell him your stock at the strike price. If the stock
doesn't rise that high, your stock won't be called away. In either case,
you get to keep the premium the other investor paid you for the option
contract. Because you own the underlying stock against which the
option contract is written, you are said to be writing a "covered" call. If
you didn't own the underlying stock, you would be writing a "naked"
option (which is much riskier).
To see how covered calls work in actual practice, assume you

annualized basis. You can now sell another three options' contracts and
