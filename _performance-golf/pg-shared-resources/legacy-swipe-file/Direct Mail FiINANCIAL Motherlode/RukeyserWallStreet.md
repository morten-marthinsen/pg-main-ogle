# AW all Falls and


# Opportunity Rises

Almost 10 years to the day
after the fall of the Berlin Wall,
another divisive barrier also fell.
On October 22, 1999, Congress
repealed the 66-year-old Glass
Steagall Act, tearing down the
"fire wall" separating commercial
banks, investment banks and
insurance companies.
Glass-Steagall was meant to
promote financial stability and pub
lic confidence in the banking sys
tem following the 1929 crash. But
by the end of the century it had
deteriorated into little more than a
protection system for underper
forming financial players.
The repeal of the law ended the
Cold War among financial blocs,
paving the way for banks, insur
ance companies and brokerage
firms to become one. For the first
time in modem history, financial
conglomerates around the world
can now merge without regard to
function or national borders.
Is this good for us as investors
in these stocks? You bet it is. The
repeal marks the culmination of six
decades of lobbying by financial
firms eager to become one-stop
shops for financial services. You
can be sure that they weren't ask
mg Congress to make it harder for
them to make money.
