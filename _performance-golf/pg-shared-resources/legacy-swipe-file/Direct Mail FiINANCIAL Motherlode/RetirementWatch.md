# The Biggest Retirement Surprise


# "Retirement" Could Mean More ''Work"

Retirement often doesn't come
when you expect. In fact, the biggest
surprise is that retirement no longer is
a point in time; it's a process.
The truth is, 65 is an arbitrary and
artificial retirement age. Most people
don't retire at 65.
About half of current retirees say
they retired before they planned.
Corporate restructuring, illnesses,
injuries, and other events force many
people into the world of retirement
years before they planned.
For others, retirement comes later
than expected, and many baby
boomers are likely to fall into this
category. About 70% of those over 50
say they expect to continue working
past 65, according to an AARP sur
vey. The data show this isn't a new
trend. The percentage of those over 55
in the work force has increased since
the mid-1990s. The rate for the 65 to
69 age group increased from 20% to
33%. Even the 70 to 74 group's
participation doubled from about 10%
to almost 20%, though it recently
declined a bit. Here's why ...
• Some people, including
many boomers, simply won't have
sufficient nest eggs for them to stop
working at age 65.
• Many people believe they are
too healthy and active to stop working.
Surveys show many boomers plan to
work at least part-time after age 65,
and a number hope to work until at
least age 70.
• Social Security's normal
retirement age now is 66, will rise to
67, and the big payoff comes from
delaying benefits until 70.
• Doing some work is healthy.
Studies show that those who moved
from full-time work to part-time
work had better health than those
who stopped working.

"retire" from one job, then, either
