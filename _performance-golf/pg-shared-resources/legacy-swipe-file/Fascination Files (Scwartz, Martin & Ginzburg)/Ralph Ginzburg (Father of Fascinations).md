Now, see how Moneysworth does it.
You won’t find a feel-good or tepid

they’re all about one thing: results.
The testimonials which follow were
published again and again, and vetted

They reveal that there are only four
The four testimonials are ones that:

2) Make you money
3) Enhance your prestige
4) Give you insider status
Most of the Moneysworth testimonial
firepower focuses on either making

enhanced prestige, as well.
Getting such testimonials is a topic

once again, the standard disclaimer that
your author is not an attorney and cannot

However, there are two main schools of
thought about testimonials.
“School A” says to create the best
products and services you’re capable of
and actively solicit testimonials from
your customers and subscribers.
“School B” says write your dream
testimonial yourself and get one of your
satisfied customers to stand behind it.
I’ve never been a fan of “School B” for
several reasons, having little to do with
kinds of testimonials that really make a
difference.
business ethics.
First, it’s impossible to get outside of
your own head when you write a
testimonial. No matter how original you
think you’re being, testimonials which
have been cooked up tend to read that
way, irrespective of the ad writer’s
talent.
Second, and probably more important,
your customers will always find a
nuance, hidden benefit or value


proposition you would never have
thought of – and say it in a voice you’re

that it’s next to impossible to get out of
your own head.
The following testimonials were one of
the motors of the Moneysworth machine
– stretching its paid subscribership to
well over a million. And it’s because of
that sizeable subscribership that

As you read the testimonial-laden

After Eros: Ginzburg Pushes

Mail Order Lists
(NEW YORK) March 18, 1980 - "The
two biggest appeals to mail-order sales
are lust and greed."
That's the credo of publisher Ralph
Ginzburg, who until recently, was best
known for appealing to lust. In 1972,
after a celebrated Supreme Court
decision, he was jailed for sending Eros,
his sexy quarterly magazine through the
mail.
Now Mr. Ginzburg is concentrating on
greed. Note the first few lines of a recent
full page newspaper advertisement that
features a close-up of an alluring
woman: "DARLING: I like men who
like money, who know how to get it,
enjoy it. I'm betting you're one or them
and I'd like to give you a subscription to
a magazine that's SINFULLY
enriching."
"Give" is an apt word to use in this
come-on for Mr. Ginzburg's American
Business periodical. One year of issues
costs "a mere dollar bill," and a lifetime
subscription is $9.95.
Sound unbelievable? It's for real.
Subscribers to this Ginzburg tabloid -
and to another called Moneysworth
which costs $5 a year - do indeed receive
12 issues a year. But they receive even
more than they bargained for - a load of
junk mail.
