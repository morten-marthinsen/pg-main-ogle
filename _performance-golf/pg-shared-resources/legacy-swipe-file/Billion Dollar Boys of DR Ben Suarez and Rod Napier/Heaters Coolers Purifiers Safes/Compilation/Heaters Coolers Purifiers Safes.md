years there’s no doubt you’ve heard of the world famous
Heat Surge Amish Fireplace that slashes heat bills, while
keeping everyone warm.
And you’ve probably wondered, what the heck do the Amish
have to do with electric fireplaces?
The Amish are known for their superior craftsmanship. And
people who can afford it insist on buying Amish built homes and
Amish crafted furniture because of the high quality and because

And if you thought you could never afford the luxury of Amish

But before we do, here’s a fascinating never-before-told story

are saving folks big money on heating bills. Our journey begins in
our country’s heartland, a little town in Ohio.
In early 2006, John T. White, a fifty-seven year old farmer, coin
collector, writer and all around jack-of-all-trades, was asked by
his longtime friend to help solve a really big problem.
They just wouldn’t accept that it had to cost an arm and a leg to
heat a home safely to stay warm and comfortable. Though space
heaters have been around a long time, many are dangerous, out­
dated and down right ugly. So they set out to revolutionize the
way we heat our homes and make staying warm affordable.
It just so happened that Mr. White’s farm was near a large
Amish community. And over time, Mr. White became friends
with his Amish neighbors and discovered something. The Amish
were much more than just farmers and carpenters. They were in­
genious inventors, shroud in simple clothes.
One of the men that Mr. White had befriended just so happened
to be a master Amish craftsman who owned a successful wood­
working business.
Amish craftsmen, experienced in building homes, know that a
built-in fireplace can add over $7,000 to the value of a home.
Altogether they envisioned a modern fireplace, not a simple
space heater, but a fireplace for zone heating. One that was every
bit as beautiful and inviting as a fire burning in a hearth without
the hassle, mess, danger or smoke of a wood burning fireplace. A
fireplace that would be clean, safe, efficient, and beautiful. The
Amish craftsman dreamt up a real wood mantle and hearth to
create the fireplace, making each one a lasting piece of heirloom
furniture.
Initially, the Amish craftsman set out to make just one, a pro­
totype. Though Mr. White wanted all the fireplace mantles to be
Amish made, he thought the Amish could never keep up with ac-

ber and assembling their community of wood-working craftsmen,
Heat Surge worked with teams of engineers from across the Pa­
cific to create the heart of the fireplace, the micro-furnace and
thermal heat exchanger.
Mr. White and his friend obsessed over every detail, insisting
the flames needed to flicker just right and the hand rubbed finish
