BY RHETT MORGAN
World Staf Writer
OWASSO — A three-mem-
ber panel of school parents
and educators rallied against
Common Core standards
Thursday night at Friendship
Baptist Church.
Speakers who included
Kristal Picolet, the leader of
Owasso Against Common
Core, addressed a crowd of
about 50 people for about two
hours, detailing the group’s
reasons for disagreement
with the national education
reform measure.
Nikki Jones, a prekinder-
garten teacher in Tulsa Pub-
lic Schools, and Linda Mur-
phy, a veteran educator and
former Republican candidate
for state superintendent, also
made presentations that con-
cluded with a question-and-
answer session.
Picolet, whose group has
grown to more than 1,250
members in about three
months, called Common
Core a “train wreck.” She said
her third-grade son, enrolled
in Owasso Public Schools, al-
ready had taken more than 40
tests this school year.
“Local authority has been
stripped away,” said Picolet,
who said she moved away
from Colorado to escape
Common Core but now it’s in
Oklahoma schools, too.
“God gave these children to
us,” she said. “If we don’t pro-
tect them, who will?”
Common Core originated
out of a National Governors
Association efort to bolster
and standardize K-12 edu-
cation across the country.
Oklahoma adopted the pro-
gram in 2010, in part to boost
state schools’ chances in the
Obama administration’s Race
to the Top grant competition.
Common Core standards
were adopted by 45 states
and the District of Colum-
bia. Only Alaska, Minnesota,
Nebraska, Texas and Virginia
haven’t adopted the stan-
dards.
Opponents claim that the
standards represent a federal
intrusion into state and lo-
cal education policy and will
raise the amount of class-
room time devoted to testing.
In the Oklahoma Legisla-
ture, House Bill 3399, a mea-
sure that would repeal Com-
mon Core in the state, could
be heard by the full Senate
next week.
Picolet and Jones stressed
that standardized tests zap
creativity and stymie teach-
ing latitude.
Picolet brought a school
math sample from her son,
who arrived at the right sum
for his addition problem but
whose grade sufered be-
cause he failed to explain its
“associative property.”
Murphy said federal mon-
ey was used to entrap state
education systems through
grants given to two national
assessment consortia run by
top state school oicials via
the Partnership for Assess-
ment of Readiness for College
and Careers, and the Smarter
Balanced Assessment Con-
sortium.
She added that states are
collecting extensive personal
student data stored in data
banks without parental con-
sent and are ditching superi-
or state academic standards.
Two years ago, Jones
said she monitored third-
grade state testing and wit-
nessed students who were
so stressed from the exams
that they threw up and soiled
themselves.
Jones, who said she works
at a school where at least 90
percent of the students are at
or below poverty level, claims
that standardized testing
failed to take demographics
into account.
“The testing now is ridicu-
lous,” said Jones, who urged
parents to have their children
opt out of the tests. “It’s just
not worth it.”
