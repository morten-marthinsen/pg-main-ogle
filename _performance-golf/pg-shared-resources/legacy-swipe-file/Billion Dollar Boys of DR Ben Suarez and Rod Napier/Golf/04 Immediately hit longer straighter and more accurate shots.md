As you know, a major part of the
power you generate during the golf
swing is from the shaft loading and
unloading. How your shaft loads and
then unloads is a function of your
swing. How much you benefit from
it is a function of the stiffness of the
shaft you are using.
It has always been accepted that
the faster you swing, the stiffer the
shaft; and the slower, the more
flexible. But, that alone is not true.
Since the club is a spring system
with a weight (club head) on the end,
each club has a frequency, like a
tuning fork, that is measured in
cycles per minute. The stiffer the
shaft, the higher the frequency. Just
as each club has its own frequency,
each golfer's swing has a timing that
should match the club's natural
frequency. It is this natural
frequency that determines the
response of the club head from the
time of release, or maximum load,
back to a point where the club shaft
returns to a straight position. When
the club shaft returns to the straight
position, the club head obtains the
maximum speed due to shaft flex.
The pros have known this for
years. However, they have the luxury
of visiting corporate testing ranges
and mobile vans to have their swing
examined with all the latest, high-
tech gadgets.

Why should you care? Look at
the illustrations above. If your shaft
unloads too early (before ball
contact) or too late (after striking the
ball), the stored energy never gets
transferred to the ball, resulting in a
loss of tremendous power.
In addition, you will hit off-line
shots because the club face is not
square at impact.
Remember, this is not conjecture
or opinion, rather the facts from
scientific tests and data analysis. The
shaft must return to the straight line
position at impact for maximum
distance and accuracy.
To achieve this position, it is
critical that you use the shaft flex
that matches your swing. However,
it is estimated that current shaft flex
ranges will not fit more than 50% of
the players.
Unfortunately, shaft manufactur-
ers can have a variation of 15 cycles
or more between their flex range
(ladies', regular, stiff or X).
Such a wide range is not close
enough to provide maximum
performance for each individual
golfer.
The Fit-Chip allows us to match a
shaft to a given swing with plus or
minus three cycles per minute of
accuracy.
In order to identify a player's
swing frequency, the point in the
swing at which a player reaches
maximum acceleration (not to be
confused with maximum club head
speed) needs to be determined.
Various players obtain this peak
acceleration at different times. Some
players initiate it immediately at the
top of the swing, while other players
start down slowly, then accelerate
during the downswing. Still others
accelerate more at the bottom of the
swing with wrist action. The
different swings have different time
lapses between maximum
acceleration and ball impact. Since
the goal is to achieve the impact of
maximum speed at ball impact, the
flexed shaft needs to return straight
at impact. Therefore, a player's
swing frequency is determined by
the time between maximum
acceleration and ball impact.
The Fit-Chip is an instrument the
size of a package of chewing gum,
containing an accelerometer sensor,
computer chip and batteries, which
is placed on the shaft of the club. As
the player accelerates the club during
the swing, the accelerometer in the
package records an acceleration
value at each millisecond. The data
is recorded until impact with the
ball. Once the ball is impacted, the
Fit-Chip analyzes the data for the
time between maximum acceleration
and impact during a player's golf
swing. With this timing information,
club frequencies can be tuned to
match a player's natural swing
frequency.

There are occasions when players
