If you look between January 2008 and January 2009, you’ll see a little downward blip in the
gold price. That little downturn was actually, percentage-wise, much larger than the current
correction.
Back then $1,000 gold drew a lot of secondary gold melt onto the market, knocking the price of
gold down. As the price fell, the secondary melt dried up, and the price of gold more than doubled
to $1,920 an ounce.
At that price, another round of selling goldware, jewelry and family heirlooms began all over
again. But now the price of gold has fallen $340 an ounce, and the secondary melt is drying up.
So I expect gold to rebound ferociously, as it did in 2009. When gold doubles again, today’s
downturn will appear like a little blip, just like the 2008 downturn appears today.

Most of the world’s silver is mined as a
by-product of gold, copper, and other mining
operations. What Silver Wheaton does is
finance the gold and copper mining operations
in exchange for a specified fraction of their
silver by-product at a specified low price.
When I say low price, I mean it. The
average price that the company pays for silver
is a mere $4 an ounce.
These are called silver streaming deals.
Miners like them because they get money
in advance that they can use to pay for
exploration, production, acquisitions, or to pay
down debt.
And because someone else is taking the
silver by-product off their hands, they’re free
to concentrate on their gold or copper business.
Silver Wheaton is the global behemoth of
silver streaming. It has a portfolio of contracts
that gives it the right to purchase all or a
portion of the silver production of 14 mines
scattered across Europe, North America, and
South America.
Five of them (including the 4 largest in
Silver Wheaton’s portfolio) have expected life
spans of more than 20 years. So Silver Wheaton
is going to be getting large amounts of silver at
very cheap prices for a long, long time.
This year, Silver Wheaton will purchase
approximately 27 million ounces of silver
under these contracts. That’s scheduled to
rise to about 43 million ounces by 2016.
Since it pays only about $4 an ounce,
and silver sells for $28 an ounce, you can see
how wildly profitable this is. In fact, Silver
Wheaton’s operating profit margin runs a
whopping 76%.
