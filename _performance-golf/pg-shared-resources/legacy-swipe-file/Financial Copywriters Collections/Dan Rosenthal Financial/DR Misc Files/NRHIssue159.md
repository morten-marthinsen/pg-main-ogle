## The Jefferson Liberty half ounce pure


## gold coins rose from $800 to $983 -- a


## $366 increase per ounce. More gains


## coming. HOLD

These are coins minted in 2007 and graded
MS69 by PCGS, one of the two top coin grading
and certification firms in America. MS69
means “Mint State” 69. The grade is on a scale
from 1 to 70, so MS69 means the coin is as close
to perfect as a coin can get.
The Proof Jefferson Liberty half-ouncers
are also good buys, if graded Proof 69 Deep
Cameo. A “Proof” coin, if you recall, is made
from planchets (coin blanks) that have been
specially-polished to bring out a mirror-like
finish. The coins themselves are struck twice
to bring out all the tiny little details that coin
weenies, like me, love.
The Jefferson Liberties increased 7.0 times
more than gold during the same period, almost
double the 4-fold increase I forecasted.
The Jefferson Liberty is still next to
impossible to buy in any sizeable quantities, so
I expect significant further price increases. I’m
holding my Jefferson half-ounce gold Liberties,
and that’s what I recommend you do.
But for new purchases, the US Mint has
recently released even rarer gold bullion coins,
one of which I’ll recommend below and others
which I’ll recommend in upcoming issues.

## The one-ounce Statue of Liberty $100


## proof platinum coins actually gained


## value while platinum was falling


## sharply. More gains coming

The $100 one-ounce platinum bullion coins,
like the Jefferson Liberties, come in both mint
state and proof editions.
Unlike the Jefferson half-ouncers, where
the proofs are just as good as the mint states
(as investments), with these Statue of Liberty
platinum coins, it is essential to buy the
proofs only. That’s because the mint state
one ouncers (with one exception) all have the
same reverse, while the platinum PROOF one
ouncers have a unique reverse each year.
That makes each year’s proof platinum
one-ouncer a unique “type” coin. There are


The Natural Resource Hunter • 3
tens of thousands of “type collectors” -- people
who avidly collect one of each possible type of
US coin. So that makes the PROOF $100 one
ounce platinum coins a much better investment
than the mint state specimens (and the proofs
look better in the bargain).
We’ve been using the price of the 2010 date
platinum bullion coins in our portfolio, though
other dates were also recommended -- most of
them actually outperforming the 2010 date. As
I mentioned, these proof platinum one-ouncers
radically outperformed platinum itself.
Platinum is an industrial metal and
industry, especially car sales, were weak.
So platinum prices crashed. But the coins I
recommended you buy are collector coins and
are additionally welcomed into IRAs. So they
prospered.
The recent rise in platinum prices is due, in
equal parts, to a recovery in automobile sales
-- where platinum is required for catalytic
converters to clean up the exhaust. And it’s also
due to troubles in the South African mining
industry, which normally accounts for more
than half of the world’s newly mined platinum.
The mine owners have been harassed by
threats of nationalization; aggressive demands
and strikes for higher wages by the mine
workers; and insufficient energy.
Mine production of platinum was cut
sharply. The recently higher prices for platinum
are the results.
Looking forward, Russia and South Africa
account for 80% of newly-mined platinum each
year. They’ve began talking about a platinum/
palladium OPEC-like cartel. I don’t know if
they’ll pull it off, but if they do, I think you can
expect sharply higher platinum and palladium
prices.
You should buy the platinum bullion coins
with dates 2000 through 2010, because these
are the proofs with the lowest mintages. The
earlier or later coins have higher mintages. Go
for the lower mintages in the years I slotted off
for you.
