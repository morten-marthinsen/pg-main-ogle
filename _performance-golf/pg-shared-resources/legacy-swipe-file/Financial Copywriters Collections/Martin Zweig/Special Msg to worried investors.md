# stock market from Martin Zweig

If you worry about the stock market-won
dering which way it's going to go next and
what to do about your investments-you are
not alone. So do I.
Looking back, I find that. my worries
always come from the same cause: I start
doubting my own market indicators. Yet an
enormous amount of computer research and
practical experience tells me that this kind
of doubting is the worst mistake I can make.
This is not to say that the Technical and
Monetary indicators I have spent my entire
professional life developing and refining are
infallible. But they are far and away the
most reliable means of forecasting market
trends that I know.
For instance, late last August I alerted my
readers to the severely bearish implications
of the "Negative Yield Spread" between
Aaa Bonds and Commercial Paper Rates.
At the time, my own feelings about the mar
ket were mixed. Yet here was a clearly neg
ative signal. So I took another look at the
long-range backtrack of this vital indicator.
HERE IS WHAT I FOUND
Going back 60 years, all the way to 1919,
there were only nine previous occasions
when short-term interest rates, such as those
on Commercial Paper, rose more than one
percentage point above high-grade Bond
Yields. :rhat's the negative spread I require
to produce a "sell" signal on this indicator.
During the periods when this situation
has existed over the past six decades, the
Dow Jones Industrials have plunged, on
average, at the annualized rate of minus
16.4%. This ominous condition prevailed
throughout most of the 1974 bear market as
well as the infamous 1929 crash. This time
around, my indicator gave a "sell" signal at
886 on the Dow, just a dozen points away
from the eventual top a few weeks later in
early October. Shortly thereafter, the Oc
tober "massacre" of '79 ensued. This yield
spread was even more negative on the eve of
the recent February-March plunge.
(NOTE: It should not be assumed, the
above case or any other, that future results
will match or be profitable.)

ON THE RIGHT SIDE
Do my indicators work? Yes, they've kept
me on the right side of the market more
often than not. But let's face it-anyone who
claims he's always right is a liar. Now, in my
tenth year of publishing The Zweig Fore
cast, I look back on two really poor calls:
giving up my bearish position about four
months prematurely in 1974 (after having
been bearish nearly all of 1973 except dur
ing the summer rally), and missing the
January 1976 advance (I was neutral). I
might also throw in missing the April 1978
takeoff, but I was in gold stocks then and
turned short-term bullish in May. So much
for mistakes.

HOW TO STOP WORRYING
While, as I've mentioned, my market indi
cators are not infallible-nor are they de
