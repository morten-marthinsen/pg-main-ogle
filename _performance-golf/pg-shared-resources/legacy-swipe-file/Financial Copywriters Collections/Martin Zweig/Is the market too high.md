The market has more than tripled in five years
and more than doubled in the past three years.
There hasn't been a 10% correction since the first
half of 1984. In its powerful advance. the market
has managed to become grossly overvalued ac
cording to any reasonable reading of price/earn
ings ratios. dividends, book values and the like.
Lately, stocks have had to absorb three Prime
Rate hikes, a violent breakdown in the Bond mar
ket, upticks in Inflation and the battering of the
Dollar. But for all that, stocks seem to bend but
they don't break. Why? The answer is Liquidity.
Liquidity is the key to major movements in
the stock market. If there is pler1ty of it, then
there is buying power to take stocks higher. Sop
enough of it up, and stocks will fall. In a recent
issue of The Zweig Forecast. Dr. Zweig enumer
ated seven ways in which liquidity could be re
duced. When. finally, enough of these are in
place, a bear market will follow. We're probably
not there yet, but in just a few weeks the mea
sures of liquidity have worsened.
DON'T GET SUCKERED!
In his book, WINNING ON WALL STREET, Or.
Zweig devoted a chapter to three characteristics,
one of which, at the least, has been present dur
ing all bear markets since 1920. We've finally
reached one of them here; overvaluation (the
others are an inverted yield curve and extreme
deflation). The appearance of such a trait does
not guarantee a bear market, but it does make
it possible to have one. In the mid-1960s the
market stayed overvalued for a couple of years
before a bear evolved. But it is important now
to be aware of the risks and not to get suckered
in by unrealistic rationalizations of market
negatives. That can trap you later when trouble
begins.
