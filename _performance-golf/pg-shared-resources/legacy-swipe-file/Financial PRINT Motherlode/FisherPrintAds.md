The Wrong Approach of Some Wealth Managers
If you have a portfolio of $500,000 or more, you’ve probably been approached by financial
advisers calling themselves “Wealth Managers” offering to manage your money. Wealth
management may sound appealing—you can have all your financial needs taken care of in one
place!—but in reality it’s a term many financial advisers use to lure you in so they can sell you an
array of products and services you may not even need. That’s because “Wealth Managers” often
have incentives to sell you whatever they can, or even whatever generates the highest fees, instead
of what’s best for your financial goals. Don’t fall into the “Wealth Manager” trap—call right
now for your FREE report, TheSixSecretsWealthManagersDon’tWantYoutoKnow. It could
save you thousands of dollars and help you avoid getting locked into products or services you
don’t need.
Consider this hypothetical example. Bob and Mary, both 65 years old and retired, interview
two different “Wealth Managers” about managing their $1,000,000 portfolio. Their goal is to
have enough money for a comfortable retirement, and leave a little to their two kids if there’s
any money left over.
WealthManager#1 recommends they put $500,000 into an annuity and sells them
long-term care insurance because he is focused on insurance products (and he happens to
get an 8% commission on every annuity he sells!). He then recommends some mutual fund
investments, and refers them to his buddy for estate planning (knowing that his buddy
will send him some referrals in return).
WealthManager#2 offers to put together an elaborate financial plan (for a fee), an estate plan
(for a fee), set up a trust (for a fee) and manage their portfolio using a combination of mutual
funds and bonds (for yet another fee). He also highly recommends they work with his CPA
buddy to develop a tax strategy (knowing his buddy will pay him a fee for the referral).
Two different “Wealth Managers” offer Bob and Mary two very different approaches. Is either
one right? And how can they know what’s really going on?
The problem lies in the obvious conflicts of interest. When doing the right or optimal thing
for a client collides with what is financially best for the “Wealth Manager,” what do you think is
likely to happen? Do you get the best advice for your unique situation or do you get the advice
that lines the adviser’s pocket? Either way—you are left to decide for yourself, and the truth can
be hard to uncover. Bob and Mary really need a true expert with a transparent fee structure,
and without incentives to make referrals or sell products.
