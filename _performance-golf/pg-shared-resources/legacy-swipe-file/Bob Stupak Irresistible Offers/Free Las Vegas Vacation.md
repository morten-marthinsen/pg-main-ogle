# Smokers grudgingly comply


# with flight no-smoking rule

By Doug J. Swanson
Dallas Morning News
ABOARD FLIGHT 1 — Passen-
gers on this American Airlines DC-
10 had a smooth ride last Sunday on
the six-hour flight from snowy New
York to sunny Los Angeles. But it
was still a rough flight for some:
The no-smoking sign was lit the en-
tire way.
"One lady came up to me and
asked, 'Are we in the United States,
or is this Russia?'" said flight at-
tendant Alicia Lutz.
Feb. 25 was Day One of the new
federal no-smoking regulations for
commercial flights. Henceforth, al-
most every domestic flight will be
smoke-free.
For someone accustomed to
lighting up 20 or 30 times a day, jet-
ting coast to coast makes for a long
stretch of mandatory cold turkey.
"A six-hour flight is a long flight.
I think they should allow smoking
for a short time," said passenger
Anita Brennan of Garden City, N.Y.
"Maybe for the last hour."'
Instead, Brennan relied partly
on the bubble gum brought along, by
her friend and fellow smoker, Doro-
thy McCabe.
"Six packs, sugarless," said
McCabe.
Since April 1988, smoking has
been banned on all two-hour flights.
The new congressionally ordered
regulation extends the prohibition
to flights of six hours or less.
That includes virtually every do-
mestic flight, except some to
Alaska and Hawaii — or about 99
percent of the 17,000 daily flights
within the United States.
Proponents say the new ban was
necessary for reasons of health and
comfort. "It's going to save the
lives of our members," Juliette D.
Lenoir, vice president of the Associ-
ation of Flight Attendants, said.

"It's just one person's desire to
control the behavior of another per-
son," said Tom Lauria, a spokes-
man for the Tobacco Institute, a
lobbying group for the cigarette in-
dustry.
Lenoir cited a recent govern-
ment-commissioned study that said
cigarette smoke in passenger airlin-
ers could kill 12 to 15 flight crew
members of every 100,000 in the
next 20 years.
And in the short term, she said,
flight attendants routinely suffer
from "a tingling and burning in our
eyes, nausea, dizziness and hoarse-
ness."
The Tobacco Institute isn't shed-
ding tears over the attendants' re-
ports of illness.
"That's what they claim, isn't
it?" Lauria said. "I would perceive
flight attendants as being in the ser-
vice business. And people in the ser-
vice business accommodate their
customers."
On board American Flight 1,
among flight attendants taking a
break in what used to be the

rio.
x

breathe back here now," said Lutz,
a- 14-year employee. "This is
great." \
This flight, which left New York
at 9 a.m. EST, was uncrowded,
smooth and on time — which may
have mitigated the crankiness fac-
tor among passengers. Flight at-
tendants said the mood among
would-be smokers was generally
one of acceptance.
The regulations apply to a
flight's scheduled duration and not
its actual time, so passengers who
smoke expressed concern about
delays that will increase their non-
smoking time.
"Scheduled for six hours and
holding for four. Who has the rights
there?" said passenger Robert L.
Morris of Bay Shore, N.Y. "That
sounds like a court case to me."
For those suffering tobacco
withdrawal, American Airlines
flight attendants armed themselves
with cinnamon candy to give away.
The American Lung Association
has published a list of "survival
tips" for fliers who smoke. Among
them: "Keep your hands and mind
busy," take walks up and down the
aisle and "try rubbing a smooth
stone."
Violators of the federal regula-
tion can be fined as much as $1,000
for smoking on board and $2,000 if
they tamper with the smoke detec-
tors in the rest rooms.
But if the past is any indication,
the government will not get rich on
penalties. In the first year of the
two-hour ban, only 113 people were
cited and four were fined, govern-
ment records show.
"The law is unfair and unneces-
sary," said Lauria of the Tobacco
Institute. "But we'll obey it."
American Flight 1 landed at Los
Angeles International Airport at
noon PST. "I've got to get off here,"
McCabe muttered as she hastened
toward the exit.
Her friend, Brennan, said it had
not been so bad. "We survived," she
said.
