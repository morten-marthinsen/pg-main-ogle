AUSTIN (AP) - Some voca-
tional education teachers are not
ready to admit that boys can
become nurses and girls can be
construction workers, a State
Board of Education member said
Saturday.
The board's initial attempts to
set up a program to battle sex
bias and stereotyping in voca-
tional education was greeted by a
disappointing response from
school districts. Twenty-three
districts sought money, but only
five met the criteria.
So the board Saturday voted to
offer the remaining $340,000 to the
regional education service
centers.
Maria Elena Flood, board
member from El Paso, said many
phases of education still suffer
from sex stereotypes, but "in
vocational education it has a
greater direct relationship.''
"Many times the faculty or
teachers that have been in the
system for a long time are not
ready to cope with the opposite
gender entering a particular
field, "she said.
"In the allied health field there
is a very clear-cut feeling still to-
day that nursing is for women. (A
male) can be a lab technician or
an X-ray technician, but the young
men who go into nursing are still
looked at sort of sideways. We
need male nurses," Ms. Flood
said.
"In many of the construction
trades we have a tendency to feel
they would be inappropriate for
women. Yet we find many, many
women going into construction
trades as young people, in certain
urban settings where sex
stereotyping has seemed to have
been less of a problem than in the
smaller communities, who then go
into engineering and higher level
training, "she said.
The programs will include
workshops at which vocational
education staff members will
"become aware of subtleties that
they may not even have recogniz-
ed they were still performing,"
according to Ms. Flood.
She said many districts were
reluctant to apply for the pro-
grams because of the relatively
small amount of money offered.
