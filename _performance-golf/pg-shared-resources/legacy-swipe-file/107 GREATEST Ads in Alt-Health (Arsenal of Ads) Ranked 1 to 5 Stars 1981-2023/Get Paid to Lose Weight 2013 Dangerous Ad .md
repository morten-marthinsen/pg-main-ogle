BY ERIC BROWN
Greeley Tribune
Colette West and Asad Abdi
have devoted their careers to
helping an influx of African
refugees in Greeley function
in their new community, but
now they’re ready to watch the
displaced people thrive here
by creating a camel farm.
For about a year-and-a-half,
West and Abdi, co-directors
at the local Global Refugee
Center, have been crusad­
ing to bring camels to Weld
County — an effort that’s in­
cluded writing letters to the
U.S. Food and Drug Adminis­
tration, the U.S. Department
of Agriculture and President
Barack Obama.
For Africans raised in their
native countries on camel’s
milk, the milk from a cow
causes stomach discomfort.
Beyond wanting to produce
camel’s milk for consumption,
West and Abdi said starting a
local camel farm could be a
job creator and source of in­
come for refugees in Greeley.
Many come from countries
saturated in violence and tur­
moil and arrive with no family
and little or no money, often
to take jobs at area meatpack­
ing facilities that others don’t
want.

More and more, camel’s milk

Those uses have driven up the
price of the animals.
West and Abdi said camels
in the U.S. range from about
$8,000 to $16,000 each.
Camels from Africa are
much cheaper but, because of
concerns regarding foot-and-
mouth disease, camels from
that continent aren’t allowed
in the U.S.

milk for making and selling
butter, ice cream, soaps and

School District 6 has leased a
portion of Cameron Elemen­

the Global Refugee Center to
operate at that location.

has been working with Christ

a new home within the dis­
trict.
At present, they are looking
for a new home for the cen­

Despite the abundance of

continue their crusade to
help camels take their place

There are federal regulations
