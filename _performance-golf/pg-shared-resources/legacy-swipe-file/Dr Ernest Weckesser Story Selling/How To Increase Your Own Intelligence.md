David Borgcnicht and Joshua Piven,
the authors of the popular Worst-Case
Scenario books, spelled out how to stay
safe on the beach this summer. But what
about on a picnic? There, the worst-case
scenario might be an onslaught by sting-
ing army ants. Here's their advice on how
to deal with these unwelcome guests.
1. Pick your picnic spot carefully. Look
beneath you for masses of ants and
avoid stepping on them, says Bill Got-
wald, a biology professor at Utica Col-
lege of Syracuse University in New "York
and the author of Army Ants: The
Biology of Social Predation (CORNEU.
UNIVERSITY PRESS, $45>. However, even
the most observant person still might
step into a mass of army ants, because
the ants often forage, well hidden,
beneath leaf litter.
2. If the ants climb on you, don't panic.
Try to step out of the column or mass
of ants. Jump to dislodge the ants, and
then sweep the remaining ants from
your clothing and body. You may be
stung if you pick the ants off with your
fingers, so instead try sweeping them
from your clothing with a cloth or stick
3.Treat the stings. Ant stings can pro-
duce intense burning and local pain,
swelling and itching. Ireat the sting of
an army ant as you would the sting of
a bee or wasp, Gotwald says. Apply a
cool compress to the stung area, as well
as an antibiotic, if available. People
who are allergic to ant venom may
need antihistamines and epinephrine.
