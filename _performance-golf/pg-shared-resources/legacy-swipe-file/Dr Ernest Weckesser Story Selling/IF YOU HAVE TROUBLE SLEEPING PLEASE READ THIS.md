# I

n the "80s, we learned to graze on all sorts
of goodies: buffalo wings, tiny gounnct
pizzas, fried wontons, a little of this, a lit-
tle or that. The list of appetizers grew in every
restaurant or bar and grill worth its salt.
Feasting .on first courses is not a practice
we're abandoning in the new decade. Just
look at some of the latest appetizers: bite-sized
crabcakes with, dipping sauces, classic Caesar
salad, Vietnamese spring rolls, quesadillas
stuffed with brie and grapes and topped with
sweet pea guacamole, and blue corn hoecakes
with Boursin cheese and caviar.
"People now make entire meals out of ap-
petizers," says industry analyst George Rice.
Chefs arc going wild with appetizers, ex-
perimenting with exotic flavors that might be
too offbeat for mainstream menu entrees.
Sometimes, the results are fabulous. Other
times, disastrous. A nibble of what's new:
Lighter Mexican: We've downed enough
nachos to last a lifetime, so it's time to move
on to taquitos. These tasty, baby tacos can be
filled with just about anything, and trendy
West Coast restaurants are stuffing the crispy
corn or flour shells with duck and crab.
Look, too, for new variations on cholester-
ol-loaded guacamole. Trumps in L.A. does a
sweet pea version — still avocado-green but
lower in fat and healthier. Mexican appetizers
of all kinds — ceviche, quesadillas, guacamole
— have been the hottest hors d'oeuvre in
more ways than one. South-of-the-border in-
spirations now account for 13 percent of all
appetizers, according to a recent Gallup poll
That puts them ahead of chicken wings, pota-
to skins and pasta in popularity.
Indian imports: Take a look at City Restau-
rant in LA. and you just might glimpse the
future. Poona (rice) pancakes and spicy little
potato fritters called bhujia are exotic but pop
ular offerings.
And at Chicago's Eurasia, you'll find cha-
pati (fried Indian bread) topped with exotic
fare like herbed goat cheese, tomato-ginger
chutney and fermented black bean pesto.
Rare meat: Yes, the tartarcs are back, joined
now by a new variation on raw: slices of thinly
shaved meat or fish called carpaccios. Tuna
tartare is a hit on the West Coast; beef, goose
and tuna carpaccios are popping up at trendy
restaurants. At Chicago's Spiaggia: a goose
carpaccio with shaved white truffles.
New wave in crab: Aside from crabcakes, try
Chinese-style crab wontons, hefty crab ravioli
and crab-filled jalapeno peppers.
