There's a totally legal "numbers
game" being played in the men's
clothing industry; Morty Dorman and
Jay Winthrop thought you ought to
know how to beat it.
The Amalgamated Clothing Workers
of America (ACW A) has established
a grading or "numbers" system-by
which the tailor gets paid for his piece
work-based on the quality of the
garment being worked on.
-If a suit is going to sell for under
$100 (retail), a tailor will spend a lot
less time sewing the shoulder seams
on a jacket; h03'll sew a lot of seams in
a single day and get paid by the
garment.
If a suit is going to sell for over
$150, for instance, the tailor will
spend more time on each shoulder
seam, do less seams in a day, but get
paid more for each garment.
And each of these operations
(called piece work) will be inspected,
' and done over until perfect.
For your information, the ACWA
numbering system goes like this:
#X Make: The very cheapest con·
struction in a suit that retails for
under $60.
#1 Make: Next to the bottom line;
suits that retail for $60 to $100.
#2 Make: Mostly machine-made
suits with some hand work; popular
priced to retailfor $100 to $150.
#4 Make: A few machine operations
but a lot of hand work; better than
average suits to retail for $150 to

#6 Make: Almost all hand tailored;
the finest ready-to-wear suits in the
business to retail for $200 to $375.
The more hand work, the more
careful the tailoring, the more inspec·
