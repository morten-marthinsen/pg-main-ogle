## How to guarantee your own success

Even though talent, brains, education, and people skills are nice to have, they don't
guarantee your success. The fact is, all those things won't take you anywhere if you don't have
a consistent drive to succeed. But if you do have that drive, you will always get what you want
in the end, even if you have little else going for you.
For example, a popular TV news magazine aired a segment recently that featured
a young woman. Her parents were both drug addicts, and her mother had AIDS and
was partially blind. They lived in squalor in a shabby, broken-down apartment until
she was in her early teens.
Then, her mother died, and her father moved into a shelter and also developed
AIDS. The young woman didn't follow him, anq she lived on the streets for
a few years. She survived by eating out of garbage cans.
While she was on the streets, she decided she was going to change her life. She
registered for high school, and she finished in two years. All the while, she was still
homeless, and she studied in stairwells and buildings she was able to
get into.
After she graduated, she applied to Harvard University and was accepted. But
she didn't have the money to pay for it. At the time, The New York Times was offering
a scholarship contest, in which contestants had to write about what they had to over
come. She won the scholarship. On top of that, The New York Times gave her a job
while she waited to begin at Harvard in the fall of 2000.
This young woman knew how to stay motivated, in spite of numerous
obstacles. She was persistent and driven, and she changed her life.
Ask yourself:
Where are you right now? Do people envy you? Or do you envy others?
Do people want to be like you? Or do you wish you were living other people's lives?
Are people in awe of you? Do they wonder at your success, accomplishments, and
amazingly good fortune? Or is it the other way around?
When you're DRIVEN like the people who have changed their lives and reached the pinnacle

Because when you know how to stay motivated, you will reach the top -guaranteed.

## Overcoming the seeds of failure

The tenth of 12 children, Ziglar's father died when he was only five
years old. Shortly afterward, his baby sister died. It was during the heart
of the Great Depression in Yazoo City, Mississippi, and six of the 12 children
were too young to work.
They survived because they had five milk cows and a large garden in
which they grew the food they needed to eat and earn a living.
After several difficult years, Ziglar left to serve in World War 2. When
he returned, he married and went to college at the University of South
Carolina. He supported the marriage by selling sandwiches in the dormito
ries at night. Although business was great during school, it died during the
summer. Zig needed a more reliable source of income.
It was at that time that he got involved in sales. He landed a job selling cookware, and for
two and a half years, he barely survived.
There were times when he literally had to make a sale to buy SO cents worth of gasoline so
he could make his next sales call. His electricity was cut off. His telephone was disconnected.
Half the time, he worried about having enough money to put food on the table.
On many nights, he would go to bed and say·to himself, "I'm going to knock 'em dead
tomorrow!" But it's one thing to say that in the comfort of your own bed, and quite another to
do it in the real world.
He was a miserable performer, a miserable producer, and a miserable human being. He
wasn't happy with what he was doing. He tried to get other jobs, but he was on a major losing
streak, and he couldn't get hired anywhere else.

## Turning failure into massive success

Finally, after two and a half years of failure, he attended a sales training meeting offered by
his company. Mr. P.C. Merrill was the teacher, and everyone respected him highly because of his
position and accomplishments. After the training was over, Mr. Merrill told Ziglar to stay behind
so he could talk to him.
Once the others had left the training room, Mr. Merrill said, "You know, Zig, I've watched
you for the last two and a half years, and I have never seen such a waste."
Zig said, "Mr. Merrill, what do you mean?"
He said, "Well Zig, I believe you could be a great one. I believe you could be a national
champion. I believe you could go to the very top of this company. And I believe you could even
become an executive in this company if you'd just be more disciplined and if you really recognized
your own ability."
Now, one of the lessons Zig teaches today is that you always perform in a manner that's
consistent with your self-image.
And ever since his first day of work, he had tried to be disciplined. But he never succeeded
because he had a poor self-image. He thought of himself as a little guy from a little town who
was going to struggle all his life and never amount to anything. So he had ignored the advice
until then.
Mr. Merrill told Zig he thought he could be a national champion. Zig believed him, because
Mr. Merrill's entire career had been built on honesty, character, and integrity, and Zig looked up
to him and trusted him.
