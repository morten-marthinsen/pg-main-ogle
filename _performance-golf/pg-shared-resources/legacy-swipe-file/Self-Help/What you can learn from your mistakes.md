# Y ou have probably failed some

time in your career. The only way
to avoid failure is never to strive
for success. But you can learn from fail
ure, figure out what went wrong and cor
rect it. You have the power to change.
Scrutiny of defeat is critical. You have
to confront failure to avoid repeating it.
Based on interviews with people who
survived major career defeats, here are
the six most common reasons for failure.
1. Lack of social skills. Many people
who fail talk of "office politics"" doing
them in, but the politics may be nothing
more than normal interactions among
people. If you have trouble with "office
politics," you may really be having trou
ble dealing with people. You can have
great academic intelligence and still lack
social intelligence-the ability to be a
good listener, to be sensitive toward oth
ers, to give and take criticism well. Peo
ple with high social intelligence admit
their mistakes, take their share of blame
and move on. They know how to build
team support.
If people don't like you, they may help
you fail. One day at an airport, a
businessman was yelling at a porter
about his handling of the luggage. The
more abusive the businessman became,
the calmer the porter seemed. After the
businessman left, the porter said, "That
man's going to Miami, but his bags
they're going to Kalamazoo." Co
workers-even subordinates-if poorly
treated, can do you in.
On the other hand, you can get away
with serious mistakes if you are socially
intelligent. This is why many mediocre

upheavals. Sensitive in their dealings
with others, they are well liked; when
they make mistakes, their supporters
usually help them recover.
2. Wrong fit. You may not have failed
at all. You may simply be suffering a
case of mismatch. Success requires
fitting your abilities, interests, style and
values with your work.
David Brown, a successful movie pro
ducer, was fired from three jobs before
he figured out that corporate life was not
for him. He was fired from Twentieth
Century Fox, New American Library,
then Twentieth Century Fox again.
Brown began to examine his working
behavior. The way he operated in corpo
rations-being outspoken, risk-oriented,
eager to move on his own instincts
was more the style of an owner than an
employee.
Analysis of failure made Brown and
former Fox president, Richard D.
Zanuck, go out on their own and pro
duce The Sting, Jaws, The Verdict,
and Cocoon. Brown wasn't a failed
corporate executive; he was a hidden
entrepreneur.
3. Absence of commitment. One law
yer we interviewed cushions himself
against failure by never really trying. If
he doesn't put himself on the line, he can
always tell himself, I didn't really care
about that so much anyway.
After graduating from a.prestigious
law school, he joined a large firm out
west, hoping to specialize in the enter
taimnent department. Somehow it never
happened. He moved east and joined a
corporate law firm. Six months later he
was asked to leave because he lacked
motivation. "I didn't like the firm
anyway," he says. Noncommitters try to
prevent failure by not involving them
selves emotionally. Of course, what
they 're doing by their halfhearted actions
is increasing the likelihood of their
downfall. To be committed, to be
successful at anything-you have to
believe you can do it. Employers search
for this as much as any other job
qualification.
4. Too scattered a focus. Some people
do so many things that they end up doing
none of them well. One real-estate entre
preneur in our study reached the point
that he could no longer remember how
many deals he was involved with. "It
was exciting," he recalls. Then one day
the bank informed him he was overex
tended and his credit was ended. He
blamed the banks, the economy, his
staff. Finally, he says, "I realized I'd
gone too far, too fast." By trying to do
everything, he had lost focus and failed
to set priorities. The answer was to
refocus, to sort out what he did best. He
went through several lean years, but
gradually rebuilt. Today he is once again
a successful businessman - with a clearer
sense of his limits.
Recognizing your limitations, estab
lishing priorities and organizing your
ventures are essential to success.
5. Hidden barriers. Many times, age
ism, sexism and racism are used as the
excuse for failure, but many more times
they are the real reason.
Take the case of a New York sculptor
whose work had been shown for years at
a prestigious gallery that went out of
business. A woman in her40s, the sculp
tor found that no other gallery wanted
her work. Finally, a dealer explained the
problem: "You 're too old."
Galleries wanted either new artists
who could be "discovered" by critics or
established stars who brought top prices.
She was middle-aged and middle-priced.
She did not like what she heard, but she
listened. She stopped going to estab
lished art dealers and began to market
her own work-extremely successfully.
Hidden barriers are hard to fight.
More and more people, like this sculp
tor, when confronted with such obsta
cles are going into their own businesses.
Hanging on to the status quo, even in
failure, can be relatively comfortable.
You have to force yourself to explore
options-based on a truthful analysis of
your situation - to regain control of
your life and your future.
6. Bad luck. Sometimes things happen
that you just can't help. One day you
learn that top-management upheavals
have occurred and a stranger is now your
boss. This stranger wants his or her own
team. Through no fault of your own,
you are fired.
What can you do? First, don't blame
yourself if it happens. Second, remem
ber that you always have choices, even
if some aren" t apparent.
Consider Nick and Liz Thomas. After
leaving the Air Force, Nick went into the
insurance business in Philadelphia.
When a long-promised promotion didn't
materialize, he quit. His next job was
with a smaller insurance company. After
nine years he was fired. Then another
job failed. "Here I was, 47, under enor
mous financial pressure and no place to
go," he says. "I felt I was a failure."
But Liz said she kept hearing three
words from Nick's mother-who had
died the year before. The three words
were "Make the mustard." Liz's family
had a recipe from Russia, and the
Thomases made mustard for friends. At
first Nick thought his wife was mad. On
the other hand, at least this was a chal
lenging idea. He called on the local
cheese shop. The owner tasted the mus
tard and ordered Nick and Liz's entire
inventory.
After the Thomases built up their busi
ness locally, they decided to tackle
Zabar's in New York. For three months
Nick pestered Murray Klein, who
controls Zabar's empire. Finally, Klein
agreed to an in-store tasting. "You'll
have till two o'clock," he said.
Liz Thomas's voice floated over the
crowd as she hawked the mustard. In
five hours 30 cases disappeared. At
exactly two o'clock, the floor manager
came over and announced, "Mr. Klein
said I should make room on the shelves."
Chance, accident, a hunch or a
voice-who's to say where ideas come
from? All of us can listen to our inner
voices. Somewhere an idea may be
lurking.
Why do smart people fail? They can
fail for many reasons. But failure is not
the point-the best of people experience
that. It is learning from failure that is
special. The distinguishing characteristic
about really smart people? They learn.
