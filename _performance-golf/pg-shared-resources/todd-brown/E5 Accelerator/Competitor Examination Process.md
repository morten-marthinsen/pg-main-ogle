# Competitor Examination Process

This lesson covers the competitor examination process—the second part of the examination triad. We always start with prospects, then examine competitors, and only after both do we examine your product.

## The Examination Triad

1. **Prospects** (covered previously)
2. **Competitors** (this lesson)
3. **Product** (comes after competitors)

---

## Overview: What We're Looking For

The competitor examination is much simpler and quicker than the prospect examination. There are three core things to examine:

### 1. What Are They Promising?
- The transformation
- The result
- The "now and after" grid
- What outcome are they presenting?

### 2. What Are They Offering?
- Their offers
- Deliverables
- Price
- Terms
- Guarantee
- Bonuses

### 3. How Are They Positioning Their Mechanism?
- Are they talking about the unique way their product or service works to deliver the promise?
- What method, system, or framework are they presenting?

---

## Why We Examine Competitors

We use competitor information in two different ways:

### For Promise and Mechanism
1. **See if there's a promise we should model** from competitors
2. **Know what NOT to do** regarding mechanism positioning—so we know what not to say, what not to model
3. **Determine where to differentiate**—through promise or through mechanism

### For Offer Development
We look at their offer so we can put together a **SIN offer**—a **Superior, Irresistible, No-brainer** offer. To create a superior offer, we need to know what competitors are offering.

---

## Who Is Considered a Competitor?

**A competitor is anyone claiming to offer a solution with the same results to the problem you're addressing.**

You're not just competing with people selling the same product or service—you're competing with anyone promising the same outcome or result.

### Example: Chiropractor for Low Back Pain

A chiropractor who works with people with low back pain is competing with:
- Other chiropractors
- Massage therapy
- Physical therapy
- In-home treatments
- Inversion tables
- Creams
- Medication

**Why?** Because people buy products and services for the result, not the product itself. Anyone promising to alleviate the pain associated with the same problem you're addressing is a competitor.

---

## How to Identify Competitors

Identify your **top 5-7 competitors** that are using **direct response marketing**. We're not interested in analyzing competitors using brand/institutional advertising or those not making offers.

### Where to Search

**Google:**
- Search for phrases your prospects might use when looking for solutions
- Click on ads and visit websites
- Look for direct response characteristics: headlines, offers, calls to action, copy-driven presentations

**Social Media (Facebook, etc.):**
- Search for terms related to your market
- Search identification terms (e.g., "dentists," "dental marketing," "growing a dental practice")
- Look for direct response marketing

**YouTube:**
- Search relevant terms
- Click on videos and check the bio for links
- Visit sites to determine if they use direct response marketing

---

## What If You Can't Find Competitors?

### If you've done the legwork and found NO competitors at all:
This is a **red flag**. When no one is marketing to an audience, it could mean:
- The market doesn't spend money
- The market doesn't want what you're offering
- There may be legal issues around advertising to that audience

**Where there's opportunity, you'll find entrepreneurs and marketers.** Unless it's a brand new, completely green market, no competitors is a warning sign.

### If there are marketers but none using direct response:
This is actually a **good sign**—a ripe opportunity. You can skip this examination step since we don't analyze brand/institutional advertisers just for the sake of doing it.

### The Ideal Scenario
- **Lots of competitors = good sign** (indicates opportunity)
- **Competitors using direct response = great**
- **Competitors NOT using direct response = fantastic opportunity for you**

## Part 1: What Are They Promising?

### Questions to Answer

1. **What's the big transformation, result, or outcome they're promising?**
   - Note this for each of your 5-7 competitors

2. **What are all the sub-benefits they're claiming?**
   - Example: If the core promise is "lose up to 20 pounds in 30 days"
   - Sub-benefits might be: get healthier, gain more energy, gain more confidence

3. **What trends and common promises appear across multiple competitors?**
   - Highlight these—they indicate what resonates with the market

### Why This Matters

You don't have to differentiate based on your promise. It's very difficult to differentiate through what you're promising unless you have a completely innovative, disruptive product.

**The opportunity:** Learn from competitors. Model the promises and sub-benefits that are working. If you see something trending across multiple competitors, highlight those—you'll use them to your benefit.

---

## Part 2: How Are They Positioning Their Mechanism?

### Questions to Answer

1. **Does the competitor present any kind of mechanism?**
   - Any unique method behind how their product or service works?
   - Any unique process, framework, system, ingredient, aspect, or algorithm?

2. **Are they presenting a common mechanism?**
   - Something available in multiple products?
   - Example: A supplement claiming "the secret is 500mg of magnesium"—magnesium is available in many products

3. **Is there a name they're using for their mechanism?**
   - Note this specifically

### Why We Note This

We're noting this **to know what NOT to do**. We're going to differentiate through YOUR mechanism—how your product or service delivers the result.

To come up with a unique mechanism, we need to know what competitors are saying. The only way to know if you have a unique mechanism is to compare and contrast it with what competitors are claiming about theirs.

**Important:** Most competitors won't have a mechanism—this is a savvy strategy that most don't understand or know how to utilize.

---

## Part 3: What Are They Offering?

An offer is: **What does the prospect get, and what do they need to give to get it?**

### Elements to Examine

#### Deliverables
- What do they actually give to prospects?
- What does the prospect actually get?

#### Features
- Do they talk about features of their product?
- Facts about the product or service (small, gray, easy to hold, quick, quiet, etc.)
- For information products: videos (HD?), cheat sheets, transcripts, workbooks, exercises
- Coaching details: certified coaches? Executive team?

#### Price and Terms
- What's the price?
- How do you pay? (Terms)
  - One lump sum?
  - Payment plan?
  - Soft offer (deferred payments)?
  - Pay nothing today, balance in 30 days?

#### Bonuses and Premiums
- What are they offering?
- What topics are they addressing?
- What pain points are they hitting?

#### Guarantee / Risk Reversal
- Do they offer any guarantee?
- What are they doing to reduce or eliminate risk?
- Unconditional guarantee?
- Conditional guarantee?
- What is the specific guarantee?

### Why We Collect This Information

1. **Learn from it**—stimulate ideas for bonuses, premiums, deliverables
2. **Create a superior offer**—we can only know if our offer is superior by knowing what competitors offer

---

## Summary

The competitor examination process helps you understand:

| What to Examine | Why |
|-----------------|-----|
| **Their Promise** | Find promises to model; know where differentiation is needed |
| **Their Mechanism** | Know what NOT to do; find gaps for your unique mechanism |
| **Their Offer** | Create a superior offer; generate ideas for deliverables and bonuses |

**Remember:** We differentiate through mechanism, not promise. Understanding competitors' mechanisms tells us what to avoid so we can position yours as unique.

Now you know what to look for and why when examining competitors.
