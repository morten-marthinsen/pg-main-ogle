# 25 Core Copywriting Principles

This lesson covers 25 core copywriting principles—the foundation of effective marketing communication. At the root of everything in copywriting, you need to remain focused on the prospect. It's all about understanding what they want, their needs, concerns, fears, and emotions.

## The Foundation: Focus on the Prospect

Your copy and marketing message is not about you, it's not about your company, it's not even really about your product—it's about how all of those things help the prospect experience the life results, transformation, and impact that they want.

> "When it comes to writing great copy, it's not so much a matter of knowing how to write as it is knowing what to write."
> — Gary Halbert

This isn't about becoming a whiz-bang writer or crafting amazing prose. It's about focusing on your prospect, overcoming their situation, and proving to them how and why you can help them achieve the outcome they want.

---

## The 25 Principles

### 1. Start with Empathy for Your Prospect

This requires that you know your prospect—that you've gone through the examination process and understand what they want and what they're dealing with: their desires, emotions, feelings, and beliefs.

**Key practices:**
- Walk a mile in their shoes
- See the world through their eyes
- Understand their pain, struggle, and frustration at your core
- Before writing, put yourself into the prospect's mindset

Ask yourself:
- How is the prospect feeling right now?
- What are they thinking?
- When they interact with your marketing message, what will they feel, think, and believe?
- What will they doubt or question?
- What are they looking for?

---

### 2. Clarity Over Creativity

What's most important is that you communicate clearly. Your prospect needs to understand what you are saying.

**Best practices:**
- Use simple, everyday language
- Have a conversation with somebody
- Use words and phrases you would use in conversation
- Don't try to come up with whiz-bang copy or slick sales presentations
- Be clear, be simple, talk to somebody

---

### 3. Choose the Appropriate Tone for the Subject Matter

Recognize that you're not dealing with a blank canvas. Depending on your topic, you may be talking to somebody who is:
- Somber, disappointed, anxious, or upset
- Excited, elated, pumped, and focused on the positive

**Example:** Don't start a marketing campaign all excited and positive if you're talking to somebody who just received terrible news about a health condition.

---

### 4. Activate the Appropriate Dominant Emotion

Based on the tone, determine what core emotion you are trying to tap into. Good marketing, effective marketing, moves people emotionally—it triggers an emotional response because people are driven by emotions when it comes to actions.

Your task: Be clear on what is that core emotion or "dominant resident emotion" (as Clayton Makepeace used to say).

---

### 5. Write Like You Talk

Regardless of whether you're using a long-form sales letter, you want to write like you talk. What we're trying to capture is the spoken word, not the written word.

**Characteristics:**
- It should read like dialogue
- It should sound like you talk
- When people read, the majority hear the words in their mind

**Practical tips:**
- When done writing, read it out loud
- Where it's choppy or doesn't read like you'd talk, edit it
- Talk out loud as you're writing it
- Keep it conversational—you're having a conversation, not giving a formal presentation

---

### 6. Speak to One Person at a Time

Even though potentially thousands, hundreds of thousands, or millions of people will interact with your marketing campaign, speak to one person at a time.

**How to apply:**
- Picture the ideal prospect in your mind
- If you have an existing business, picture the ideal client, customer, student, or subscriber
- Have one person and talk to that one person
- You're having one conversation at a time

---

### 7. Focus on Your Prospect, Not Yourself

Use words like:
- **You, your, yours, you will** (talking about the prospect)

Instead of:
- I, me, mine, we (talking about yourself)

**Example transformation:**
- ❌ "I'm going to teach you XYZ"
- ✅ "You'll learn XYZ"

The prospect doesn't care about you—they care about themselves, their situation, and what this means for them and their life.

---

### 8. Use Your Prospect's Language and Familiar Words

Use the terms and vocabulary that your prospect would use, regardless of whether it's not the way you would say it.

**Guidelines:**
- If they wouldn't use the word "profit" but would use "income," use "income"
- Don't use fancy $10 words
- Use the simplest, clearest words possible
- A confused prospect does not remain engaged and certainly does not buy

---

### 9. Leverage Curiosity

Curiosity (sometimes called "the knowledge gap") is one of the most powerful triggers in marketing.

**How to create curiosity:**
- Share snippets and tease information that is coming
- Create info gaps or knowledge gaps in the prospect's mind that you fill in later
- Promise to reveal something later in your campaign
- Talk about things without giving the complete picture

When you make your prospect curious, they remain engaged because the brain wants completion—they want to know, they want the complete picture.

---

### 10. Dramatize the Impact of What You're Presenting

The impact of what you're presenting should feel:
- Monumental
- Like a game changer
- Life-transforming
- The biggest thing they can learn
- The most valuable thing they engage with this year, possibly ever
- Capable of radically altering their current situation

**Important:** This isn't about embellishing, lying, or stretching the truth. It's about how dramatically you present the truth.

**Example (lawn care):**
Instead of talking about "having a greener lawn," talk about "having pride of ownership, one of the most impressive-looking homes in your entire neighborhood, homes that get you attention, that change your status in the community, that make people want to know your secret."

---

### 11. Show, Don't Tell

This is about painting pictures. When talking about benefits, the payoff, or the transformation, don't just tell them—paint a picture, show them what it will look like in their mind.

**Functional (Telling) vs. Dimensionalized (Showing):**

| Telling | Showing |
|---------|---------|
| "You'll make more money" | "Every Thursday at 3 p.m., you'll get a notification from your bank letting you know another $1,500 has been deposited" |
| "Bill was mad" | "Bill came home, slammed the door, threw his briefcase down, and swatted the glass off the counter" |
| "It was hot outside" | "Within three seconds, I was soaked in sweat with beads running down my temple" |

---

### 12. Include Facts Within Narrative

When presenting facts or proof points, incorporate them into a story rather than stating them plainly.

**Why:** People are trained to pay attention to stories. People remember stories better than facts.

**Example:**
Instead of: "One out of five people who smoke end up overweight"

Try: "Dr. Smith, who lost a parent to lung cancer, decided to run a study on people who smoke and obesity. He recruited people from Arkansas, and when the study was done, Dr. Smith found that one out of every five people who smoke ended up being overweight within..."

---

### 13. Use Repetition (Without Being Repetitious)

Use repetition to communicate over and over the core promise, the core payoff, what's on the line for your prospect. But don't be repetitious—don't hit that button with the same exact words, phrases, and sentence structure.

**Repetitious (Bad):**
- "You'll lose weight every week"
- "Every week you're gonna lose weight"
- "Once a week you're going to lose weight"

**Repetition (Good):**
- "Every week you're gonna lose weight"
- "Four times a month you're gonna jump on the scale and be down body fat"
- "Regularly, every time you measure yourself, you're gonna see fat is coming off"

Same core promise, different ways of saying it.

---

### 14. Good Copy is Verb-Driven

Good marketing messages are verb-driven. Novice marketers fill their copy with adjectives and adverbs (descriptors), which feels hyperbolic and exaggerated.

**Adjective-heavy (Weak):**
"The explosive, steroid-like, incredible way to build muscle"

**Verb-driven (Strong):**
- You'll shed body fat
- You'll drop body fat
- You'll burn body fat
- You'll melt body fat

**Choose verbs that paint a picture:**
- Instead of "Bob stood behind the counter" → "Bob lurched behind the counter" or "Bob hovered behind the counter"

**Replace adverb + weak verb with a strong verb:**
- Instead of "Bob leisurely walked down the street" → "Bob strolled down the street"

---

### 15. Use Descriptors That Add Information

When you do use an adjective or descriptor, make sure it gives the prospect more information.

**Adds no information:**
- "The incredible method"
- "The awesome method"
- "The fantastic method"

**Adds information:**
- "The three-step method"
- "The beginner-friendly method"
- "The one-step method"
- "The two-click method"

---

### 16. Use "Reason Why" Copy

Use "reason why" in two contexts:

**1. To support claims:**
If you make a claim and don't have another proof point (stat, study, credible authority), give the reason why.
- "This works quickly. The reason why is because..."
- "This is easy to use. The reason why is because..."
- "Anybody can do this regardless of experience. The reason why is because..."

**2. To add credibility to offers:**
If you give any kind of discount or special deal, give the reason why.
- If someone qualifies for something, give a reason why
- If the offer is exclusive, explain why you're only inviting a certain number of people

---

### 17. Speak About Benefits in Present or Future Tense

Don't talk about benefits as if they were experienced in the past. People value what is working now and what will work in the future more than what worked in the past.

**Instead of:** | **Use:**
----------------|----------
What they did | What they're doing
What they used | What they're using
What worked | What's working
What happened | What's happening
What trended | What's trending

---

### 18. Pluralize the Verb When Talking About the Solution

This principle conveys that the product does the work, not the prospect.

**Classic example:** TDK audio cassettes
- Original: "Put music in your life" (you do the work)
- Typo that increased response 400%: "Puts music in your life" (the product does the work)

**More examples:**

| Prospect does the work | Product does the work |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| "Take this pill and strip off 10 pounds" | "Take this pill which strips off 10 pounds" |
| "Use this and grow more grass" | "This grows more grass for you" |
| "Get a lush lawn" | "This gives you a lusher lawn" |

This takes the onus off the prospect and puts it onto the system, mechanism, or product.

---

### 19. Use Contrast

Contrast is enormously valuable in marketing. Use it to show:

**1. Now State vs. After State:**
Contrast what they're dealing with now (problems, frustrations, pain, absence of pleasure) with what life can be like after (no pain, enjoying the pleasure).

**2. Their Approach vs. Your Approach:**
"Unlike the typical method which requires you to do X, Y, Z, when you use [your mechanism], you don't have to... you get to..."

Contrast makes things pop and stand out. Without contrast, messages become monotone.

---

### 20. Be Concrete and Specific

Good marketing copy, promises, and benefits are all concrete and specific. Vague, general statements don't persuade.

**Why specificity matters:**
- Concrete, specific language paints vivid, clear pictures
- General, vague language makes it difficult for prospects to picture in their mind's eye
- When prospects can't picture something, they don't have the same visceral emotional response

Use language that allows them to sense it—see it, hear it, feel it, smell it, or taste it.

---

### 21. Repeat Core Promises (Without Being Repetitious)

Continue to bring up your core promise and keep your prospect focused on the outcome. (This reinforces Principle #13)

---

### 22. Open and Close Loops

Open and closed loops are cliffhangers that leverage curiosity and knowledge gaps.

**How news programs do it:**
"Famous celebrity confesses critical crime—more about that in a few minutes. Right now, let's go to the weather."

**How to apply:**
- Promise to reveal something
- Move on to something else
- Tease and promise something new
- Close the original loop
- Continue opening and closing loops throughout

This keeps people engaged, reading, and watching. Never take their engagement for granted.

---

### 23. Share Emotional Benefits Along with Functional Benefits

It's the emotional payoff that people are really buying—the feelings, not just the functional benefit.

**Examples:**

| Functional Benefit | Emotional Benefit |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Lose weight | Feel more confident, feel more attractive |
| Make more money | Feel more successful, feel like a better provider |
| Buy a luxury watch | Feel successful, feel worthy, feel like you've made it |
| Home security system | Feel secure, feel like a better provider |

People buy out of emotion and then rationalize with logic.

---

### 24. Share Details in Stories (Avoid the "Bald Story")

A "bald story" is absent of detail and difficult to picture in your mind's eye.

**Why details matter:**
- Details make images vivid
- Details draw people in
- Good books allow you to see characters, how they walk, how they look

**What to include:**
- Description of clothes, brands
- Whether things are wrinkled or finely pressed
- Specific sensory details

**Balance:** Don't give every last minute detail, but detail is what makes a story compelling.

---

### 25. Address and Overcome All Possible Prospect Objections

All the reasons they may have for not buying, all doubts, questions, and possible objections need to be addressed in your marketing.

**The difference from face-to-face selling:**
In person, you can narrow down and address specific objections. In marketing, you don't have that luxury—you need to be cognizant of all possible objections.

**What to address:**
- Reasons, doubts, questions, and objections any prospect could have
- If you make a claim they might doubt, address it head-on
- Don't ignore it, sweep it under the carpet, or pretend it doesn't exist

**Example:**
"I know it sounds hard to believe, but in a minute, I'm going to show you an example that will blow you away."

**Best approach:** The best time to deal with and overcome an objection is before it ever comes up.

Even if you've covered everything else, one outstanding objection or concern can stop the sale:
- "I don't know if this will work for my situation"
- "I don't know if this is appropriate for my business"
- "I don't know if this will work for me because I have a thyroid condition"

You must address and overcome all possible questions, objections, and concerns so that you leave no gaps.

---

## Summary

These 25 core copywriting principles form the foundation of effective marketing communication. Remember: everything stems from knowing and understanding your prospect. Focus on benefits, paint vivid pictures, maintain clarity, and always address what matters most to the person on the other side of your marketing message.
