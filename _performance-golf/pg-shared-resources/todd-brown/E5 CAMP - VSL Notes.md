## **Lesson 2: Conducting the Competitor Analysis**

The point of this analysis is NOT to uncover your competitors’ traffic sources or funnel steps, but rather to analyze your competitors from a pure marketing perspective: How they’re positioning their product, presenting their product, what exactly they’re offering, etc. 

Looking for 2 things at this stage: 

1 Looking for what your competitors are doing so you know what NOT to do (positioning statements, claims, benefits, mechanisms, etc.). 

a. You must to appear DIFFERENT from all competitors. You want to be able to come at your market from a different angle, with a different hook, different promise, etc. 

2 Looking for gaps in the marketplace. (Where your competitors are missing the mark, what they’re not touching on, not offering, etc. that you can offer.) 

11-Point Competitor Analysis 

● Use with the top 5-7 direct response competitors, measured by popularity in the marketplace. If there aren’t that many, use as many as there are. If there aren’t any at all, skip this analysis. Include not just direct competitors, but anyone your prospects could see as a potential alternative to your product or service. (Other solutions that can provide the end result they want.) 

● Not all competitors will have everything below. If they don’t, just note that it doesn’t exist. Each thing they’re missing makes your job that much easier. 

1 Hook/Angle 

a. This is what is being used to get the market’s attention. 

2 Primary Marketing Promise 

a. This can usually be found in the headline. If not, look for the primary benefit statement. 

3 Delivery Mechanism  
a. What is the piece/part/component/aspect of your competitor’s product or service that is responsible for delivering the promise? 

b. Many competitors may not focus on this. 

c. Pretend your competitor is saying, “The reason you will get the results we promise is because .” Whatever is in the blank is the mechanism. 

4 Unique Selling Proposition (USP) 

a. Don’t try to come up with their USP on your own; Look for *their* claim about what makes their product or service unique. 

5 General Marketing Claims 

a. Are they claiming that it works quickly? That it’s easy to use? It’s cheap? Elite? Comes with massive support? Etc. 

6 Proof Points 

a. What proof are they making about their claims? About their primary marketing promise? 

b. Is their only proof their testimonials? Do they have case studies? Statistics? Screen shots? 

7 Benefit Statements 

a. Benefit statements are what the marketing claims mean for the prospect. 8 Deliverables/Features 

a. What comes with their offer? (For an information marketer, it could be videos, audios, PDFs, cheat sheets, worksheets, action guide, software, DVDs, CDs, etc.) 

9 Price & Terms 

a. Terms: Hard offer or soft offer? (Pay now and get the product now, get the product now and pay later?) Payment plan? Monthly? Lifetime? 

10 Bonuses/Premiums 

a. What are they? How many? Are they digital or physical? Where is the value in the premiums? (What problems are the premiums addressing? Additional problems? Same core problem as the offer? Are they used to overcome 

objections?) 

11 Risk Reversal 

a. Do they offer a guarantee, and if so, what is it? (Conditional, unconditional, 2-part guarantee, 30-day guarantee, etc.) 

## **Lesson 3: Conducting the Product Analysis**

This is an analysis of the product or service you will be offering at the end of your marketing funnel. It will help find points of differentiation between your product or service and your competitors’. 

10-Point Product Analysis 

1 Primary Promise  
a. What is it in one sentence? 

b. What is the prospect going to experience? What benefit, outcome, result, transformation, change in their life are you promising them they’re going to experience? 

c. This is not to develop the perfect promise. This is just an analysis. Don’t think about it or try to make it better. Just write it down. 

2 Proof Points 

a. What can you present to prospects to prove that you can fulfill on your promise? 3 Credibility Factors 

a. What is it that is credible about you, your company, the information you’re providing, the proof points you’re offering, etc.? 

4 Unique Selling Proposition 

5 Delivery Mechanism 

a. The Unique Mechanism can be developed later. For now, just note if there is a clear mechanism that delivers the promise. 

6 Track Record 

a. How has your product or service performed in the past? 

7 Performance Metrics 

a. Any information that shows it works 9 out of 10 times, works on certain headaches, with certain websites, etc. 

8 Interest Points 

a. What is it about your product or service that is intellectually interesting? Is there a story behind the product or service, behind its development, or behind the creator? Is there something interesting about a user of the product or service? About the delivery? 

b. Look for points of potential interest to your market. Don’t try to create any right now; this is just an analysis. 

9 Features 

a. Soft grip, never needs to be refilled, runs on solar energy, etc. 

10 Benefits 

a. List the functional benefit, dimensionalized benefit, and emotional benefit for each feature. Then rank them in order of importance to your prospect based off the emotional benefits. (This is why it’s so important to know what your prospects truly want and desire) 

The 3 types of benefits 

(Each one is a level deeper than the last.) 

● Functional Benefit 

○ The typical first superficial benefit 

○ Example: A mechanical pencil. The soft, spongy grip is a feature. The functional benefit is that it’s easier to hold. 

● Dimensionalized Benefit  
○ What the functional benefit actually looks like in the real life of a prospect. How that benefit plays out. 

○ Paint the picture of the benefit playing out in their life. You are the director of the movie that plays in your prospect’s mind as it relates to what their experience will be like after they get your product. 

○ You can look at this type of benefit as transformational future pacing mixed with storytelling. 

○ Example: If you were marketing to writers, you could say, “You can write for hours everyday with no pain in your hand or wrist.” 

● Emotional Benefit 

○ The emotional impact of the dimensionalized benefit. How does the 

dimensionalized benefit make them feel? 

○ Example: “Feel great knowing that you can get up everyday and write to your heart’s content without ever having to worry about being in pain again.”

## **Lesson 4: Conducting the Promise Exposure Spectrum Analysis**

What promises have your prospects already heard from your competitors? You need to know this because differentiation is critical. In fact, **the single biggest reason for marketing failure is an immature marketing message**. (Meaning, a message that’s designed for prospects that are lower on the promise exposure spectrum. In other words, something they’ve already seen.)  
NOTE: The Promise Exposure Spectrum is a replacement for the market sophistication model (5 stages of awareness). It’s an easier way to understand it. 

3-Step Promise Exposure Spectrum (P.E.S.) Analysis 

1 Determine the Primary Promise of your marketing funnel. 

a. What is your prospect going to experience? What benefit, outcome, result, transformation, change in their life are you promising they will experience? 2 Review the primary promises of each of your top competitors (from the competitor analysis in module 1). 

a. You’re looking for common themes, trends, common promises, as well as unique promises. 

3 Determine what stage your prospects are at on the P.E.S. 

a. You will determine this based on your primary promise and the promises that have already been made by your competitors. (Step 2, above.) 

The 5 Stages of the Promise Exposure Spectrum 

1 Promise 

a. You are at this stage if your prospects have never been exposed to your promise. b. Example: “Take this pill and you’ll lose weight.” 

2 Promise Expanded 

a. You are at this stage if your promise has already been presented to prospects but has not been expanded on by competitors. 

b. Example: “Take this pill and you’ll lose 20 pounds.” “Take this pill and lose 20 pounds and 4 inches around your waist.” 

c. At some point, the promise cannot be expanded anymore either because it’s not true or because it’s no longer believable or credible. 

3 Promise  Unique Mechanism 

a. You are at this stage if prospects have heard your promise and the promise has been expanded but has never been presented with your mechanism. 

b. The Unique Mechanism is the reason your promise works – the unique piece of your product or service that is the engine behind delivering the promise. 

c. The Unique Mechanism gives your prospects HOPE because when they see it, it’s new to them, so they feel that maybe this time they’ll finally experience the transformation, where in the past they haven’t. 

d. Example: Fat burner pill that works because it has garcinia cambogia in it. e. Unique Mechanisms wear out as other companies copy them. For example, the fat burner pill that contained garcinia cambogia. At first, it was the only pill to contain that ingredient. But then competitors saw the success of that pill and came out with their own pill that contained garcinia cambogia. 

f. When a Unique Mechanism is copied, response rates decline. 

4 Promise  Expanded Unique Mechanism  
a. You are at this stage if your promise and mechanism have been presented but has not been expanded on. 

b. Example: Instead of just garcinia cambogia, it’s now “pharmaceutical grade garcinia cambogia”, or “freeze dried garcinia cambogia”, or garcinia cambogia from the rainforest, or garcinia cambogia along with a digestive enzyme. 5 Prospect 

a. You are at this stage if both your promise and mechanism have been fully expanded. 

b. At this stage, the marketing message ceases to be about the product at all and focuses 100% on the prospect. 

c. Example: “Are you sick and tired of not losing weight even though you’ve been dieting, exercising, and taking pills?” 

A stage 3 marketing message will work for markets in stages 1, 2, and 3 but it will not work for markets in stages 4 or 5 So why not just start with a message at a higher stage? Because creating a marketing message becomes progressively more difficult at each stage. That’s why you should go with the lowest appropriate level on the P.E.S.. 

When a mechanism has been expanded, you either have to move on to the next stage or find or create a new unique mechanism. 

## **Lesson 5: Conducting the Prospect Awareness Pyramid Analysis**

This analysis tells you what your prospects already know and think; What they’re already aware of as it relates to the problem your product or service solves, the solutions, the benefits of one solution over another, you, your product or service, and your offer. 

You must tailor your marketing message to your prospect’s awareness level. 

Every market has a segment within each of the 5 levels. So the question is not, “Which level of awareness is my market at?”, but rather, “Which level of awareness am I going to design my marketing funnel for?” 

The 5 Prospect Awareness Levels 

1 Completely Unaware. No awareness of the problem or the need for a solution. 2 Problem Aware. Awareness only of the problem. 

a. Example: The prospect has back pain but doesn’t know about any solutions. 3 Solution Aware. An awareness of the problem and the various solutions available. a. Example: The prospect knows that possible solutions to back pain include inversion tables, chiropractic, deep tissue massages, etc.  
4 Product Aware. Awareness of the problem and the type of solution they want. a. At this point, the prospect has decided on the TYPE of solution they want but hasn’t decided which company to buy from. 

b. Example: They have back pain, they’ve explored possible solutions, and they decided to go to a chiropractor. They just haven’t decided which one. 

5 Most Aware. Awareness of what you offer and how it meets their needs. (All about “the deal”.) 

a. At this point, the prospect is aware of YOU and YOUR PRODUCT OR SERVICE. 

Consider what phrases someone at each awareness level would be searching for on Google. 1 Not searching for anything related to the problem or solution 

2 “back pain relief” – They’re looking to find out what their options are. 

3 “chiropractic vs massage for lower back pain” – They’re exploring the different options they have at their disposal to find out which is the most appropriate for their needs. 4 “best chiropractor in their city” – Now they’re narrowing the search to decide which solution they want to go with. 

5 Anything related to finding the best offer for the solution they want. 

When designing a marketing funnel for any awareness level, it only works with that level or above (more aware). The higher on the pyramid you go (towards “most aware”), the smaller the universe becomes. 

So why not always design funnels for “completely unaware” prospects? You could, but be aware that the lower on the pyramid you go (towards “completely unaware”), the harder it is to convert prospects. The higher on the pyramid, the easier it becomes to convert prospects. 

Also, the lower the awareness level, the longer the funnel will be. (Because you have to spend more time educating the prospect.) Example: Elderly golfers who are unaware of a problem will have to first be educated about their susceptibility to back pain. Then, once they’re problem aware, they will need to be educated about the various solutions and what makes chiropractic the best. Then why you are the best chiropractor. 

Basically, the lower the awareness level, the bigger the opportunity, but the harder it is to make it work. 

## **Lesson 6: Creating the Three Dimensional Prospect Psyche Profile**

This lesson is about dimensionalizing what your prospect is experiencing on a day-to-day basis… experiencing the problem your prospect has from his or her perspective… entering the conversation already going on in their head.  
Put yourself in your prospect’s shoes and really feel what they’re going through. (This is not an exact science, so don’t worry about getting it 100% right.) You don’t have to get this information from your prospects – you just imagine being them and how they would feel. 

3D Prospect Profile (Empathy Map) 

1 What are your prospects SEEING? 

a. From others suffering with the problem? 

b. From others succeeding with a solution? 

c. From experts (online articles, videos), professionals, vendors, family, friends, co-workers? 

d. On popular websites, social media, television, movies, periodicals? 

e. In advertisements, marketing campaigns? 

f. In their life that they would like to see more of? Less of? (Consequences of the problem as they appear day-to-day. For example, weeds in the garden, pants getting tighter.) 

2 What are your prospects HEARING? 

a. From experts, professionals, vendors? 

b. From family, friends, co-workers, social media? 

c. About the problem/situation? 

d. About the solutions available? 

e. About their fears? Desires? (That they’re normal, that they’re never going to happen, etc.) 

f. About the likelihood of success, failure? 

g. About their future, as it relates to their situation? 

3 What are your prospects SAYING? (Similar to John Carlton’s Bar Conversation) a. To experts, professionals, vendors? 

b. To family, friends, co-workers, social media? 

c. About the problem/situation? 

d. About the solutions available? 

e. About their fears? Desires? 

f. About the likelihood of success, failure? 

g. About their future? 

4 What are your prospects THINKING? 

a. About what they’re hearing from experts, professionals, vendors, family, friends, co-workers, social media? 

b. About the problem/situation? 

c. About the solutions available? 

d. About the likelihood of success, failure? 

e. About their fears? Desires? 

f. About their future? 

g. What are they thinking that’s correct? Incorrect?  
5 What are your prospects FEELING? 

a. About what they’re hearing from experts, professionals, vendors, family, friends, co-workers, social media? 

b. About the problem/situation? 

c. About the solutions available? 

d. About the likelihood of success, failure? 

e. About their fears? Desires? 

f. About their future? 

6 What are your prospects DOING? 

(This is a direct result of everything above.) 

a. What are they doing right now to address their problem? (Day to day, month to month.) 

b. What are they NOT doing right now to address their problem? 

c. What are they doing correctly? Incorrectly? 

d. What are they doing that’s working? Not working? 

e. What are they doing that they would like to do more of? Less of? (Shopping for smaller clothes, going out on dates, etc.)

## **Lesson 8: Engineering the Perfect       Customer-Generating Product**

This step is for information marketers and information publishers.

Strategic Marketing-Based Product Creation 

For the A-Player marketer, there is NO separation between content and marketing. Content is always created for a marketing purpose. For example, every blog post should move a prospect closer to buying. One piece of content should always link to the next piece of content. One product should always link to the next product. 

NOTE: This does NOT mean you should leave content out. You still need to deliver what you promised and more. 

Front-End Product Objectives 

● Fulfill on the hook and ALL product promises 

● Over-deliver value with stellar content 

○ The front end is all about customer acquisition. All the profit is in the back end. So if you don’t blow your new customers away on the front end, they won’t buy any backend products and you won’t make any money. 

● Exceed new customer expectations 

● Provide a stellar buying experience 

○ Think through the entire buying experience from start to finish, and make it great. ○ Todd is not a fan of putting people through multiple add-on offers, up-sell offers, and down-sell offers because it’s an uncomfortable experience. He doesn’t want to start a new relationship with the customer being uncomfortable and feeling like he’s trying to squeeze every last dime out of their pocket. 

● Provide a stellar consumption experience 

○ Follow up with your customers to aid in their consumption. Make it as easy for them as possible. 

● Demonstrate your expertise/positioning 

○ Make sure you’re establishing credibility in the eyes of your new customer. Establish your authority positioning. Make sure your new customer is comfortable learning from you and hearing your methodology/process/system/etc.. Don’t assume it’s enough to only do it during your marketing and selling process. 

○ Continue to demonstrate your authority and positioning throughout your entire front end product. 

● Set up the sale of your first backend offer 

**Pre-Seeding the Backend Sale** 

This is NOT blatant pitching or selling in the product. It’s about piquing curiosity, creating anticipation and excitement, planting seeds in the mind of the customer for what’s to come. ● Nonchalant comments 

○ About other products you have. About the next product you’re going to offer to them.  
○ Example: “Hi, welcome to the E5 Masterclass. I’m Todd Brown, author of  book.” 

● Alluding to a backend product 

○ “This is a technique I talk a lot about in my XYZ program. For now, what I want you to understand is this: … ” 

○ Telling a story about creating that product 

○ Reference that product 

● Referencing information provided in a backend product 

○ If the product is a $10,000 seminar, you’re also establishing authority at the same time. 

○ “There are five things I cover in-depth over the course of three hours in my XYZ program. For right now, for THIS program, I want you to understand the first two.” ● Referencing client/customer results from the backend product 

○ “This is a topic I talk about over the course of 2 days at my Big Idea Bootcamp. I’m about to give you a chunk of what I share there, and let me show you how valuable this information is. For example, one attendee, Andre Chaperon, took what he learned at the Big Idea Bootcamp and went on to launch an entirely new business that did $XMM in the first 3 months” etc. 

○ These are not random references. You bring it up when you’re talking about a topic that you expand on in a backend product. 

● Useful (but incomplete) extra information 

○ This is information that was NOT promised in the sales material. It helps the customer and also leaves them wanting more at the same time. 

When you pre-seed on top of delivering high-value content, it’s very well-received and *incredibly* effective. 

## **Lesson 9: Engineering the Perfect Offer**

The 4 Message Elements of a High-Converting Funnel 

1 The big idea 

2 The lead 

a. The first 500-800 words of your funnel, regardless of the funnel model, the pieces you use (video, audio, long-form sales letter, etc.) 

3 The marketing argument 

a. The body of your funnel 

4 The offer 

**Objectives of Each Element** 

● Intrigued by the Big Idea 

● Sucked in by the Lead  
● Convinced of the Marketing Thesis by the Marketing Argument 

● Propelled to buy from the Offer 

**Create in the following order:** 

1 Offer 

2 Marketing thesis 

3 Big idea 

4 Lead 

5 Marketing argument 

The Offer 

A fantastic offer will shoot response through the roof. A weak offer will dramatically reduce response. 

**Why create the offer first?** 

1 Because you have to know where you’re going in order to make an effective plan to get there, and… 

2 Other than the audience, the offer is the most critical element in a marketing funnel. 

**You’re selling the offer, NOT your product or service** View the offer as the product. The actual product or service is just a piece of the offer. 

11 Types of Offers 

1 Hard Offer – payment up front 

a. Hard offers make up 99.9% of the offers you see, but they are the least compelling and least effective type of offer 

2 Soft Offer – payment later 

a. Soft offers will almost always outpull a hard offer and can dramatically increase response 

3 Negative Option Offer – continuity 

4 Installment Offer – payment plan 

5 Charter Offer – early one-time discount 

6 Exclusive Offer – only for certain people 

7 Limited Offer – scarcity-driven offer 

8 Limited-Time Offer – deadline-driven offer 

9 One-Time Offer – only seen once 

10 Qualified Offer – application required 

11 Combination Offer – combining two or more of the above

Superior & Irresistible 

You want your offers to be superior to your competitors’ offers in every way. You make the offer irresistible with the way you articulate the deliverables, features, the why, and the benefits. 

When engineering your offer, be looking at your Competitor Analysis worksheet. 

Deliverable, Feature, Why 

Fantastic article: 

[http://www.makepeacetotalpackage.com/archives/make-your-products-benefits-sparkle/](http://www.makepeacetotalpackage.com/archives/make-your-products-benefits-sparkle/) 

● Deliverables: What they get 

● Features: Facts about the products or service 

● Why: Why that feature is valuable, beneficial, helpful, or advantageous to the prospect ○ This is a great place to overcome an objection 

You can come up with dozens of offers for one product, which is a way for you to be prolific as a marketer without a lot of products. 

Always strive to fulfill your prospect’s desire for instant gratification. When possible, build a deliverable into your offer that fulfills that. 

List out all the deliverables, all the features, and all the reasons why for each feature. 

**Example:** Free massage (deliverable)... with a certified massage therapist (feature)... to ensure you enjoy a quality, professional experience (why)... functional benefit, dimensionalized benefit, emotional benefit. 

**3 types of benefits example:** “So you have a soothing hour of relaxation and rejuvenation (functional benefit). From the moment you sit up after your massage, you’ll feel a deep sense of muscle warmth and loosening (dimensionalized benefit)... and you’ll feel proud of yourself for doing something healthy and positive for yourself (emotional benefit).” 

**Deliverable → Feature → Why → Functional Benefit → Dimensionalized Benefit → Emotional Benefit** 

Full example 

“You get a free massage with a certified massage therapist to ensure you enjoy a quality, professional experience. So you have a soothing hour of relaxation and rejuvenation.  
“From the moment you sit up after your massage, you’ll feel a deep sense of muscle warmth and loosening, and you’ll feel proud of yourself for doing something healthy and positive for yourself.” 

Do the above for each of your deliverables. 

Negative Selling Tactics 

● Telling the prospect what it does NOT do 

● Telling the prospect what it does NOT have 

● The takeaway 

○ Tell them who it’s NOT for. Pull it away. 

● The damaging admission 

○ Specific to the product. An admission of a non-positive. Not necessarily a flaw, but something small and trivial that the prospect could see as negative. 

○ This is a good tactic to use if you left something out of the product or offer on purpose. 

○ Example: “There’s nothing pretty about this product.” (Black letters on white text, no photos, etc.) This was okay for the info product it was used in because nobody cared what it looked like. They wanted the information, not a beautiful binder of material. 

Price and Terms 

Regardless of pricing, always make a lopsided value proposition. (Make them feel like they’re getting an incredible value.) This is not built through salesman trickery, but through engineering offer that truly does deliver incredible value. 

Present price in the following order: 

● The regular price (or full value, if there’s no discount) 

● The reason(s) for the discount 

● The offer price 

● The savings percentage 

● The savings amount 

● Price trivialization (reduction to the ridiculous) 

Another great tactic is to use an “apples to oranges” comparison. It’s a great way to increase the perceived value. 

Risk Reversal 

Goal is to reduce ALL of your prospect’s risk, and their FEAR of risk 

**Types of risk** 

● Financial loss  
● Loss of face 

● Loss of time 

● Inconvenience 

● Hassle or intimidation 

● Unsatisfactory results 

● Disappointment and frustration 

Ask yourself, “Which of the above risks is my prospect most likely to have as it relates to my offer?” Then ask, “How can I make those risks go away?” 

Rank the 7 risks above in order of importance to your prospect. 

**Risk-Reversal Basics** 

● Use the biggest, boldest guarantee possible. 

○ Make it so bold that your competitors will think you’re crazy. So generous that they don’t understand how you can make it without going out of business. ○ Check with your payment processor for long guarantees. Some will only hold money for a certain length of time. 

● Show prospects why and how it’s truly risk-free. Be able to show them how and demonstrate why. 

● Give it a benefit-oriented/exciting name. 

○ “The 30 pounds in 30 days money-back guarantee” 

○ “The 7-day gorgeous green lawn money-back guarantee” 

The goal is to increase sales, not to lower refunds. If your refund rate is close to zero, you’re not selling hard enough 

**10 Types of Risk-Reversal** 

1 Conditional 

2 Unconditional 

3 Money-back plus 

a. Money back, plus more 

4 Double-your-money-back 

5 Performance-based 

a. Based on the fulfillment of your promise – the product works or they get their money back 

6 Time-based 

a. 30 days, 6 months, etc. 

7 Lifetime 

a. Todd does not advise ever using this one 

8 Competitor challenge 

a. Will perform better than any competitor 

9 Unique / Off-the-wall  
a. Unique example: Money back plus $100 to your favorite charity 

b. Off-the-wall example: Money back, plus I’ll chew on a dirty sock for you for a minute 

10 Keep premiums 

You can combine guarantees. You can have more than one guarantee. The ultimate goal is the biggest, boldest, most audacious guarantee – something far superior to what your competitors offer. 

**Risk-Reversal Language** 

● 100% money-back guarantee 

● 100% satisfaction 

● No questions asked 

● No hassles 

● Better than risk-free 

● We’ll buy it back 

● Try it before you decide 

If, for some reason, you can’t offer a better guarantee than your competitors, you can still state it differently. For example, instead of a “30-day money back guarantee”, you can say, “Try it before you decide” or “We’ll buy it back”. It’s the same guarantee – it’s all in the language. 

State the risk-reversal with positive language. Most guarantees are stated negatively. 

Example of a guarantee stated negatively: “We provide the finest widgets in the world. If you are not fully satisfied for any reason, just return the widget within 60 days for a full refund of your purchase price.” 

Example of the same guarantee, stated positively: “We provide the finest widgets in the world. You’re either fully satisfied, head-over-heels thrilled with your new widget, or just return your widget within 60 days for a full refund of your purchase price.” 

Include benefits in risk-reversal language. “You’re incredibly happy with your lush, green lawn, or …” 

Premiums/Bonuses 

● Use premiums/bonuses to fill in product gaps and remove excuses for not buying ○ Example: They don’t have the tool to spread the seed (for a green lawn product), so include the tool as a bonus. 

● Give a reason why you’re including the premiums/bonuses 

● Don’t make them throw-ins – stuff you have sitting around that you want to get rid of that has no relevance to the prospect  
● For each bonus, include: features, why, functional, dimensionalized, and emotional benefits. Sell premiums/bonuses just as hard as the main product. 

Reason to Respond Now 

Delay is the death of a sale 

● Urgency (deadline) 

● Scarcity (tied to speed of response) 

● Limited discount 

● Limited supply 

● Limited spots 

● Pending price increase 

● Fast-action bonuses 

● etc. 

Always give a reason WHY for the scarcity and urgency 

Not all urgency has scarcity built in, but all scarcity has innate urgency. 

If the deadline is 3 days away and that’s the only urgency being applied, the prospect will say he’ll come back in 2.5 days (even to himself), but he won’t. That’s why you should always include a reason to buy NOW. Something that will make them realize that if they go away and come back, that thing might not be there anymore. 

People love getting things for free. 

Break out a piece of the product and offer it with the product for free. 

Relevant bonuses 

Irrelevant bonuses 

Close and Call To Action 

● Associate positive outcomes with taking action – future pace the transformation, the “after state” 

● Use future tense – “when your quick start video arrives…” 

● Contrast technique – tell the prospect what the prospect will experience if they DON’T buy (consequence amplification) 

● Tell them exactly what to do 

After creating an offer, ask yourself (and answer honestly) the following two questions: ● “Is this offer strong enough to get ME to act, and act NOW?”  
● “Do the positive outcomes and contrast make me feel like I’d have to be CRAZY not to buy right now?” 

If the answer to either of those is NO, you need to continue to work on the offer. 

## **Lesson 10: Engineering the Perfect Marketing Thesis**

The better your marketing is, the less you have to sell. 

The Marketing Funnel Timeline 

The first 75% is all about the prospect. It’s about building a case for the offer before the offer is even presented. The last 25% is about selling the offer. 

**Big Idea → Lead → Marketing Argument → Thesis Accepted | Offer Presented |-----------------------------------MARKETING---------------------------------|-----SELLING-----| 75% 25%** 

The purpose of the marketing argument is to get the prospect to accept the marketing thesis. Once the thesis is accepted, you can make the offer (because the prospect is ready for it). 

The Marketing Thesis 

**What is the Marketing Thesis?** 

The marketing thesis is the ONE main thing your prospects need to believe before you present your offer in order for them to buy. 

The one thing they need to believe so they see your offer as the answer. The thesis must be accepted by the prospect before the offer is made. 

**Think like a prosecutor.** 

A prosecutor is not going to ask the jury to make a decision until both an airtight logical and emotional case have been built. 

A prosecutor starts with the thesis (“this person is guilty”). Then EVERYTHING they do after that (until the closing comments) is for the purpose of building an airtight emotional and logical case to prove the thesis. ONLY once the thesis has been proven and accepted do they go into the closing comments (the offer) and closing argument (the CTA).  
The ONLY goal of the first 75% of the funnel (the marketing portion) is to get the thesis accepted by the prospect. 

The 2 Parts of the Marketing Thesis 

1 They will experience the Promised Desirable Result 

2 The Promised Desirable Result will happen because of your Unique Mechanism (which will be referred to as the Thesis Solution during this lesson) 

The marketing thesis is really just a statement that promises to deliver the result through your unique mechanism. 

Marketing Thesis Examples 

**“The easiest and most profitable way for any pizzeria to get new customers using the internet is with Precision Targeted Facebook Advertising.”** 

Desirable result: Get new customers into your pizzeria easily and profitably using the internet Unique Mechanism: Precision Targeted Facebook Advertising 

**“To get your marketing conversions above 10%, you need to have an adaptive, responsive Smart Funnel.”** 

Desirable result: Conversions above 10% 

Unique Mechanism: An adaptive, responsive Smart Funnel 

**“The unique combination of a vegetarian diet and a Mediterranean diet, called Vegeterranean, is the most effective way to lower cholesterol naturally, and keep it down.”** 

Desirable result: Lower cholesterol and keep it down 

Unique Mechanism: Vegeterranean diet 

The Marketing Thesis Solution 

Your thesis solution is the method behind your unique mechanism. 

**In order for prospects to accept your marketing thesis, they must believe in your marketing thesis solution (unique mechanism).** It’s believing in your thesis solution that leads to believing in your thesis. Meaning, you have to build a case for your unique mechanism before prospects will accept your marketing thesis.  
The first 75% of your marketing funnel timeline uses education-based marketing. The purpose is to educate the prospect in a way that establishes the thesis solution as the answer. 

When the prospect believes the marketing thesis, the only question left is “How do I get it?” 

How to Craft Your Marketing Thesis 

1 Determine the desirable result your prospect wants that your product provides 2 Add in the unique mechanism that will give it to them 

## **Lesson 11: Engineering the Perfect Big Idea / Hook**

We are in the idea business 

The Big Idea is what engages your prospect. They’ve seen so many marketing messages that they’ve become almost numb to them. The Big Idea is something fresh and new that gets them to pay attention. 

**The Big Idea prevents mental opt-out.** (*“I’ve seen that before. I’ve heard that before.”*) Your success, your conversion rate, is based on the quality of your ideas. 

Why a BIG IDEA? 

● Every marketing promotion is built on an idea/concept. 

● Prospects are bombarded with claims (most of which they ignore), and are consequently very skeptical. 

○ The internet is the least trusted medium because anyone can create a website and an offer. 

● Prospects are bored They want to know what’s new. 

○ A Big Idea gives them something new. 

● The “Proliferation of the Formulaic” 

○ A Big Idea does not follow a formula, so the prospect won’t be able to say “I’ve seen this type of thing before” 

● Overcome the “Categorical Imperative” 

○ A Big Idea prevents the prospect from viewing your funnel as advertising ● To serve as a “Pattern Interrupt” 

● To differentiate your message from others 

○ A BETTER message doesn’t always get attention, but a DIFFERENT message does.  
The Big Idea 

The Big Idea is the advertisement for your funnel. Not the offer 

The Big Idea and the headline are NOT the same thing. The headline is an *expression of* the Big Idea. 

**The Big Idea must match the stage on the Promise Exposure Spectrum that your prospects are in.** 

Your Big Idea must stem from your understanding of what stage of the P.E.S. your prospects are in. 

The single biggest reason for marketing failure is an immature marketing message, which stems from an immature marketing idea. 

The Core of the Big Idea 

● Taps into ONE emotion 

● Based on ONE promise 

● Tells ONE story (theme) 

● Leads prospects to think about ONE thing (the marketing thesis) 

A great marketing idea is ONE idea. Weak marketing messages are crowded with mediocre ideas. You must narrow your focus. 

Elements of the Big Idea 

● ONE great idea 

● Big, believable promise 

○ Of transformation, result, outcome 

● Unique 

○ Original, unexpected, different from what the prospect has seen before ● New and fresh 

○ It needs to feel timely, like it’s about something happening right now, or something that’s about to happen. 

● Specific 

○ Concrete, tangible 

● Arresting or Startling 

○ Shock prospects out of their complacency 

● Easy to Grasp 

○ Immediately understandable. Instant appeal. Simplicity encourages belief.  
**Avoid the curse of knowledge.** Most marketers overestimate what other people will understand. The more you learn, the worse your accuracy in assuming what others know (because you know so much). Odds are, you’re an expert, your prospects are not. 

Simple is better. Simplicity encourages belief. 

A confused mind will not pay attention, will not engage.

It is usually pale green to yellow when ripe, with sweet white (or more rarely, pink or red) flesh and a very thick, spongy rind. It is the largest citrus fruit, 15-25 cm in diameter, and usually weighing 1-2 kg.

The “Big Idea Formula” 

**E.C.** (P.P.  U.M.)  **I.I.  B.I.** 

**Emotionally Compelling** (Primary Promise  Unique Mechanism)  **Intellectually Interesting  BIG IDEA** 

A true Big Idea appeals to logic as well as emotion. 

The Primary Promise 

● Must be big, bold, audacious, bold, true, and believable. 

○ The believability comes from the specificity 

● Prospects must lust after the payoff 

● The transformation/result/outcome 

● Backed by a preponderance of proof 

○ The promise can’t be bigger than the biggest proof point 

○ The better the proof, the bigger the promise can be 

● Solves an urgent problem (when possible) 

○ The more urgent and pressing the problem, the more valuable the solution ● You want prospects to say to themselves, “This is exactly what I’ve been waiting for” “This changes everything” 

● The “magic wand” technique: If you could grant your prospects anything, what would the magic result be? From there, back it down until it’s true and believable (and you have the proof to back it up) 

● The ideal client exercise: Imagine your greatest future success story. What EXACT results did the client get? What they experience? What was the transformation? What additional benefits are they experiencing? This should play a role in the promise you design.  
The Unique Mechanism 

● The unique piece, part, component, aspect, process, or system that delivers on the Primary Promise for prospects. The reason why your product works. 

● Gives your prospect hope that maybe this time they will experience the result. (They view it as the thing that’s been missing.) 

● Prevents prospects from searching for other products because you’re not selling something that anyone else has. You’re not selling a commodity. You’re selling a proprietary solution. 

The 3 Types of Unique Mechanisms 

1 Actual Mechanism 

a. When you actually have a unique mechanism in the product or service b. When things get patented, those are unique mechanisms 

2 Unspoken Mechanism 

a. An “unspoken mechanism” is not unique to your product or service, but you’re the only one who’s talking about it. 

b. Typically a temporary mechanism, since competitors will start using it too, once they see you using it 

3 Transubstantiated Mechanism 

a. Turning something ordinary into something extraordinary (name it)

## **Module 3 Implementation Session**

**You don’t always need a big idea.** If you’re making a promise that has never been made, there’s no need for a big idea. In that case, the promise in itself is the big idea because it’s compelling on its own. 

The big idea starts at stage 3 of the P.E.S. 

The goal of the big idea is to get attention. 

The 3 ingredients of a BIG IDEA: BOLD PRIMARY PROMISE powered by UNIQUE MECHANISM wrapped in a story that is INTELLECTUALLY INTERESTING. 

If you took out the promise and mechanism, would the story still be interesting? It should be. Do NOT write copy until the Big Idea is clear. 

For info marketers, a framework is the ULTIMATE UNIQUE MECHANISM. It allows to avoid ALL perception of the generic, the general, the commodity. 

In every market, a field of expertise starts as general, then becomes specialized over time.  
Example: How to make money on the internet splintered into dozens of specialties (PPC, video ads, etc.) 

Front End / Ascension 

The entire E5 CAMP Masterclass is, as a product, designed to funnel people to the core offer, which is the masterclass. Example: Any individual lesson can be used as a front end offer because each one covers a different, specific topic. When someone buys any piece of the whole, they’re primed to buy the full masterclass… and all the messaging can be congruent because all pieces part of the same whole, which is the masterclass. Any lesson can be sold on its own, as well as any module, and it naturally leads into the next piece. 

If you’re going to launch a front end product, you should always know where it’s leading. You should already know the ascension path. 

The objective of a front end funnel is customer acquisition – to acquire maximum customers for the back end. So if your front end doesn’t lead to your back end, the entire plan is broken. There’s no value in making front end sales if there’s not a clear, congruent ascension path that customers are going to follow. 

Example of two machines 

The back end machine pumps out profit every time someone gets put into it. It’s the job of the front end machine to pull people out of your market and put them into the back end machine. 

Stepdown Approach 

You can start with a low-priced product OR a high-priced product. At the beginning, Todd built the MFA list with a $497 front end offer, and it worked VERY well. Personally, he’s a fan of starting with a higher-priced front end product then going down, rather than starting with a low-cost product and going up. 

**Reasons to start high** 

● With a low-cost Front End Product (FEP), you have to have an optimized upsell plus a few more offers. You have to have a minimum of 2-3 optimized offers in order to recoup your ad spend. With a mid- to high-priced offer, you don’t have to do that. 

● There is wildly different psychological positioning when starting with a higher-priced product. Start high to set the frame – set the value of the information. Then go back and sell it in different formats for less.

## **Lesson 12: Flawless Funnel Copywriting**

*A good marketing message is one that doesn’t feel like a marketing message*

“When it comes to writing great copy, it’s not so much a matter of knowing how to write as it is knowing what to write.” –Gary Halbert 

Great copywriting that sells is 100% built on the examination and research process. “The key to writing great copy… is **empathy**.” –Jay Abraham

**24 General Copy Principles** 

1 Get yourself in the right frame of mind. 

a. Get excited about the product or service. Prospects buy on YOUR enthusiasm and passion. Show it. 

b. Do whatever you can do to get into the proper state to write. 

2 Shoot for clarity over creativity. 

a. Your marketing message needs to be impossible to misunderstand. 

3 Choose the appropriate tone for the subject matter. 

a. Example: You’re going to talk about arthritis much differently than you’re going to talk about an investment. 

b. Does your subject warrant a more serious tone? Humorous? Down to earth? etc. 4 Activate the appropriate DOMINANT EMOTION in the prospect. 

a. Example: If you’re talking to someone about an investment, the appropriate emotion would be greed. But if you’re talking to someone about a cure for a serious health problem, the dominant emotion would be hope. 

b. Use language, stories, examples that activates the dominant emotion 5 Write like you talk (conversational tone). 

6 Speak to ONE person at a time. 

a. Write as if you’re having an actual conversation with them. This includes word choice and sentence structure. 

7 Speak your prospects’ language. 

a. Use words and phrases they’re familiar with, including “insider” language and “lingo”. 

b. Never speak down to them 

8 Focus on the prospect, not yourself. 

a. YOU, not I or THEY 

9 Leverage curiosity (Open the “Knowledge Gap”.) 

a. Make them feel like there’s something they don’t know, something they don’t understand, that would be of value to them to know. (That’s what it means to “open the knowledge gap”.) 

10 Dramatize your copy. 

a. You want excitement, mystery, challenge, … a compelling story in your copy.  
11 Show, don’t tell. 

a. Paint vivid word pictures. 

b. Don’t use the word; use a description. 

c. Example: Instead of “He was very excited”, you could say, “With anticipation in his eyes, the child ripped open the package as fast as he could and, when he saw what was inside, smiled from ear to ear as he leapt to his feet, thrust his hands in the air, and let out a big whoop.” 

12 Use story when possible. 

a. Stories are highly engaging and they bypass the mind’s sales filter. b. Story of discovery. Hero’s journey. 

c. Incorporate detail. This will allow the prospect to see it play out in their mind, which will keep them engaged. 

13 Use repetition. 

a. Repeat the same promise, but with different words, phrases, examples, and stories. 

14 Use presuppositional questions to state benefits. 

a. Move away from an explicit marketing message to implicit. 

b. When talking about the deeper emotional benefits the prospect will experience, saying “You’ll have more respect from your neighbors” doesn’t have the same impact as showing it through a story or example and letting the prospect see it unfolding in their own mind’s eye. In their own head and heart. 

c. Example of a presuppositional question: “How much are you enjoying going through the E5 CAMP Masterclass?” The question presupposes (assumes) you’re enjoying going through the class. 

d. Example: “Why does this produce such quick results?” 

e. Asking questions like this implies the benefits. Use this technique when you don’t want to explicitly state something. One situation to use it in would be when you want to avoid boasting. 

f. Example: Instead of “I am the world’s most recognized authority on this topic.”, you could ask, “What is it about the method I teach – and the success behind it – that has allowed me to be seen as the world’s most recognized authority on this topic?” 

15 Open and close loops. 

a. The mind likes closure. Tease the prospect with upcoming info, stories, etc. to create emotional tension and keep their attention. 

b. Nested loops are *extremely* powerful. (Open loop 1 Open loop 2, close loop 1 Open loop 3, close loop 2 And so on.) With nested loops, there is at least one loop open at all times. 

c. Any time you open a loop, you MUST close it before you get to the offer. All loops must be closed before the offer is made. 

16 Use “Reason Why” copy.  
a. Always explain the WHY behind everything you say. Giving a reason WHY acts as a proof point, supports your claim, and shows your prospect that there’s reasoning behind what you’re doing/saying. 

17 Speak in the present tense. 

a. “This method was used by hundreds of people to lose 10-20 pounds…” vs. “Hundreds of people are using this method to lose 10-20 pounds…” 

b. This is important because speaking in the past tense can cause your prospect to wonder if the information is dated and doesn’t work anymore. This is especially true in markets that change rapidly. 

18 Pluralize the verb when talking about the solution. 

a. This is about you placing the outcome or transformation on the solution rather than on the prospect. 

b. Example: The difference between saying “Burn 10 pounds of belly fat…” vs. “It burns 10 pounds of belly fat…” The first places the responsibility to burn the fat on the prospect, whereas the second places the responsibility on the product. Instead of the prospect doing the work, the pill/diet/supplement is doing the work. 

c. Instead of “Follow this diet and boost your metabolism”, say, “Follow this diet, which boosts your metabolism.” 

19 When you write, don’t edit. Just write, and write FAST. 

a. All first drafts are terrible. It doesn’t matter. Don’t go back and fix ANYTHING. 20 Use short, choppy sentences. More periods. Fewer commas. 

a. It gives the copy rhythm and momentum. 

21 Mix up the rhythm. 

a. Sentence length, tempo, etc. It makes the reading more enjoyable and is easier on the eye when looking at the copy. 

22 Write in chunks. 

a. Assemble the copy. Write in chunks and pieces, then put them together. 23 Write more copy than necessary, then trim it down during editing. 

a. It’s infinitely easier to trim out what you don’t need than try to add in pieces you missed. 

24 Put every line of your copy through the “So what?” test. 

a. When editing, ask “So what?” from your prospect’s point of view for every sentence. 

b. Every sentence and word of the copy needs to drive towards the sale. When you find pieces that can be removed without weakening the message, remove them. 

11 Things to Avoid when Writing Copy 

1 Avoid Vague generalities 

a. Be specific. Use specific information, facts, etc. 

2 Avoid Unsubstantiated claims 

a. Every claim you make must be backed up by one or more proof points.  
b. Your claims should only be as big as your biggest proof point. Don’t make a claim that’s bigger than your biggest proof point. 

3 Avoid Superlatives and Platitudes 

a. The “Best” “Biggest” “Fastest” “Most exciting” 

4 Avoid Boasting 

a. There’s a big difference between stating why you have credibility and boasting. 5 Avoid Faux Benefits 

a. Example of a “faux benefit”: “This pill helps you lose weight because it flushes fat out of your intestines.” That’s not the benefit. That’s just the mechanism. The benefit is the RESULT of the fat being flushed out. 

6 Avoid Naked Facts 

a. “Naked facts” are facts presented out of the context of a story. When possible, weave your facts into a story. 

b. Example: Instead of stating that your weight loss program was proven to work for 9 out of 10 people, convey that same fact in a story. “The professors at UCLA ran a 90-day test of 117 people from 16 countries around the world, and what they found was …” 

7 Avoid “Passive Voice” 

a. When the passive voice is used, it’s difficult to tell who’s doing the action. Instead, use the active voice. It adds a subtle, subconscious impact to the way your copy reads. 

b. Passive voice makes the copy harder to read and understand. Writing in the active voice is easier to read and comprehend. 

c. Example of passive voice: “The weight was burned off by Barbara” is passive. That same sentence in the active voice would read, “Barbara burned the fat.” Instead of “Tim was hit by the ball”, write “The ball slammed into Tim.” 

8 Avoid Weasel Words 

a. Words that qualify. “I think it’s good for you.” “I believe it’s good for you.” “It may.” “It might.” “It could.” These words weaken the marketing message and robs you of authority 

9 Avoid Excess Adverbs (words that end in ly) 

a. This bloats copy and can make it feel hyped and exaggerated. 

b. Excess adverbs are usually a sign of poor verb choice. 

c. Example: Instead of “He walked leisurely down the street”, say “He strolled down the street.” 

10 Avoid Weak Verbs 

a. Reference John Carlton’s Power Words report for great verbs 

11 Avoid Hype 

a. Don’t exaggerate your claims with excessive adjectives and adverbs. b. This is different than making an exaggeration to make a point, which the prospect knows is an exaggeration.

## **Lesson 15: Engineering the Perfect Minimum Viable Funnel**

What is a Minimum Viable Funnel (MVF)? 

It’s a marketing campaign with the minimum number of elements necessary to evaluate the 4 core elements. (Big Idea, Lead, Marketing Argument, Offer) 

Always start with a MVF when creating a new marketing campaign. You do this to test the 4 core elements of the campaign. 

The 4 Pieces of a Minimum Viable Funnel 

1 Traffic Captivation Page (Lead capture page) 

2 Marketing/Sales Vehicle 

a. This is the page (or pages) where you present the marketing message, the offer, and the call to action. 

3 Order Form 

a. For processing orders 

4 Thank You Page 

a. For thanking the new customer and delivering the product. 

You must have all 4 pieces in place for a viable test. Leaving out the order form, for example, will throw off the test because not everyone who clicks over to the order form will order. (It’s common to have a very high order form abandonment rate.) 

There is no follow-up sequence in a MVF. There is no upsell or downsell sequence or addon offer. It’s a waste of time at this point because you don’t even know if your funnel works yet. 

What’s Being Tested Where 

● The BIG IDEA is being tested on the Traffic Captivation Page.  
○ The goal is to find out if the Big Idea resonates enough with the market to generate an opt in. 

● The LEAD and MARKETING ARGUMENT is being tested in the Marketing/Sales Vehicle. 

● The OFFER is being tested in the Marketing/Sales Vehicle as well as on the Order Form. 

## **Lesson 16: Engineering the Perfect Traffic Captivation Page**

What is a Traffic Captivation Page (TCP)? 

It’s designed to do one thing, and one thing only, and that’s to get your target prospects who are interested in your Big Idea / Hook to raise their hand and identify themselves by opting in for more information. 

Unlike the typical squeeze page, **the Traffic Captivation Page does NOT offer a lead magnet.** The only thing it offers is the ability for your prospect to consume your marketing message, which therefore identifies the most targeted prospects. 

The type and look and feel of your TCP is going to differ depending on the market you’re in *and the traffic source you’re using*. **There is no one “correct” TCP.** It’s much more important to understand the function of the page rather than the format. 

Shorter pages generally perform better, especially when you’ve done proper research and you know what moves and motivates your prospects and can state it in a simple headline. Any additional information just acts as noise and can suppress response. 

True 2-Step Marketing vs. Distorted 2-Step Marketing 

The TCP is built on the principle of 2-step marketing. 

The typical squeeze pages that you see all over the internet are a distorted form of 2-step marketing. The difference is that those pages give away a lead magnet in exchange for an email address, whereas with TRUE 2-step marketing, the information that the prospect is getting access to is the first step of information in your marketing funnel. 

*In distorted 2-step marketing, prospects opt in to get some kind of free information (a.k.a. the “lead magnet”). In TRUE 2-step marketing, prospects opt in to get access to the marketing message.* Then, through the delivery of that information, you’re doing the marketing… shaping their beliefs.  
Many marketers today believe the purpose of 2-step marketing is simply to get an opt in – a lead. So they offer something free that the majority of the market will be interested in. Then after they deliver that freebie, only then do they start the marketing process. This approach is a mistake because it generates a lot of freebie seekers and tire kickers. 

TRUE 2-step marketing is all about getting prospects to identify themselves as being interested. It’s to get them to raise their hand and show that they’re interested by requesting more information. The information you provide to them IS the marketing message. 

**Education-based marketing is valuable in and of itself, so you don’t need some extra freebie or widget to give away because you already have something valuable in the form of your marketing message.**

## **Lesson 20: Understanding Your MVF Response**

Evaluate Flow Chart 

Test to a segment of your house list first. If sales conversion rate (S.C.) is above 2%, move on to cold traffic. If not, review consumption of sales funnel. (Where are people dropping out? Fix that part of the funnel.) 

When sending to your house list, if the clickthrough rate is lower than average, take another look at the big idea.

When testing to cold traffic, if you’re getting 60% ROI or greater ($0.60 revenue for every dollar invested), move on to the enhance stage. If the ROI is less than 60%, take a look at the funnel economics and performance. 

Look at actual CPC. Where does it fall within the CPC margin of safety? Were you over the margin of safety? If so, you need to find a different source of traffic that will allow you to get clicks within your margin of safety. 

Great marketing will not overcome poor economics. 

If your CPC is within the margin of safety, then look at funnel performance. You want a 20% or greater opt-in rate and a 2% or greater sales conversion rate. 

If opt-in or SC rates are too low, go to the reengineer stage. Find out where visitors are dropping out. 

**There are only 3 possible issues:** 

1 CPC too high 

2 Opt-in rate too low 

3 Sales conversion rate too low 

When evaluating a low opt-in rate, you’re looking at the Big Idea. How do you know if the problem is the Big Idea or if it’s the copy (headline) on the opt-in page? Look at the click-through rate (CTR) to cold traffic. If CTR is average or above average, the Big Idea is good and you need to tweak the copy. If CTR is low, the Big Idea is probably off and will need to be fixed. 

The margin of safety formula is set up to make it easy to get to break-even when you have 60% ROI or greater on your ad spend.

# **Module 7: Enhance**

## **Lesson 21: Enhancing with the Perfect Upsell Sequence**

a.k.a. the Transaction Maximization Sequence  
Upsells are nothing more than a series of additional offers that are presented to the customer after they complete and submit their order form for the main product. 

The upsell sequence is still part of the front end. Meaning, you want to break even once the upsells have been offered. (You don’t need to break even on the main product alone.) The job of the upsell sequence is NOT to generate profit – it’s to raise the AOV to breakeven. 

Purpose of this sequence 

● Increase Average Order Value (AOV) 

○ The higher the AOV, the more money you can invest to acquire a new customer. ● Increase indoctrination touch points 

○ Upsells give you more opportunities to interact with the customer, deliver value, build trust with the customer. (The more the customer hears from you and buys from you, the stronger the bond.) 

○ Getting your customer to take advantage of more offers at the beginning of the relationship increased the speed at which you can bond with them. Increases the speed at which you can indoctrinate them into your tribe. 

○ This is enormously valuable when you recognize that the entire goal of the front end is to acquire customers, then transition them into the back end. 

● Decrease refund rate 

○ There is an inverse correlation between the number of upsell offers a customer takes, and their refund rate. (The refund rate goes down by 10-15% for each additional upsell offer purchased.) 

○ The goal is to get the maximum amount of customers to take advantage of the maximum number of upsell offers, because it not only increases average order value, but also increases indoctrination touch points and decreases the refund rate with the maximum number of customers. 

○ Example: 20 buyers taking an upsell of $30 each is better than 1 buyer taking a $600 upsell 

● Identify hyper-buyers for segmentation 

○ Hyper buyers can be segmented for an additional back end offer a lot sooner than regular buyers. 

○ Example: Someone who buys only your main offer is rated a “C” buyer. Someone who buys the main offer and one upsell is rated “B”. Someone who buys the main offer and both upsells is rated “A”. 

○ On your back end, you should be investing the most attention and resources into your “A” buyers. 

The Main Question 

The main question that should drive your thought process when putting together your upsell sequence: **What else would this customer want, need, or value?**  
Think in congruence with what they just bought. 

Types of Upsell Offers 

● Make getting results FASTER 

● Make using your product MORE ENJOYABLE 

○ Increase the “fun factor” 

○ Example: Say they just bought a blender. You could add additional cups and a custom color. 

● Make getting results EASIER 

● AUTOMATE the work required 

○ How can you take elements of the work off their shoulders? Better yet, ALL the work. (That would be a “done for you” solution.) 

● REDUCE RISK of ERROR 

○ How can you reduce likelihood of them making a mistake? Of not using the product correctly? Of not getting the result they want? 

● Equips them for the NEXT STEP of their journey 

○ Example: Say you’re selling a service to business owners on how to set up their main website. What would they need next after the site is set up? They’ll want website traffic. That could be an upsell offer. What do they need after traffic? Conversions. 

Upsell Offer Page Elements 

● Progress acknowledgement at top of page 

○ Lets the customer know where they are in the buying process 

● Offer messaging 

● Buy button 

● “No thanks” link 

Mid-ticket → Order Form → Offer 1 

If your first offer is mid- to high-ticket, your first upsell offer should be a “reverse add-on” offer. This offer is priced at 60-80% of your main offer. 

This is typical and acceptable consumer experience. The idea that your first upsell should be 3x the price of the main offer is something that marketers made up, but where else have you seen that? You don’t go buy a suit for $500 then get offered something for $1,500. You get offered a pair of pants or a shirt for maybe $100. 

At 80% of the main offer price, you might get 30-40% of the customers take advantage of that offer.  
At 60%, don’t be surprised to see up to a 60% conversion rate. 

Low-ticket → Order Form → Offer 1 

If your first offer is a low-ticket offer, offer 1 is typically 2-3x the price of the initial product. 

The Perfect Sequence 

To be used for mid- to high-priced main offers. NOT used for low-ticket “loss leader”-type products. 

Reverse Add-On (Price: 60-80% of initial offer) → 

Soft Offer Add-On (Price: 2-3x of initial, pay nothing for 14 days, money-back guarantee) → Mid-Tier Add-On (Price: UP TO 10x initial) → 

TY Page Gift Offer (Price: Continuity with free trial) 

**SUPER IMPORTANT:** When a customer says NO twice, they must be removed from this sequence. 

Having “too many” offers is not a problem. It’s only a problem if you have too many offers going to a new customer who isn’t interested in them. 

But if a new customer is saying YES, they’re showing you they want more, so it would be foolish not to make another offer.  
The **Thank You Page Gift Offer** is presented on the thank you page. It doesn’t look like your other offers – it looks like a thank you page. It’s presented in a video. The video thanks the customer for buying and rewards them with a free trial to a continuity program. 

Example of TY Page Gift Offer video script: “Hey, this is Todd Brown. Thanks so much for taking advantage of our ABC product today. So grateful to have you. In the next 10 minutes you’re going to get an email with your access details. In the mean time I’ve got a $97 free gift that I want to give you as my way of saying thank you for responding to this offer. Thank you for trusting me enough to take advantage of this offer. Here’s what it’s about. It’s called XYZ newsletter, this is what it’s going to teach you, benefit benefit benefit benefit. It normally costs $97/mo but today I want to give you a month for free as my gift. And then, if after a month, you love it, instead of $97 you’ll be at just $69. To get started with your free trial, just click the button below that says ‘YES, I want my free gift’” 

When bringing a new customer through the upsell sequence, it is critical to *keep them in the buying sequence mentally*. They must recognize that their order is not done yet – that they have not completed it.

NOTE: In some sophisticated markets (the internet marketing market is one example), a progress bar telegraphs that there is a coming upsell sequence. This can suppress response. In those markets, don’t include the progress bar on the order form. DO include it on the upsell pages. 

Offer Messaging 

You should not attempt to re-sell them. You’re presenting an offer, a reason for the offer, and showing them why this is perfectly congruent with what they just bought. 

When selling a low-priced upsell offer, use a sales letter. 

● Include a 1-minute video at the top explaining what’s on the page and why you’re making the offer. The point of the video is to get them to read. You don’t have to do this, but it can help.
