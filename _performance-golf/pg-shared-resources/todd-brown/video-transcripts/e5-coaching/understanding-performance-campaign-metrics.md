# Understanding Performance Campaign Metrics

**Date:** 2021-02-17 | **Duration:** 10m | **Folder:** E5 COACHING

---

In this lesson, we're going to cover your performance campaign metrics. Now, you'll remember there are two categories of marketing metrics that we use. Financial campaign metrics, which have to do with the cost and the revenue side of
your campaign. Those metrics are used to assess the viability of a campaign,
whether it is working from a financial or return on investment perspective,
whether a campaign is ready to scale, and so on. And then we have the performance campaign metrics.

And these are the metrics that tell us how well each piece or step or stage or page of a marketing
campaign is performing. Ultimately, these metrics are used to improve the results of a campaign. Now, there are five performance campaign metrics that we're going to use. Now,
this can vary a bit depending on the type of campaign that you have in place. If your campaign
has more steps than what it is that I'm about to show you, then you'll have more metrics.

If your campaign has less steps than what I'm about to show you, then of course,
you'll be using less performance campaign metrics. But let's say that your campaign consists of
an opt-in page, a marketing and sales page, an order form, an upsell, and then a thank you page. So the first performance metric that we look at is opt-in rate. This tells us how well the lead
generation page in your campaign is performing. What percentage of visitors to that page are actually
being converted into leads.

Next, we've got sales conversion rate. And of course, this tells us what
percentage of people that reach or watch or consume your marketing and sales message actually turn into
visits to your order form. How many people are actually clicking the add to cart button or the
checkout button? And then we've got order form completion rate. This is the percentage of people
that hit your order form and actually enter their details, press the button, and complete their
transaction.

Now, these two are different. We look at and separate sales conversion rate and order form
completion rate from one another because these are two different steps in the checkout process. You could have a marketing and sales message that does really well, sends a lot of people over to your
order form and you could have an issue on your order form causing a low order form completion rate. And so we separate these two out so that we can better assess what's going on. If we didn't look
at these as two different numbers, it would be extremely difficult for us to understand whether the
problem is the marketing and sales page or whether the problem is the order form.

So we separate them
out and we look at both of them. Next is your transaction maximization offer conversion rate. Now,
this is another way of saying your upsell conversion rate. If you have one upsell,
it's the conversion rate on that one upsell. If you have two, it's both of those.

If you have three,
it is the conversion rate for each of those three. And then finally, we have the average order value
contribution. Now, this is different from average order value, which we're going to talk about
in a different lesson. Average order value, just as a reference, is the average amount of money
that you collect when a buyer goes through your campaign. Now, if you have offers like an upsell,
a downsell, a bump, as well as, of course, your core offer, your core product, if you will, right?

Well, you're going to have an average amount of money that a buyer spends with you because
some buyers may take advantage of just the core offer. Some may take advantage of, let's say,
the core offer and a bump offer. Some may take advantage of the core offer, bump offer,
and the first upsell. Some may take advantage of every offer that you have in there. Average order
value tells us what's the average amount of money that you collect when a buyer goes through your
campaign.

That number, average order value, is an extremely critical number that falls under the
financial campaign metrics, tells us a lot about the financial viability of a campaign. Average order
value contribution is how much money does a particular upsell or downsell or bump offer contribute
to your overall average order value? Does having an upsell contribute an additional $9 on average,
for example, to the average amount of money that a buyer spends with you? Or does a different upsell
contribute $12 to the average amount of money that a buyer spends with you? That's an important number
to know as you're about to see because it tells us which one is the better performing upsell or
downsell or bump offer.

So let's look at this and let's look at how you calculate these numbers because
this is very simple. Let's say you are running an ad that goes to an opt-in page and you send a thousand
people to that opt-in page and out of those one thousand people, 500 people give you their email
address. 500 people fill out your form on that page and actually become a lead. 1,000 visitors,
500 leads. That tells us that you have a 50% opt-in rate.

50% of the visitors gave you their email
address and became a lead. That tells us how well that page is performing. Why? Because that page is
there to do one thing and one thing only and that is turn a visitor into a lead. Turn a visitor into
an opt-in.

Now let's say out of the 500 people that visit the sales page, 50 of them click the
add to cart button and go over to your order form. Well now that tells us that we have a 10% sales
conversion rate and in this context, the 10% sales conversion rate, what we're talking about here is
what percentage of people that hit the sales page, click the button and go over to the order form. So we're not talking about what percentage of people are actually checking out, entering their
credit card information. We're trying to assess how well your marketing and sales page is doing
to move people to the next step in the campaign. And so here we see that the sales page has a 10%
sales conversion rate.

We take the total number of visitors divided by the total number of people that
took the action that gives us 10%. Now let's say out of the 50 people that visited the order form,
40 people enter their, their contact details, their payment details, and click the checkout button. Well, that means we have an 80% order form completion rate. 80% of the people that hit the page completed
the order form and checked out, right? We take the 50 divided by 40 and that gives us 80%.

Now let's
say we have 40 people that go over to the upsell page and we see that eight people took advantage
of the upsell, meaning eight people click the button saying, add this to my order. Well, 40
divided by eight gives us a 20% upsell conversion rate. That's how we determine the upsell conversion
rate or the transaction maximization offer conversion rate. So you can see right off the bat, those first
four metrics, very simple to calculate the value of this is you're going to learn later on is an
understanding the performance of each step, each stage in the campaign. You need to know how each
step or each stage is performing so that we can identify later on.

If there is an issue, what is the
issue? We don't want to operate in the dark and wonder, is it the opt-in page? Is it the sales page? Is it
the order form? Is it the upsell page?

We don't want to operate in the dark wondering what step is
performing, what step isn't performing. We don't want to have to operate in the dark when identifying
the constraint, the weak link in the chain. We want to use metrics and data to tell us. These metrics give
us insight into what's underperforming, what is performing well, so that we can identify an issue when an issue
exists. Now let's talk about the AOV contribution number, the average order value contribution
number.

Now the way to calculate this, we look at the upsell conversion rate, 20%, but we need to know
the price of the upsell. In other words, what people are paying. So let's say in this example here,
we have a $60 upsell. So this is irregardless of what the core offer price is, right? Regardless of what
people have paid up to this point, to determine the contribution to average order value of this
offer right here, we take the price and we multiply it by the conversion rate.

So 20% of $60 tells us
that this upsell has a $12 average order value contribution. What this means is that for every
body that hits this page on average, they are worth an extra 12 bucks because of this upsell,
right? Some people will take advantage of this offer, right? We know that some people in this
case, 20% will take advantage of it, right? They'll pay $60.

The other 80% won't take advantage of this
offer. And so to them, this offer is worth zero, but with direct response, when it comes to performance
campaign metrics, we look at averages, having this offer in place adds on average, an additional $12
to the transaction amount of every buyer that goes through this campaign. So these metrics right here
are your performance campaign metrics. These are the metrics that we look at. This is how you tally
those metrics and you understand now what these metrics mean.

Now, again, these metrics don't tell us
whether a campaign is viable or not, meaning you could have a 50% opt-in rate. You could have a 10%
sales conversion rate. You can have an 80% order form completion rate, a 20% upsell take rate. That
upsell could be contributing $12 to your average order value, but that doesn't tell us whether the
campaign is profitable, whether it's ready to be scaled, whether it's viable, meaning that from a
financial perspective, we want to continue to run it. It doesn't tell us whether this is a poorly
performing.

It only tells us the performance of the different pages or stages in your campaign
still critical, but these metrics are used when we want to identify the constraint or the problem in a
campaign. We want to know what we need to work on test, tweak, enhance, optimize. That's what these
metrics are used for. And so there you go. Those are your performance campaign metrics.
