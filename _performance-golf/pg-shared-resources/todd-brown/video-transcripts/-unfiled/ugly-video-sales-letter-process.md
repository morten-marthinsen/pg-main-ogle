# Ugly Video Sales Letter Process

**Date:** 2015-01-12 | **Duration:** 13m | **Folder:** _unfiled

Hey, this is Todd Brown from MarketingFunnelAutomation.com and I've got a little kind of tutorial video
for you today. I want to explain the process that I follow today and have for the last handful of years
for recording and publishing all of our ugly video sales letters. You know, the video sales letters that are just white background with text. Now up until a handful of years ago, I used to record these videos kind of the same way
that most marketers do. I would create the slides in Keynote or PowerPoint and then I would basically turn on an audio
recording software, put my mic in front of me and I would record the video sales letter.

And the video sales letters turned out okay, but then I learned a process and let me give
credit where credit's due. I learned this process from Andy Jenkins. You might know him as the video boss and I followed his process for the first time,
which totally took me out of my comfort zone. And that video sales letter turned out awesome in terms of the quality, the quality of the
audio and just the quality of the overall video. And so since then, whenever we publish an ugly video sales letter, this is the exact process
that I follow.

And this is the exact process that I advise all of my clients and coaching students to
follow as well. And so let me walk you through, there are eight steps. Most of these are really, really simple. There's a couple that are really key and I'll make sure that I point those out to you. And I'll also explain to you the software that I use and even some of the equipment that
I use.

It's really nothing fancy. And so the very first step, and this was a big change for me personally, is that I write
out the VSL script in advance. So before I even think about recording the video, before you should think about recording
the video or anything, you want to write out the VSL script from A to Z. And you want to write it out word for word, exactly how it's going to be said on the video
sales letter. Now, the key when you're writing the VSL script is not only that you write like you talk, again,
you're going to, you know, you write the exact way it's going to be said in the video sales
letter, but you also document places in the VSL script where you're going to pause, where
maybe you'll chuckle, where maybe you're going to emphasize a word or a phrase, whatever
it is that you're going to do and say in the video sales letter gets scripted in advance,
right?

So you write out from A to Z, the word for word VSL script that you're going to follow. Then you don't record the video at the same time that you are reading the script. You record the audio separately. And this right here, these two steps were a big change for me, right? And the reason why you want to record the audio separately, you want to record the audio as
its own track is so that you can focus on the VSL script so that you can focus on pace
and tone, volume, articulation, right?

So that you can focus on where you want to pause, where you want to emphasize certain
words and phrases. You want to make sure that you deliver the VSL script in the most animated, clear, and concise
way possible. And so you want to do that by recording the audio first and recording the audio separately. Now, the software that I use is Adobe Audition. You don't have to use Adobe Audition.

I don't even know if it's still available. You can also use Adobe Soundbooth and you can even use something like Audacity. The reason why I like Adobe Audition is because it allows you to remove all background noise
and it allows you to really fine tune the actual audio file. So it sounds clear, it's got more bass, it just becomes a sharp audio file. And the thing to really understand with your VSLs, your videos in general, is that audio
is critical.

People will not watch a video, no matter how good the slides are, no matter how engaging
the slides may be or the words on the screen may be, if the audio is just really low quality,
if the volume is too low, if it's not clear, if there's some kind of hum or buzz or background
noise where, you know, you can hear a fan in the room or something like that. And so you want to make sure that you create the sharpest audio possible. And so I use Adobe Audition. I've got a bunch of different microphones. For example, right now, I think, what am I using?

I'm using a Blue Yeti microphone for this particular video. But typically for all of our video sales letters, I use an Audio-Technica. I couldn't find the model number on there, but it's an Audio-Technica that's plugged in
with like an XLR into like a little soundboard. And so it's just a better quality audio that you get from that mic. Today, there's a lot of quality microphones that you can get.

Even USB mics that are just as good as some studio mics. You just want to make sure that you've got a good quality microphone. And then you record that audio, like I said, using a tool like Adobe Audition. Remove the background noise and produce a great audio. Then what you want to do is you want to export the audio in the highest quality form that you
can, the highest quality format that you can.

So when you record this audio separately for your VSL, don't export it as an MP3. That's a compressed file. And so you're losing quality in the audio file if you export it as an MP3. So export it as a pure WAV file, again, in the best format that you can. Once you've done that, then you create the slides.

Now, typically, up until recently, the way I would create all these slides for the ugly
video sales letters is I would just go through the script and one by one, I would copy and
paste the text onto a slide. Really time consuming, but it is what it is, right? Taking your script and putting it into PowerPoint or what I use on the Mac is Keynote. Now, one little quick tip when you're creating your ugly video sales letter slides is it's
better to have more slides with less text than less slides with more text. The animation, the movement on the screen keeps attention.

And so you're better off having a lot more slides. We've had VSLs that have been 12 to 15 minutes that have been 300 and some odd slides. And so you want more movement. So you're better off chopping up longer sentences, certainly longer paragraphs, putting less text
on each slide so that you have more movement. Once you have the slides created, then what you do is you record the slides.

And I use a screen capture software on the Mac called ScreenFlow. You can use Camtasia on the Mac. You can also use Camtasia on the PC. Here's the way this works. So when recording the slides, what we do is we play, we press play on the audio file that
we just exported, the WAV file.

And then we press record on ScreenFlow in our case. And then we press play on the keynote slide presentation that we just created. So basically what we're doing is we are playing the audio. We are presenting the keynote slides and we're recording the keynote slides with the screen
capture software. Now, one tip that I forgot to mention that's important is when you record the audio, when
you record the VSL script, leave about 15 seconds of dead air at the very beginning.

I typically use that dead air for two reasons. One, when removing the noise using Adobe Audition. So in other words, that 15 seconds of dead air gives the software the background noise,

you can find online if you Google it. The second thing that that 15 seconds of dead air does is it gives you the ability to press
play on something like, you know, quick time when you press play on the audio and then
it'll, you know, the 15 seconds will start.

Press play on the slide presentation and press record on ScreenFlow. So your slides will open up, ScreenFlow will be recording, and then after 15 seconds, your
audio file will begin. And then what you want to do is you just progress through the slides according to the audio. So basically, if you recorded a 15-minute VSL script, right, if the file is 15 minutes,
for 15 minutes, you're just sitting at the computer, listening to the audio, progressing through
your slides based on the audio. At the end, you will have this screen capture of your slides and the audio that came from
your computer speakers.

Then what you want to do is you want to replace the audio on the screen capture with the audio
file that you exported in WAV format. So basically, what you're doing is you are removing the poor quality audio from the screen

ScreenFlow, you're going to have two tracks inside of your, typically two tracks, at least

So in ScreenFlow, what we do is we just import the WAV file, and then we drag the WAV file
onto as its own track, right? And we line up the WAV file with the audio from the screen capture, just to make sure
that it's lined up. And then we mute the audio track from the screen capture. So basically, we're getting rid of the audio file, the poor quality audio file that we captured
with the screen capture. Hopefully, that makes sense.

Once you've done that, then you simply export the file. And depending on the platform that you're going to use to distribute or play your video,

We use Vimeo for most of our educational videos. We use Wistia for a lot of our funnel videos that we're driving a lot of external traffic

There's nothing worse than having an ugly video sales letter that either has really bad audio,

you follow.
