## Understanding Mechanisms

### What is a Mechanism?

In order to talk about what is a unique mechanism, we need to first and foremost talk about what is a mechanism.

Every product or service that produces a result - any product or service that works to deliver a result, to deliver an outcome, to deliver a change, to deliver a solution to a problem - has a mechanism.

**The mechanism is how the product or service works to deliver that result.** It's how the product or service works to deliver the result. That is what's called a mechanism.

### Ordinary or Commoditized Mechanism

Out in the wild, you're going to see a lot of products and services that have what I call an ordinary or a commoditized mechanism. A commoditized mechanism, or an ordinary mechanism, is your common mechanism. You can use these descriptions interchangeably.

**A commoditized mechanism is a mechanism that is available elsewhere.** It's available in other products or services.

#### Example: Magnesium Sulfate

Let's say it's 500 milligrams of magnesium sulfate. If we put together a marketing campaign and we talked about this fancy product to lower blood pressure, and we said: "The secret in our product to lower your blood pressure is 500 milligrams of magnesium sulfate."

All we've done in that case is introduced a commoditized mechanism. We've just sold the prospect on a mechanism that they can get anywhere else.

If we educate them saying the secret is that this product contains 500 milligrams of magnesium sulfate - that's why it's able to lower blood pressure - we've introduced a commoditized mechanism, a commoditized solution.

This allows the prospect to say: "Thank you so much for informing me for educating me on this mechanism on magnesium sulfate." Then they pause your video sales letter, go over to Amazon and search for magnesium sulfate 500 milligrams. They go to Google, the vitamin shop, a health food store.

**You've now just sold them on a commoditized mechanism.** You've opened up the door for them to go and find that elsewhere, more convenient, less expensive.

### The Problem with Commoditized Mechanisms

We never ever want to present a commoditized mechanism. What we want to create is an **"all roads lead to you" solution**, meaning you are offering a solution that is proprietary to you. You are the only purveyor of that solution. They cannot go and get it from Amazon. They cannot go and get it on Google. They cannot go and get it anywhere else.

**We never ever present a commoditized mechanism. We only present unique mechanisms.**

## What is a Unique Mechanism?

A unique mechanism is the system, the process, the framework, the method - any of those - behind your product or service, which produces the result for the prospect.

- It's the reason why
- It's the method behind your approach
- It's the system, the steps, the framework, the approach
- It's how and why your product or service is able to fulfill the promise

**And it's unique, meaning it's proprietary. It's only available through you, period.**

### Characteristics of a Unique Mechanism

A unique mechanism:

1. **Is the reason why your product or service is able to produce the result**
2. **Produces a better result** - either faster, easier, more consistent, or just bigger
3. **Is different from the mechanisms in other products and services**
4. **Is superior in terms of delivering results** to other products or services
5. **Is only available through you**

#### Example: Dr. Luke's Proprietary Method

For example, a unique herbal supplement prepared according to Dr. Luke's proprietary method. This isn't the greatest example of a unique mechanism, but the reality is that if you want a supplement to lower blood pressure that is based on Dr. Luke's proprietary method, that is the only source - that's the only place that you can get it.

## Unique Mechanism vs. Ordinary Mechanism

### Key Differences

| Unique Mechanism | Ordinary Mechanism |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Proprietary - only available through you | Common - available elsewhere |
| Prevents the "Google slap trigger" | Stimulates the "Google slap trigger" |
| Only leads back to you | Leads to others |
| Creates demand for YOUR offer | Creates demand for the commoditized mechanism |

### The Google Slap Trigger

**What is the Google slap trigger?**

The Google slap trigger is when somebody hears your solution, they hear your mechanism. When you say "the secret behind this approach is Facebook advertising" or "the secret behind this approach is retargeting" - common mechanisms - and then they say: "Thank you. Let me pause and Google 'retargeting' or 'Facebook advertising' or 'magnesium sulfate 500 milligrams.'"

The Google slap trigger is when you present this commoditized mechanism and it allows for the prospect to now go shop elsewhere, to now go do an apples-to-apples comparison.

**We want to eliminate an apples-to-apples comparison.** We don't ever want them to say "I can go and find this elsewhere."

With a unique mechanism, if they were to go to Google, the reality is that over time, the only things that they're going to find are things that bring them back to you. The only thing that's going to show up are links back to your campaign, your marketing campaign, or your website, or the places that you're talking about your unique mechanism.

## Real-World Examples

### The Chiropractor Example

Many years ago, I worked with chiropractors helping them to grow their practice and do better, more effective marketing. Most chiropractors would say: "My job is to really educate people on chiropractic care and the value of chiropractic care and what chiropractic care can do for somebody."

I would immediately say to them: **"No, your job is not to convince somebody of the value of chiropractic care. Your job is to convince them of the value of chiropractic care with you and what chiropractic care with you can do for them."**

If all we're doing is educating on chiropractic care, all we're doing is educating on the commoditized mechanism. All we're doing is leading the prospect to want that commoditized mechanism, leading them elsewhere, leading them to shop around, giving them the ability to say: "Oh, let me see if there's a book on that. Let me see if there's a YouTube video on that. Let me see if there's somebody cheaper."

## The Power of Unique Mechanisms

### Creating Demand Before the Offer

One of the beautiful things about the unique mechanism is that when you base your marketing around a unique mechanism:

1. **You prevent that Google slap**
2. **You create this "all roads lead to you" approach**
3. **You actually create demand for your offer before you even present the offer**

Before you ever even tell the prospect about how they can spend money with you, you've created demand for that. In essence, what you've really done is **you've pre-sold them on your offer**.

Before we present the offer (which is how they get the unique mechanism, how they benefit from the unique mechanism, how they learn the unique mechanism, how they can experience the transformation of the unique mechanism), we've led the prospect to want the unique mechanism - to see that it's not only different, but it's better than every other method out there.

Once we've shown them that your unique mechanism is different and superior, they want it. **And the only place for them to get it is through your offer.** They cannot go and get it anywhere else.

### Contrast with Ordinary Mechanisms

Whereas the ordinary mechanism simply creates demand for the product type or for the commoditized mechanism:

- If we educate somebody on the value and benefit of magnesium sulfate 500 milligrams, all we've done is created demand for 500 milligrams of magnesium sulfate
- If we educate on the value of chiropractic care for relieving somebody's back pain, all we've done is created demand for chiropractic care

**That's not what we want to do.** We want to create demand for your offer, for your product, for your service. We want to create where the only way that they can benefit from this unique mechanism, the only place that you can get the unique mechanism, is from you.

## Unique Selling Proposition vs. Unique Mechanism

### What is a Unique Selling Proposition (USP)?

The unique selling proposition (USP) has been around for many years. It was the brainchild of a direct response legend by the name of Rosser Reeves. It came about in the early days of direct response marketing.

**A USP is a unique benefit offered only by your product or service and not available through any other competitors.**

It's when you have a product or service that is able to deliver a benefit - a desirable benefit that your prospect wants - and you are the only one in your marketplace who has a product or service that is able to deliver that benefit.

### USPs Are Rare Today

The reality is that today, unique selling propositions are rare. The overwhelming majority of products and services don't today have a USP. That's okay. That's common.

Typically you see USPs associated with:
- New technologies
- True innovations
- Disruptive products or disruptive technology that's been introduced

#### Examples of USPs

**When Uber was introduced:** That introduced a whole new benefit that you couldn't get anywhere else - security, you could pull it up on your phone and have a car there in minutes, you don't have to have cash on you, you don't have to give a tip. At the time, that was a USP.

**When Netflix first came about:** The streaming platform was a USP. You no longer had to go to the store, you didn't have to return DVDs, you didn't have any late fees, you got access to everything instantaneously.

### Most Don't Have a USP - And That's Okay

It's far more likely that you do not have, or do not offer, a benefit or benefits that your competitors don't. A couple of you may, but it's possible. Often when I'm with clients and they say "I offer a benefit that nobody else does," usually when we dig a little bit, that's not the case, or it's just not really a benefit - they're talking about a feature of the product or service, and that feature doesn't even really deliver a benefit that is super compelling to the marketplace.

**More likely than not, the majority of you will not have a USP for your product or service. That's okay.**

### The Beauty of Unique Mechanisms

**The beauty is that you don't have to differentiate with a USP. You can differentiate with a unique mechanism.**

When you have a unique benefit, truly a unique benefit that your product or service offers, you're able to differentiate what it is that you do with that unique benefit. If you do have a USP, that becomes the differentiator - your product, your service is the only one that offers XYZ benefit.

**But when you don't have a USP, when your product or service offers the same benefits, the same outcome, the same result as what your competitors are promising, that's where we use a unique mechanism.**

### The Key Difference

**USP:** A unique benefit that is only offered by your product or service (rare)

**Unique Mechanism:** The unique way that the product or service works to deliver the result - the same result being promised by competitors (easily identified)

The difference between USP and a unique mechanism is:

- **The unique mechanism is how the product or service works to deliver the result** - it's the unique way that the product or service works to deliver the result, the same result being promised by competitors
- **A USP is a unique benefit** that is only offered by your product or service

For USP, it's rare. For a unique mechanism, it is easily identified.
