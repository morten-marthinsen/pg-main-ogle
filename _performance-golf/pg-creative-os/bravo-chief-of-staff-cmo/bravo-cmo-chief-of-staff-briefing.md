# PERFORMANCE GOLF
## CMO Chief of Staff Agent Briefing
**Prepared by the CMO | February 2026**

## 1. High-Level Vision

I joined Performance Golf as CMO approximately one year ago because I recognized a significant opportunity. The company had strong traction, a large and growing customer database, a talented team, and genuine product-market fit for everyday golfers that differentiated it from other golf brands. In conversations with our CEO and founder, Brixton Albert, it became clear there was a real desire to evolve from a direct-response company into a legitimate, enduring brand.

The legacy model was built on paid ads driving traffic to sales pages with long-form VSLs that would upsell products at the point of conversion. This approach required every touchpoint to do the heavy lifting of communicating the brand, explaining the product, establishing credibility, and converting the customer, all in a single interaction with someone who had little to no prior awareness of who we are.

When I assessed Performance Golf's innovation pipeline, product quality, marketing team horsepower, and the founder's story, I saw the clear potential to build a brand that would fuel organic awareness, reduce dependency on paid acquisition, and simultaneously make our paid efforts more effective. I have been at companies before that invested significantly in brand-building while spending less on performance advertising. The results were always strong: higher lifetime value, deeper customer loyalty, and a more defensible market position. It took longer, and the metrics looked different early on, but it compounded over time.

### The Current Reality

What makes Performance Golf's situation unique is that the business was built almost entirely on paid media. As a result, customers are not brand-loyal. Their purchasing behavior is driven by price, promotions, and our ability to outspend competitors on Google. When the ad spend stops, the traffic disappears. There is no lasting residual impact from marketing activities because the entire model operates as an ignite-and-burn cycle: launch an offer, pour fuel on it if it converts, abandon it if it doesn't, and repeat. Nothing compounds. Nothing is evergreen. It is a constant state of tactical innovation, new offers, and new funnels.

Currently, our customers understand Performance Golf as a company that makes unique, effective products but operates with a heavy sales approach. Some customers feel somewhat embarrassed by the brand's presentation, even though they know the products work. They return when there is a compelling deal, but there is limited emotional connection or brand loyalty beyond that.

### The Two-to-Three-Year Vision

My mission is to change that trajectory. In two to three years, I envision Performance Golf having a community and customer base that understands our singular mission: to help everyday golfers, the players with real flaws in their game, play better golf faster. Customers will understand that we are building specifically for them, with genuine innovation behind every product and a passionate founding story behind everything we do.

### The Transition Challenge

The core challenge of any transformation like this is executing the shift without destroying the growth engine that built a $125 million-plus business over eight years. The objective is to transition the team, the processes, and the organizational mindset gradually so that in two to three years the business is operating on a fundamentally different model, one that blends brand equity with performance marketing, without experiencing a dramatic revenue disruption along the way.

The human element of this transition is the most complex variable. People have been trained in specific methods for eight years. They rely on what they know works. They reference historical data to justify current tactics. Many are resistant to change, and the moment a new approach underperforms, there is an instinctive reversion to legacy tactics.

There is nothing inherently wrong with using historical data to inform the future model; much of it reveals which hooks, angles, and products resonate with our audience. But reimagining how we market, borrowing inspiration from brands growing at faster rates with different strategies, requires time, education, alignment, and most critically, trust:

- Trust in leadership
- Trust in the vision
- Trust in the plan
- Trust in the willingness to fail forward

### Talent and Organizational Transformation

A key component of this transition has been identifying who within the current organization can evolve into this new way of operating versus those who are entrenched in legacy thinking. We have spent considerable time evaluating the team and have made difficult decisions, including terminations and organizational restructuring, to address cultural and capability gaps.

However, we have not yet backfilled the right roles with the right talent, specifically people who bring a new level of creative thinking, ownership, and momentum to push Performance Golf into a brand-plus-performance model. Identifying, recruiting, and onboarding this talent is a critical priority that directly intersects with the Chief of Staff function: I need protected time to focus on ensuring we have the right people in the right seats, people who think about where Performance Golf can go rather than where it has been.

The talent gap also has a cascading effect on the broader team. Without empowered leaders at the mid-level who can own execution end to end, every strategic decision flows upward to a small group of senior stakeholders. This was manageable when the company had 10 to 15 people. With over 200 employees and the same handful of decision-makers, those senior leaders are trapped in the weeds, unable to dedicate bandwidth to strategy, growth, or forward-looking initiatives.

### Conviction in the Model

I want to be unambiguous about this: I know the model we are building works. Investing in brand is a proven marketing strategy with a long track record across industries. Performance Golf has the foundational DNA required to build a strong following and brand perception, with true product-market fit that can far exceed the ceiling we are hitting today under the current model.

The current direct-response-only approach has a glass ceiling. It will become increasingly expensive to operate, and if we do not evolve, we will not only fail to reach our potential but will begin to see decline. What we are building has been done successfully many times before, and it will work here if we remain consistent, adhere to the strategy, eliminate noise and distractions, and assemble the right team with clear ownership and creative freedom.

A historical note worth capturing: the previous solution for doing more was always to add more bodies. More headcount created more communication overhead, which meant more meetings, more Slack threads, more emails, and ironically, the same small group of stakeholders still making every decision. That dynamic is precisely what we need to break, and it is directly connected to the talent, AI empowerment, and process improvements this Chief of Staff agent is designed to help facilitate.

## 2. Role & Responsibilities

### Typical Day Structure

My current schedule is heavily dominated by meetings. Monday through Wednesday and portions of Thursday are filled with back-to-back meetings from approximately 9:00 AM to 3:30 or 4:00 PM. These include alignment sessions across departments (paid media, email, partnerships), meetings with direct reports, and calls with external partners (Shopify contractors, PR agency, partnerships consultants, and others).

By the time I have any uninterrupted time to focus on actual work, it is typically a 90-minute block at the end of the day, or I end up working late at night, which is not sustainable. Thursday afternoons and Fridays are generally more open and represent my primary windows for execution and strategic thinking.

Even during those open windows, a significant portion of time is consumed by responding to Slack messages: fielding questions, providing status updates, making decisions the team should be making independently, connecting people on cross-functional projects, and managing the constant stream of ideas and requests that arrive without any formal intake process. My day is frequently derailed by ad-hoc messages requesting visibility into projects I am already actively working on. Stakeholders will ask about a project's status repeatedly, even after being briefed, because there is no centralized system providing ongoing visibility.

### Core Functions and Scope

As CMO, the following functions and teams roll up to me:

- **Creative Organization**: Responsible for all creative output across the company, including paid ad creative, email design, UX and sales pages, brand-level activities (packaging design, event activations, partner decks, celebrity and influencer presentation materials), and overall visual identity.
- **Growth Department**: Encompasses new customer acquisition, paid advertising, paid ad creative, offer development, sales page creation, CRO, and back-end LTV optimization. The LTV function in particular is an area where I am not currently able to invest sufficient attention, despite it being one of the business's most critical initiatives.
- **Go-to-Market Management**: Product launch planning and execution, including the calendar of launches, campaign components, and cross-functional coordination for each go-to-market motion.
- **Partnerships**: Influencer program, affiliate program, event activations, brand collaborations, celebrity partnerships, and product seeding.
- **Organic Content and Social Media**: Content strategy aligned to the brand mission and marketing strategy, social media planning and execution. This team now sits within the creative structure but requires significant coordination and direction.

We are making a change in this role within the next week, transitioning leadership to Chris Ogle, who will lead the creative team into an AI-empowered organization that leverages automation and optimized workflows to deliver higher-quality output with fewer resources and less meeting overhead.
- **Todd (SVP, Growth)**: Oversees acquisition, new offer acquisition, scaling, CRO, and back-end LTV. The LTV workstream under Todd is one of the areas that most urgently needs more of my strategic attention.
- **Charlie Gross (Partnerships Manager)**: Manages influencers, email affiliates, partnerships, celebrity signings, and event activations. Requires very close collaboration with me. I am also evaluating whether we need a stronger fit in this role.
- **Jenny Nagel (Go-to-Market Director)**: Coordinates all product launches throughout the year, including planning and sequencing every step of each go-to-market motion.

### Key Cross-Functional Stakeholders

When visibility gaps exist, he will follow up, sometimes daily, to confirm items are being tracked. This is a completely fair expectation, but it creates a feedback loop where the time spent providing updates reduces the time available to execute the work itself.
- **CFO (Faraz)**: Heavily focused on LTV and repeat purchase metrics. We work together closely given that these numbers are currently below where they need to be for the business.
- **Chris McGinley (Chief Product Officer)**: Develops the new products on our GTM calendar. I work with him on product representation, delivery timelines, and ideation around naming, positioning, and launch timing.

## 3. Priorities & Goals

### Priority 1: Successful Product Launches with a 360-Degree Campaign Approach

Ensuring our 2026 product launches are planned thoroughly and executed successfully. The shift here is moving from our legacy approach (paid ads to sales pages and funnels with an email back-end component) to a full 360-degree campaign model that includes influencer marketing, partnerships, organic content, social media, product seeding, and other brand-building components. We have approximately two major launches per quarter from February through July on the current roadmap.

### Priority 2: Unlocking Lifetime Value (LTV)

Increasing repeat purchases from our existing customer base is arguably the single most impactful lever in the business. Our current LTV metrics are low for an e-commerce company, despite having a strong product catalog. We have a dedicated team focused on this, but I need significantly more time to work with them on strategy and execution. Contributing factors include brand positioning issues that may attract discount-oriented shoppers rather than brand-loyal customers, as well as opportunities in automated email and SMS workflows, app-triggered notifications, and broadcast campaigns. If we can achieve a 3:1 LTV-to-CAC ratio, it unlocks the funding and cash flow needed to drive every other initiative in the business. This is the single thing that, if it went well this quarter, would change everything.

### Priority 3: Getting the Team Right (AI-Empowered Organization)

This may be the true first priority. It involves not only placing the right people in the right roles but also transitioning the team into an AI-first operating model. The historical approach at Performance Golf was to scale output by adding headcount. We are reversing that by bringing in higher-caliber talent who can leverage AI to automate steps of the production and execution process, connect people without excessive meetings, and reduce the coordination overhead that currently bogs down the organization.

### Priority 4: Brand Rollout and Repositioning

Following the brand exercise and rebrand strategy completed in November, we need to roll the new brand identity across every touchpoint: the new website, email templates, packaging and box design, organic content and social media, the founder's story, PR outreach, partnership materials, celebrity and influencer programs, and event activations. The brand guidelines are documented in the skills section of this AI system. These brand-building activities compound over time but need to begin this quarter to make a meaningful impact. I cannot afford to delay another quarter on the website release, organic content momentum, influencer program retooling, or social media strategy.

### Company-Level KPIs

1. Revenue
2. LTV (Lifetime Value)
3. CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
4. Brand Awareness

The primary lens is how many customers we can acquire with a healthy LTV-to-CAC ratio. I am also tracking organic traffic and organic search as leading indicators of brand growth; as brand-building activities take hold, I want organic to become a larger portion of our traffic mix, which will bring down our blended CAC.

Specific metrics we monitor include trials entering our program, product units sold, whether our CAC supports scalable acquisition with LTV attached, and the success and timeline adherence of product launches.

### Sequencing and Capacity Concerns

The business is currently pursuing an aggressive number of initiatives, and the team is approaching burnout from being spread too thin. One of my key responsibilities is helping the business sequence priorities more effectively and communicating that sequencing back up to the CEO, CFO, and product team. Until the AI empowerment and systems improvements are in place to unlock additional capacity, we need to be more selective about what we take on in parallel.

## 4. Time & Energy

### Where I Want to Spend My Time

I want to be amplifying the brand and creating partnerships and incremental activities that put our platform in front of more users. We have a capable team that knows how to optimize funnels, build strong landing page experiences, create paid ads, and we are figuring out the email and LTV side. My highest-value contribution is creating ideas that compound growth and reduce our dependency on paid acquisition. The creative energy I get from connecting dots on partnerships, organic content ideas that lead to events, influencer strategies, and brand collaborations, those are the activities that amplify the brand and create lasting leverage.

### Where I Currently Spend My Time

The majority of my time is spent below the waterline: helping teams execute on landing pages and ads, getting the right people in place, calibrating KPIs, managing reporting, attending meetings, being looped into every conversation, and making decisions that should be made further down the org chart. I want a team that is fully connected and aligned, has clear priorities, and has the ownership to deliver independently, where I am reviewing work and being impressed by what they present because they already understand the mission, the strategies, and their role within them.

### Tasks That Should Not Require the CMO

Several categories of work regularly land on my desk that do not warrant CMO involvement: determining promotional pricing and discount percentages (where historical data should guide the decision), relaying information between meetings when attendees were not present (a communication system problem, not a leadership problem), and various tactical execution items that the team should own independently.

There are some execution tasks I do not mind doing, such as building strategy decks for celebrity partnerships or event activations like the ACC event. Building these helps me think through the strategy. However, as someone with a design background, I frequently end up designing these decks myself, which is not where a CMO's time should go.

### Meetings That Could Be Eliminated or Made Async

Many meetings include 10 to 20 people, with only two or three actively contributing. This is a cultural pattern we need to unwind. Because only a small number of people have a full 360-degree view of the business strategy, products, history, and future direction, those individuals get pulled into nearly every conversation. As my Chief of Staff, I need you to help protect my time and train the team toward empowered decision-making with smaller, more structured meetings, or to use AI tools to get answers themselves rather than defaulting to scheduling a meeting.

### Ideal Daily Schedule

- **Morning (before 10:00 AM)**: Protected focus time for clearing previous-day action items, preparing for upcoming meetings, and getting into the right headspace for the day.
- **Midday (10:00 AM to 2:00 PM)**: Meeting block for alignment, decisions, and collaboration.
- **Afternoon (2:00 PM onward)**: Strategic and creative time for deep work, brand thinking, partnership development, and guiding the team.

My best strategic thinking typically happens later in the day, but only if I have not been drained by wall-to-wall meetings. When I am in meetings all day with only an hour of real work time, my energy for deep thinking in the evening is gone.

## 5. Frustrations & Bottlenecks

### Recurring Fires

- **Unstructured idea flow**: Miscellaneous ideas arrive via Slack without being on the roadmap and without any formal submission or prioritization process. This needs to be solved.
- **Questions the team should answer independently**: People escalate questions that they have the context and authority to resolve on their own, or seek alignment on topics that were already covered. There is a persistent communication and alignment gap.
- **Visibility gaps**: Team members and stakeholders lack visibility into what is being worked on, leading to repeated status check-ins. People will ask three or four times about a project simply because they want confirmation it is tracked somewhere.
- **CEO follow-ups**: A significant amount of this comes from our CEO, which is entirely reasonable, but the cycle of being reminded about projects I am already working on diverts time from actually executing on those projects. This is one of the most energy-draining patterns in my day.

### Where Work Gets Stuck

Work stalls when ideas or strategies change in silos without being communicated to the broader team. Side conversations happen while projects are in flight, and when changes are not cascaded, misalignment and rework follow. Jenny, our Go-to-Market Manager, has done a strong job of helping centralize alignment, but she is now essentially chasing information full-time to keep everyone on the same page.

I want to build a system, ideally AI-powered, that serves as a shared source of truth. When the team pulls their execution orders and strategic guidance, it comes from this single source. When that source is updated, changes are automatically communicated to the affected team members with clear instructions on what they need to adjust.

Work also gets stuck in indecision. Although we value testing, there is a tendency toward pixel-perfect execution before launch, which slows us down. When something does launch and underperforms, the instinct is to move on to the next product or initiative rather than optimizing. I want us to ship our best effort, collect first-touch data, and optimize from there rather than getting stuck in the weeds pre-launch.

### Information I Am Constantly Hunting Down

- Current LTV metrics and trends
- Email performance across campaigns and automated flows
- Volume of ads created each week
- Whether the team has what they need to keep moving forward
- Whether we have adequate resourcing for active initiatives
- Whether scheduled meetings actually happened
- Whether people followed through on commitments

Beyond data, I spend significant time ensuring people are communicating with each other: sharing victories and celebrations, flagging urgent needs, broadcasting priority changes, and sending updates to the broader team. Everyone is busy, and proactive communication is often the first thing to slip.

## 6. Communication & Information Flow

### Preferred Communication Channels

My preferred channel for receiving updates is Slack, with email as a secondary option. I am open to evolving this format. For consuming detailed information, I prefer Loom videos and audio recordings over written summaries. I process information better when I can hear the person explain it rather than reading long blocks of text. I communicate the same way: I would rather talk through something than write a lengthy document.

I do not like long meetings where the substance could be covered in five minutes but takes an hour. Quick stand-ups are effective. The key issue with Slack is prioritization: I am highly responsive to messages, which means I get pulled into low-priority items in real time. I need a system that can triage incoming Slack messages, identifying which require immediate attention, which can wait, and which can be deferred entirely, so I can manage when to engage and when to stay in deep work.

### Reporting and Dashboards

I regularly check Domo for reporting, Shopify for sales data, organic traffic and content performance metrics, and GTM dashboards for project status. However, during busy periods, I do not review these consistently. I need these delivered to me in a concise format each morning or end of day so I can stay informed without having to go find the data.

A daily or weekly briefing summarizing what happened the previous day, where the week stands, and progress against goals, delivered in a way that does not derail my focus, would be a significant improvement.

### Task and Commitment Tracking

My current system is largely manual. I maintain a notepad on my computer that I update weekly. At the beginning of each week and each day, I map out high-priority items and carry over unfinished items from the prior period. I also use ClickUp, though I need to build out my board more effectively, or have the Chief of Staff maintain it on my behalf.

This system works to a point, but as volume increases, prioritization becomes difficult. I can default to whatever feels most urgent based on timeline, the seniority of the person requesting it, or who is loudest in the room, rather than staying disciplined on quarterly and annual goals and saying no to things that are not aligned.

I do not frequently drop things. Follow-ups rarely fall through the cracks. But I am sometimes slow to respond, with delays of a day or two, because I am working through a backlog. The bigger issue is depth: I am executing at roughly 70% quality across too many items rather than delivering at 100% on fewer, more strategic priorities.

### Repetitive Outputs with Predictable Structure

The team produces numerous recurring outputs that follow predictable patterns and could benefit from automation or templatization: ads performance reporting, ad creative production tracking, GTM calendar status updates, project workflow stages (research, copy, sales pages, emails, ads), organic content production processes, and more. These represent significant opportunities to build structured, repeatable systems. I want to inventory these with the Chief of Staff agent and systematically automate or streamline each one.

### Vendor and Agency Management

Several external relationships require coordination time. Most are managed by the team, but certain partnerships, particularly the PR agency, require hands-on involvement that I am struggling to find time for given my current meeting load.

### Meeting Preparation

Because my days are filled with meetings, much of my morning is spent preparing for what is coming up. This could be templated and automated: pulling notes and action items from previous meetings, ensuring those items are on my weekly priority list, and either delegating tasks with a clear feedback loop or flagging decisions I need to make before the meeting. There is also an opportunity to push meeting owners to send pre-reads, determine whether a meeting is necessary at all, or convert it to a Loom or Slack update.

### What I Would Hand Off to a Human Chief of Staff Tomorrow

1. Triage and prioritization: Organizing all incoming requests, questions, projects, and decisions, determining when to push back and tell the team to solve it themselves, and sequencing the items that do need my attention in the right order.
2. Meeting preparation: Templating and preparing me for each day's meetings, including what decisions need to be made and what context I need going in.
3. Vendor management: Owning the external relationships and bringing me in only when needed.
4. Calendar protection: Ensuring I am not in meetings I do not need to attend and that my focus blocks are defended.

## 8. Decision-Making Standards

### Non-Negotiables

1. **Brand mission alignment**: Everything we produce must point back to Performance Golf's brand mission. Sometimes that means a piece of content is not directly salesy, sometimes there is no hard call to action, but every output must ladder up to the themes and pillars of what our brand stands for.
2. **Premium creative quality**: We need to look like a brand that can compete with the best golf brands in the world. This means selecting quality imagery, presenting ourselves as trusted and professional, and delivering an experience that makes customers pleasantly surprised by the value relative to the price.
3. **Avoid defaulting to legacy playbooks**: A non-negotiable in campaign strategy is not doing something simply because we have always done it that way. Every campaign should consider what additional elements could make it more authentic, more connected, and more likely to reach users in the right place at the right time.
4. **Surround-sound campaigns**: We want campaigns where our brand and products are being discussed by credible, exciting, and entertaining voices beyond our own paid media. We should not depend solely on our media spend to carry the message, because that means we are the only ones talking about how great we are.

### Decision-Making Framework

I use our brand guidelines and brand positioning as the primary filter for every idea. My decision-making process starts with one question: Does this align to our brand position, brand message, and the mission we are communicating, which is the smarter journey of playing better golf in a faster way? If the answer is yes, I then back into logistics: What is possible? What would it cost? What is the expected return? How would our audience receive it? Do we have the talent on the team or do we need to resource it externally?

Everything comes back to brand coherence. If a customer saw this campaign, strategy, or product, would it reinforce Performance Golf's brand position? Or would it feel disconnected, like it came from an entirely different company?

### Common Creative Feedback Patterns

The most frequent creative feedback I give stems from a lack of holistic visibility among the people executing the work. Individual contributors are not connecting the dots between their piece of copy and how it relates to the creative, or between a video asset and how it aligns with the landing page, or between the landing page and the email follow-up. The result is inconsistency in theme, messaging, and positioning across the campaign. Most of my feedback is about connecting these elements into a cohesive narrative.

Additionally, because we come from a direct-response background, I find myself pushing back daily against overly salesy copy. We have great products with genuine product-market fit; we should not have to try this hard to sell them. I am constantly coaching the team to find the intersection between the look and feel of a premium brand that inspires trust and desire to buy, while still including the clear calls to action needed to convert. We are also fighting against legacy design conventions from traditional direct-response companies that convert well but do not look like a premium brand.

## 9. Personal Working Style

### Communication Style

I lead with the bigger picture and the why. I want people to understand the strategy and the purpose behind their tactics before diving into execution details. I try to paint the picture from an inspirational lens, framing the work in terms of what it means for Performance Golf and the customer rather than just what needs to get done.

I intentionally avoid getting lost in the details, partly because I do not have the time, but more importantly because I want people to feel empowered and take ownership of their work. Performance Golf has historically created a culture where people wait for the CEO, the chief copywriter, or other key stakeholders to tell them what to do. They are less practiced at thinking independently and owning a project end to end.

My approach is to share the vision and the why, offer some initial ideas to spark their thinking, and then expect them to come back with the detailed plan for how they would execute it and what they need to make it happen.

### What Drains My Energy

Being in too many meetings throughout the day is the single biggest energy drain. A close second is having to re-articulate and recap work I have already done. When I have to go back after a full, productive day and write up what happened, it feels repetitive and, frankly, like being micromanaged. I understand that visibility is critical for the organization, but I do not want to be the one compiling those summaries.

This is a specific and important function for the Chief of Staff agent: capturing what I am working on throughout the day and compiling it into reports and summaries (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly) so that I and the executive team have visibility without me having to manually reconstruct it.

### What Gives Me Energy

Being creative and thinking about new ways to build and grow the business. Partnership opportunities, creative campaigns, visuals that elevate the brand, connecting with influencers, event activations, brand collaborations, and reviewing fully connected campaigns to make sure every piece works together. These are the activities that give me energy and represent my highest-value contribution to Performance Golf.

### What I Am Actively Working to Change

I am actively working to spend more time helping people align to the vision, get excited about their work, and have the tools they need to stay connected. I want to bring on team members who share a sense of ownership and creativity, reduce time spent in repetitive meetings or providing status updates, and ultimately have a system, a shadow of sorts, that captures everything I do on a daily basis and recaps it in a way that can be tracked and measured by myself, our CEO, and the rest of the executive team.
