## P1 — The Chronic Slicer [DEEP]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Slices every round, has tried everything. Knows it's a problem but can't fix it. Been told to change his swing, take lessons, buy a different driver. Nothing sticks.
- **Primary Emotion**: Frustration compounded by shame
- **They'd Say**: "I've tried everything. Lessons, YouTube, new grips — nothing fixes my slice."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: The exhaustion of a problem that defines his game. The slice isn't just a miss — it's his identity on the course. His buddies expect it. He expects it. Every tee shot is a public performance of a flaw he can't fix. The shame isn't the slice itself — it's that he CAN'T fix it despite trying.
- **Physical Sensation**: The flinch before impact. Watching the ball start right and KNOWING where it's going. That sinking feeling before the ball even lands. The tight forearms from overgripping, trying to steer it straight.
- **Internal Dialogue**: "Here it goes again." "I know it's going to slice before I even swing." "Maybe I should just play a fade and pretend it's intentional." "Another 200 bucks on something that won't work."
- **Identity Statement**: He IS a golfer — plays regularly, cares about his game. But the slice has become his identity on the course. He's "the guy who slices." It's humiliating because it's PUBLIC and REPEATED.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: Something that ACTUALLY works. Not promises — proof. He's been burned by fixes before (lessons, training aids, YouTube drills). Needs overwhelming evidence.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: The combination of empathy ("you're not alone") and proof ("60,000 golfers found something"). He needs to feel SEEN before he'll believe.
- **Social Proof Function**: Validation + hope. "If 60,000 others had this same problem and THIS worked... maybe this time is different."

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "My slice is a swing problem I need to fix through practice and lessons."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "What if 60,000 golfers found something that actually works — and it had nothing to do with changing your swing?"
- **Visual Identity**: On camera or close-up B-roll of the slice experience — the grip, the flinch, the ball curving. Then the CONTRAST: straight drive, exhale of relief. The visual story is frustration -> hope.
- **Voice Direction**: Christopher on camera — empathetic, direct. Like a friend who gets it. NOT pitying. Tone: "I know what you're going through, and here's what I found."
- **Color Palette**: Neutral/natural. Course greens. Nothing stylized — keep it real and relatable.

## P2 — The Equipment Skeptic [DEEP]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Burned before by "miracle" golf products. Sees ads and thinks "yeah right." Needs overwhelming proof to believe anything works.
- **Primary Emotion**: Distrust armoring guarded hope
- **They'd Say**: "I've been burned by golf gadgets before. Why would this be any different?"

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: Betrayed trust. He's bought the miracle training aid, the "game-changing" driver, the online program that promised 30 yards. None of them worked. Each failed purchase made the armor thicker. But underneath the cynicism is a man who WANTS to believe — he just can't afford to be the sucker again.
- **Physical Sensation**: The eye-roll reflex when he sees a golf ad. Arms crossing. That internal "yeah, right" wall going up before the first sentence is finished. The tension of wanting to believe but not letting himself.
- **Internal Dialogue**: "If this actually worked, everyone would know about it." "Another miracle driver — seen it a thousand times." "I'll believe it when I see the return rate." "My wife is going to kill me if I buy another golf thing that doesn't work."
- **Identity Statement**: He's the pragmatist. The one who warns his buddies about gimmicks. His skepticism is a badge — it proves he's smart, not gullible. Buying something that doesn't work isn't just a waste of money — it's an identity threat.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: PROOF he can't argue with. Not testimonials (those are fake). Not claims (those are inflated). He wants data: return rates, specific numbers, volume proof. The kind of evidence that would survive his cross-examination.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: The moment his skepticism cracks — when one piece of evidence is too strong to dismiss. "60,000 golfers weren't coming back for a refund" works because it addresses his EXACT objection (returns = the BS test).
- **Social Proof Function**: Proof of quality, not permission. He doesn't care that others bought it — he cares that others KEPT it. Non-return data > purchase data.

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "Every golf product ad is BS designed to separate me from my money."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "What if the reason 60,000 golfers AREN'T returning this is that it actually works? What if your skepticism — which has protected you from gimmicks — is now keeping you from the one thing that isn't?"
- **Visual Identity**: Christopher on camera — he has to MODEL the skeptic's journey. Opening energy: skeptical, arms-crossy, "I used to be you." Then the shift. Consider opening with a quick visual flash of generic "miracle driver" ads (split screen or over-the-shoulder) before Christopher's reaction — gives Meta a visually distinct first frame from H1.
- **Voice Direction**: Christopher on camera — skeptical-then-convinced tone. The arc matters: he starts WHERE THEY ARE (rolling his eyes) and ends where he wants them to go (convinced by evidence). Never dismissive of their skepticism — it's VALIDATED before it's redirected.
- **Color Palette**: Slightly desaturated opening (matching the "I've seen this before" energy), warming as the hook progresses.

## P3 — The Peer-Influenced Golfer [BASELINE]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Doesn't research deeply. Buys what his buddies are talking about. If everyone in his foursome says something works, he's in.
- **Primary Emotion**: FOMO + social belonging
- **They'd Say**: "If everyone's hitting it, I don't want to be the only one still slicing."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: Not a deep wound — more a social itch. He doesn't want to be the last one in his group still using old equipment. When his buddy pulls out a new driver and crushes one, his stomach drops slightly — not jealousy exactly, just "I want that too."
- **Physical Sensation**: The moment his buddy's drive sails past his by 30 yards. Watching the group admire someone else's new club. That slight tightness of being left out.
- **Internal Dialogue**: "What driver is that?" "Where'd you get it?" "Is it worth it?" "If Dave and Mike both switched, it must be good."
- **Identity Statement**: Social golfer. Golf is about the group, not the scorecard. He buys what his network validates. He trusts his buddies more than any ad or review.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: Peer validation above all. If his foursome says it works, that's the most powerful ad in the world.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: Seeing/hearing someone he trusts use and endorse the product. Volume proof ("60,000") works but personal endorsement works better.
- **Social Proof Function**: Direct purchase motivation. This is the one persona where social proof isn't just supporting evidence — it IS the reason to buy.

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: N/A — this persona doesn't need a belief challenged. He needs social proof delivered through the right channel (peers, not ads).
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "Everyone in your foursome is already using this. You're the last one still slicing."
- **Visual Identity**: Group golf energy — buddies on the course, one guy crushing it with a new driver, others asking about it. The ad should LOOK like a group conversation, not a pitch.
- **Voice Direction**: TBD — Christopher could work if framed as a "buddy telling you." AIVO could work if framed as a narrator describing a social scene. Depends on the creative format.
- **Color Palette**: Warm, social, natural light. Group energy.

## P4 — The Smart Shopper [DEEP]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Researches before buying. Wants value for money. Compares specs, reads reviews. Not impulsive — needs to feel like this is the intelligent choice.
- **Primary Emotion**: The satisfaction of making the smart call
- **They'd Say**: "I want the best driver for MY game, not what TaylorMade tells me to buy."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: Not a wound in the pain sense — a DRIVE. His deepest fear is being the guy who got sold. Being a sucker. He needs to feel like he CONCLUDED this was the right call, not that he was persuaded. The purchase decision IS an intelligence test — if he buys something smart, he wins. If he gets marketed into a bad buy, he loses.
- **Physical Sensation**: The leaning-in feeling when he encounters genuinely new information. Eyes narrowing at a spec sheet. That satisfying click when data confirms an intuition. Conversely: the instant tension when something feels like "marketing" — his walls go UP.
- **Internal Dialogue**: "What's actually different about this driver?" "Can I see the engineering, not just the marketing?" "What does this do that my Callaway doesn't?" "If 60,000 people bought this and the data checks out... there's something here."
- **Identity Statement**: The researcher. The one who finds the diamond in the rough before anyone else. He doesn't buy popular — he buys SMART. His identity is wrapped in making better decisions than the average buyer.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: DATA. SPECS. ENGINEERING. COMPARISON. He wants the product to earn his decision, not charm him into it. He also values being early — finding the smart choice before the masses.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: The "I didn't know that" moment. One genuinely new piece of information that his existing research didn't surface. Per the Golf Suggestibility Principle, this persona is the purest expression of "new information creates the emotional response." He literally gets a dopamine hit from discovering something he didn't know.
- **Social Proof Function**: Volume as quality signal. He trusts consensus of many over endorsement of few. 60,000 = product-market fit at scale, not emotional safety. He's thinking: "This many people can't all be wrong — there's real signal in this data."

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "Draw-biased drivers sacrifice distance for forgiveness — that's the tradeoff." (Or any established belief about driver design he considers settled knowledge.)
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: One mechanism insight that challenges what he THINKS he knows. Not stats (he distrusts marketing stats). A "how it works" reveal that earns the next 10 seconds. "Most draw drivers sacrifice distance. This one was engineered to do both. Here's how."
- **Visual Identity**: Data visualization, engineering diagrams, spec comparisons, blueprint overlays. This ad should LOOK like research material, not advertising. Clean, modern typography (Bloomberg/Reuters energy). When his feed shows this, his brain categorizes it as "information" not "ad" — and that's exactly how you get past his wall.
- **Voice Direction**: NO VO in hook (visual-only pattern interrupt). Body transitions to Christopher as the engineering expert. The Smart Shopper trusts expertise presented through data, then validated by a knowledgeable human — in that order.
- **Color Palette**: Cool, clean, slightly dark. Tech/engineering palette. Apple product reveal or Tesla spec page energy. Noticeably different from the warm tones of emotional hooks — this visual temperature difference helps Meta distinguish it at the pixel level.

## P5 — The Comeback Golfer [DEEP]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Used to play regularly, fell off. Getting back into it. Doesn't want to start over with lessons — wants gear that helps him pick up where he left off.
- **Primary Emotion**: Nostalgia tinged with grief for a version of himself
- **They'd Say**: "I used to shoot in the 80s. I just need to get my drive back."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: The gap between who he WAS on the course and who he IS now — and that gap is about more than golf. This is a man in his 50s or 60s who used to have a handicap. He had a game. Then life happened — kids, career, maybe a back issue, maybe just decades of not playing. Now he's standing on the first tee feeling like a stranger in a place that used to feel like home. The club feels heavier. The swing feels foreign. His body won't do what his mind remembers. This isn't just "I want to play again." It's grief for a version of himself. The Comeback Golfer isn't buying a driver — he's buying RESTORATION.
- **Physical Sensation**: Standing on the first tee and feeling like a stranger. The club feeling heavier than it used to. The swing feeling foreign — the body won't cooperate with the muscle memory. That specific frustration of KNOWING what the swing should feel like but not being able to produce it. And then — when a shot does go right — the rush of recognition. "There it is. That's the swing I remember."
- **Internal Dialogue**: "I used to drive it 250 down the middle." "My body just won't cooperate anymore." "I don't want to start over with lessons — I don't have time for that." "Maybe it's just age." "That felt like the old me."
- **Identity Statement**: He used to be "a golfer." Not a casual player — a golfer. He had a handicap, a regular game, probably a regular foursome. That identity has been on pause, not deleted. He doesn't want to learn a new game — he wants his OLD game back. Starting over as a beginner is an identity threat. He needs to feel like he's RETURNING, not starting over.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: RESTORATION, not renovation. Equipment that works WITH what his body still knows, not equipment that requires him to rebuild. Zero learning curve. The feeling of picking up where he left off.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: The reframe from "I've lost my skill" to "my equipment needs to catch up to me." This single insight shifts the problem from HIM (aging, rusty, out of practice) to the DRIVER (outdated, not built for his needs). That reframe is emotionally liberating — it tells him he's NOT starting over.
- **Social Proof Function**: PEERS. Not abstract volume proof — other men his age who came back and found it works. "60,000 golfers" becomes "people who were in my exact position chose this, and it worked." The social proof is personal, not statistical.

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "I need lessons and months of practice to get my game back. I've gotten too rusty/old."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "Your body still remembers the swing. It's the equipment that needs to catch up to the golfer you still are." This reframe is emotionally powerful because it tells him the golfer is still IN THERE. He hasn't lost it — it's been waiting.
- **Visual Identity**: Cinematic. Warm. An older golfer (50s-60s, real-looking, NOT a model) on the course. Close on his hands (older hands, weathered). The private moment where muscle memory fires and the ball does something he forgot his body could do. Optional: a brief flash of his younger self (same swing mechanics, different decade) — a faded photo on the cart, or a split-second flashback. The visual metaphor: the past lives in the present.
- **Voice Direction**: AIVO — mature male, 50s-60s via ElevenLabs. Warm, reflective, unhurried. Porch-conversation energy — a narrator who understands what it means to come back to something you love. Think Peter Coyote in a reflective mode. Slower delivery than H3 — this hook earns its emotional weight through pacing, not speed.
- **Color Palette**: Golden hour. Warm amber tones. Morning mist on the course. Cinematic but not glossy — real warmth, not manufactured. Noticeably different from H3's brighter Saturday-morning energy.

## P6 — The Gift Buyer [BASELINE]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Buying for a golfer in their life (husband, dad, friend). Doesn't know golf tech — needs to trust the product is genuinely special and appreciated.
- **Primary Emotion**: Desire to impress + uncertainty
- **They'd Say**: "I want to get him something he'll actually use, not another golf gimmick."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: The anxiety of buying in a domain they don't understand. They don't know what a "draw-biased driver" is. They can't evaluate specs. Their fear: buying something expensive that ends up in the garage — and looking clueless for buying it.
- **Physical Sensation**: That blank stare at golf equipment online. Overwhelmed by jargon. The relief when something has clear social proof ("if 60,000 people bought this, it must be safe").
- **Internal Dialogue**: "I don't know what any of this means." "He's impossible to buy for." "I just want to get him something he'll actually USE." "Will he think this is a gimmick?"
- **Identity Statement**: Not a golfer — a gift giver. A spouse, a parent, a friend who cares enough to find the right thing. Their identity in this purchase is "I found something special."

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: Social proof as safety net ("60,000 golfers chose this"), gift-worthiness (it feels premium, not gimmicky), guarantee/return policy (safety from the "what if he doesn't like it" fear).
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: Confidence that this will be appreciated. Testimonials from RECIPIENTS ("my wife got me this and...") are more powerful than user reviews.
- **Social Proof Function**: Risk elimination. "If 60,000 golfers love it, he will too." Volume proof converts uncertainty into confidence.

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "I can't buy golf equipment because I don't understand golf."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "You don't need to know golf. You just need to know this is the one 60,000 golfers chose — and they can return it for a full year if it's not right."
- **Visual Identity**: Gift-giving context. NOT a golf course — a home, a birthday, a holiday. Someone wrapping it. Someone opening it. The golfer's face when he realizes it's the real thing. The ad speaks to the GIVER, not the player.
- **Voice Direction**: TBD — potentially female VO or a relatable "I had no idea what to get him" story approach. Christopher likely doesn't resonate here. Consider a warm, reassuring female AIVO or a male AIVO in "husband who received this as a gift" testimonial frame.
- **Color Palette**: Warm, domestic. Holiday/birthday tones depending on seasonal targeting. Premium but approachable.

## P7 — The Tech/Innovation Enthusiast [BASELINE]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Loves new technology in all areas. Drawn to engineering stories, materials science, "how it works." The aerospace-grade titanium and SR-71 story would captivate him.
- **Primary Emotion**: Wonder + early-adopter pride
- **They'd Say**: "Aerospace-grade forged titanium? Tell me more about the engineering."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: Not a wound — a HUNGER. He's drawn to innovation the way P5 is drawn to nostalgia. His satisfaction comes from understanding HOW things work and being among the first to adopt what's genuinely new. His frustration: most golf equipment marketing is surface-level hype with no engineering substance.
- **Physical Sensation**: The leaning-in feeling when someone explains manufacturing processes. Eyes widening at material science details. The satisfaction of understanding a mechanism — "so THAT'S how it works."
- **Internal Dialogue**: "How is the face forged?" "What makes this different at the molecular level?" "3-piece construction — what does each piece do?" "This is actually interesting engineering."
- **Identity Statement**: Early adopter. Appreciates craftsmanship. He's the guy who reads the specs page before the reviews. He buys innovation, not marketing — and he can tell the difference.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: The engineering story. Materials science. The "how it works" narrative. He wants the product to TEACH him something about its construction. If he learns something genuinely new about driver design, he's hooked.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: The "under the hood" reveal. Show him the engineering and he'll sell himself. He doesn't need convincing — he needs information.
- **Social Proof Function**: Validation of his assessment. He decides based on engineering merit first, then checks social proof to confirm his analysis. "60,000 golfers" = "my assessment was correct."

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "Golf equipment innovation peaked with the major brands. Nothing new is genuinely novel."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "The same forging process behind the SR-71 Blackbird — applied to a golf club. Here's what that means for face angle at impact."
- **Visual Identity**: Technical. Engineering diagrams, cross-sections, exploded views, callout lines. Looks like a product teardown video or engineering presentation. This is sf2-0003's territory (Brand Values commercial).
- **Voice Direction**: Christopher works well here — the engineering story needs a knowledgeable presenter who can make technical detail accessible. Alternatively: a more technical AIVO (documentary narrator energy, think "How It's Made").
- **Color Palette**: Clean, modern, slightly industrial. Steel/titanium tones. Engineering aesthetic.

## P8 — The Weekend Warrior [DEEP]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Plays once a week or less. Not trying to go pro — just wants to enjoy the round and not embarrass himself off the tee. Low practice time.
- **Primary Emotion**: Dread masquerading as recreation
- **They'd Say**: "I just want to have fun out there without dreading every tee shot."

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: Golf has become something he endures, not enjoys. He plays once a week — Saturday morning. It's supposed to be the best part of his weekend. But the tee shot has turned into a public performance of his worst skill. He DREADS the one moment everyone's watching. The shame isn't explosive (like P1's chronic slicer frustration) — it's a low-grade anxiety that takes the fun out of something that's supposed to BE fun. He can't even admit this to his buddies because "it's just a game."
- **Physical Sensation**: Tight grip on the driver before the first tee. Chest getting heavy. Shoulders climbing. Already calculating whether he can lay up with an iron and avoid the driver entirely. That specific relief when a tee shot just... goes straight — the exhale, the loosening grip, the moment where golf is fun again.
- **Internal Dialogue**: "I should just hit a hybrid off the tee." "This is supposed to be fun." "I don't even want to hit driver in front of these guys." "Maybe I'll just say I'm warming up my iron game today." "Why do I keep paying to do something I dread?"
- **Identity Statement**: NOT a serious golfer. Not even close. A dad. A weekend guy. A man whose life has enough stress — golf is supposed to be the escape, not another source of it. He plays for the social aspect, the outdoors, the beer at the turn. Golf is recreation, not religion. He will never practice five days a week and he knows it.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: SIMPLICITY. EASE. Zero practice required. No lessons. No work. No learning curve. He doesn't have time to fix things — he has one Saturday morning round and he wants to enjoy it. The product needs to feel effortless.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: The contrast between dread and relief. "This could be easy. I don't have to change anything." The reframe from "I need to get better" to "I just need different equipment" is emotionally liberating because it removes the WORK he doesn't have time for.
- **Social Proof Function**: Permission. Safety. "If 60,000 regular guys like me are using it, I won't look stupid buying it." He doesn't research — so social proof IS his research. Volume = validation = safe to buy.

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "Enjoying golf means getting better at golf. I need to practice more, take lessons, put in the work."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "It's not a skill problem — it's an equipment problem. You don't need to change your swing, practice more, or take lessons. You need a driver that does the hard part for you."
- **Visual Identity**: Saturday morning. Casual clothes. Natural light. A regular-looking guy (not athletic, not polished) pulling clubs from his trunk, joking with buddies on the cart path. Nothing aspirational — deeply relatable. He looks like your neighbor. Consider split-screen for the key moment: left = tense grip (frozen), right = easy swing (in motion). The contrast IS the ad.
- **Voice Direction**: AIVO — mature male, 50s-60s via ElevenLabs. Warm, conversational, unhurried. Buddy-at-the-turn energy — not narrating a documentary, talking like the wisest guy in the foursome who's letting you in on something. Think Peter Coyote doing a Toyota truck commercial, not Ken Burns. Delivery is casual and grounded, not dramatic.
- **Color Palette**: Warm, bright, Saturday-morning golden. Natural greens. Relaxed energy. Visually distinct from H4's cinematic golden-hour — this is brighter, more casual, more "weekend."

## P9 — The Competitive Amateur [BASELINE]

### Surface Profile
- **Description**: Plays regularly, mid-handicap. Doesn't slice badly but wants more consistency + distance. Already decent — looking for an edge.
- **Primary Emotion**: Ambition + optimization hunger
- **They'd Say**: "I'm already pretty good. Can this get me to single digits?"

### Deep Psychology
- **Core Wound**: Plateau. He's already better than most of his playing partners. He doesn't have a dramatic slice problem — he has a consistency and distance ceiling he can't break through. The frustration is subtle: he KNOWS he's capable of more, but his current equipment and approach have taken him as far as they can.
- **Physical Sensation**: That frustration of hitting a good drive — but not a GREAT drive. Watching it land 10 yards short of where he pictured it. Knowing there are yards sitting on the table that he can't access.
- **Internal Dialogue**: "I'm leaving yards on the table." "My miss is a slight fade, not a slice — do I even need an anti-slice driver?" "What's my ceiling with better equipment?" "If this adds 15-20 yards of distance AND tightens my dispersion, that's worth it."
- **Identity Statement**: Competitive. Tracks his stats. He's the best golfer in his regular group and he wants to stay that way — or get better. Golf improvement is a project he actively works on. He's not emotional about it — he's analytical.

### Purchase Psychology
- **What They Value**: Marginal gains. Distance + consistency. Data-backed improvement. He doesn't need emotional persuasion — he needs performance evidence. Show him the ball flight data and let him draw his own conclusions.
- **Emotional Buying Trigger**: Performance proof. Specific numbers (yards gained, dispersion tightened). Not "fix your slice" messaging — "add distance without sacrificing accuracy" messaging.
- **Social Proof Function**: Peer performance validation. He doesn't care that 60,000 people bought it — he cares about the performance data of people at HIS level. "What did single-digit handicappers think?"

### Creative Architecture
- **Belief to Destabilize (Focus)**: "I don't slice badly enough to need an anti-slice driver. This product isn't for me."
- **Suggestibility Seed (Reframe)**: "This isn't just anti-slice — it's straight AND long. The forgiveness is a bonus. The DISTANCE is the reason to switch. Even golfers who don't slice are gaining 15-20 yards."
- **Visual Identity**: Performance-focused. Ball flight data overlays, distance metrics, side-by-side comparisons with his current driver. Clean, stats-forward visual language. He responds to EVIDENCE, not emotion.
- **Voice Direction**: Christopher works — performance/authority tone. Direct, data-backed, respects the viewer's intelligence. No emotional manipulation needed — just give him the numbers.
- **Color Palette**: Clean, modern, slightly cooler tones. Performance aesthetic — think Trackman UI, launch monitor data. Not emotional warmth — analytical clarity.

### Last Validated
- **Date**: 2026-02-17 (S031)
- **Source**: Baseline derived from matrix. To be enriched when performance-targeted creative is developed.
