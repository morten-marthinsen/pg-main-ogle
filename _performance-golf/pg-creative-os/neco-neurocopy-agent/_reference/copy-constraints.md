# Copy Constraints: The Non-Negotiable Rules

This document defines the absolute constraints that govern all NeuroCopy output. These are not suggestions—they are hard rules. Copy that violates these constraints is rejected before it reaches production.

The goal: Copy so good people don't even know it's copy. Copy that moves people emotionally before they realize they've been moved. Copy that converts at 10-figure levels.

---

## PART 1: THE 25 FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES (Todd Brown)

Every piece of copy must adhere to these principles. Violation = rejection.

### Principle 1: Start with Empathy
- Walk a mile in the prospect's shoes
- Understand their pain, struggle, and frustration at your core
- Before writing, ask: How is the prospect feeling right now? What are they thinking? What will they doubt?

### Principle 2: Clarity Over Creativity
- Use simple, everyday language
- Have a conversation, don't give a presentation
- A confused prospect does not buy

### Principle 3: Match Tone to Subject Matter
- Don't be excited if they're frustrated
- Don't be somber if they're hopeful
- Read the emotional room before you write

### Principle 4: Activate the Dominant Emotion
- Identify the ONE core emotion you're tapping into
- Good marketing moves people emotionally—it triggers action
- Name the emotion explicitly in your process

### Principle 5: Write Like You Talk
- Capture the spoken word, not the written word
- Read it out loud—if it's choppy, edit it
- Keep it conversational

### Principle 6: Speak to ONE Person
- Even if millions will see it, write to one person
- Picture the ideal prospect in your mind
- One conversation at a time

### Principle 7: Focus on Prospect, Not Yourself
- Use: You, your, yours, you will
- Minimize: I, me, mine, we
- Transform "I'm going to teach you" → "You'll learn"

### Principle 8: Use Their Language
- Use the terms and vocabulary THEY would use
- Don't use fancy $10 words
- If they say "income" not "profit," say "income"

### Principle 9: Leverage Curiosity
- Create information gaps the brain wants to close
- Tease and promise reveals
- Open loops that demand closure

### Principle 10: Dramatize the Impact
- Make it feel monumental, game-changing, life-transforming
- This isn't embellishing—it's presenting the truth dramatically
- Don't just say "greener lawn"—say "pride of ownership, most impressive home in your neighborhood"

### Principle 11: Show, Don't Tell
- Paint pictures, don't state facts
- "You'll make more money" → "Every Thursday at 3 PM, you'll get a notification that another $1,500 has been deposited"
- Dimensionalize every benefit

### Principle 12: Include Facts Within Narrative
- Wrap proof points in stories
- People remember stories better than facts
- "One out of five smokers..." → "Dr. Smith, who lost a parent to lung cancer, decided to run a study..."

### Principle 13: Use Repetition Without Being Repetitious
- Repeat the core promise, but say it different ways each time
- "Every week you'll lose weight" → "Four times a month you'll step on the scale and be down body fat" → "Regularly, every time you measure, fat is coming off"

### Principle 14: Good Copy is Verb-Driven
- Novice copy is adjective-heavy (hyperbolic, exaggerated)
- Pro copy is verb-driven (action, movement, sensation)
- "The explosive, incredible way to build muscle" → "You'll shed body fat, burn fat, melt fat"
- Replace adverb + weak verb with strong verb: "leisurely walked" → "strolled"

### Principle 15: Descriptors Must Add Information
- "The incredible method" adds nothing
- "The three-step method" adds information
- "The beginner-friendly method" adds information
- Every adjective must earn its place

### Principle 16: Use "Reason Why" Copy
- Support claims with reasons: "This works quickly. The reason why is because..."
- Add credibility to offers: "The reason we're offering this discount is..."
- Never leave claims hanging unsupported

### Principle 17: Benefits in Present or Future Tense
- People value what IS working and what WILL work more than what worked
- "What they did" → "What they're doing"
- "What worked" → "What's working"

### Principle 18: Product Does the Work (Pluralize the Verb)
- "Take this pill and strip off 10 pounds" → "Take this pill which strips off 10 pounds"
- The product does the work, not the prospect
- This single change increased response 400% in the classic TDK test

### Principle 19: Use Contrast
- Now State vs. After State
- Their Approach vs. Your Approach
- Without contrast, messages become monotone

### Principle 20: Be Concrete and Specific
- Vague, general statements don't persuade
- Concrete language paints vivid pictures
- Use language they can sense—see it, hear it, feel it, smell it, taste it

### Principle 21: Repeat Core Promises
- Continue bringing up the core promise
- Keep them focused on the outcome
- Different words, same promise, throughout

### Principle 22: Open and Close Loops
- Use cliffhangers to maintain engagement
- Promise to reveal something, move on, tease something new, then close the original loop
- Never take their engagement for granted

### Principle 23: Emotional Benefits with Functional Benefits
- It's the emotional payoff they're really buying
- "Lose weight" → "Feel more confident, feel more attractive"
- People buy emotion, rationalize with logic

### Principle 24: Details in Stories (Avoid "Bald Stories")
- Details make images vivid and draw people in
- Include sensory details: clothes, brands, textures, sounds
- A bald story (absent of detail) fails to engage

### Principle 25: Address ALL Possible Objections
- Every doubt, question, and objection must be addressed
- The best time to overcome an objection is before it comes up
- One outstanding objection can kill the sale

## PART 2: PERFORMANCE GOLF BRAND CONSTRAINTS

These constraints are specific to Performance Golf copy. They define the voice, the forbidden patterns, and the non-negotiables.

### Voice: Brixton
- Encouraging without being rah-rah
- Confident without being dismissive
- Calm, driven, methodical
- Personalized, pragmatic, clarifying
- Inspiring without being idealized or insincere

**Test:** Would Brixton say this? If not, rewrite.

### The 11-Point Copy Checklist (Mandatory)

Every piece of copy must pass ALL 11 checks:

1. **Name the transformation** — FROM frustration TO confidence, not just "improvement"
2. **Name the specific emotion** — frustrated, embarrassed, confident, fired up (not vague)
3. **Include physical sensations** — tight chest, death grip, shoulders climbing
4. **Put them in a specific moment** — Saturday morning, first tee, playing with clients
5. **Make progress feel attainable** — real gains, not fantasy outcomes
6. **Emphasize personalization without tech jargon** — "your personalized path" not "AI-powered algorithm"
7. **Lead with feeling, then show data** — emotion first, proof second
8. **Name The Scattered Playbook as the enemy** — call out conflicting YouTube tips
9. **Position PG1 as the weapon** — One Connected System defeats scattered chaos
10. **Use Brixton's voice** — encouraging without rah-rah, confident without dismissive
11. **Focus on loving the game** — joy, confidence, looking forward to next round

### Forbidden Patterns (Automatic Rejection)

❌ **OVERHYPE** — No "CRUSH 50 YARDS!!!" No excessive punctuation. No unrealistic promises.

❌ **TECH JARGON** — No "AI-powered ML algorithms." No "biomechanical analysis." Always translate to human benefit.

❌ **STERN/DISMISSIVE** — Never talk down. Never make them feel stupid. Stay encouraging.

❌ **GOLF CLICHES** — "Take your game to the next level" is DEAD. "Unlock your potential" is DEAD. "Game-changer" is DEAD.

❌ **PROMISING PERFECTION** — Progress, not perfection. Real gains, not fantasy. "Play BETTER" not "play PERFECT."

❌ **ROBOTIC/CLINICAL** — If it sounds like a textbook, rewrite it. Brixton talks like a knowledgeable friend.

❌ **SKIPPING THE FEELING** — Never lead with data. Emotion opens the door; data walks through it.

❌ **FOCUSING ON PROS** — This is for everyday golfers with real lives, not tour pros.

❌ **VAGUE EMOTIONS** — "Not happy with your game" is weak. "That frustrated walk back to the cart after another chunked wedge" is strong.

❌ **PASSIVE VOICE** — "Your inquiry will be replied to" → "We'll get back to you soon"

❌ **ADJECTIVE OVERLOAD** — "The amazing, incredible, revolutionary system" → "The 10-shot system that fixes contact permanently"

## PART 3: STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINTS FOR SHORT-FORM COPY

### Hook Constraints

**MUST include:**
- Pattern interrupt in first 3 seconds of read
- Specificity (numbers, timeframes, concrete details)
- Curiosity gap OR bold claim OR contrarian angle
- Audience resonance signal (who this is for)

**MUST NOT include:**
- Vague openings ("Are you tired of...")
- Questions that can be answered "no"
- Claims without proof signals nearby
- Generic benefits without specificity

### Body Constraints

**MUST include:**
- Credibility element (who is this from, why should I trust them)
- Root cause identification (The Scattered Playbook, the REAL problem)
- Mechanism explanation (how this actually works)
- Proof/demonstration (results, case studies, numbers)
- Objection handling (why this is different, why it works for YOU)

**MUST NOT include:**
- Unsupported claims
- Features without benefits
- Benefits without emotional payoff
- Generic "works for anyone" without specificity

### CTA Constraints

**MUST include:**
- Clear action verb (click, watch, grab, get)
- Outcome reminder (what they get)
- Urgency or scarcity element (if available and true)
- Platform-specific direction (if relevant)

**MUST NOT include:**
- Weak verbs ("learn more")
- Missing outcome (just "click here")
- False urgency (invented scarcity)
- Multiple competing actions

## PART 4: LANGUAGE PRECISION RULES

### Banned Phrases (Never Use)

| Banned | Why | Use Instead |
|--------|-----|-------------|
| "Are you tired of..." | Overused, triggers ad blindness | Start with specific moment or bold claim |
| "What if I told you..." | Cliche, feels manipulative | Just tell them |
| "Imagine if..." | Weak, doesn't commit | Paint the picture directly: "Picture this..." or just show it |
| "Take your game to the next level" | Dead cliche | Name the specific transformation |
| "Unlock your potential" | Vague, meaningless | Name the specific outcome |
| "Game-changer" | Overused to meaninglessness | Show the change, don't label it |
| "Revolutionary" | Empty superlative | Describe what makes it different |
| "Best-kept secret" | Manipulative, cliche | Just present the mechanism |
| "Once and for all" | Overused | "Permanently" or show the permanence |
| "Finally" (as opener) | Weak, implies you've failed before | Start with the solution or transformation |

### Required Substitutions

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Get better at golf" | "Add 15 yards to your drive" / "Cut 5 strokes off your round" |
| "Improve your game" | Name the specific improvement |
| "Works fast" | "In 10 shots" / "In 5 minutes" |
| "Easy to use" | "Just two steps" / "No equipment needed" |
| "Anyone can do it" | "Even if you've never broken 100" / "Even with limited flexibility" |
| "Results" | Name the specific results |
| "Benefits" | Name the specific benefits |

### Specificity Requirements

- Every claim must have a number OR a timeframe OR a proof element
- "Works fast" → "Works in 10 shots"
- "Adds distance" → "Adds 15-25 yards"
- "Improves contact" → "Hit 5 more fairways per round"
- "Many golfers" → "50,000 golfers" OR "Golfers like Jake, a busy executive who..."

## PART 5: PROOF ELEMENT REQUIREMENTS

Proof is required at the AD level—not forced into every hook. No "proof stuffing."

### Proof Sourcing Protocol
1. **Primary Source:** Extract proof elements from context docs provided by human
2. **If Missing:** Ask human: "Do we have additional proof elements for this offer?"
3. **If Unavailable:** Ask human where to find them OR conduct web search for suggested proof elements
4. **Verification Required:** ALL proof elements must be verified as accurate before use (see Part 9)

### Tier 1: Hardest Proof (Most Persuasive)
- Demonstration (show it happening)
- Before/After (visual transformation)
- Specific numbers from real results
- Named case studies with details

### Tier 2: Authority Proof
- Credentials (titles, certifications, awards)
- Celebrity/known figure association
- Media mentions
- Expert endorsements

### Tier 3: Volume Proof
- Number of lessons given
- Number of customers served
- Years of experience
- Size of community

### Tier 4: Social Proof
- Testimonials (named, specific)
- User counts
- Reviews/ratings
- Community evidence

### Proof Distribution Rules
- **AD Level:** Every ad must contain proof elements (minimum one Tier 1 or Tier 2)
- **Hook Level:** Proof is OPTIONAL in hooks—do not proof-stuff. Many winning hooks have no proof.
- **Body Level:** Must have at least two proof elements across any tiers
- **Pattern:** Hooks grab attention; bodies build belief with proof

## PART 6: EMOTIONAL PRECISION REQUIREMENTS

### Name the Exact Emotion

| Vague (Rejected) | Precise (Accepted) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "Unhappy with your game" | "Frustrated" |
| "Feeling bad about golf" | "Embarrassed walking off the first tee" |
| "Want to improve" | "Hungry to finally break 90" |
| "Not confident" | "That death grip on the club, tension creeping up your forearms" |
| "Having trouble" | "Standing over the ball paralyzed with 12 swing thoughts" |

### Include Physical Sensations

Every piece of copy that addresses pain/frustration must include at least ONE physical sensation:
- Tight chest
- Death grip on the club
- Shoulders climbing
- Stomach dropping
- Jaw clenched
- Tension creeping up forearms
- Heart racing on the first tee

### Put Them in a Specific Moment

Every piece of copy must ground the reader in a SPECIFIC moment:
- "Saturday morning, 8:47am"
- "First tee, three foursomes waiting"
- "Playing with clients"
- "Walking back to the cart after another chunked wedge"
- "Making excuses to skip your Saturday morning round"

## PART 7: QUALITY SCORING RUBRIC

Before any output is finalized, score it against these criteria:

### Hook Strength Score (Must average 4+ out of 5)

| Criteria | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| Pattern Interrupt | Generic opening | Decent hook | Stops the scroll immediately |
| Specificity | Vague | Some numbers | Concrete, precise, believable |
| Curiosity Gap | No gap | Mild curiosity | Must know what comes next |
| Audience Resonance | Generic | Somewhat targeted | "This is for ME" feeling |
| Believability | Unbelievable claim | Plausible | Proven and credible |
| Framework Alignment | Misapplied | Partially applied | Perfect framework deployment |

### Body Strength Score (Must average 4+ out of 5)

| Criteria | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| Credibility | No proof | Some authority | Multiple proof tiers |
| Root Cause | Surface-level | Addresses symptom | Names the real enemy |
| Mechanism | Unclear | Explained | Crystal clear and unique |
| Objection Handling | Ignores objections | Addresses some | Preemptively handles all |
| Emotional Arc | Flat | Some emotion | Takes them on a journey |
| Transformation | Vague promise | Clear benefit | Vivid before/after |

### CTA Strength Score (Must score 4+ out of 5)

| Criteria | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| Clarity | Confusing | Clear | Impossible to misunderstand |
| Action | Weak verb | Action verb | Strong, specific command |
| Outcome | Missing | Implied | Explicitly stated |
| Urgency | None | Implied | Genuine and specific |

## PART 9: HALLUCINATION PREVENTION (CRITICAL)

**Zero tolerance for hallucinations.** This is the most important validation layer.

### What Must Be Verified
- **Credentials:** Every title, certification, award mentioned must be real and accurate
- **Statistics:** Every number (lessons given, customers served, results achieved) must be verified
- **Claims:** Every claim about what a product/method does must be supportable
- **Names:** Every person mentioned must be real and accurately described
- **Associations:** Every celebrity/authority connection must be true

### Verification Protocol
Before any output is finalized:
1. **Flag all factual claims** with ❓ VERIFY tags
2. **List specific items requiring human confirmation:**
   - "❓ VERIFY: '70,000 lessons' — confirm this number is accurate"
   - "❓ VERIFY: 'Hall of Fame coach' — confirm this credential"
   - "❓ VERIFY: 'Tiger Woods' former coach' — confirm this association"
3. **Do not deliver copy with unverified claims** — human must confirm

### What Happens If Verification Fails
- If a claim cannot be verified, REMOVE IT
- If a number cannot be confirmed, ask human for accurate number
- If a credential is uncertain, ask human to confirm or provide alternative
- NEVER guess. NEVER assume. NEVER fabricate.

### The Standard
Copy must be a conversation directly to their soul—but it must be TRUTHFUL.
Every credential, statistic, and claim must be real.
Copy that lies is not 10-figure copy. It's liability.

## PART 10: TIMEFRAME & EASE ANCHORS (RECOMMENDED)

**Classification: RECOMMENDATION, not fail constraint**

Timeframe and ease anchors strengthen copy but are not mandatory. Many winning ads do not have explicit timeframes.

### When to Recommend Adding Timeframe
If copy lacks specificity on speed/ease, suggest:
- "⚡ SUGGESTION: Consider adding a timeframe anchor (e.g., '5 minutes,' '10 shots,' 'one swing')"
- "⚡ SUGGESTION: Consider adding an ease anchor (e.g., 'just two steps,' 'no equipment needed')"

### Do NOT Reject Copy For Missing Timeframe
This is a copy improvement suggestion, not a requirement.
Human may choose to proceed without timeframe if strategically appropriate.

## PART 11: COMPETITIVE DIFFERENTIATION

### "What It's Not" Format
Every ad should include competitive differentiation—calling out what the audience has tried that DIDN'T work.

**Standard Format:**
- "It doesn't matter if you've tried [failed approach]..."
- "Stop [ineffective method]..."
- "Unlike [competitor approach], this..."

### Stale Element Detection
The agent should flag potentially stale, overused, or "me too" elements:
- "⚠️ STALE ALERT: This angle ('stop watching YouTube videos') is heavily used in current market. Consider: [alternative differentiation suggestion]"

### Agent Freedom
The agent may suggest alternative differentiation approaches when it detects:
- Overused angles in the market
- Generic differentiation that doesn't stand out
- Opportunities for sharper positioning

## PART 12: A/B VARIANT GENERATION

For each audience segment, generate variants to enable testing:

### Output Structure
```
AUDIENCE: [Segment Name]
CORE ANGLE: [The one angle this ad is built around]

HOOK 1A [Primary] — [Framework] + [Style]
[Copy]

HOOK 1A-VAR [Style Variant] — [Same Framework] + [Different Style]
[Copy]

HOOK 1A-ALT [Framework Variant] — [Different Framework] + [Style]
[Copy]
```

### Variant Types
- **Style Variant:** Same framework, different style (Curiosity vs. Proclamation)
- **Framework Variant:** Same audience, different psychological framework
- **Angle Variant:** Same audience, different angle within the core angle

## PART 13: CORE ANGLE CONGRUENCE (CRITICAL)

Every ad has ONE core angle. All hooks and body MUST be congruent to that core angle.

### What This Means
- Multiple hooks can exist (for different audiences)
- Hooks can use pattern interrupts (intentional non-coherence between hook style and body style)
- BUT all hooks and body must serve the SAME core angle

### What to Flag
If any hook or body section drifts from the core angle:
- "🚨 ANGLE DRIFT: Hook 3 appears to shift from core angle [X] to [Y]. All elements must support the ONE core angle. Please confirm this is intentional or revise."

### Hook-Body Coherence (Recommendation Only)
Pattern interrupts are intentional and valuable. Hook can be emotional → body logical, or vice versa.
- Do NOT flag hook-body style mismatches as errors
- DO flag if they serve different core angles

### Human Verification Checkpoint
Before generation begins, the agent must:
1. Present identified angles to human
2. Get human confirmation of the ONE core angle
3. Only then proceed to generation
4. Check all output against that confirmed core angle

## ENFORCEMENT

This document is not optional. It is the quality floor.

### Hard Stops (Automatic Rejection)
- Hallucinations (unverified credentials, statistics, claims)
- Forbidden patterns (see Part 2)
- Banned phrases (see Part 4)
- Core angle drift without confirmation

### Soft Stops (Recommendations)
- Missing timeframe/ease anchors → Suggest improvement
- Hook-body style mismatch → Note as intentional pattern interrupt
- Stale differentiation → Suggest alternatives

### Quality Score Requirements
- Hook score: 4+ average
- Body score: 4+ average
- CTA score: 4+

There is no "good enough." There is only "meets the standard" or "does not meet the standard."

The standard is 10-figure copy. Copy that doesn't read like copy. Copy that moves people before they realize they've been moved. A conversation directly to their soul.

That's the only acceptable output.
