# FATE Model — Ancestral Triggers

The FATE Model identifies four ancestral triggers that bypass rational thought by activating survival scripts hardwired into the human brain over millions of years. These triggers operate below conscious awareness.

**Core Principle:** Decisions are made emotionally, then rationalized logically. FATE triggers the emotional decision.

---

## The Four Triggers

### F — FOCUS

**What It Is:** The brain's attention allocation system. Novelty, threat, or high-value signals capture focus automatically.

**Ancestral Origin:** Survival required constant environmental scanning. Anything unexpected (stick snapping behind the bush) demanded immediate attention—it could mean predator or prey.

**The Stick-Behind-the-Bush Effect:** When our ancestors heard an unexpected sound, focus became laser-sharp. Replicate this in copy by creating the same effect.

**Activation Mechanisms:**
| Mechanism | How It Works |
|-----------|--------------|
| Pattern Interrupt | Unexpected statements that break mental scripts |
| Novelty | Something new or unusual (new mechanism name) |
| Threat Signal | What they might lose |
| Value Signal | What they might gain |
| Incomplete Pattern | Curiosity gap that demands closure |

**Copy Application:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Learn how to grow your business" | "The growth strategy most agencies hope you never discover" |
| "Save money on your electric bill" | "Your electric company charges you for electricity you never use. Here's how to stop it." |
| "Get better sleep tonight" | "The 11pm mistake destroying your sleep (it's not your phone)" |
| "Improve your golf swing" | "Stop hitting balls. (Yes, really.)" |

**Focus Killers to Avoid:**
- Clichés (brain recognizes pattern, tunes out)
- Predictable claims
- Vague language
- Excessive qualifiers (somewhat, fairly, kind of)

**Audience Variation:**
- Skeptical audiences need stronger novelty signals
- Aware audiences need more specific value signals
- Cold audiences need pattern interrupts first
- Sophisticated audiences need contrarian angles

---

### A — AUTHORITY

**What It Is:** The brain's shortcut for determining who to trust and follow. Authority signals bypass analysis—we defer to perceived experts automatically.

**Ancestral Origin:** Disobeying tribal leaders meant death. Following skilled hunters, experienced elders, and tribal leaders increased survival odds. Challenging authority was often fatal.

**The Authority Triangle:**
1. **Behavior:** Confidence, discipline, composure
2. **Effect:** What others perceive (movement, appearance, intent)
3. **Habits:** Lifestyle signals of high-status individuals

**Authority Types:**
| Type | Examples | Best For |
|------|----------|----------|
| Credential | "Hall of Fame coach," "PGA teaching pro" | Cold audiences |
| Association | "Tiger Woods' former coach," "Worked with Major winners" | Aspiration audiences |
| Volume | "70,000 lessons," "50,000 amateur golfers," "35 years" | Skeptical audiences |
| Demonstration | Before/after, live results, case studies | Show-me audiences |
| Third-Party | Media mentions, awards, endorsements | High-status audiences |
| Specificity | "After 847 client campaigns and $43M in tracked revenue..." | Data-driven audiences |

**Copy Application:**
| Weak Authority | Strong Authority |
|----------------|------------------|
| "I've been doing this for years" | "After 847 client campaigns and $43M in tracked revenue..." |
| "Experts say this works" | "Stanford's behavioral economics lab tested this on 12,000 subjects..." |
| "This is a great product" | "The exact system I use with Fortune 500 clients (finally available publicly)" |
| "Top golf instructor" | "Hall of Fame coach who's given 70,000 lessons to amateur golfers" |

**Authority Language Patterns:**
- Specific numbers over round numbers (70,000 > "thousands")
- Named institutions over "experts"
- First-person experience over third-person claims
- Confident declarations over hedged suggestions
- Non-hedging language (no "somewhat," "fairly," "kind of")

**Audience Variation:**
- Older audiences respond to experience/longevity
- Younger audiences respond to results/demonstration
- High-income audiences respond to exclusivity/celebrity
- Skeptical audiences need demonstration proof
- Blue-collar audiences respond to practical experience

---

### T — TRIBE

**What It Is:** The brain's social identity system. Humans are tribal—we seek belonging and fear exclusion.

**Ancestral Origin:** Exclusion from the tribe meant death. Conforming to group norms, signaling membership, and identifying outsiders were survival necessities.

**The Tribe Script (Automatic Responses):**
1. Pay attention to the group
2. Keep behavior in line with group norms
3. Enforce group behavior on others
4. Create discomfort for non-conformers
5. Ensure personal behavior won't trigger social consequences

**Tribal Triggers:**
| Trigger | Copy Pattern | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Identity Call-Out | "If you're a [identity] who..." | "If you're a golfer who's tried everything..." |
| In-Group Language | Jargon, shared experiences | "You know that feeling when you stripe one down the middle..." |
| Shared Enemy | Name the common enemy | "The Scattered Playbook," "YouTube chaos" |
| Shared Mission | Unite around purpose | "Helping everyday golfers love their game" |
| FOMO | Show what others are doing | "Join 47,000+ golfers who..." |
| Exclusion Threat | "This isn't for everyone..." | "This isn't for golfers who want a quick fix..." |
| Out-Group Contrast | "While most people..." | "While most golfers waste money on random tips..." |

**Copy Application:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Buy our software" | "Join the operators who refuse to leave money on the table" |
| "This course teaches marketing" | "The private community where 7-figure marketers share what actually works" |
| "Save time on meal prep" | "For busy professionals who refuse to sacrifice health for success" |
| "Golfers" | "Everyday golfers with real lives, not tour pros" |

**Tribal Fear Triggers:**
- Fear of missing out (FOMO)
- Fear of being left behind
- Fear of being seen as inferior
- Fear of social judgment
- Fear of exclusion

**Audience Variation:**
- Community-oriented audiences respond to belonging
- Achievement-oriented audiences respond to elite identity
- Fear-driven audiences respond to exclusion threats
- Aspiration-driven audiences respond to identity transformation

---

### E — EMOTION

**What It Is:** The brain's decision accelerator. Emotional responses create chemical cascades (dopamine, cortisol, oxytocin) that encode memories and drive behavior.

**Ancestral Origin:** Emotions evolved as rapid response systems. Fear triggered flight. Anger triggered fight. Desire triggered pursuit. Emotions made us act before thinking.

**Two Types of Emotional Scripts:**
1. **Ancestral Scripts:** Automatic reactions inherited through DNA (fight/flight, attraction, disgust)
2. **Life Scripts:** Learned responses from personal experience (phobias, preferences, associations)

**Primary Emotions in Copy:**
| Emotion | Trigger | Copy Approach |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Frustration | Ongoing problem, failed attempts | Name it precisely, validate it |
| Embarrassment | Social failure, public mistakes | Name the specific moment |
| Fear | Loss, missing out, failure | What they'll lose without action |
| Hope | Possibility, transformation | Paint the after state vividly |
| Desire | Wanting, aspiration | Describe the feeling of achievement |
| Relief | Problem solved, burden lifted | Show the weight coming off |

**Show, Don't Tell:**
| Telling (Weak) | Showing (Strong) |
|----------------|------------------|
| "You'll feel confident" | "Walk into any room knowing every eye tracks toward you" |
| "Save time" | "Reclaim those 3 hours you lose every Sunday dreading Monday" |
| "Make more money" | "Open your bank app and see a balance that makes you smile" |
| "Improve your golf" | "That pure contact that sends it 12 yards further than you've ever hit your 7-iron" |

**Emotional Contrast (Before → After):**

**Before State (Pain):**
- Specific frustrations
- Vivid sensory details of the problem
- Social/emotional consequences
- Physical sensations

**After State (Desire):**
- Specific outcomes
- Vivid sensory details of the solution
- Social/emotional rewards
- Physical sensations of success

**Example:**
```
Before: "That sinking feeling when you check your ad spend and see another
$500 gone—with nothing to show for it. You're working harder than your
competitors but somehow falling further behind."

After: "Imagine opening your dashboard tomorrow and seeing a ROAS you
actually want to screenshot. The kind of results that make your agency
clients beg to give you more budget."
```

**Physical Sensations (Must Include):**
- Tight chest
- Death grip on the club
- Shoulders climbing
- Stomach dropping
- Jaw clenched
- Tension creeping up forearms
- Heart racing

**Audience Variation:**
- Pain-aware audiences: Lead with frustration/embarrassment
- Solution-aware audiences: Lead with hope/desire
- Deal-seeking audiences: Lead with fear of missing out
- Transformation-seeking audiences: Lead with identity emotion

## FATE Sequencing by Format

### Short-Form Ads (Hooks + Body + CTA)
```
Hook: FOCUS (Pattern interrupt, curiosity gap)
Body: EMOTION → AUTHORITY → TRIBE
CTA: EMOTION → TRIBE (urgency + belonging)
```

**Recommended sequence:** Focus → Emotion → Authority → Tribe

### Landing Pages
```
Headline: FOCUS
Subhead: AUTHORITY
Body: EMOTION → TRIBE → AUTHORITY (stacked)
CTA: EMOTION + TRIBE
```

**Full FATE sequence deployed throughout**

### Video Scripts
```
0-3 sec: FOCUS (must capture immediately)
3-30 sec: EMOTION (build connection)
30-60 sec: AUTHORITY (establish trust)
60+ sec: TRIBE + EMOTION (close with belonging + desire)
```

### Email Sequences
```
Email 1: FOCUS (pattern interrupt, story hook)
Email 2-3: AUTHORITY (case studies, credentials)
Email 4: TRIBE (testimonials, community)
Email 5: EMOTION (urgency, vision, scarcity)
```

## FATE Analysis Template

For each audience, analyze:

```
AUDIENCE: [Segment Name]

FOCUS:
- What pattern interrupt will stop their scroll?
- What novelty will capture attention?
- What curiosity gap can we open?
- What threat or value signal is most compelling?

AUTHORITY:
- What credentials matter to THIS audience?
- What proof will they believe?
- What demonstration will convince them?
- What authority type is most effective? (Credential/Association/Volume/Demo)

TRIBE:
- What identity do they want to claim?
- What group do they want to belong to?
- Who is the shared enemy?
- What tribal language do they use?
- What exclusion do they fear?

EMOTION:
- What is their dominant negative emotion? (frustration, embarrassment, fear)
- What positive emotion do they seek? (confidence, pride, relief)
- What physical sensations accompany these emotions?
- What specific moment captures this emotion?
```

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Using Authority Before Focus** — They won't see your credentials if you don't capture attention first

❌ **Vague Emotions** — "Unhappy with your game" instead of "That frustrated walk back to the cart"

❌ **Generic Tribe Signals** — "Golfers" instead of "everyday golfers with real lives"

❌ **Unverified Authority** — Claims that can't be substantiated (hallucination risk)

❌ **Missing Physical Sensations** — Emotions without body feelings lack impact

❌ **Skipping Contrast** — Before/After creates emotional distance that drives action

❌ **Focus Killers** — Clichés, hedging language, vague claims

## Integration with Other Frameworks

- **Six-Axis Model:** FATE triggers work on all six axes but are most effective when matched to elevated axes
- **Behavior Compass:** FATE triggers should align with audience Needs, Decision patterns, and Values
- **PCP Model:** FATE is the mechanism; PCP is the sequence (Perception shift via FOCUS, Context via AUTHORITY/TRIBE, Permission via EMOTION)
- **Authority Triangle:** Authority (A) in FATE directly connects to the Authority Triangle framework for deeper application
