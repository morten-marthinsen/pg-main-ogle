# The Six-Axis Model of Influence

The Six-Axis Model shows precisely what factors need to be present for influence to occur. Only **3 of 6 axes** need to be elevated for persuasion—but they must be elevated significantly.

## The Six Axes Defined

### 1. Suggestibility
**Definition**: The degree to which a person will accept and act on a suggestion.

- High suggestibility = open to new ideas and willing to take action
- Required when: audience is skeptical, offer seems "too good," or behavior change is significant

**Indicators of Low Suggestibility**:
- "I've tried everything"
- "I'm not easily convinced"
- High prior experience with similar offers

### 2. Focus
**Definition**: The degree to which a person maintains attention and excludes competing stimuli.

- High focus = deeply engaged, not distracted
- Required when: message is complex, competition for attention is high, or time is limited

**Focus Disruptors**:
- Predictable messaging
- Information overload
- Weak opening hooks

### 3. Openness
**Definition**: The degree to which a person feels they can become vulnerable without fear of judgment.

- High openness = willing to share real problems and desires
- Required when: offer requires significant behavior change or personal investment

**Openness Barriers**:
- Fear of judgment
- Prior negative experiences
- Lack of trust signals

### 4. Connection
**Definition**: The degree to which a person feels social connection and desire for future contact.

- High connection = sees brand/person as ally, wants ongoing relationship
- Required when: building long-term customers, community, or high-ticket relationships

**Connection Builders**:
- Shared values and identity
- Demonstrated understanding of their world
- Genuine empathy (not performative)

### 5. Compliance
**Definition**: The degree to which a person will comply with direction to perform an action.

- High compliance = ready to take action when asked
- Required when: immediate conversion is needed, or building toward large ask

**Compliance Escalation**:
- Start with micro-commitments
- Build momentum through small yeses
- Each action increases compliance for next action

### 6. Expectancy
**Definition**: The degree to which a person can predict positive outcomes and enjoys anticipation.

- High expectancy = excited about future, makes decisions based on positive predictions
- Required when: offer is aspirational, transformation-focused, or investment-heavy

**Expectancy Triggers**:
- Vivid future pacing
- Social proof of outcomes
- Clear path to result

## Axis Prioritization by Use Case

### Paid Ads (Cold Traffic)
**Priority Order**: Focus → Suggestibility → Compliance

Rationale: Cold audience needs attention captured (Focus), willingness to consider new solution (Suggestibility), and readiness to click (Compliance). Openness, Connection, and Expectancy are built post-click.

### Sales Page (Warm Traffic)
**Priority Order**: Expectancy → Focus → Suggestibility → Compliance → Connection → Openness

Rationale: Visitor already has some interest. Build positive predictions (Expectancy), maintain engagement (Focus), address objections (Suggestibility), then ask for action (Compliance).

### Email Nurture Sequence
**Priority Order**: Connection → Openness → Expectancy → Suggestibility → Compliance

Rationale: Email is relationship-building. Lead with Connection and Openness, build Expectancy through stories, then convert.

### High-Ticket Sales Call
**Priority Order**: Openness → Connection → Suggestibility → Expectancy → Compliance → Focus

Rationale: Focus is already high (they showed up). Priority is getting them to open up (Openness), building relationship (Connection), then guiding decision.

### Door-to-Door / Cold Outreach
**Priority Order**: Focus → Connection → Expectancy → Openness → Suggestibility → Compliance

Rationale: Must capture attention instantly (Focus), quickly build rapport (Connection), create positive predictions (Expectancy) before asking for commitment.

### Retargeting Ads
**Priority Order**: Expectancy → Compliance → Focus

Rationale: They already know you. Remind them of the outcome (Expectancy), make action easy (Compliance), and recapture attention (Focus).

### Community / Course Offer
**Priority Order**: Connection → Expectancy → Openness → Suggestibility → Compliance

Rationale: Community offers sell belonging (Connection) and transformation (Expectancy). Create safety to learn (Openness).

### Urgency/Scarcity Campaign
**Priority Order**: Focus → Compliance → Expectancy

Rationale: Deadline creates Focus. Build Compliance momentum. Anchor on what they'll miss (Expectancy reversal).

## How to Elevate Each Axis

### Elevating Suggestibility

**Language Patterns**:
- "What if..." (opens possibility)
- "Imagine for a moment..." (bypasses resistance)
- "You've probably noticed..." (presupposes agreement)
- "Most people find that..." (social proof of acceptance)

**Structural Techniques**:
- Address objections before they arise
- Use "Even if..." statements
- Provide logical justification for emotional decision
- Reduce perceived risk (guarantees, proof)

### Elevating Focus

**Hooks That Create Focus**:
- Curiosity gaps (incomplete information)
- Contradiction (unexpected claims)
- Specificity (unusual details)
- Threat/value signals
- Story loops (open narratives)

**Maintenance Techniques**:
- Vary sentence length
- Use white space
- Insert pattern interrupts
- Ask rhetorical questions
- Use "bucket brigades" (And here's the thing...)

### Elevating Openness

**Trust Signals**:
- Vulnerability first (you share before they do)
- Acknowledge their skepticism
- Show understanding of their specific situation
- No judgment on current state

**Permission Granting**:
- "It's okay to feel..."
- "You're not alone in..."
- "Most people in your position..."
- "There's no wrong answer..."

### Elevating Connection

**Identity Mirroring**:
- Use their language patterns
- Reference shared experiences
- Demonstrate insider knowledge
- Acknowledge their worldview

**Tribal Belonging**:
- "People like us..."
- "In this community..."
- "What separates [identity] from everyone else..."
- Shared enemy/mission framing

### Elevating Compliance

**Micro-Commitment Sequence**:
1. Easy agree (rhetorical questions)
2. Small action (watch video, read article)
3. Medium action (opt-in, download)
4. Large action (purchase, apply)

**Compliance Language**:
- Clear, specific instructions
- Single call-to-action
- Action verbs (Get, Start, Claim, Join)
- Urgency when appropriate

### Elevating Expectancy

**Future Pacing**:
- "Imagine waking up tomorrow and..."
- "Picture yourself 30 days from now..."
- "Here's what happens after you..."
- Sensory-rich outcome descriptions

**Proof of Possibility**:
- Case studies with specific results
- Testimonials describing transformation journey
- Before/after comparisons
- Timeline of expected outcomes

## Diagnostic Tool

### Pre-Writing Checklist

For each piece of copy, identify:

1. **Which 3 axes are most critical for this format/audience?**
2. **What is the current state of each axis for this audience?**
   - Already elevated? → Leverage it
   - Low? → Build it
3. **What specific techniques will I use to elevate each priority axis?**

### Post-Mortem Analysis

If copy underperforms, diagnose using Six-Axis:

| Symptom | Likely Axis Issue |
|---------|------------------|
| Low engagement/views | Focus |
| High bounce rate | Suggestibility or Focus |
| Low opt-in rate | Compliance or Expectancy |
| No response to CTA | Compliance |
| Comments but no action | Connection without Compliance |
| Objections in responses | Suggestibility |
| "Sounds too good" feedback | Suggestibility + Authority |

### Milgram Experiment Analysis

Example of how 3 axes created extreme behavior change:

| Axis | Level | How It Was Created |
|------|-------|-------------------|
| Focus | HIGH | Novel situation, authority presence |
| Suggestibility | HIGH | Authority figure, scientific context |
| Compliance | HIGH | Graduated commitment, authority commands |
| Openness | LOW | Formal setting, no personal disclosure |
| Connection | LOW | Stranger interactions |
| Expectancy | LOW | Couldn't predict outcomes |

Result: 65% of participants administered "lethal" shocks with only 3 axes elevated.

**Implication**: You don't need all 6 axes. Identify the 3 most relevant and maximize them.
