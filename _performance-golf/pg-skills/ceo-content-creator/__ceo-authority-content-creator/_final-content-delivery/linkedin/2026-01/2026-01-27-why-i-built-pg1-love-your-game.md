# Why I Built PG1: The Real Story Behind "Love Your Game"

When I was five years old, I cried at my grandparents' condo in Myrtle Beach.

My mom asked what was wrong. I told her I'd just realized that life goes by fast — and it would be a shame to not reach my potential.

Strange thing for a kid to say. But I felt it in my chest. Not inspirational. Sobering.

That feeling never left. It's shaped everything I've built since.

**Why do most golfers stop loving golf — and what would it take to help them fall back in love with the game?**

## Who This Is For

If you've ever stood on the first tee with a tight chest instead of excitement — this is for you.

If you've watched your handicap stay stuck while your enthusiasm slowly drains — this is for you.

If you've tried YouTube tips, training aids, lessons, and range sessions, only to feel more confused than when you started — this is for you.

Here's what's at stake: You'll keep grinding on the wrong things. You'll keep feeling frustrated. And eventually, you'll stop texting your buddies for tee times. You'll start making excuses instead.

Golf will become something you used to love.

I've seen it happen thousands of times. And I refuse to let it keep happening.

## The Moment I Understood What Was Possible

I was 14 years old, caddying at Lancaster Country Club.

I'd show up at 3:45 in the morning to get a loop. No status. No guarantees. Just a kid who wanted to play golf in the afternoon and needed to earn it first.

One summer, I started caddying for a guy who was an 18-handicap. Solid guy, but stuck. He'd been the same handicap for years.

Something clicked between us. He trusted me to read his putts. To help him with club selection. To think through each shot with him.

Over that summer, he shot 78 three different times.

An 18-handicap. Shooting 78.

Not because he suddenly got more talented. Not because he practiced more. But because he had someone helping him work on the right things, in the right order, for his game.

That summer showed me something I've never forgotten:

Most golfers aren't far from a breakthrough. They're just working on the wrong things.

## The Scattered Playbook Problem

Here's what happens to most golfers:

You slice the ball. So you Google "how to fix a slice." You get 47 different tips. You try three of them at the range. One kind of works. Then you play a round and the slice comes back.

So you try something else. A training aid. A new grip. A tip your buddy gave you. A video from a tour pro.

Now you're standing over the ball with 12 swing thoughts. Your shoulders are tight. Your grip is a death squeeze. And you're praying instead of playing.

I call this The Scattered Playbook. Conflicting advice from conflicting sources, with zero integration and maximum confusion.

The Scattered Playbook doesn't make you better. It makes you paralyzed.

And the worst part? It makes you stop loving the game.

## What I Wish Had Existed

After that summer caddying, I knew there had to be a better way.

Not more information. A smarter system.

Something that could diagnose your actual root flaw — not just symptoms. Something that could sequence your improvement so you're always working on the right thing at the right time. Something personalized to your game, not generic advice for everyone.

I looked for it. It didn't exist.

So I spent the next 15 years building it.

I worked 100-hour weeks for five years. Kept my full-time job until Performance Golf hit $29 million in annual sales. Documented everything — what worked, what stalled, what created real breakthroughs versus temporary patches.

I tested it on myself. I tested it on thousands of golfers. I refined it obsessively.

That's PG1.

## What "Love Your Game" Actually Means

"Love Your Game" isn't a tagline. It's the whole point.

Here's what I mean:

Last month, I got an email from an 88-year-old man. He told me he's playing better than he has in years. That he looks forward to his rounds again. That golf feels fun instead of frustrating.

Eighty-eight years old. Still improving. Still loving it.

That email sits in a folder I read when I need to remember why we do this.

Because here's the truth: Better scores matter. Dropping strokes matters. But the real win?

The real win is when you text your buddies asking for tee times instead of making excuses to skip.

The real win is when you walk off 18 already thinking about when you can play again.

The real win is when golf becomes something you look forward to — not something you tolerate.

## My Grandfather's Note

Before my grandfather passed away, he left me a note about golf.

He wrote that the game teaches integrity, character, and endurance. That it's a mirror for how you handle life. And that I should remember: golf is part of life, not the other way around.

He told me to enjoy it. To pursue my dreams. To strive for perfectionism but settle for excellence.

That note hangs in my office.

And it reminds me daily what PG1 is really about:

Not just improvement. Enjoyment.

Not just lower scores. More joy.

Not just reaching your potential in golf — but getting the most out of the time you have to play it.

## The Bottom Line

Most golfers stop loving golf because they can't figure out how to get better. They drown in information. They grind on the wrong things. And eventually, they give up.

PG1 exists to change that. One connected system. The right things, in the right order, built for your game.

So you can stop surviving your rounds and start loving them again.

— Brixton
