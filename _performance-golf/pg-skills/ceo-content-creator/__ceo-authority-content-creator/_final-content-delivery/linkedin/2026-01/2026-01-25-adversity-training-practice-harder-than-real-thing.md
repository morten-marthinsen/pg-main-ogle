# Why Practice Should Be Harder Than the Real Thing

I used to hit balls at a guy's feet on purpose.

Not out of anger—out of curiosity. I wanted to know: could I control my wedge well enough to land it exactly where I aimed, even with someone standing there?

That drill taught me something I still use today. And it explains why most golfers choke under pressure while a few seem immune to it.

**Why do some golfers perform better when it matters—while others fall apart?**

---

If you've ever played your best golf on the range, then watched it evaporate on the first tee with your buddies watching, this is for you.

Here's what's at stake: You'll keep practicing in a way that feels productive but doesn't prepare you for the moments that actually count. Worse, you'll blame your "mental game" when the real problem is your training.

The common approach—more reps, more range sessions, more drills—feels right but fails because it trains you for conditions that don't exist on the course. No pressure. No consequences. No pulse.

After years of testing this on myself—from junior tournaments to a Division I scholarship to playing in a U.S. Amateur—I developed a framework I call Adversity Training.

---

## The Adversity Training Framework

Think of this as Navy SEAL training for your golf game. The principle is simple: always make practice harder than what you'll face in competition. When you do, the real thing feels easier.

### 1. Manufacture Consequences

Practice without consequences is rehearsal, not training. Your brain treats it differently. Add something at stake—even something small—and your nervous system wakes up.

Example: Instead of hitting 50 wedges to a target, I'll bet myself: hit 5 in a row inside 10 feet or start over. Miss on shot 4? Back to zero. That frustration? That's the training.

Why this matters: Competition has consequences. If your practice doesn't, you're building a skill that only works when nothing matters.

### 2. Create Discomfort

Comfort is the enemy of preparation. The range is temperature-controlled, the lies are perfect, nobody's watching. The course is none of those things.

Example: I'll practice shots I hate. Downhill lies. Awkward stances. The punch shot under branches that I'd rather not face. When I eventually face them on the course, they're familiar enemies—not surprises.

Why this matters: The shot that beats you is usually the one you avoided practicing. Discomfort in practice creates confidence on the course.

### 3. Add Pressure Triggers

Your body responds to specific triggers—people watching, money on the line, the first tee with a full deck. Recreate those triggers in practice.

Example: That drill where I hit wedges at someone's feet? Sounds crazy, but it forced me to commit fully to every shot. No half-swings. No tentative contact. Full commitment or you hit your buddy. I'd take the hardest version of that pressure in practice so the tournament felt like a relief.

Why this matters: Pressure isn't something you overcome through willpower. It's something you inoculate yourself against through exposure.

### 4. Test Before You Trust

A change isn't real until it holds up under pressure. The range will lie to you. Your backyard will lie to you. Only the course—with something on the line—tells the truth.

Example: I finished 9th in a 185-person field after discovering that humming during my swing reduced tension. But I didn't trust it until I'd tested it in three pressure situations first. Only then did I know it was real.

Why this matters: Confidence comes from evidence. Pressure-tested skills become trusted skills.

---

## How to Apply This

Here's how to build adversity training into your practice this week:

1. **Add a consequence to your next range session**
   Pick one drill and add stakes. Ten putts from 5 feet—if you miss one, start over. Make par on 3 imaginary holes before you leave. Something on the line, even if it's just your ego.

2. **Practice one shot you hate**
   Think about the shot that makes your stomach tighten. The one you pray you won't face. That's the shot you need to practice. Spend 15 minutes on it. Not fixing it—just getting familiar with it.

3. **Schedule a pressure test**
   Book a round with something on the line. A $10 nassau. A grudge match with your buddy. Anything that raises your heart rate on the first tee. That's your laboratory for testing what actually holds up.

## The Adversity Training Checklist

Before your next practice session, ask yourself:

□ Does this session have real consequences—or just reps?
□ Am I practicing something that makes me uncomfortable?
□ Is there any pressure trigger present (observers, stakes, time limit)?
□ Have I tested this change under pressure yet?
□ Would this session prepare me for the hardest moment I'll face on the course?

If you answer "no" to most of these, you're rehearsing—not training.

---

## The Bottom Line

The golfers who perform under pressure aren't mentally tougher than you. They've just trained under harder conditions. When you make practice harder than the real thing, competition becomes the easy part.

Part of getting better at golf is adversity training. Always making the situation harder than what you're gonna face. That way, when you go play, you don't feel that pressure—you're ready for it.
