The golfers who perform under pressure aren't mentally tougher than you.

They've just trained under harder conditions.

Here's the framework I call Adversity Training:

🧵
```

### TWEET 2
```
Context:

I used to hit wedge shots at my buddy's feet. On purpose.

Not anger—training.

If I could commit fully with someone standing there, tournament pressure would feel like relief.

That single idea changed how I think about practice:
```

### TWEET 3
```
1. Practice without consequences = rehearsal

Your brain treats it differently.

Add stakes—even small ones. Hit 5 in a row or start over. That frustration? That's the training.
```

### TWEET 4
```
2. Comfort is the enemy

The range: perfect lies, perfect weather, no pressure.

The course: none of that.

Practice the shot that makes your stomach tighten. Make it a familiar enemy, not a surprise.
```

### TWEET 5
```
3. Add pressure triggers

People watching. Money on the line. Time limits.

Recreate them in practice.

Pressure isn't overcome through willpower. It's inoculated against through exposure.
```

### TWEET 6
```
4. Test before you trust

The range will lie to you. Your backyard will lie to you.

Only the course—with something on the line—tells the truth.

Don't call it "fixed" until pressure has validated it.
```

### TWEET 7
```
TL;DR:

• Add consequences to every practice
• Train the shots you hate
• Create real pressure triggers
• Only the course tells the truth
• Harder practice = easier competition
```

### TWEET 8
```
This is Navy SEAL training for golf.

Make practice harder than competition—so competition becomes the easy part.
