It was the summer of my junior year of high school.

The window when college coaches actually pay attention.

This was the summer I needed to play well.
Instead... everything unraveled.

I had just finished a tournament where I played the final 12 holes at 13 over par.

I completely lost my swing.

I had 5 swing thoughts.
5 fixes.
5 things I was trying to remember mid-swing.

The harder I searched, the further away everything felt.

The very next day, I was flying to PGA National to play the Optimist International.

One of the biggest junior events in the world.
An event Tiger Woods, Phil Mickelson, and many others had won.
And one of the hardest courses in the world.

I was playing the worst golf of my life.

On that flight, I read one line that flipped everything:

Simplify.

So I tried something that sounded ridiculous.
I started humming while I hit golf balls.

The humming forced my body to relax.

It quieted the noise and released tension I didn’t even realize I was carrying.

I finished 9th in a field of 180 players.
And for the first time that summer, college coaches noticed.

The next day, I brought that same simplicity home.

I shot 66.
Won a junior tournament and broke Jim Furyk’s tournament record.

Over the next six weeks, I shot three 65s and three 66s.

The funny thing is, I didn't suddenly find confidence. 
I just stopped getting in my way.

It's a testament to the fact that when you think you’re far off, you’re often not.

Most breakthroughs are actually a result from simplifying, not starting over completely.

One small adjustment.
One more day of making the effort.

That belief is what shaped everything I’ve built since.
