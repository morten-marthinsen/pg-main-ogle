I built a $29M company before I quit my full-time job.
Not to prove I could work long hours.
But to test something early in my life:

Could I create value so undeniable that momentum eventually took over?

So when I graduated college, I turned my life into a 10-year experiment:

Was it possible to do in 10 years what most people try to do in 40?

I took a job at a $2M company.
I was one of the first employees and the youngest sales rep by 15 years.

At the same time, I started Performance Golf on the side.

For five years, I worked 100-hour weeks and quietly built Performance Golf to $30M in annual revenue before I ever left my job.

Most people had no idea.

What they didn’t see was that I never shortchanged my employer.

I was consistently the #1 or #2 sales rep out of 25.
One year, I outsold the second, third, and fourth reps combined.

I even took screenshots of my results every year.
Not for ego, but for proof.

I knew that one day, people would say:
“I have kids.”
“I'm too busy.”
“Now isn’t the right time.”

And I wanted to remove that excuse before it ever came up.

I was a slow starter.

So this wasn’t about proving how fast I could win.

I simply wanted to learn how breakthroughs actually happen for people who have to earn them.

And by staying focused, failing forward, and pushing long enough for momentum to build, it was clear:

Breakthroughs aren’t sudden.
They build quietly over time.
Then one day, everyone notices all at once.
