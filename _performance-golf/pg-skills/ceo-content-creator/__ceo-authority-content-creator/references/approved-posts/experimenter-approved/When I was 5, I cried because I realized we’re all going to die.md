I was 5 years old when I told my mom we're all going to die.

It changed the way I live my entire life.

I was staying at my grandparents’ place in Myrtle Beach.

Nothing sad was happening. 
But something hit me. Hard.

I broke down.

My mom came into the room and asked what was wrong.

I looked at her and said,
“Life’s going to go fast. We’re all going to die.”

That moment never left me.

I think most people have a version of this moment.

The first time you realize time is moving.
That your life has an expiration date.

And then my mind went somewhere else.

Wouldn’t it be a tragedy to never reach your full potential?

Years later, a teacher put words to that feeling. He said:

“What if you were the person born with the cure for cancer, but you never pushed yourself to find it? Think of all the people you would let down.”

It's an extreme example, but the point stuck.

When you don’t become who you’re capable of becoming, it doesn’t just affect you.

It affects all the people you’ll never help and the impact you'll never have.

That idea became my fuel.

It’s why after college, I wanted to see if I could do in 10 years what most people stretch across 40 or 50.

I worked 100-hour weeks.
Built a company to $30M while still working a full-time job.

Not for balance or comfort.

But to prove what’s possible when you treat time like it actually matters.

The truth is, you’re never going to feel ready.
Life is going to go fast whether you prepare for it or not.

So the real question is simple:

Are you going to live like you have all the time in the world?

Or are you going to push to become the person you’re actually capable of becoming?
