“Hell is meeting the person you could’ve become.”

That quote has haunted me since I first heard it.

But honestly, it resonated with me much earlier.

I remember being four years old at my grandparents’ place and getting emotional out of nowhere.

My mom asked what was wrong.

And I said something that still gives me chills:

“Life’s gonna go fast. Wouldn’t it be a shame to not make the very most of this and really make it count and meaningful?”

That feeling has always been my fuel.

Whenever I question myself, I anchor back to that moment.

Years later, a teacher said something else that stuck with me just as deeply.

He said, “What if you were born with the cure for cancer, but you never pushed yourself to reach your full potential? Think of all the people you would let down.”

What if there’s a version of you capable of something meaningful and you never even try to find out?

That fear became my fuel.

From that moment on, I became obsessed with running experiments and taking different routes to see how far I could really go.

When I graduated college, I turned my life into a 10-year study.

Could I do in 10 years what most people do in 50?

It’s why I worked 100-hour weeks for years while building Performance Golf.

Why I’ve filmed my swing thousands of times.
Why I’ve tested routines, schedules, my body, my limits.
Why I’ve pushed myself in ways that probably looked obsessive from the outside.

I strongly believe most people never dig deep enough to find out what they’re actually capable of.

They stay comfortable and tell themselves, “maybe later.”
Until later never comes.

Looking back, that four-year-old was right.
Life does go fast.

And that lifelong desire to get better faster is a big part of why I built Performance Golf.

It was never just about golf.

It started from a passion to help people reach their full potential so they can enjoy the game and their lives more.

So I’ll end here:

I’d rather turn every stone and run experiments, tired, uncomfortable, and uncertain than live comfortable, left wondering what might have been.
