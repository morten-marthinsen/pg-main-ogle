The golf industry is beautiful.

But it's also overwhelming.

Magazines. YouTube. Instagram. Podcasts. Swing tips everywhere.

It's like saying,
“Hey, I have a problem I want to solve.”

And someone replies:

“Great. Go read this 86,000-page book. Your answer is in there somewhere.”

If you have ever felt stuck in your golf game, I've been there.

I was playing the best golf of my life when I decided to really commit to improving.

I filmed my swing over 2,000 times a year.

I broke multiple phones from using the camera so much, to the point where people thought I had an addiction.

And I got worse.
Not a little worse. Five shots worse.

Looking back, the problem was obvious.

I was trying to perfect my swing and working on random things instead of addressing my single most important, weighted swing flaw.

I would watch a YouTube video and try that.

Work on one part of my swing, then abandon it for something else.

Even get advice from one instructor, then hear something completely different from another.

It's like going in for heart surgery and the surgeon starts poking at your lungs, kidneys, and liver.

You don't get better that way.
You get better by identifying the root problem first.

This is what I see with most golfers.
They lack a plan, not effort.

They throw mud against the wall and wonder why nothing sticks.

And it's no surprise.

The golf industry today feels like pulling advice from a hundred different places with no clear priority and no sense of what to work on first.

But golf is the one game where you can practice more and actually get worse. Most people do.

What changed everything for me was understanding one simple principle:
Work on the right things, in the right order.

When I was playing my best, I knew I had four or five swing issues.

But I focused on the one lead domino first.
Fix the root flaw, and two or three other problems often fix themselves.
