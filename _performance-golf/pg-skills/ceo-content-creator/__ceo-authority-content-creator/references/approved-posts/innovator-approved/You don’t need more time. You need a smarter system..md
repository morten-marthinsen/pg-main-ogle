I grew a side business to $29M annually while working a full-time job and 100+ hour weeks.

I wouldn't do it that way again.
But I wouldn't erase it either.

While those years were expensive, they were also incredibly educational.

It all goes back to a question I have been obsessed with for as long as I can remember:

Life is short.
So how do you reach your maximum potential before it is too late?

It's why when someone tells me something takes five years, my instinct has always been to ask how it could be done faster, without wasting the reps that matter.

Early on, I believed the answer was simple:

Outwork everyone.

So when I started building Performance Golf alongside my sales job, that's exactly what I did.

I had two computers, and worked 100-hour weeks for five years straight.

But when most people hear that story, they don't realize:

I wasn't just working harder.
I was also running experiments the entire time.

Every week was a test to see:

- What actually moves the needle?
- What feels productive but is not?
- What scales?
- What breaks?

I failed constantly.
But I also adapted, and documented what worked.

That process is what carried the business to $29M before I ever left my day job.

On paper, everything looked right.
Internally, I was exhausted.

That's when I started looking around. 

And the people I admired most, the ones actually sustaining success, weren't exhausting themselves.

They were precise.
They had systems built from years of iteration, not opinion.

Things like real feedback loops and leverage instead of burnout. 

That's when it clicked for me:

You can't compress time by effort alone.
You have to learn faster than everyone else.

When effort becomes your identity, you become the ceiling.

But when effort is used to generate insight, you build systems that outlive you.

That realization changed everything.

From how I run the company, to how I practice golf, even how I think about time itself.

I see it all the time now.

How someone can practice four hours a day and get worse...
While someone else can improve in twenty minutes.

The only difference is direction, not discipline.

A change from “how can I work more?” to “what did I learn from the last rep?”

I have spent years documenting those lessons.
In business, golf and performance overall.

That is what I share here.
The experiments, the failures, and the systems that came out of them.

If you care about reaching your potential before time runs out, you will understand why this matters.
