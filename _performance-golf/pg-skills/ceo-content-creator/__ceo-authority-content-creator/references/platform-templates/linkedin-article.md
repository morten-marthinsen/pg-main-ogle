**Example:**
```
## The Right Things, Right Order Framework

Most golfers work on whatever feels wrong today. This creates random effort with random results. This framework flips that approach: identify the one root cause, fix it completely, then move to the next thing.

### 1. Diagnose the Root, Not the Symptom

Your slice isn't the problem—it's a symptom. The root might be grip, alignment, or swing path. Most golfers attack symptoms, which is why they fix one thing and another breaks.

Example: A golfer comes to me with a slice. Instead of giving slice tips, we discover his grip is causing an open face at impact. Fix the grip, the slice disappears—and so do three other "problems" he was planning to work on.

Why this matters: One root cause often creates multiple symptoms. Fix the root, and the symptoms vanish.

### 2. One Thing Until Automatic

Don't work on three things. Work on one thing until it's automatic under pressure. This feels slower but produces permanent change instead of temporary patches.

Example: After we identified the grip issue, that golfer spent three weeks on nothing but grip. By week two, he was frustrated. By week four, he'd dropped four strokes and couldn't remember the last time he sliced.

Why this matters: Scattered focus produces scattered results. Deep focus on one thing compounds.

### 3. Test Under Pressure Before Moving On

The range lies. Your backyard lies. Until a change holds up on the course—with something on the line—it's not real. Don't call it "fixed" until pressure has validated it.

Example: That same golfer went to the range after week one and said "I've got it." I said, "Play 9 holes with something on the line, then tell me." He lost the bet to his buddy. Two more weeks of practice. Then he won it back.

Why this matters: The course is the only truth-teller. Pressure reveals what actually works.
```

Here's how to put this framework into action this week:

1. **Identify your root flaw**
   Film your swing from two angles. Watch for the earliest thing that goes wrong—not the result. Or, simpler: what's the one piece of feedback you've heard from multiple sources? That's probably your root.

2. **Commit to one thing for 3 weeks**

3. **Schedule a pressure test**
   Book a round where something's on the line—even a $5 bet with a buddy. That's your validation moment. If the change holds, move to the next thing. If it breaks, you need more reps.

## LinkedIn Platform Optimization

While following the Deep Dive structure, apply these LinkedIn-specific optimizations:

### Formatting
- 2-4 sentence paragraphs max
- Bold for emphasis (sparingly—<10% of text)
- Numbered lists for steps/frameworks
- Bullet points for lists of 3+
- White space between sections
- Headers (##) to break up content

### Hashtags
- 3-5 hashtags at bottom only
- Mix of broad (#leadership #golf) and niche (#golfimprovement #systemsthinking)
- NEVER hashtags in body text

### Algorithm Signals (2025)
- Dwell time (longer reads = better)
- Native content (no external links in body)
- Early engagement (first 60 minutes critical)
- Authentic voice over corporate polish

### Voice Reminders
- No "In this article, I'll share..."
- No "Let's dive in..."
- Lead with the content, not meta-commentary
- Brixton voice: UNWAVERING, CONSTRUCTIVE, UPLIFTING
