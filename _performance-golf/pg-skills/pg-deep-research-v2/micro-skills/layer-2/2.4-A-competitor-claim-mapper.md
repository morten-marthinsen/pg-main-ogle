# Micro-Skill 2.4-A: Competitor Claim Mapper

**Layer:** 2 - Intelligence
**Purpose:** Map what competitors CLAIM, HOW they claim it works, and what prospects HATE about them
**Dependency:** Layer 1 competitor data (1.4-F Ads, 1.4-G Funnels), Context Expansion (1.0-A), Quote Extraction (1.3)
**Parallel:** Can run simultaneously with 2.3 (Gap Detection)
**Version:** 3.1 (Mechanism-Focused Claim Mapping + Villain Extraction)
**PRD Reference:** [[RESEARCH-PRD#Section 3.2 - Bucket 6]]
**E5 Reference:** Todd Brown's Competitor Examination - Claims AND Prospect Beliefs About Competitors

---

## CRITICAL: Mechanism-Focused Mapping + Villain Extraction

This skill maps not just WHAT competitors claim, but HOW they claim it works AND what prospects HATE about them.

**Focus Areas:**
1. **Technology Claims** - What proprietary technologies they name
2. **How-It-Works** - The mechanism explanation
3. **Benefit Claims** - What outcomes they promise
4. **Positioning** - How they differentiate
5. **Villain Extraction** - What prospects DISLIKE about competitor products/features (for contrast)

**Why Villain Extraction Matters:**
- Enables powerful "unlike [competitor weakness]" positioning
- Surfaces features/products to contrast AGAINST
- Reveals what the market REJECTS (not just what it wants)
- Provides ammunition for "us vs them" copy angles

**Minimum Requirement:** Map claims from ALL competitors AND extract villain data from prospect quotes

## Claim Categories to Map

### 1. Technology Claims
Named proprietary technologies that differentiate.

```yaml
extraction_focus:
  - Trademarked technology names (®, ™)
  - "Our proprietary..." statements
  - Technical feature names
  - Capitalized technology terms

examples:
  - "ZipCore" (Cleveland)
  - "Groove-in-Groove" (Callaway)
  - "HydraFlow Micro Grooves" (Mizuno)
  - "Extreme CG" (Mizuno)
```

### 2. Mechanism Explanations
HOW the technology claims to work.

```yaml
extraction_focus:
  - "Because..." explanations
  - "This allows..." statements
  - Technical process descriptions
  - Cause-effect relationships

examples:
  - "The lightweight core allows weight to be redistributed to the perimeter"
  - "Sharper groove edges channel water away from the contact point"
  - "Wider sole design glides through turf rather than digging"
```

### 3. Benefit Claims
WHAT outcomes the product promises.

```yaml
extraction_focus:
  - Outcome statements
  - Performance improvements
  - Experience improvements
  - Emotional benefits

examples:
  - "Higher launch and more forgiveness"
  - "Maintains spin in wet conditions"
  - "Chunk a little less"
  - "Hit more greens"
```

### 4. Positioning Statements
HOW the brand differentiates.

```yaml
extraction_focus:
  - "For [audience]" statements
  - "Unlike [competitor]" statements
  - Identity positioning
  - Market positioning

examples:
  - "For the everyday golfer"
  - "Elite forgiveness"
  - "Tour-proven performance"
  - "The smart choice"
```

## Mapping Process

### Step 1: Load Competitor List
From context expansion (1.0-A):
1. Get all competitors identified
2. Get all products per competitor
3. Ensure all competitors have data

### Step 2: Extract from Ads
From 1.4-F output:
1. Headlines → Primary claims
2. Body copy → Supporting claims
3. CTAs → Action drivers
4. Track ad duration (longer = more successful)

### Step 3: Extract from Product Pages
From scraped content:
1. Hero section → Primary positioning
2. Feature lists → Technology claims
3. Technical specs → Mechanism explanations
4. Benefit bullets → Outcome claims

### Step 4: Extract from Funnels
From 1.4-G output:
1. Landing page headlines
2. Video script claims
3. Email sequence messaging
4. Order page final pitch

### Step 5: Document Mechanisms
For each technology claim:
1. Name the technology
2. Document HOW it works (mechanism)
3. Document WHAT benefit it delivers
4. Capture verbatim explanation
5. Assess uniqueness level

### Step 6: Categorize Claims
Assign each claim to category:
- Performance (spin, distance, consistency)
- Emotional (confidence, trust, freedom)
- Identity (for whom, what type of golfer)
- Value (price, quality, worth)

### Step 7: Count Frequencies
Track for saturation analysis:
- How many competitors make this claim?
- Where is it placed (headline vs body)?
- How often does it appear?

### Step 8: Identify White Space
Analyze for gaps:
1. What claims are MISSING from market?
2. What claims are WEAK (like "chunk a little less")?
3. What positioning is UNOWNED?
4. What benefits are UNDER-LEVERAGED?

### Step 9: Document Vulnerabilities
For each competitor:
1. What is their weakest claim?
2. Where can they be attacked?
3. What do they fail to address?
4. What language reveals weakness?

### Step 10: VILLAIN EXTRACTION (CRITICAL)

Excavate what prospects HATE about competitor products, features, and messaging.

```yaml
villain_sources:
  - Forum complaints about specific products
  - Negative reviews (Amazon, MyGolfSpy, GolfWRX)
  - "I returned it because..." statements
  - "I hate when they..." rants
  - Comparisons where competitor loses
  - Social stigma mentions ("my buddies would laugh")

extraction_categories:
  hated_features:
    - What specific features do prospects complain about?
    - What claims fell flat in their experience?
    - What "improvements" actually made things worse?

  hated_products:
    - Which specific products have negative sentiment?
    - What products are considered "gimmicks"?
    - What products carry social stigma?

  hated_messaging:
    - What positioning language offends prospects?
    - What claims feel condescending or false?
    - What marketing approaches backfire?

  hated_experiences:
    - What purchase/use experiences frustrate them?
    - What expectations were violated?
    - What made them feel ripped off?

output_per_villain:
  1. What they hate (specific)
  2. Verbatim quote expressing the hate
  3. Source of the quote
  4. Contrast opportunity (how to position AGAINST this)
  5. Frequency (how common is this complaint)
```

### Step 11: Synthesize Contrast Opportunities

For each villain identified, create ready-to-use contrast angles:

```yaml
contrast_formula:
  template: "Unlike [villain], [our product/approach] [positive contrast]"

  examples:
    - "Unlike wedges that CLAIM forgiveness but still let you dig, [product] physically PREVENTS the leading edge from catching"
    - "Unlike 'tour-proven' clubs designed for pros, [product] is engineered for YOUR swing"
    - "Unlike gimmick clubs you'd be embarrassed to pull out, [product] looks like a real wedge"
```

## Saturation Signals

| Signal | Criteria | Strategic Implication |
|--------|----------|----------------------|
| **SATURATED** | 80%+ of advertisers claim | Avoid - no differentiation possible |
| **CROWDED** | 50-79% of advertisers claim | Risky - need unique angle |
| **PARTIAL** | 20-49% claim, concentrated | Opportunity - room to own |
| **LOW** | <20% claim | Strong opportunity |
| **WHITE SPACE** | No one claims | Major opportunity if market evidence supports |

## Failure Protocol

```
IF competitor missing data:
  - Search for additional sources
  - Check product pages directly
  - Document partial data with gaps noted
  - DO NOT skip competitor entirely

IF mechanism not explained:
  - Extract the claim anyway
  - Mark mechanism as "UNCLEAR"
  - Note for competitive intelligence gap

IF white space not found:
  - Re-analyze with fresh perspective
  - Look for messaging gaps, not just technology gaps
  - Consider emotional/psychological positioning gaps
  - There is ALWAYS a gap - dig deeper

IF villain data sparse (<50 data points):
  - Expand search to Amazon reviews (1-3 star specifically)
  - Search Reddit/forums for complaint threads
  - Look for "I returned..." or "waste of money" language
  - Search for competitor brand + "disappointed" or "doesn't work"
  - Check YouTube comments on competitor product videos
  - There is ALWAYS something they hate - excavate deeper

IF no contrast opportunities emerge:
  - Review villain data for implicit contrasts
  - Ask: "What would make them NOT hate this?"
  - The opposite of the villain IS the contrast opportunity
  - Every complaint implies a desired alternative
```

---

## Notes

- This is COMPETITIVE INTELLIGENCE, not product evaluation
- Focus on what competitors CLAIM, not what's technically true
- Verbatim language is valuable for counter-messaging
- White space opportunities are the most valuable output
- Weak claims are attack vectors
- **VILLAIN DATA IS GOLD** - what they hate is what we contrast against
- Contrast opportunities should be copy-ready, not abstract
- This feeds 2.4-B Saturation Scorer, 2.4-C Mechanism Mapper, and 3.1-C Proactive Objection Handler
