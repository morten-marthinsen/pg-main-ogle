# Micro-Skill 2.4-C: Mechanism Mapper

**Layer:** 2 - Intelligence
**Purpose:** Map competitor mechanisms with explicit NAME + ARTICULATION for exclusion in mechanism creation
**Dependency:** 2.4-A Competitor Claim Mapper complete, Layer 1 competitor data available
**Version:** 3.1 (NAME + ARTICULATION Explicit Structure)
**PRD Reference:** [[RESEARCH-PRD#Section 3.2 - Bucket 6]]
**E5 Reference:** Todd Brown's Competitor Examination - What Are They Saying and HOW They Say It Works

---

## CRITICAL: NAME + ARTICULATION Structure

Every competitor mechanism MUST be captured with two explicit components:

### THE NAME
The proprietary, branded, or descriptive name for the mechanism.
- Examples: "ZipCore", "Groove-in-Groove", "Anti-Chunk Slide Ramp"
- This is what they CALL it

### THE ARTICULATION
The explanation of HOW and WHY it works - the story/logic behind the mechanism.
- Examples: "Lightweight core material shifts center of gravity to perimeter, which creates higher launch and more forgiveness because..."
- This is their EXPLANATION of how it delivers the result

**WHY THIS MATTERS:**
The mechanism mapping feeds into the MECHANISM CREATION AGENT (separate system) which must know:
1. What mechanisms already EXIST (to avoid copying)
2. How they're ARTICULATED (to differentiate explanation)
3. What explanations are CLAIMED (to find fresh angles)

**Minimum Requirement:** 15 unique mechanisms with BOTH name AND articulation documented

## Mechanism Categories

### Core/CG Technology
Technology related to center of gravity positioning and internal structure.

```yaml
examples:
  - ZipCore (Cleveland) - Lightweight core for CG manipulation
  - Extreme CG (Mizuno) - CG positioned at face center
  - Perimeter Weighting (PING) - Weight distributed to edges
```

### Groove Technology
Technology related to face grooves and spin generation.

```yaml
examples:
  - HydraZip (Cleveland) - Wet condition spin retention
  - Groove-in-Groove (Callaway) - Nested groove pattern
  - Full-face Grooves (Multiple) - Grooves extend entire face
  - Milled Face (Vokey) - Precision milled grooves
  - Hydroflow Microgrooves (Mizuno) - Water channeling
```

### Sole Technology
Technology related to sole design and turf interaction.

```yaml
examples:
  - Dynamic Sole Grinds (Cleveland) - Loft-specific sole designs
  - Anti-Chunk Slide Ramp (Square Strike) - Chipper-style sole
  - Wide Sole (Smart Sole, Tour Edge) - Extra-wide for forgiveness
  - Beveled Leading Edge (Tour Edge) - Prevents digging
```

### Material Technology
Technology related to materials and construction.

```yaml
examples:
  - Forged Construction - Traditional soft feel
  - Cast Construction - Precision and consistency
  - TPU Insert (Tour Edge) - Vibration dampening
  - Multi-material Construction - Different materials for different properties
```

### Face Technology
Technology related to face design beyond grooves.

```yaml
examples:
  - Laser Milling - Texture for consistent spin
  - Raw Face - Unplated for maximum friction
  - Heat Treatment - Durability without losing spin
```

## Mapping Process

### Step 1: Extract Technology Names

For each competitor:
1. Read product page content
2. Identify proprietary technology names (usually capitalized, trademarked)
3. List all named technologies

```yaml
extraction_signals:
  - Capitalized terms: "ZipCore", "HydraZip", "JAWS"
  - Trademark symbols: ®, ™
  - "Our proprietary..."
  - "Featuring [technology name]..."
  - Bullet points on product pages
```

### Step 2: Document Mechanism Claims

For each technology:
1. Find the claim statement (what it does)
2. Find the mechanism explanation (how it works)
3. Find the benefit claim (why it matters)

```yaml
claim_structure:
  technology_name: "ZipCore"
  mechanism_claim: "Shifts CG for higher launch"
  how_it_works: "Lightweight material in core allows weight redistribution"
  benefit_claim: "More forgiveness and better feel"
```

### Step 3: Capture Verbatim Descriptions

Extract exact language used by competitors:
- Marketing copy from product pages
- Technical explanations from spec sheets
- Review site summaries

**CRITICAL:** Preserve exact language for competitive messaging analysis.

### Step 4: Categorize Mechanisms

Assign each mechanism to a category:
- Core/CG Technology
- Groove Technology
- Sole Technology
- Material Technology
- Face Technology
- Other (specify)

### Step 5: Assess Differentiation Level

```yaml
differentiation_levels:
  HIGH:
    - Unique to this brand
    - Proprietary name and technology
    - Not replicated by competitors
    example: "ZipCore is unique to Cleveland"

  MEDIUM:
    - Similar concept exists elsewhere
    - Brand has unique implementation
    - Differentiated by execution not concept
    example: "Full-face grooves exist at multiple brands"

  LOW:
    - Common across industry
    - Table stakes feature
    - Not a differentiation point
    example: "Multiple loft options"
```

### Step 6: Identify Competitive Gaps

Analyze the mechanism landscape for:
1. What mechanisms are MISSING from the market
2. What claims are WEAK (like "chunk a little less")
3. What positioning is UNOWNED
4. What benefits are UNDER-LEVERAGED

```yaml
gap_analysis:
  missing_mechanisms:
    - Psychological benefit positioning (confidence, fear elimination)
    - Bold outcome claims ("stop chunking" vs "chunk less")

  weak_claims:
    - Cleveland: "chunk a little less" - weak, admits failure
    - Callaway: "for the everyday golfer" - stigma risk

  unowned_positioning:
    - "The anti-dig wedge" - no one leads with this
    - "Confidence restored" - no one leads with emotional benefit

  under_leveraged:
    - Academic research on yips and fear
    - Psychological trigger of "fear of digging"
```

## Competitor Template (NAME + ARTICULATION Format)

For each competitor, document with explicit NAME and ARTICULATION separation:

```yaml
competitor_profile:
  name: "Cleveland"
  products_analyzed:
    - CBX4 ZipCore
    - Smart Sole
    - RTX ZipCore

  root_cause_claim: "Lack of forgiveness in traditional wedges"
  marketing_message: "Chunk a little less / Elite forgiveness"
  implicit_belief: "Problem is off-center strikes"
  weakness: "Admits you'll STILL chunk"

  mechanisms:
    - NAME: "ZipCore"
      category: "core_technology"
      ARTICULATION: "Lightweight core allows weight redistribution to perimeter for higher MOI and forgiveness"
      causal_chain: "Light core → Free weight → Perimeter weighting → Higher MOI → Forgiveness"
      differentiation: "HIGH"
      exclusion_flag: "Avoid 'core technology' and 'weight redistribution' angles"

    - NAME: "HydraZip"
      category: "groove_technology"
      ARTICULATION: "Sharper groove edges with channels displace water for consistent spin in wet"
      causal_chain: "Sharp edges + channels → Water displaced → Friction retained → Spin maintained"
      differentiation: "MEDIUM"
      exclusion_flag: "Avoid water-displacement spin stories"

    - NAME: "UtiliZip Grooves"
      category: "groove_technology"
      ARTICULATION: "Tightly packed sharper grooves increase spin on partial shots"
      causal_chain: "More grooves + sharper → More ball contact → More spin"
      differentiation: "MEDIUM"
      exclusion_flag: "Avoid 'sharper grooves' as primary story"

    - NAME: "Dynamic Sole Grinds"
      category: "sole_technology"
      ARTICULATION: "V/S/C sole grinds optimized for each loft's typical swing characteristics"
      causal_chain: "Different lofts = different swings → Optimized sole per loft → Better turf interaction"
      differentiation: "MEDIUM"
      exclusion_flag: "Avoid loft-specific grind story"

  primary_differentiation: "Forgiveness through core technology and sole design"

  articulation_themes_to_avoid:
    - "Weight redistribution"
    - "Center of gravity optimization"
    - "Water displacement for spin"
    - "Loft-specific sole design"
```

      - "CG positioning"
      competitors: ["Cleveland", "Mizuno", "PING"]

    groove_related:
      - "Sharper groove edges for spin"
      - "Full-face grooves for open face"
      - "Nested/layered grooves"
      - "Water displacement channels"
      competitors: ["Cleveland", "Callaway", "Titleist", "Mizuno"]

    sole_related:
      - "Wide sole for turf glide"
      - "Loft-specific grinds"
      - "Beveled leading edge"
      competitors: ["Cleveland", "Tour Edge", "Square Strike"]

  available_articulation_gaps:
    - "PSYCHOLOGICAL mechanism (fear/confidence)"
    - "BEHAVIORAL mechanism (swing pattern change)"
    - "PHYSICS mechanism (angle of attack control)"
    - "GEOMETRY mechanism (leading edge positioning)"

  naming_patterns_to_avoid:
    suffixes: ["Core", "Zip", "Flow", "Glide"]
    prefixes: ["Hydra", "Ultra", "Pro", "Tour"]
    compound_patterns: ["[Element]+[Part]", "[Action]+[Result]"]

## Failure Protocol

```
IF mechanism count < 15:
  - Expand competitor research
  - Add additional competitors
  - Search for more detailed product pages
  - DO NOT proceed until 15+ mapped

IF competitor has no mechanisms documented:
  - Search for technical spec sheets
  - Look at review sites for technology descriptions
  - Describe features even if not branded
  - Flag as "generic mechanism" if no proprietary tech

IF competitive gap not found:
  - Re-analyze with fresh perspective
  - Look for messaging gaps, not just technology gaps
  - Consider emotional/psychological positioning gaps
  - There is ALWAYS a gap - dig deeper
```

## Notes

- Mechanism mapping is for COMPETITIVE INTELLIGENCE AND EXCLUSION, not product development
- Focus on what competitors CLAIM, not what's technically true
- Verbatim language is valuable for counter-messaging
- **NAME + ARTICULATION separation is CRITICAL for mechanism creation agent**
- **Exclusion registry prevents accidental mechanism copying**
- Gaps are the most valuable output - these become opportunities
- This feeds directly into Layer 3 opportunity scoring AND FINAL_HANDOFF.md
