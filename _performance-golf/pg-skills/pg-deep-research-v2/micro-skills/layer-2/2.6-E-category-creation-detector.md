## What We're Detecting

### Category Creation Signs
When a new category might be emerging:
- New vocabulary appearing
- Rejection of existing frames
- "Why doesn't anyone make X" statements
- Mashup descriptions (combining existing terms)
- Frustration with existing options

### Why It Matters
- First to name a category often owns it
- Category creation > category competition
- New frames can reposition entire markets

---

## Detection Signals

### 1. New Vocabulary
People inventing terms for things:
- "I call them 'practice premium' balls"
- "Budget-tour quality"
- "Affordable performance"
- Mashup phrases appearing

### 2. Rejection of Existing Frames
People dismissing current categories:
- "I'm not a premium OR a budget player"
- "Neither of these categories fits me"
- "Why is it all or nothing?"
- "The whole setup is wrong"

### 3. "Why Doesn't Anyone" Statements
Explicit requests for non-existent thing:
- "Why doesn't anyone make a..."
- "Someone should create..."
- "I just want X without Y"
- "Is there no option for..."

### 4. Hybrid Seeking
Looking for combinations:
- "Premium feel without premium price"
- "Tour performance for amateurs"
- "Professional quality, recreational price"

### 5. Segment Self-Definition
People defining themselves differently:
- "I'm a smart golfer, not cheap"
- "Serious but not obsessed"
- "Quality-conscious value seeker"

---

## Detection Process

### Step 1: Vocabulary Scan
Search for:
- Novel phrases
- Combination terms
- Self-invented labels
- Repeated unique descriptions

### Step 2: Rejection Pattern Detection
Search for:
- "Not X, not Y"
- "Neither"
- "I don't fit"
- "Wrong framework"

### Step 3: Demand Signal Detection
Search for:
- "Why doesn't anyone"
- "Someone should"
- "Wish there was"
- "Looking for"

### Step 4: Hybrid Pattern Detection
Search for combinations:
- "X but also Y"
- "X without Y"
- "X for people who want Y"

### Step 5: Opportunity Assessment
Could this become a category?
- Is there critical mass?
- Is there a good name available?
- Would it resonate broadly?

## Signal Strength Rating

| Strength | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **Strong** | Multiple signal types, 50+ evidence points, clear naming opportunity |
| **Medium** | 2-3 signal types, 20-50 evidence points, partial naming opportunity |
| **Weak** | 1 signal type, <20 evidence points, unclear if real category |

---

## Quality Criteria

- [ ] All signal types scanned
- [ ] Evidence quantified
- [ ] Naming opportunities assessed
- [ ] Existing categories mapped
- [ ] Gaps identified
- [ ] Strategic recommendation provided

---

## Category Naming Principles

Good category names are:
- **Memorable:** Easy to recall and repeat
- **Descriptive:** Hint at what it means
- **Flattering:** Make users feel good about belonging
- **Ownable:** Not generic, can be claimed

---

## Notes

- Category creation is high-risk, high-reward
- First to name often owns
- Must have real demand, not just invented
- Category names can become positioning
