## The Psychology

### Loss Aversion Principle
Humans feel losses ~2x more intensely than equivalent gains.
- Losing $20 feels worse than gaining $20 feels good
- Avoiding pain is more motivating than seeking pleasure
- "What you'll lose" beats "what you'll gain"

### Application to Research
- Pain-based opportunities should be weighted higher
- Fear/loss language signals stronger motivation
- "Eliminates" is stronger than "provides"

---

## Weighting Methodology

### Step 1: Categorize Opportunities
For each opportunity from Layer 2:
- Is it pain-based (eliminates problem)?
- Is it desire-based (provides benefit)?
- Is it mixed?

### Step 2: Calculate Loss Aversion Factor

**Pure Pain-Based:** Factor = 2.0
- Opportunity addresses avoiding loss
- Language is about elimination/relief
- Primary driver is escaping negative

**Mixed (Pain + Desire):** Factor = 1.5
- Opportunity has both elements
- Some pain avoidance, some gain seeking
- Balanced motivation

**Pure Desire-Based:** Factor = 1.0
- Opportunity is about gaining positive
- Language is about achievement/attainment
- Primary driver is reaching positive

### Step 3: Apply Weighting
```
Weighted Score = Original Score × Loss Aversion Factor
(Cap at 10)
```

### Step 4: Re-rank Opportunities
Sort by weighted score, not original score

---

## Classification Criteria

### Pain-Based Indicators
- "Eliminates" / "removes" / "stops"
- "No more" / "never again"
- "Relief from" / "freedom from"
- "Avoids" / "prevents"
- Negative sentiment in supporting quotes

### Desire-Based Indicators
- "Provides" / "gives" / "delivers"
- "Achieve" / "reach" / "gain"
- "More" / "better" / "improved"
- Positive sentiment in supporting quotes

### Mixed Indicators
- Both pain and desire language present
- Quotes contain both negative and positive
- Before/after structure with equal weight
