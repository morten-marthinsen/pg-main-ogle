# Micro-Skill 2.6-C: Villain Identifier

**Layer:** 2 - Intelligence
**Purpose:** Identify what the market blames, hates, and resents
**Dependency:** Layer 1 negative sentiment data
**Parallel:** Can run simultaneously with 2.6-A, 2.6-B, 2.6-D through 2.6-F

## What We're Finding

### Named Villains
Specific targets of blame:
- Competitors/brands
- Products/categories
- Methods/approaches
- Beliefs/conventional wisdom
- Industries/institutions

### Why Villains Matter
- Villains create positioning opportunities
- "Against" positioning is powerful
- Shared enemies create tribal bonds
- Liberation from villain = value proposition

---

## Villain Types

### Brand Villains
Specific companies/products blamed:
- "Titleist charges $50 for what costs $5 to make"
- "Nike abandoned us"
- "The big brands don't care"

### Method Villains
Approaches/techniques blamed:
- "Traditional instruction doesn't work"
- "One-size-fits-all approach"
- "Old-school thinking"

### Belief Villains
Conventional wisdom blamed:
- "Everyone says you need premium balls"
- "The 'you get what you pay for' lie"
- "The fitting-industrial complex"

### Institution Villains
Systems/industries blamed:
- "Golf equipment industry is a scam"
- "PGA marketing machine"
- "Retail golf stores"

### Self-Villains
Internal attributions (trickier):
- "I'm not good enough"
- "I'm throwing money away"
- "I keep making the same mistake"

---

## Detection Process

### Step 1: Negative Quote Extraction
Filter Layer 1 for:
- Negative sentiment
- Blame language
- Frustration/anger
- Criticism/complaint

### Step 2: Villain Identification
Scan for targets:
- Who/what is blamed?
- What language is used?
- How intense is the blame?

### Step 3: Frequency Analysis
Count:
- How often is each villain mentioned?
- Across how many sources?
- With what intensity?

### Step 4: Language Cataloging
Capture exact language:
- What words describe the villain?
- What metaphors are used?
- What emotions are expressed?

### Step 5: Opportunity Assessment
For each villain:
- Can we position against this?
- Would market resonate?
- Is it authentic to our product?

---

## Blame Language Indicators

### Direct Blame
- "X is a rip-off"
- "Y doesn't work"
- "Z is lying"
- "[Brand] is the problem"

### Indirect Blame
- "They just want your money"
- "Nobody tells you the truth"
- "The industry is broken"
- "Marketing BS"

### Frustration Markers
- "Tired of..."
- "Sick of..."
- "Fed up with..."
- "Why does [X] always..."

### Resentment Markers
- "While they get rich..."
- "We're the suckers"
- "They don't care about..."
- "Just another way to..."

## Quality Criteria

- [ ] Villains identified from negative data
- [ ] Intensity rated with evidence
- [ ] Language patterns captured exactly
- [ ] Types categorized
- [ ] Positioning opportunities assessed
- [ ] Self-villains handled differently

---

## Self-Villain Handling

Self-blame is different:
- Don't attack the person
- Reframe the belief
- Provide permission/liberation
- "It's not your fault" framing

---

## Notes

- Villains create powerful positioning
- "Against" can be stronger than "for"
- Must be authentic - can't fake villain positioning
- Self-villains need reframe, not attack
- Output feeds Layer 3 opportunity synthesis
