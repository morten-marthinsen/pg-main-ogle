# 2.1 — Testimonial Intro Writer

**Skill:** LP-10-social-proof-writer
**Position:** Layer 2, Microskill 1
**Purpose:** Write the section headers and intro copy that open each proof block. These intros frame the reader's expectations before they encounter testimonials, reviews, or other proof. A weak intro ("See what customers say") kills engagement. A strong intro amplifies every testimonial below it.
**Effort Level:** high
**Dependencies:** `proof-slot-analysis.md` (1.1), `testimonial-plan.md` (1.2), `brief-loaded.md` (0.1), `specimen-proof-copy.md` (0.3)
**Output:** `testimonial-intros.md`

## Pre-Generation Protocol

1. **Load specimen intro patterns** from `specimen-proof-copy.md`. Hold the 2-3 best intro examples in active context as structural references.

2. **Review the anti-slop word list** from LP-10-AGENT.md. Commit to zero generic proof intros.

3. **For each proof block,** state:
   - What specific proof types are in this block? (testimonials, expert endorsements, before/afters, etc.)
   - What problem/outcome do these proof elements demonstrate?
   - What is the reader's emotional state at this point in the page? (curious, skeptical, excited, comparing)

---

## Generation Rules

### Type A (Long-Form Sales Page) Intros

Write 2-3 sentence narrative intros. These are prose paragraphs that feel like a natural continuation of the page's voice, not section headers.

**Requirements:**
- Reference the specific audience segment OR the specific problem the testimonials address
- Create anticipation for the proof that follows (don't just announce it)
- Match the page's voice direction — conversational pages get conversational intros
- Include a specificity signal (number of testimonials, timeframe, geographic range)

**Templates (adapt, don't copy):**
- Problem-specific: "If [specific problem] sounds familiar, you're not alone. [Number] people reached out to us last [timeframe] with the exact same frustration. Here's what happened after they [took action]."
- Outcome-specific: "[Number] [specific audience type] have now [specific outcome]. Their stories share a pattern you'll recognize."
- Challenge-frame: "I could list every clinical study. Instead, let me introduce you to [Name] — [one sentence about them]."

### Type B (Ecomm/PDP) Intros

Write section headers and brief context lines. These are scannable, not narrative.

**Requirements:**
- Include exact review/testimonial count where available
- Include star rating where available
- Keep to 1-2 lines maximum
- Can include filter options if review cascade follows

**Templates (adapt, don't copy):**
- "[Star rating] out of 5 — from [exact count] Verified Reviews"
- "[Exact count] customers rated [product name]. Here's what they report."
- "Real results. Real numbers. [Count] verified buyer reviews."

### Case Study Intros

Write 2-3 sentence framing that makes the reader WANT to read the full case study.

**Requirements:**
- Lead with the specific result (not the person's name)
- Include a curiosity hook about HOW
- Include timeframe

**Template:** "CASE STUDY: [Specific Result] in [Timeframe] — [Name/Company] went from [specific before state] to [specific after state]. The turning point was something they almost didn't try."

---

## Execution Steps

1. **Write intro copy for each assigned proof block** per the rules above.
2. **Write intro copy for each case study slot** per the case study rules.
3. **Verify uniqueness** — no two intros use the same opening technique or phrasing pattern.
4. **Verify specificity** — every intro contains at least one specific element (number, audience descriptor, outcome type).
5. **Run anti-slop check** on all intros — zero generic phrases.

## Quality Criteria
- Every assigned proof block and case study has intro copy
- Zero generic intros ("See what customers say", "Real results from real people")
- Every intro contains at least one specificity signal
- All intros are unique — no repeated techniques or phrasing patterns
- Type A intros are narrative prose; Type B intros are scannable headers
- Case study intros lead with the result and include a curiosity hook
- Zero AI telltales

## Failure Modes
- Writing the same intro template for every proof block -> Each intro must be unique in technique and phrasing. If you notice yourself repeating, stop and generate a fresh approach.
- Generic framing that could apply to any product -> Each intro must reference THIS audience, THIS problem, or THIS outcome type.
- Case study intros that lead with the person's name instead of the result -> The result is the hook. The person is the vehicle. Lead with what they achieved.
- Intros that reveal too much -> The intro should create anticipation, not summarize the proof. Leave the specific outcomes for the testimonials themselves.
