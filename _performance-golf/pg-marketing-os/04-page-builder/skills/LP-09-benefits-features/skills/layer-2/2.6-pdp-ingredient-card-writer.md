# 2.6 — PDP Ingredient Card Writer

**Skill:** LP-09-benefits-features
**Position:** Layer 2, Microskill 6
**Purpose:** Write PDP-specific ingredient cards using the "X for Y" naming pattern ("[Ingredient] for [Benefit]"). Each card is a self-contained, vertically-stacked unit with ingredient image direction, name, dose, one-line mechanism, and outcome-focused benefit statement. This microskill REPLACES 2.2 for PDP ingredient sections — 2.2 is Type A deep-dives; 2.6 is Type B/Hybrid card format.

## Pre-Generation Protocol

**Step 1: Load PDP specimens.** Study LP-SPEC-09 (LMNT — ingredient transparency) and LP-SPEC-10 (BIOpt — ingredient breakdowns). Extract: card structure, naming patterns, dose display, benefit language register.

**Step 2: Review ingredient strategy cards** from 1.3. Each card provides the scaffold (dose, mechanism, felt benefit, presentation priority).

**Step 3: Build the "X for Y" naming map.** For EVERY ingredient, draft the "[Ingredient] for [Benefit]" headline BEFORE writing the full card. This naming is the most critical element — it's what gets scanned.

**Step 4: Read hero threading anchor.** Card benefit language must reinforce the hero promise, not diverge from it.

**Step 5: Review anti-slop word list.** Commit to zero AI telltales. PDP cards are especially prone to "Supports overall wellness" type vague language because of their short format.

## Execution Logic

### The "X for Y" Naming Pattern

Every ingredient card headline follows this exact pattern:

```
[Ingredient Name] for [Specific Benefit]
```

**Examples:**
- "Magnesium Glycinate for Deep Sleep"
- "Ashwagandha KSM-66 for Stress Resilience"
- "Vitamin D3 for Immune Defense"
- "L-Theanine for Calm Focus"
- "Zinc Picolinate for Faster Recovery"

**Rules for "X for Y" naming:**
- The benefit (Y) must be SPECIFIC — not "overall health" or "general wellness"
- The benefit must be the PRIMARY felt benefit of that ingredient (not secondary)
- The ingredient name (X) uses the specific FORM if the form matters (e.g., "Magnesium Glycinate" not just "Magnesium")
- If the form doesn't meaningfully differentiate, use the common name
- Max 6 words total in the "X for Y" headline (typically 4-5)
- Each ingredient's benefit (Y) must be DISTINCT from every other card's benefit

### Per-Card Structure

Write each ingredient card in this format:

```markdown
---

### [Ingredient] for [Benefit]

**Icon/Image Direction:** [Brief direction for designer — e.g., "molecular structure illustration" or "ingredient source photo (ashwagandha root)" or "benefit-associated icon (moon/stars for sleep)"]

**Dose:** [exact dose per serving from brief]

**How it works:** [One-line mechanism — plain language, not scientific jargon. 12-20 words. Explains the action, not the chemistry.]

**The benefit:** [Outcome-focused benefit statement, 8-15 words. What the customer FEELS or EXPERIENCES. Present tense, active voice.]

<details>
<summary>Learn More</summary>

[2-4 sentences: expanded mechanism explanation + clinical citation if available. This is the accordion/expandable content for readers who want depth.]

</details>

---
```

### Presentation Order

Follow the order from ingredient-strategy.md (1.3):
1. Hero ingredient (most directly connected to headline promise) — appears first
2. Supporting ingredients — middle positions
3. Secondary ingredients — final positions

### Vertical Stacking Requirement

Cards are stacked VERTICALLY (one per row, full-width). NOT in a horizontal grid. Baymard research: 27% of items overlooked in horizontal card layouts vs. 8% in vertical. Every card is a self-contained unit that can be read independently without context from adjacent cards.

### Section Framing

**Section opening (before first card):**
```markdown
## What's Inside — And Why It Matters

[1-2 sentences: Frame the ingredient section. Connect to the hero promise. Not "here are our ingredients" but "here's what makes [product] work."]
```

**Section closing (after last card):**
```markdown
### The Complete Formula

[1-2 sentences: Tie ingredients together. Why the COMBINATION matters. Short — this is PDP, not Type A.]
```

## Quality Criteria
- Every card uses the "X for Y" naming pattern — no exceptions
- Each "X for Y" headline has a DISTINCT benefit (no two cards share the same benefit word/concept)
- Doses match exactly what the brief states (not estimated, not rounded)
- One-line mechanisms are plain language (12-20 words, no jargon)
- Benefit statements are outcome-focused, 8-15 words, present tense
- Expandable detail includes mechanism depth + citation where available
- Icon/image direction is specific enough for a designer to execute
- Cards are structured for vertical stacking (NOT horizontal grid)
- Tone matches hero threading anchor
- Zero forbidden words from anti-slop list
- D-F-W-B-P chain complete for every card

## Failure Modes
- "X for Y" headline uses vague benefit ("Magnesium for Health") -> Benefit must be specific and picturable. "Health" is not a benefit. "Deep Sleep" is.
- Two cards have overlapping benefits ("for Energy" and "for Natural Energy") -> Each card must own a DISTINCT benefit dimension. Deduplicate or differentiate.
- Mechanism line is scientific jargon ("modulates GABAergic neurotransmission") -> Translate: "calms overactive brain signals so you fall asleep naturally." Plain language only.
- Dose is omitted or estimated -> Dose MUST come from the brief. If missing, the card is BLOCKED for that ingredient.
- Card is not self-contained (references another card: "like the ingredient above...") -> Every card must be independently scannable. No cross-references between cards.
- Horizontal layout specified -> NEVER. Vertical stacking only per Baymard research (27% vs. 8% oversight rate).
- Benefit statement starts with ingredient name -> Benefit statement starts with outcome. "Experience deeper, more restorative sleep" not "Magnesium Glycinate helps you sleep."
- Running this microskill for Type A pages -> BLOCKED. Type A uses 2.2 (Ingredient Deep-Dive Writer). This skill is Type B/Hybrid only.
