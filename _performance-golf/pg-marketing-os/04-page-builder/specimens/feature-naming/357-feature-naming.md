## Feature Hierarchy

### Hero Feature

**Progressed Face (AKA "The Bulldozer")**
- **Naming Format:** Technical Term + Nickname
- **Caveman Test:** Bulldozer clears away rough → makes solid contact
- **Micro-script:** "Clears away thick rough that can get between the club and the ball. Plus, since the face is bigger, the sweet spot is bigger too."
- **Benefit headline:** "You'll make solid, consistent contact"
- **Copy technique:** The nickname "The Bulldozer" does more work than the technical name. Prospect instantly visualizes what the face does.
- **Naming verdict:** STRONG — The nickname is pure caveman; the technical name (Progressed Face) is functional but forgettable. The nickname carries the load.

### Supporting Features

**Power Launch Crown**
- **Naming Format:** Benefit + Component
- **Caveman Test:** Crown pressed down → ball launches higher → lands softer
- **Micro-script:** "We pressed the crown down into the top of the golf club to lower its center of gravity for you... shots naturally launch higher and land softer on greens without any manipulation."
- **Benefit headline:** "You'll launch the ball higher so it stops faster on the green"
- **Naming verdict:** GOOD — "Power Launch" front-loads the benefit; "Crown" identifies the component. Naming does double duty.

**C-Cup Construction**
- **Naming Format:** Descriptive Shape + Component
- **Caveman Test:** Welded face flexes more → more ball speed → more distance
- **Micro-script:** "The C-Cup Construction we use to weld the clubface to the head gives the face more room to flex. This is called the 'Elastic Effect.'"
- **Benefit headline:** "You'll carry the ball as far (or farther) than a 3-wood"
- **Sub-mechanism:** "Elastic Effect" — a named sub-phenomenon within C-Cup Construction
- **Naming verdict:** MODERATE — "C-Cup" is descriptive of manufacturing, not benefit. The "Elastic Effect" sub-name is more compelling. Copy correctly leans on "Elastic Effect" more than "C-Cup."

**Parallel Dual Stability Rails**
- **Naming Format:** Technical Descriptor Stack
- **Caveman Test:** Rails sit square → face squares at impact → stop slicing
- **Micro-script:** "Parallel Dual Stability Rails sit square on the ground at setup. Plus, there's extra weight in the heel to square the clubface at impact FOR YOU."
- **Benefit headline:** "You'll dial in your dispersion and stop slicing"
- **Naming verdict:** MODERATE — Four-word stack is heavy. "Stability Rails" is the core; "Parallel Dual" is engineering detail the prospect doesn't need. Would be stronger as "Stability Rails" alone with the "parallel dual" in the explanation.

**Key Insight:** The 357 uses a VARIETY of naming formats, not a single pattern. The hero feature gets the most accessible name (nickname). Supporting features lean more technical. This creates a natural hierarchy — the hero name is instantly comprehensible; supporting names reward closer reading.

## Micro-Script Craft

Each feature uses a consistent copy structure:
1. **Name the feature** (bold, often with the named technology)
2. **Explain what it does** mechanistically (1-2 sentences)
3. **Connect to felt benefit** ("This means you'll...")
4. **Dimensionalize** with a specific scenario (thick rough, 2 inches behind the ball)

The "This means..." bridge is a signature PG pattern. It forces the copy to translate every mechanism into a plain-language benefit.
