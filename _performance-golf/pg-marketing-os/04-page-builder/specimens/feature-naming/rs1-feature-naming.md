## Umbrella Technology Name

**Roll Straight Technology** — a "7-feature system" brand name.

- Format: Benefit-Action + Tech Suffix
- Why it works: "Roll Straight" is the desired outcome in putting, stated as a command. The name IS the promise.
- Structural role: Unlike 357's "Tri-Fusion" (which names the engineering approach), Roll Straight Technology names the RESULT. The prospect doesn't care about the fusion — they care about straight rolls.
- Copy usage: "The first putter with Forward Axis Balance — designed to eliminate face drift and work with your natural stroke."

## Category Creation

**"The Active-Assist Putter"** — RS1 doesn't just name features; it names a NEW CATEGORY.

The three-chapter evolution creates a named spectrum:
1. **Traditional** — "Fights you" (rear-weighted, face drifts open)
2. **Neutral** (LAB Golf) — "Stops fighting" (zero-torque, no assistance)
3. **Active-Assist** (RS1) — "Fights FOR you" (forward-weighted, counteracting force)

This is a naming specimen at the CATEGORY level, not just the feature level. It reframes the entire competitive landscape through named positions.

## Problem Naming

**"Face Drift"** — RS1 names the PROBLEM, not just the solution.

- The industry calls it "toe flow" (euphemistic, implies it's a feature)
- RS1 renames it "Face Drift" (clinical, implies it's a flaw)
- This is preemptive naming (Hopkins): take something everyone experiences, name it first, own the diagnosis
- Copy: "The industry has a name for it: 'toe flow.' They've been teaching golfers to time it for a hundred years. They've convinced you that missing short putts is a personal failing. It's not. It's a design flaw. And it has a real name: Face Drift."

**Key Insight:** Naming the problem creates a named villain that the product defeats. "Face Drift" becomes a diagnosis that only the RS1 can treat.

## Feature Hierarchy

### Hero Feature

**Forward Axis Balance**
- **Naming Format:** Spatial + Physics + Engineering Concept
- **Caveman Test:** Weight sits in front of the shaft → face stays square → putts roll straight
- **Micro-script:** "The center of gravity in the RS1 sits in front of the shaft axis. Not behind it. Not centered. In front."
- **Dimensionalization:** "Set the RS1 on a table with the head hanging off the edge, and the face points toward the ground. Your current putter probably flips face-up."
- **Proof mechanism:** "The Table Test" — a named proof-of-concept that prospects can verify themselves in 2 seconds
- **Named physics:** "Pulling vs. Pushing" — "Think of towing a trailer forward versus backing one up. One tracks perfectly. The other jackknifes."
- **Naming verdict:** STRONG — "Forward Axis" is spatial-physics language that sounds engineered and precise. "Balance" is universally understood. The name positions the feature as a fundamental physics principle, not just a product gimmick.

### Supporting Features

**High Forward MOI**
- **Naming Format:** Superlative + Spatial + Technical Acronym
- **Caveman Test:** Heavier face → doesn't twist on mishits → consistent distance
- **Micro-script:** "75% of the RS1's head weight is packed into the front 25% of the putter head. The front section alone weighs 265 grams — roughly the same as a typical 7-iron head."
- **Dimensionalization:** "You catch one toward the toe on a 30-footer. Instead of pulling up 5 feet short, the ball finishes close."
- **Naming verdict:** MODERATE — "MOI" is an acronym that golfers with tech interest understand but casual golfers don't. However, the name is SUPPORTED by a strong micro-script that translates it. Works because the target audience (putter enthusiasts) knows MOI.

**74-Degree Square-to-Square Lie Angle**
- **Naming Format:** Spec Number + Benefit Description + Technical Term
- **Caveman Test:** More upright = simpler stroke = less face rotation
- **Micro-script:** "A more upright lie keeps your hands higher and your eyes directly over the ball. It creates less stroke arc, which means less opportunity for the face to rotate."
- **Naming verdict:** MODERATE — The 74-degree spec is a differentiation point (industry standard is ~70), but the name is long. "Square-to-Square" does the heavy lifting as the benefit name.

**Face-Square Focus Alignment System**
- **Naming Format:** Benefit Stack + Feature Type
- **Caveman Test:** Three lines → instantly see if face is square → no guessing
- **Micro-script:** "Three parallel alignment lines — 1mm, 2mm, and 3mm wide — run from tail to front, drawing your eye to a perpendicular line at the face."
- **Naming verdict:** MODERATE — Long name (5 words). "Face-Square Focus" is the core; "Alignment System" is generic.

**Face-Forward Shaft Position**
- **Naming Format:** Spatial + Component + Descriptor
- **Caveman Test:** Shaft sits behind face → you see everything → clean sight line
- **Micro-script:** "The RS1 uses an onset shaft design that sits behind and below the face line. No hosel blocking your view."
- **Naming verdict:** GOOD — "Face-Forward" immediately communicates the visual benefit (you see the face). "Shaft Position" identifies the feature type.

**Multi-Material Construction**
- **Naming Format:** Compound + Manufacturing Process
- **Caveman Test:** Three materials, each with a specific job → weight where it matters
- **Micro-script:** "Three materials. Three jobs. CNC-machined steel in the front. Lightweight carbon composite woven crown. CNC-machined aluminum tail."
- **Naming verdict:** WEAK — "Multi-Material" is generic (every modern club uses multiple materials). The micro-script is strong but the name doesn't differentiate.

**Sculpted Sole Relief**
- **Naming Format:** Descriptive + Component + Benefit
- **Caveman Test:** Rounded edges → doesn't snag on turf → smooth stroke
- **Micro-script:** "Rounded front edges. A negative-space midsole that doesn't contact the ground. A sculpted tail wing that resists catching on the backstroke."
- **Naming verdict:** GOOD — "Sculpted" adds craft; "Relief" implies the problem it solves (turf interference). Compact and communicative.
