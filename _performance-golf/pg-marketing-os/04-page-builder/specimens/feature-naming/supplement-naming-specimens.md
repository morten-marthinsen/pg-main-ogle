**Purpose:** Non-golf naming specimens for PDP-02 calibration — demonstrates naming in supplement/health category
**Extracted:** 2026-03-13

---

## Why Non-Golf Specimens Matter

Feature naming is less common in supplements than in physical products — but the best supplement brands DO name their ingredients with proprietary branding. These specimens show:
1. How **manufacturer-created portmanteau names** (FORTIBONE, ApresFlex) become the spine of the sales argument
2. How **brand-created product names** (Dynamic Joint, Total Body Formula) wrap ingredients into a positioning claim
3. How the **naming hierarchy** shifts between trademarked manufacturer ingredients (hero treatment) and generic ingredients (supporting cast)

## Specimen 1: Healthy Bones Co. — Collagen Peptides

**Product Name:** Collagen Peptides Total Body Formula
**Brand Naming Pattern:** [Generic Ingredient] + [Scope Descriptor]
**Tagline/Differentiator:** "Not Your Average Collagen"

### Manufacturer-Created Trademarked Ingredients (Gelita AG)

All four names follow the same pattern: **Portmanteau = Benefit Root + Target Root**

| Name | Benefit Root | Target Root | Caveman Benefit | Dosage |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|--------|
| **FORTIBONE**(R) | FORTI (fortify) | BONE | "Improve bone mineral density" | 5g |
| **FORTIGEL**(R) | FORTI (fortify) | GEL (cartilage/gelatin) | "Support joint health, reduce discomfort" | 5g |
| **VERISOL**(R) | VERI (true/very) | SOL (solution/glow) | "Reduce wrinkles, strengthen nails" | 2.5g |
| **TENDOFORTE**(R) | TENDO (tendon) | FORTE (strong) | "Strengthen tendons and ligaments" | 0.5g |

**Naming Construction Rules:**
- Always ONE coined word (no spaces, no hyphens)
- Always Registered (R) trademark
- The portmanteau is self-describing — you can decode what it does from the name
- Always backed by named clinical studies with journal citations
- Each name appears in 8-11 separate page locations (badge, bullet, dedicated section, supplement facts, comparison table, FAQ, clinical results, timeline)

## Cross-Specimen Analysis: Naming Patterns

### Pattern 1: Manufacturer Portmanteau
**The most powerful naming pattern found in supplement e-comm.**

Construction: Fuse a **benefit/scientific root** + a **target/function root** into ONE coined word.

| Name | Root 1 (Benefit/Science) | Root 2 (Target/Function) |
|------|-------------------------|-------------------------|
| FORTIBONE | Fortify | Bone |
| FORTIGEL | Fortify | Gel (cartilage) |
| VERISOL | Verify/Very | Solution/Glow |
| TENDOFORTE | Tendon | Strong |
| ApresFlex | After (relief) | Flexibility |

**Rules:**
- One word, no spaces, no hyphens
- CamelCase or AllCaps
- Always trademarked (R or TM)
- Self-describing (you can decode function from the name)
- ALWAYS backed by clinical evidence

### Pattern 2: Aspirational Adjective + Target (Product Line Naming)
**Stonehenge Health pattern:** Pick ONE adjective, apply it to every product.
- "Dynamic" + Joint, Nerve, Biotics, GLP-1
- Creates instant product recognition and line extension

### Pattern 3: Scope Descriptor (Positioning Sub-Name)
**Healthy Bones Co. pattern:** Generic product category + positioning scope.
- "Collagen Peptides **Total Body Formula**"
- The scope descriptor widens the perceived benefit range

### Pattern 4: Named Expert Persona
**Healthy Bones Co. pattern:** [Target] + [Authority Role]
- "Bone Coach(TM)"
- Turns the founder into a branded credibility asset

### Pattern 5: Named Programs/Rituals
- "Bone Renewal Ritual(TM)" — usage instructions elevated to a named experience
- "Stronger Bones Kickstart" — lead magnet elevated to a named program

### Pattern 6: Time-to-Results as Named Feature
**Stonehenge Health pattern:** "Results In As Little As 5 Days"
- The speed claim is deployed as an icon badge, a bullet, a timeline milestone, a clinical stat, AND an FAQ answer
- It functions as a FEATURE even though it's technically a performance claim

## PDP-02 Calibration Notes

1. **Portmanteau names carry the most perceived proprietary value** — they sound un-copyable because they ARE un-copyable (coined words vs. descriptive phrases)
2. **Brand creates the wrapper; manufacturer creates the hero ingredient name** — this is the reverse of golf, where the BRAND creates all feature names
3. **The naming hierarchy IS the attention hierarchy** — trademarked ingredients get 10x more page real estate than generic ingredients
4. **Single-adjective product line naming** (Dynamic + X) is extremely scalable for brands with multiple products
5. **For golf/physical products:** The portmanteau pattern maps directly — "RollStraight" (RS1), "AutoGlide" (ONE.1), "SliceFix" (SF2) are already PG's version of this pattern
6. **Time-to-results claims** can be treated as named features — "Results in 5 Days" is functionally equivalent to a feature name on the page
7. **Deployment density matters** — the best-named ingredients appear in 8-11 page locations, not just one section
