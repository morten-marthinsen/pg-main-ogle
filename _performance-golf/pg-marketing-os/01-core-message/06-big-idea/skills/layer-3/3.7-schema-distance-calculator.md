---

## Purpose

Calculate precise Schema Distance Scores (SDS) for each candidate using the three-index composite model (CNI + BCI + LNI) calibrated by the Information Gap Calibration (IGC). This replaces simple "novelty" scoring with a psychologically-grounded measurement that predicts genuine cognitive disruption — the moment when a prospect's existing mental model encounters information that cannot be assimilated without restructuring.

---

## Theoretical Foundation

### Loewenstein's Curiosity Gap Theory (1994)

George Loewenstein's information-gap theory of curiosity posits that curiosity arises when individuals perceive a gap between what they know and what they want to know. The key insight: **curiosity is maximized at an intermediate information gap** — too small and there's nothing to learn; too large and the prospect disengages.

### Schema Theory (Piaget, Bartlett)

Schemas are cognitive frameworks that organize and interpret information. When new information contradicts existing schemas, one of three outcomes occurs:
1. **Assimilation** — New info absorbed into existing schema (low distance = no disruption)
2. **Accommodation** — Schema restructures to incorporate new info (optimal distance = productive disruption)
3. **Rejection** — Schema rejects info as invalid (high distance = cognitive shutdown)

### The "Finally Someone Said It" (FSSIT) Phenomenon

The most powerful Big Ideas operate in the "Finally Someone Said It" zone — where schema distance is high enough to feel revelatory but anchored enough to trigger recognition rather than rejection. This is the feeling when someone articulates what you've suspected but couldn't verbalize.

## The Three-Index Composite Model

### Index 1: Claim Novelty Index (CNI)

**Definition:** How frequently this claim (or a functionally equivalent claim) appears in competitor messaging.

```yaml
cni_calculation:
  measurement_basis: "Inverse of competitor frequency"

  scoring_rubric:
    score_0_2:
      description: "Ubiquitous claim"
      criteria: "80%+ of competitors make this or equivalent claim"
      examples:
        - "Natural ingredients"
        - "Doctor-formulated"
        - "Backed by science"

    score_3_4:
      description: "Common claim"
      criteria: "50-79% of competitors make similar claims"
      examples:
        - "Targets the root cause"
        - "Works at the cellular level"
        - "Restores natural balance"

    score_5_6:
      description: "Differentiated claim"
      criteria: "20-49% of competitors make related claims"
      examples:
        - "Addresses bile flow specifically"
        - "Targets the gut-brain axis"
        - "Based on Blue Zone research"

    score_7_8:
      description: "Rare claim"
      criteria: "5-19% of competitors touch this territory"
      examples:
        - "Reverses leptin resistance"
        - "Reactivates dormant fat-burning cells"
        - "Bypasses the blood-brain barrier"

    score_9_10:
      description: "Unprecedented claim"
      criteria: "<5% of competitors (or zero) make anything similar"
      examples:
        - "The 'second brain' in your gut controls weight more than your actual brain"
        - "Everything you've learned about cholesterol is backwards"

  calculation_process:
    step_1: "Count competitors in saturation_mapping from Layer 1"
    step_2: "Search each competitor's core claims for functional equivalents"
    step_3: "Calculate percentage of competitors with similar claims"
    step_4: "Apply inverse mapping: (100% - competitor_percentage) / 10 = CNI"
    step_5: "Round to one decimal place"
```

### Index 2: Belief Contradiction Index (BCI)

**Definition:** How many "market truths" (widely-held beliefs in the target audience) the claim directly contradicts.

```yaml
bci_calculation:
  measurement_basis: "Count of contradicted market beliefs × contradiction intensity"

  market_truth_sources:
    primary: "deep_research_summary.from_classified_quotes.market_beliefs"
    secondary: "saturation_mapping.dominant_narratives"
    tertiary: "Failed solutions that imply beliefs (e.g., 'dieting works' implied by diet products)"

  scoring_rubric:
    score_0_2:
      description: "Confirmation"
      criteria: "Claim reinforces existing beliefs"
      belief_count: "0 beliefs contradicted"
      example: "Exercise helps you lose weight"

    score_3_4:
      description: "Mild contradiction"
      criteria: "Claim contradicts 1 peripheral belief"
      belief_count: "1 belief contradicted"
      example: "Cardio isn't the best exercise for weight loss"

    score_5_6:
      description: "Moderate contradiction"
      criteria: "Claim contradicts 2-3 beliefs, including 1 core belief"
      belief_count: "2-3 beliefs contradicted"
      example: "Eating less isn't the answer — your body is working against you"

    score_7_8:
      description: "Strong contradiction"
      criteria: "Claim contradicts 3-4 beliefs, including 2+ core beliefs"
      belief_count: "3-4 beliefs contradicted"
      example: "The 'healthy foods' you eat daily are making you fat"

    score_9_10:
      description: "Paradigm violation"
      criteria: "Claim inverts fundamental worldview"
      belief_count: "5+ beliefs contradicted OR 1 foundational belief inverted"
      example: "Calories have nothing to do with weight gain"

  calculation_process:
    step_1: "Extract market beliefs from deep research"
    step_2: "For each candidate claim, identify contradicted beliefs"
    step_3: "Classify each contradiction: peripheral (1pt) or core (2pts)"
    step_4: "Sum contradiction points, cap at 10"
    step_5: "BCI = MIN(contradiction_points, 10)"
```

### Index 3: Linguistic Novelty Index (LNI)

**Definition:** How unexpected the language, framing, or conceptual metaphor is relative to market conventions.

```yaml
lni_calculation:
  measurement_basis: "Deviation from expected linguistic patterns"

  evaluation_dimensions:
    vocabulary_novelty:
      weight: 0.30
      measures: "Use of unexpected terms, neologisms, borrowed vocabulary"

    framing_novelty:
      weight: 0.35
      measures: "Unexpected angle of approach, surprising frame selection"

    metaphor_novelty:
      weight: 0.35
      measures: "Fresh conceptual metaphors, unexpected analogies"

  scoring_rubric:
    score_0_2:
      description: "Market standard language"
      criteria: "Uses expected vocabulary, framing, and metaphors"
      vocabulary: "Common market terms"
      framing: "Expected angle"
      metaphor: "Conventional analogies"
      examples:
        - "Boost your metabolism"
        - "Burn fat faster"
        - "Your body is like a machine"

    score_3_4:
      description: "Slight variation"
      criteria: "Minor vocabulary or framing deviation"
      vocabulary: "1-2 unexpected terms"
      framing: "Slightly unusual angle"
      metaphor: "Familiar metaphor with twist"
      examples:
        - "Reboot your metabolism" (slight vocab shift)
        - "Your body's thermostat is broken" (familiar metaphor)

    score_5_6:
      description: "Moderate novelty"
      criteria: "Clear linguistic differentiation"
      vocabulary: "New terms introduced"
      framing: "Different angle than competitors"
      metaphor: "Fresh metaphor selection"
      examples:
        - "Wake up your 'sleeping' fat cells"
        - "Your gut is your 'second brain'"
        - "Metabolic switching"

    score_7_8:
      description: "High novelty"
      criteria: "Memorable language that stands out"
      vocabulary: "Coined terms or surprising vocabulary"
      framing: "Contrarian or inverted frame"
      metaphor: "Novel conceptual metaphor"
      examples:
        - "Lectins: the invisible assassins in your salad"
        - "Your fat cells are 'locked' and you don't have the key"
        - "The 'second brain' rebellion"

    score_9_10:
      description: "Breakthrough language"
      criteria: "Category-defining linguistic innovation"
      vocabulary: "New vocabulary that could become market standard"
      framing: "Paradigm-shifting frame"
      metaphor: "Breakthrough conceptual metaphor"
      examples:
        - "The 'Fat Flusher' phenomenon"
        - "Your body is running a 'starvation program' from 10,000 years ago"

  calculation_process:
    step_1: "Score vocabulary_novelty (0-10)"
    step_2: "Score framing_novelty (0-10)"
    step_3: "Score metaphor_novelty (0-10)"
    step_4: "LNI = (vocab × 0.30) + (framing × 0.35) + (metaphor × 0.35)"
    step_5: "Round to one decimal place"
```

## Information Gap Calibration (IGC)

### Purpose

The IGC ensures schema distance is calibrated to the prospect's information state. A claim might score high on CNI/BCI/LNI but fail because the information gap is either too large (incomprehensible) or too small (boring).

```yaml
igc_calculation:
  measurement_basis: "Loewenstein information gap optimal zone detection"

  information_gap_zones:
    too_small:
      igc_range: "0-3"
      description: "Prospect already knows this or easily infers it"
      psychological_state: "Boredom, dismissal, 'tell me something I don't know'"
      response: "Skip, scroll, exit"

    optimal_zone:
      igc_range: "4-7"
      description: "Prospect perceives meaningful gap that feels closable"
      psychological_state: "Curiosity activated, engagement, 'I need to know more'"
      response: "Continue reading, click, engage"

    too_large:
      igc_range: "8-10"
      description: "Gap so large prospect cannot bridge it"
      psychological_state: "Confusion, skepticism, cognitive shutdown"
      response: "Dismissal as 'too good to be true' or incomprehensible"

  igc_scoring_rubric:
    score_0_2:
      description: "Minimal gap"
      criteria: "Claim is obvious or easily derivable from common knowledge"
      example: "Eating vegetables is healthy"

    score_3_4:
      description: "Small gap"
      criteria: "Claim adds minor new information to existing framework"
      example: "Some vegetables have more nutrients than others"

    score_5_6:
      description: "Optimal gap"
      criteria: "Claim presents meaningful new information that extends understanding"
      example: "Certain vegetables contain compounds that specifically target belly fat"

    score_7_8:
      description: "Large gap"
      criteria: "Claim presents surprising information that challenges but doesn't overwhelm"
      example: "Some 'healthy' vegetables actually trigger weight gain in certain people"

    score_9_10:
      description: "Maximum gap"
      criteria: "Claim presents paradigm-shifting information (requires significant anchoring)"
      example: "Vegetables are the primary cause of your weight problem"

  calculation_inputs:
    from_deep_research:
      - "Prospect awareness level (Schwartz stage)"
      - "Existing knowledge inventory from quotes"
      - "Failed solution familiarity"
    from_saturation_mapping:
      - "Market education level"
      - "Competitor claim exposure"
```

## Raw SDS to Calibrated SDS Conversion

### The Core Formula

```
Raw SDS = (CNI + BCI + LNI) / 3

Calibration Multiplier = IGC / 5

Calibrated SDS = Raw SDS × Calibration Multiplier
```

### Optimal Zone Definition

```yaml
calibrated_sds_zones:
  too_low:
    range: "0.0 - 3.5"
    interpretation: "Insufficient schema disruption"
    prospect_response: "Not compelling, easily forgotten"
    action: "Increase CNI/BCI/LNI or reframe for larger gap"

  optimal:
    range: "3.6 - 5.6"
    interpretation: "Maximum engagement zone"
    prospect_response: "Curiosity activated, 'Finally Someone Said It' resonance"
    action: "Proceed to RSF integration"

  elevated:
    range: "5.7 - 7.0"
    interpretation: "High disruption requiring strong anchoring"
    prospect_response: "Skepticism risk, requires proof stacking"
    action: "Increase Anchor-to-Distance Ratio in Resolution Accessibility"

  too_high:
    range: "7.1+"
    interpretation: "Rejection zone"
    prospect_response: "Cognitive shutdown, 'too good to be true' dismissal"
    action: "Reduce schema distance or significantly increase anchoring"
```

### Calibration Examples

```yaml
calibration_examples:
  example_1:
    raw_scores:
      CNI: 7.0

    interpretation: "TOO HIGH — requires anchoring increase or claim moderation"

      raw_sds: "(5.0 + 4.0 + 6.0) / 3 = 5.0"
      calibration_multiplier: "5.0 / 5 = 1.0"
      calibrated_sds: "5.0 × 1.0 = 5.0"
    interpretation: "OPTIMAL — proceed with standard anchoring"

  example_3:
    raw_scores:
      CNI: 3.0
      BCI: 3.0
      LNI: 4.0
    IGC: 4.0
    calculation:
      raw_sds: "(3.0 + 3.0 + 4.0) / 3 = 3.33"
      calibration_multiplier: "4.0 / 5 = 0.8"

    interpretation: "TOO LOW — increase novelty or contradiction"
```

## Process Logic

### Step 1: Calculate CNI for Each Candidate

```
FOR each candidate in candidate_architectures:

  EXTRACT core_claim from candidate.core_concept.one_paragraph_description

  LOAD competitors from saturation_mapping
  LOAD dominant_claims from saturation_mapping

  COUNT competitors_with_similar = 0

  FOR each competitor:
    FOR each competitor_claim in competitor.claims:
      IF functional_equivalence(core_claim, competitor_claim):
        competitors_with_similar += 1
        RECORD similar_claim
        BREAK

  competitor_percentage = competitors_with_similar / total_competitors × 100

  CNI = (100 - competitor_percentage) / 10
  ROUND to one decimal

  RECORD CNI with evidence and rationale
```

### Step 2: Calculate BCI for Each Candidate

```
FOR each candidate:

  EXTRACT claims from candidate architecture (root_cause_reframe, mechanism, promise)

  LOAD market_beliefs from deep_research_summary

  contradiction_points = 0
  beliefs_contradicted = []

  FOR each market_belief:
    IF candidate_claims CONTRADICT market_belief:
      DETERMINE contradiction_type:
        IF foundational_belief: points = 3
        IF core_belief: points = 2
        IF peripheral_belief: points = 1

      contradiction_points += points
      RECORD belief and type

  BCI = MIN(contradiction_points, 10)

  RECORD BCI with beliefs and rationale
```

### Step 3: Calculate LNI for Each Candidate

```
FOR each candidate:

  EXTRACT linguistic_elements:
    vocabulary: mechanism_name, named_enemies, coined_terms
    framing: angle_of_approach, perspective_chosen
    metaphors: analogies_used, conceptual_frames

  LOAD market_standard_language from saturation_mapping.dominant_narratives

  SCORE vocabulary_novelty (0-10):
    Compare vocabulary to market standards
    Award points for unexpected terms, neologisms, borrowed vocabulary

  SCORE framing_novelty (0-10):
    Compare angle to competitor angles
    Award points for unexpected perspectives, inverted frames

  SCORE metaphor_novelty (0-10):
    Compare metaphors to common market metaphors
    Award points for fresh analogies, breakthrough conceptual metaphors

  LNI = (vocabulary × 0.30) + (framing × 0.35) + (metaphor × 0.35)
  ROUND to one decimal

  RECORD LNI with dimension scores and novel elements
```

### Step 4: Calculate IGC for Each Candidate

```
FOR each candidate:

  LOAD schwartz_stage from market_config
  LOAD prospect_knowledge from deep_research quotes

  ASSESS information_gap:
    gap_size = distance between:
      - What prospect currently knows (from quotes)
      - What candidate claims (from architecture)

  SCORE IGC (0-10):
    IF gap_size is trivially small: IGC = 0-2
    IF gap_size is small but meaningful: IGC = 3-4
    IF gap_size is optimal (curiosity-inducing): IGC = 5-6
    IF gap_size is large (surprise-inducing): IGC = 7-8
    IF gap_size is paradigm-shifting: IGC = 9-10

  DETERMINE gap_zone:
    IF IGC <= 3: "too_small"
    IF IGC >= 4 AND IGC <= 7: "optimal"
    IF IGC >= 8: "too_large"

  RECORD IGC with zone and rationale
```

### Step 5: Calculate Raw and Calibrated SDS

```
FOR each candidate:

  CALCULATE Raw_SDS = (CNI + BCI + LNI) / 3
  ROUND to two decimals

  CALCULATE Calibration_Multiplier = IGC / 5
  ROUND to two decimals

  CALCULATE Calibrated_SDS = Raw_SDS × Calibration_Multiplier
  ROUND to two decimals

  DETERMINE zone:
    IF Calibrated_SDS < 3.6: "too_low"
    IF Calibrated_SDS >= 3.6 AND <= 5.6: "optimal"
    IF Calibrated_SDS > 5.6 AND <= 7.0: "elevated"
    IF Calibrated_SDS > 7.0: "too_high"

  RECORD both SDS values with formulas and zone interpretation
```

### Step 6: Determine Anchoring Requirements

```
FOR each candidate:

  BASED ON calibrated_sds.zone:

  IF zone == "too_low":
    anchoring_requirement.level = "minimal"
    anchoring_requirement.rationale = "Low schema distance requires no special anchoring"
    anchoring_requirement.recommended_anchors = []

  IF zone == "optimal":
    anchoring_requirement.level = "standard"
    anchoring_requirement.rationale = "Standard proof and authority anchoring sufficient"
    anchoring_requirement.recommended_anchors = [
      {type: "authority", description: "Single credentialed expert"},
      {type: "evidence", description: "2-3 proof elements"},
      {type: "language", description: "Familiar industry vocabulary in framing"}
    ]

  IF zone == "elevated":
    anchoring_requirement.level = "elevated"
    anchoring_requirement.rationale = "Elevated distance requires additional anchoring"
    anchoring_requirement.recommended_anchors = [
      {type: "authority_stacking", description: "Multiple credentialed sources"},
      {type: "evidence_cascade", description: "5+ proof elements with progression"},
      {type: "language", description: "Bridge unfamiliar concepts with familiar anchors"},
      {type: "incremental", description: "Step audience toward claim gradually"}
    ]

  IF zone == "too_high":
    anchoring_requirement.level = "maximum"
    anchoring_requirement.rationale = "Maximum anchoring or schema distance reduction required"
    anchoring_requirement.recommended_anchors = [
      {type: "authority_institution", description: "Institutional authority (Harvard, Mayo, etc.)"},
      {type: "evidence_overwhelming", description: "8+ proof elements with institutional backing"},
      {type: "social_proof_scale", description: "Millions served / major media validation"},
      {type: "incremental_revelation", description: "Multi-stage revelation with validation at each step"},
      {type: "familiar_entry", description: "Enter through highly familiar territory before novel claim"}
    ]
```

## Constraints

- NEVER assign CNI/BCI/LNI/IGC scores without specific evidence citations
- NEVER produce calibrated SDS without showing the full formula calculation
- NEVER skip the anchoring requirement determination
- NEVER claim "optimal" zone without calibrated SDS between 3.6-5.6
- ALWAYS round CNI, BCI, LNI to one decimal place
- ALWAYS round Raw SDS and Calibrated SDS to two decimal places
- ALWAYS cite specific competitor claims for CNI evidence
- ALWAYS cite specific market beliefs for BCI evidence
- ALWAYS cite specific linguistic elements for LNI evidence
- ALWAYS produce scores for all 5 candidates

## Quality Criteria

1. All 5 candidates have complete SDS calculations (no empty fields)
2. All CNI scores cite specific competitor evidence
3. All BCI scores cite specific contradicted beliefs with types
4. All LNI scores break down all three dimensions with examples
5. All IGC scores identify the gap zone correctly
6. All calibrated SDS calculations show the full formula
7. All anchoring requirements match the zone interpretation
8. Summary statistics are mathematically correct
