# Layer 3.4 — Coherence Marker Integrator

## Purpose

Weave coherence markers throughout the campaign argument to create continuation of thought. Coherence markers are words and phrases that connect sentences and paragraphs into a single flowing narrative — making the argument less disjointed, easier to follow, and harder to disengage from.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Understand Coherence Markers vs. Connectors

**Connectors** (from 3.3) are standalone transition phrases between CPB chunks.
**Coherence markers** are woven WITHIN and BETWEEN sentences to create flow.

Coherence markers are more granular — they make individual sentences feel like they belong together, creating a river of thought rather than a list of statements.

### Step 2: Catalog Coherence Marker Types

| Marker Type | Function | Examples |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Continuation** | Signals "there's more to this" | "And...", "Plus...", "On top of that...", "What's more..." |
| **Elaboration** | Signals "let me explain further" | "In fact...", "Specifically...", "Here's what I mean...", "To put it simply..." |
| **Cause-Effect** | Signals "this causes that" | "Because of this...", "That's why...", "As a result...", "Which is exactly why..." |
| **Contrast** | Signals "but here's the twist" | "But...", "However...", "Unlike...", "On the other hand..." |
| **Consequence** | Signals "so what does this mean?" | "So...", "Which means...", "The upshot is...", "Bottom line..." |
| **Summary** | Signals "here's the key takeaway" | "In other words...", "Put simply...", "The point is...", "What this really means is..." |
| **Temporal** | Signals progression through time | "First...", "Then...", "Once you...", "After that...", "Eventually..." |

### Step 3: Identify Coherence Needs Within Chunks

Within each CPB chunk, coherence markers should:
- Bridge the claim to the proof: "The reason why is..." / "Because..." / "Here's why..."
- Bridge the proof to the benefit: "Which means..." / "So what does this mean for you?" / "The result?"
- Connect multiple proof elements: "And not only that..." / "On top of that..." / "Plus..."

### Step 4: Identify Coherence Needs Between Chunks

Between chunks (where connectors already exist or natural transitions flow):
- Add a marker at the start of the next chunk's claim that references the previous chunk's conclusion
- Use cause-effect markers to create logical chains: "Because [previous chunk established X]... [current chunk can claim Y]"
- Use summary markers before pivots: "So now you know [summary of previous]... But there's something else..."

### Step 5: Apply the Conversational Flow Test

Per both E5 and Makepeace frameworks: the argument must sound like spoken language. Coherence markers should make the text feel like someone is TALKING to the prospect, not writing to them.

**Good coherence flow:**
> "The reason this works is because [mechanism] directly addresses [root cause]. And because it addresses the root cause — not just the symptoms — the results are fundamentally different. Which means you're not just getting temporary relief. You're getting lasting change."

**Poor coherence flow (missing markers):**
> "[Mechanism] addresses [root cause]. It addresses the root cause, not symptoms. Results are different. You get lasting change."

### Step 6: Map Coherence Markers to Positions

For each marker, document:
- Where it appears (within which chunk, between which chunks)
- What type it is (continuation, elaboration, cause-effect, etc.)
- What function it serves in the argument flow

### Step 7: Validate Marker Density

**Target density:** 2-4 coherence markers per CPB chunk (within the chunk)
- Below 2: The chunk may feel disjointed
- Above 4: The chunk may feel over-connected and wordy

**Between-chunk markers:** 0-1 per transition (in addition to any connector from 3.3)

## Quality Gates

- [ ] Every CPB chunk has 2-4 coherence markers within it
- [ ] At least 1 "cause-effect" marker per chunk (connecting claim to proof)
- [ ] At least 1 "consequence" marker per chunk (connecting proof to benefit)
- [ ] No forced markers (all pass conversational check as "natural" or "acceptable")
- [ ] Marker variety (not all the same type — minimum 3 different types used)
- [ ] Between-chunk markers don't duplicate connectors (complementary, not redundant)
- [ ] Overall flow score ≥ 7.0/10
- [ ] Markers sound spoken, not written (no "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally")

---

## Anti-Patterns

- **Academic markers:** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In addition" — these are essay markers, not spoken language
- **Over-marking:** Every sentence starts with a marker — feels mechanical and over-engineered
- **Under-marking:** No markers at all — the argument feels like a disconnected list
- **Redundant markers:** Coherence marker + connector saying the same thing — pick one
- **Wrong type:** Using a contrast marker when there's no contrast — markers must match the logical relationship
- **Filler markers:** "Now..." "So..." "Well..." used without purpose — markers must serve a specific flow function
