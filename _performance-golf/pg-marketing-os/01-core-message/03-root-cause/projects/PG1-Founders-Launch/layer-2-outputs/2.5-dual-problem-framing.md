# 2.5 — Dual Problem Framing

**Microskill:** 2.5-dual-problem-framing
**Approved Concept:** A — The Undiagnosed Golfer
**Purpose:** Explore whether two compounding problems strengthen the root cause

---

## Dual Problem Analysis

### Candidate Dual Frame: The Diagnosis Gap + The Symptom Spiral

**Problem 1 (External — The Industry):** The golf improvement industry has NEVER offered root flaw diagnosis. It operates entirely on treatment: tips, lessons, drills, data. The diagnostic step doesn't exist in the model.

**Problem 2 (Compounding — The Golfer's Response):** Without diagnosis, the golfer enters a symptom spiral. Each treatment addresses a visible symptom, which creates new compensations, which become new symptoms, which demand new treatments. The harder they work, the deeper they spiral.

**How they compound:** Problem 1 creates the conditions. Problem 2 is the predictable result. The industry's failure to diagnose (P1) traps the golfer in an endless loop of symptom-chasing (P2). Neither problem alone fully captures the dynamic — it's the COMBINATION that explains why dedicated golfers get MORE stuck over time, not less.

### DP-1: "No Diagnosis + Infinite Symptoms"

**Three-part structure:**
- **What they think:** "I have multiple swing problems and I'm working through them."
- **What's real:** "You have TWO problems, not twelve. First: nobody has ever diagnosed your root swing flaw. Second: without that diagnosis, every fix you've applied has created new compensations — which looked like new problems — which sent you chasing more fixes. It's not that you have a dozen swing issues. You have ONE undiagnosed flaw and a dozen compensations stacked on top of it."
- **Why nothing worked:** "Each solution addressed a compensation. Fixing a compensation without fixing the root flaw forced your body to compensate differently. That's why every instructor found something DIFFERENT wrong — they were each seeing a different layer of compensation. The root flaw sat beneath all of it, untouched."

**Anchor phrase:** "One flaw. A dozen compensations."
**Strength:** 8/10 — Powerful reframe that explains the "every instructor sees something different" experience. Slightly complex (dual problem requires more explanation).
**Soul.md compliance:** PASS — Revelatory, not accusatory. Hope-forward because it reduces "12 problems" to "1 flaw."

### DP-2: "Missing Diagnosis + Misdirected Effort"

**Three-part structure:**
- **What they think:** "I need to work harder or smarter."
- **What's real:** "Two things are true at once. First: the diagnostic step has been missing from every golf improvement path you've taken. Second: because it's missing, all your effort — and it was REAL effort — went in the wrong direction. You weren't lazy. You were misdirected."
- **Why nothing worked:** "Hard work + wrong direction = frustration. The industry misdirected you by skipping the diagnosis. Your effort was real. Your dedication was admirable. But without knowing THE flaw, even perfect effort goes sideways."

**Anchor phrase:** "Real effort. Wrong direction."
**Strength:** 7/10 — Strong emotional resonance (honors effort while explaining failure). Less technically precise than DP-1.
**Soul.md compliance:** STRONG PASS — Deeply validating. "Your effort was real."

---

## Dual Problem Assessment

The dual problem framing adds depth but risks complexity. For the PG1 launch, the SINGLE root cause ("nobody diagnosed your root flaw") is cleaner and more memorable. The dual frame is best used as a SUPPORTING narrative beat within the primary simple reframe — not as the lead expression.

**Recommendation:** Use dual problem elements as sub-beats within the primary expression. "One flaw, a dozen compensations" is a strong supporting line. "Real effort, wrong direction" is a strong emotional validator. Neither should be the primary anchor phrase.
