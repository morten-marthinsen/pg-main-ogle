# Does it "personify" the problem or solution?

Stories strengthen images. For tens of thousands of years, humans passed down information via stories instead of the written word.

Our minds have evolved to respond to and retain stories. Particularly stories about humans interacting with each other. (i.e. our trigger events - hosted an experiment to test the mechanism with the guru)

For ages, our ancestors personified their environment to give it meaning and understand it better. The personified trees, animals, planets, the stars, etc. and told stories about it.

We can use the same principle to create stronger images in our copywriting concepts. If we can "personify" aspects of the mechanism, that are otherwise dull or dense, we make the mental movie playing in our prospect's head more vivid, exciting, and real.

**DIFFERENTIATION SCORE**

A unique mechanism should be unique. It must be clearly different than the solutions that have failed your prospect before.

A great mechanism automatically destroys any solutions in the mind of the prospect. It does so in a way that means you only need a minimal amount of explicit destructions of other solutions.

**CENTRAL THESIS COHESION SCORE**

Ryan calls it the "central thesis".

I call it your argument's conclusion.

Both, generally, mean the core argument that your prospect MUST buy into, in order to be willing to consider buying your product.

It's the BIG presale that absolutely has to happen, for your mechanism to be effective.

In markets that have both a "pain" and "hope" element, I like to make my argument's primary conclusion binary. I write it in very "black and white" terms, that speak directly to the prospect's dream scenario, and pain scenario.

The logic is usually something like:

If [core concept of the argument] works in X manner... then [primary benefit],
regardless of [known "root causes" for their issue, that they think is responsible for it]

If [core concept of the argument] works in the opposite manner, then [primary pain point or fear]... even if you do [conventional solutions]

**SUPER POWER SCORE**

"Solution Focused" markets are driven by *pleasure* (in the moment) and *hope* (their dreams being realized).

In these markets, it makes sense to focus more on a more *benefit* driven mechanism, and more benefit driven copy in general.

It makes less sense to spend resources describing their pain, and giving them a new reason for their suffering (i.e. "mechanism of the problem").

The prospect isn't focused on their suffering, and they don't particularly want to open old wounds.

Their heart is already set on a particular outcome.

If your mechanism gives them a "super power" to easily achieve that outcome, it creates a potent effect.

Here are two questions you can ask to assess whether you've leveraged this principle...

**Does your mechanism promise your market a real and tangible "super power"?**
