Mechanism Scorecard
In this form, you will ﬁnd a the beta version of our Mechanism Scorecard. This was created 
from the insights we came to during the Mechanism Mastermind.
As with the mastermind, we are deﬁning a mechanism very broadly for the purposes of 
this scorecard. We are referring to the entire problem-solution logical argument in the VSL.
Our goal is to create a criteria for evaluating VSL mechanisms, that is predictive of a 
mechanism's chance for success.
Our writers submit mechanisms prior to writing a word of copy, which must be approved.
After review from our chiefs and their peers, this scoring system will give our writers a 
clearer idea of what elements of their mechanisms need to improve, in order for it to go 
live.
We look forward to sharing the results and optimizations of this process with you in the 
coming year, so you can borrow the parts of it that ﬁt for your business.
I have organized many of the best "mechanism questions" we came up with into 12 
categories.
In each category, you will ﬁnd an explanation of what the category is. You will ﬁnd the 
questions we came up with from our mastermind, that best help us evaluate the 
mechanism.
Please rate each category on a scale of 1-5.
A "5" means you believe this element is CRUCIAL to a great mechanism, and should be 
weighted as highly as possible.
A "1" means this element is of very low importance to a mechanism.
The categories that get the highest scores will be weighted more highly in our scoring 
system.
If you have any feedback on the categories, questions I left out, or questions I shouldn't 
have included, please leave that at the end of this google form.

IMAGE STRENGTH SCORE
Humans think in pictures.
The stronger of an image that a mechanism creates in your prospect's brain, the
easier it is to make sense of. Vague, less tangible images are less effective.
Below are some questions you can use to assess whether your mechanism
evokes a strong image in your prospect's mind:
Does it evoke a strong image?
- I.e. “erection muscle”, “penis plaque”, “muscle confusion”, "stuck poop".
Is the name of your mechanism memorable?
- i.e. using alliteration can help make it catchy… “ﬁrming ﬂavonoids"
Exorcism: Does the solution remove something tangible and scary from your
prospect, that is responsible for their pain?
- i.e. clogged body parts create a strong image in the prospect's mind
Does it “personify” the problem or solution?
Stories strengthen images. For tens of thousands of years, humans passed down
information via stories instead of the written word.
Our minds have evolved to respond to and retain stories. Particularly stories about
humans interacting with each other.
For ages, our ancestors personiﬁed their environment to understand give it
meaning and understand it better. The personiﬁed trees, animals, planets, the stars,
etc. and told stories about it.
We can use the same principle to create stronger images in our copywriting
concepts. If we can "personify" aspects of the mechanism, that are otherwise dull
or dense, we make the mental movie playing in our prospect's head more vivid,
exciting, and real.
- i.e. Fat Cells Talk
- i.e. Your penis has a brain
- i.e. These molecules are like two twins… one good and one nefarious, and they're
at war.

SIMPLICTY SCORE
The simpler, and easier to understand a mechanism is, the better it is.
The best mechanisms instantly make sense. No long, drawn out explanations
necessary.
Anything that creates confusion is our enemy.
And anytime a mechanism includes too many logical connections...
Any time it requires too much "left brain" thinking...
We risk confusing or boring our prospect and sabotaging our mechanism's
eﬃcacy.
Below are some questions you can use to assess the simplicity of your
mechanism and VSL logic:
Now that you've educated your prospect about this mechanism, does it seem
obvious in hindsight?
Could a 5th grader understand the logical argument of your VSL?
Is your core argument simple and compelling enough, that you could explain it in
under 15 seconds in the lead?
Is your core argument instantly understandable based on what they already
know? Or does it require you to teach them lots of background information or
knowledge to comprehend?
Are there too many logical connections required to make sense of the core
argument?

PROOF SCORE
Authoritative, credible, and seemingly irrefutable proof elements are the icing on a
cake that make a good mechanism great.
You do not need to prove every piece of your logical argument... too much proof
can actually hurt credibility, and cause confusion in your argument.
However, proof of your CORE conclusion, in it's simplest form, is a valuable asset
in your sales letter.
Below are some questions to ask about the proof elements in your mechanism, to
evaluate it's effectiveness:
Are there any “demonstrative” proof elements that show the power of your
mechanism, or it’s core concepts?
- i.e. Desire System = “emotions are contagious… if you make yourself feel horny,
and do this one trick, you can make her horny for you”. Proof demonstration =
yawning - i.e. demonstrations on street of sending one instagram message to get
paid, and people’s reactions - i.e. elevator demonstration, which proved safety of
elevators
Is your your mechanism veriﬁed, discovered, or developed by a credible
institution?
- i.e. Harvard doctor quote Montezuma’s Secret, that veriﬁes that primary ingredient
works in the same way as Viagra
- i.e. Pandora's Box secretly developed by CIA - The more credible the institution the
better… but if it’s the perfect quote from a UCLA doctor (i.e. the UCLA science team
saying that a weak erection muscle is the #1 cause of erection problems), then it’s
still damn good.
Is there proof that your solution works in even the “worst case scenarios”, or
prospects least likely to see success. (put another way, how well does it handle
the objection of “yeah, but this won’t work for me, because I’m unique”)
- Erection Center - study showing that reactivating the erection center revived the
erections of old, crippled WWII vets
- Chase Triggers - veriﬁed on a test group of group including disabled men, very old
men, virgins. - Erection Muscle - it’s the secret behind a tribe where 97+ year old
men have the most legendary erections

VIRALITY SCORE
Elements of "virality" and "memes" include things like:
- timeliness
- emotional appeal
- humor
- simplicity
- easy to share
- visually compelling
- controversial
- relatability
Not surprisingly, those are some of the same things we look for in a good
mechanism.
Thus, the "virality" of a mechanism is a great ﬁlter to pass our mechanisms
through.
Below are some questions to help you assess the "virality" of your mechanism:
Is the concept something they could easily share at cocktail party, and make
them feel smarter for knowing?
How memorable is this mechanism? If you're a third party evaluating the sales
letter... will you remember the core argument tomorrow?
How conﬁdent are you your audience will take away one, impossible-to-forget
core concept, and be able to share it, with no reference material?
Does it tap into a cultural zeitgeist?
Is the information they learned new and timely?

EASE OF USE SCORE
Someone at the mastermind said (I'm paraphrasing from notes, and can't
remember exactly who said it):
“Great mechanisms are fool-proof and hands off, require no creativity or thinking,
are done for you”
Our prospects don't want to do any hard work. The mechanism has to be
extremely easy to use.
Below are some questions to help you assess the "ease of use" of your
mechanism:
Does your mechanism inherently eliminate the hard work normally needed to
solve their problem?
The key word is "inherently".
 Meaning the elimination of hard work is embedded in the core argument of the
mechanism.
"[Mechanism] allows you to achieve [primary beneﬁt] without any hard work
whatsoever!" is not enough. That's just copywriting 101.
- i.e. have MORE money and success by doing LESS work (4 hour work week)
- i.e. Get the girl by NOT hitting on her. Compel HER to hit on you (Pandora's Box)
Is it clear to your audience, that the diﬃculties they face with other popular
solutions, will not be faced with your mechanism?
- i.e. dieting requires willpower and restraint... If your mechanism takes advantage
of the gut bacteria speciﬁcally responsible for hunger, cravings, willpower... you
automatically eliminate the hard work associated with solutions.
How EASY and AUTOMATED is your solution?
Does your mechanism SPEED UP results, relative to other solutions?

DIFFERENTIATION SCORE
A unique mechanism should be unique. It must be clearly different than the
solutions that have failed your prospect before.
A great mechanism automatically destroys any solutions in the mind of the
prospect. It does so in a way that means you only need a minimal amount of
explicit destructions of other solutions.
It should be obvious why your solution is unique, and different from everything
else out there, based on it's inherent properties.
Here are some questions you can ask to assess if your mechanism is unique
enough:
Does your new twist on the problem, or your mechanism, explain why traditional
solutions have failed your prospect?
One of the best argument you can make is that the most common solution actually
makes the root cause of the problem worse.
- i.e. Viagra weakens your erection muscle in the long run, making at ineffective
after a period of time
- i.e. Viagra destroys the nerve endings leading to your erection center
- i.e. Dieting actually makes you fat, because it causes "metabolic damage" (the
new problem we introduced)
- i.e. dating programs that require pickup lines or approaching a girl actually make
you less successful with women, because she's not hard-wired to respond to a man
who chases after her... she's hard-wired to become obsessed with a man she
chases.
Do you explain the problem in a way that explains the prospect's past failures,
alleviates them of responsibility, and supports their self-esteem.
“The hardest sale to make is their own self-esteem. Make them proud of what they
see as their failures. Make their weaknesses the keys to eventual success.” - Rich
Schefren
“Crystallization - take all positive intent of bad behaviors and use it as a proof point
that there’s nothing wrong with them, they are on the right path, there’s just “One
small problem” or one thing they are going about the wrong way. “My one thing,
makes them Right” - Rich Schefren
- i.e. the reason you failed at all these internet marketing program is actually X...
which is a super power
How effectively can you reframe your prospect's problem, in a way that gives
them an "aha" about the real reason they were in pain.
I believe the whole "mechanism of the problem" thing is just a technique that helps
differentiate and destroy other existing solutions. It can be a great technique if
used correctly. The argument that this technique automatically creates is: "those
other solutions you tried didn't work, because they didn't address the REAL reason
for your suffering". This works best in pain-focused markets.
- i.e. the problem is not your lack of blood ﬂow... it's that your erection muscle is too
weak to push blood in your cock and keep the blood in there.
- i.e. It’s not your lack of blood ﬂow… Your penile arteries are clogged, so the blood
just can’t get in. Clear the obstruction and blood ﬂow won't be a problem.
How different does this solution feel, compared to solutions they've been sold
before?
How NEW is this information or breakthrough?
- a scientiﬁc breakthrough that was published last month is more powerful than
one published 7 years ago. It's easier to position it as something they've never
heard before.

EMBEDDED BENEFITS SCORE
It's one thing to have your mechanism lead to your product's primary beneﬁt. This
can lead to very good copy.
It's another level to have a beneﬁt already embedded in the logic of your
mechanism. This often leads to world-class copy.
Below are some question to help you assess how well you've embedded beneﬁts
into your mechanism.
Is the primary beneﬁt of your product embedded in the name of your
mechanism?
- i.e. "erection muscle"
- i.e. "ﬁrming ﬂavonoids"
- i.e. "fat ﬂushing ﬂavanols"
Does the mechanism's name include a beneﬁt that clearly leads to their desired
beneﬁt?
- i.e. in the men's dating advice market, most men know that they'd rather have a
woman chase them, than the other way around. It's less work, and takes all the
work and frustration out of the dating process. Thus, the mechanism "chase
triggers" is very effective.
Does your mechanism solve your prospect's primary problem by resolving an
annoying secondary problem?
- i.e Resurge - the pill helps you lose weight, by helping you sleep better.
Does your taking advantage of this mechanism allow your prospect to avoid
something perilous, that they already fear?
- this is the reverse logic of the above question. And example is Montezuma's
Secret. It argues that when you clear out the clogged arteries of your penis via
ﬁrming ﬂavonoids (i.e. address your primary problem with this breakthrough), you
reduce your risk of a heart attack by a factor of 50. Weakening erections are the
ﬁrst warning sign of a heart attack, because the arteries down there are so much
smaller. A renowned doctor called ED "Early Death", because it 50x'es your risk of
heart failure.
Is the delivery mechanism enjoyable?
- i.e. making a girl chase you for sex
- i.e. eating a gummy bear to get slim
- i.e. those blackhead removing videos
- i.e. 2 girls teach sex... getting good at sex, by watching two hot girls make love.
- i.e. Resurge - lose weight by sleeping

DOOMSDAY SCENARIO SCORE
There are two types of pain points we survey for, and look for in forums.
We look for the "in the moment" pain points, that the prospect deals with in their
day-to-day life.
We also look at what they fear their current problem could escalate to in the
future.
Humans are "all or nothing" thinkers.
Your prospects worst-case doomsday scenario, that's already ﬂoating around in
their mind, is very valuable information.
We can aggravate this doomsday scenario. We can magnify. We can make it even
more dire. We make the consequences of not buying our product ominous.
In some markets - like the doomer survival market - it appears that this fear of the
future is even more important, and should probably be weighted even more
highly.
Below are some question to help you assess how well your problem logic and
mechanism speaks to this worst-case "doomsday" scenario.
Have you made a terrifying argument, that your prospect's problem is even
scarier than they assumed?
- i.e. it's not just erectile dysfunction you have to worry about... ED is the ﬁrst sign
of a heart attack, and 50x'es your risk for one.
Does it amplify their existing fears of what their problem could escalate to?
- i.e. in the BPH market they're aware that the problem can escalate to kidney
failure. If we can show them that failure to address the real reason their in pain will
lead to kidney failure, it can be very effective.
Does your problem create an “future doom countdown” if action is not taken
promptly?
- bonus points if you can make it crystal clear that that the clock is ticking, if they
don't address this

CURRENT BELIEF COMPATIBILITY SCORE
Changing someone's core beliefs is tricky.
The closer the belief is to their identity, the tougher it is to change.
If they believe "the medical establishment can't be trusted", it's inﬂuenced by their
larger world view on human nature, and the consolidated power of larger
institutions.
It would be a waste of energy to attempt to change that belief and convince them
"this doctor, who is part of the medical establishment, can be trusted."
It would be more effective to convince them our doctor is a rebel who spits in the
face of the medical establishment, and has dedicated himself to ﬁnding the cures
that the medical establishment doesn't want you to know about, and making
them available to the common man.
Some loosely held beliefs can be changed..
A general rule of thumb is that it is easier to convince your prospect of a new
reality if they want that new reality to be true (see: Embedded Beneﬁts). Diet
prospects want to believe that dieting is ineffective, and that there's a way to lose
weight while eating junk food.
Hope is a powerful drug.
Many great mechanisms leverage the prospect's existing beliefs.
Below are some questions to help you assess how well your mechanism
leverages your prospect's existing beliefs:
Does the mechanism reaﬃrm their "known" problem (the thing that THEY
believe is blocking them from success / causing success)?
- i.e. you ARE late to bitcoin (but it's OK, because we're only in phase 2 out of 3 of
the parabolic move, and you’re EARLY to these other coins = Altucher letter)
- i.e. Your metabolism IS slow… but that’s only half the problem, it also needs to be
EFFICIENT
Has your mechanism been concealed or smeared by any groups of people your
prospect is not fond of?
- i.e. Prostate drug makers have concealed the true root cause of BPH to support
their massive empire... they're trying to give you the same drugs they give trannies,
to make them into women. All in the name of the almighty dollar!
Does your mechanism reinforce your prospect's worldview?
- i.e. "It's like God's blue pill"
- i.e. “These toxins trying to penetrate your brain barrier are like immigrants trying
to pass through our border, causing havoc in our homeland”

CENTRAL THESIS COHESION SCORE
Ryan calls it the "central thesis".
I call it your argument's conclusion.
Both, generally, mean the core argument that your prospect MUST buy into, in
order to be willing to consider buying your product.
It's the BIG presale that absolutely has to happen, for your mechanism to be
effective.
In markets that have both a "pain" and "hope" element, I like to make my
argument's primary conclusion binary. I write it in very "black and white" terms,
that speak directly to the prospect's dream scenario, and pain scenario.
The logic is usually something like:
If [core concept of the argument] works in X manner... then [primary beneﬁt],
regardless of [known "root causes" for their issue, that they think is responsible
for it]
If [core concept of the argument] works in the opposite manner, then [primary
pain point or fear]... even if you do [conventional solutions]
For example...
CONCLUSION: If your erection muscle is strong… getting hard is instant, easy, and
effortless, regardless of your age, blood ﬂow, etc.
If your erection muscle is weak, like the vast majority of men over 40, it’s 
practically impossible to get hard… even if you pump yourself full of viagra…
MECHANISM: newly discovered nutrients proven to strengthen your erection
muscle.
In my book, Ryan's Pandora's Box central thesis is the Gold Standard...
CENTRAL THESIS: If YOU hit on a woman ﬁrst, 90% of the time she thinks you're
desperate and she rejects you.
If SHE hits on you ﬁrst 90% of the time you can play it cool, lay back and let her
chase her way into your pants.
And until now, no one has ﬁgured out how to make a girl horny enough to make
the ﬁrst move.
MECHANISM: Pandora's Box... system to make any girl make the ﬁrst move on
you.
Below are some questions to assess the strength and cohesion of your central
thesis:
Can your thesis, in its simplest form, be described in four words or less?
-  i.e. Erection Muscle controls boners (Ryan can probably come up with a better 
example)
Is your mechanism the perfect pay-off to your well developed central thesis?
- it has to ﬁt perfectly. See examples above.
Does the mechanism resolve the feeling (hope, fear, pain, etc.) created by the 
central thesis?
Does your central thesis speak to both their ideal outcome, and their primary
pain point, in a black and white, "all or nothing" manner?

SUPER POWER SCORE
"Solution Focused" markets are driven by pleasure (in the moment) and
hope (their dreams being realized).
In these markets, it makes sense to focus more on a more beneﬁt driven
mechanism, and more beneﬁt driven copy in general.
It makes less sense to spend resources describing their pain, and giving them a
new reason for their suffering (i.e. "mechanism of the problem").
The prospect isn't focused on their suffering, and they don't particularly want to
open old wounds.
Their heart is already set on a particular outcome.
If your mechanism gives them a "super power" to easily achieve that outcome, it
creates a potent effect.
Here are two questions you can ask to assess whether you've leveraged this
principle...
Does your mechanism promise your market a real and tangible "super power"? 
- i.e. the ability to print money on demand, through picking the right cryptos or
options trading
- i.e. the ability to make any girl horny and chase you for sex
- i.e. the ability to get hard on command, like a teenager
Does this super power speak directly to the hopes, dreams, and desires you
identiﬁed in your avatar?

"CAN YA FEEL IT??" SCORE
Great mechanisms leverage logic. Yet they evoke a visceral emotional response.
They make your prospect feel something.
Here are some questions you can ask, to assess whether your mechanism brings
out the right emotional response in your prospects...
Does your mechanism feel emotionally compelling to people reading it for the
ﬁrst time?
When you show your mechanism to a friend or prospect, do their eyes light up?
Does the problem evoke some primal discomfort?
Does the solution evoke some primal relief?
Is your solution causing controversy in some circles?
- i.e. medical establishment, Big Pharma, has a vested interest in their
pharmacological solutions… and is outraged, trying to discredit this break through.
Does your mechanism leverage secret knowledge or insider knowledge?
