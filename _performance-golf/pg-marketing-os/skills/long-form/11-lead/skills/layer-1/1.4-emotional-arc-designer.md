**Version:** 1.0

**Dependencies:** lead_context (from 0.1), vault_lead_intelligence (from 0.2), teaching_index (from 0.3), lead_type_classification (from 1.1), hook_engineering (from 1.2), four_element_mapping (from 1.3)

---

## Purpose

Design the emotional progression from the prospect's state at lead open (attention delivered by the headline) to lead close (emotionally sold, wanting proof). Map specific emotional stages and the lead components that trigger each transition. The emotional arc is the invisible architecture that makes the lead feel inevitable — each sentence moves the prospect ONE emotional step closer to "This sounds amazing. Now prove it works for me."

---

## Persona Deployment

**Primary Persona:** The Legendary Copywriter — Master Copy Composite
Designs emotional progressions from decades of direct response intuition. Knows how to build from curiosity to desire without ever letting the reader plateau. Controls the emotional temperature sentence by sentence — knowing exactly when to increase intensity, when to dip for contrast, and when to peak.

**Secondary Persona:** Marcus Webster — Pattern Synthesis Analyst
Cross-references emotional arc designs against vault extraction patterns. Validates that the designed progression matches empirically successful arcs from TIER1 controls. Identifies where elite copywriters place peak moments and how they manage emotional pacing.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Define Starting and Ending Emotional States

```
STARTING STATE:
  The prospect arrives at the lead having just read the headline.
  Their emotional state is: ATTENTION + CURIOSITY
  - The headline delivered the initial attention capture
  - The hook (from 1.2) converts attention into active curiosity
  - The prospect is asking: "What is this? Tell me more."
  - They are NOT yet: hopeful, sold, trusting, or emotionally engaged
  - They ARE: curious, alert, conditionally interested

  starting_state = "attention/curiosity — headline delivered initial interest,
                    hook has created the pull to continue reading"

ENDING STATE (NON-NEGOTIABLE):
  Per E5 Framework, the lead MUST end with the prospect in this state:
  "This sounds amazing. This sounds perfect. Now prove it works for me."

  This means:
  - EMOTIONALLY SOLD on the concept (not logically convinced — that's the argument's job)
  - WANTING PROOF that it works specifically for them
  - DESIRE activated — they WANT this to be real
  - READY for the campaign argument to provide evidence
  - NOT YET: logically persuaded, ready to buy, or fully trusting

  ending_state = "emotionally sold, wanting proof — prospect believes this
                  COULD be amazing and needs evidence it works for them"

CRITICAL: Any arc that ends at "curious," "interested," or "intrigued" FAILS.
          The prospect must have moved past curiosity into emotional investment.
```

### Step 2: Select Arc Template Based on Lead Type

```
LOAD lead_type_classification.selected_type

SELECT emotional arc template:

STATISTICAL LEAD:
  curiosity → impressed → hope → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. CURIOSITY (hook): "What are these numbers?"
    2. IMPRESSED (data cascade): "Those results are remarkable"
    3. HOPE (mechanism tease): "If this method works like that, it could work for me"
    4. WANTING PROOF (close): "Prove it — show me this isn't cherry-picked"
  Typical arc_shape: ascending
  Peak moment: The largest/most impressive data point in the cascade

STORY VULNERABILITY LEAD:
  attention → identification → empathy → hope → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. ATTENTION (hook): "Who is this person and why are they sharing this?"
    2. IDENTIFICATION (vulnerability): "That's exactly how I've felt"
    3. EMPATHY (shared struggle): "This person understands my pain"
    4. HOPE (transformation hint): "If they found a way out, maybe I can too"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "Show me this works for people like me"
  Typical arc_shape: ascending_with_dip (dip at vulnerability, then build to hope)
  Peak moment: The identification moment when prospect sees themselves in the story

AUTHORITY-FLEX LEAD:
  attention → respect → trust → intrigue → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. ATTENTION (hook): "This person has serious credentials"
    2. RESPECT (credential stacking): "These credentials are genuinely impressive"
    3. TRUST (institutional weight): "If someone with THESE credentials says it, it matters"
    4. INTRIGUE (radical claim): "What have they discovered that contradicts everything?"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "With credentials like that, I need to see what they found"
  Typical arc_shape: slow_build_to_peak
  Peak moment: The radical claim that credentials make credible

COMPRESSED DISCOVERY LEAD:
  curiosity → surprise → fascination → desire → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. CURIOSITY (hook): "What did this doctor/scientist discover?"
    2. SURPRISE (discovery reveal): "That's unexpected — I didn't know that was possible"
    3. FASCINATION (mechanism naming): "There's a name for this — it's a real thing"
    4. DESIRE (promise implication): "If this mechanism does what it suggests, I want it"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "Prove this mechanism actually works in real people"
  Typical arc_shape: spike_then_build (discovery spike, settle, build to desire)
  Peak moment: The moment the mechanism is named — making the abstract concrete

CONSPIRACY EXPOSE LEAD:
  concern → alarm → anger → "what can I do?" → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. CONCERN (hook): "Something is being hidden from me?"
    2. ALARM (evidence reveal): "This is worse than I thought"
    3. ANGER (villain identification): "Someone is deliberately causing this"
    4. "WHAT CAN I DO?" (escape/solution hint): "There must be a way to protect myself"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "Show me this actually works to protect me"
  Typical arc_shape: ascending (steady escalation of emotional intensity)
  Peak moment: Villain identification — when anger crystallizes around a specific enemy

PROPHECY WARNING LEAD:
  awareness → concern → urgency → resolution-seeking → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. AWARENESS (hook): "Something important is changing"
    2. CONCERN (data reveal): "The data shows this is real and coming"
    3. URGENCY (timeline pressure): "This is happening soon — I need to act"
    4. RESOLUTION-SEEKING (mechanism tease): "There's a specific way to prepare/protect"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "Prove this preparation method actually works"
  Typical arc_shape: ascending (urgency builds continuously)
  Peak moment: Timeline reveal — when the abstract threat becomes concrete and dated

DOCTOR DISCOVERY LEAD:
  curiosity → surprise → trust → excitement → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. CURIOSITY (hook): "What did this doctor discover?"
    2. SURPRISE (shocking finding): "Even the doctor was surprised by this"
    3. TRUST (medical authority): "A medical professional validates this"
    4. EXCITEMENT (mechanism + promise): "This could solve my problem through a proven pathway"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "Show me the clinical evidence for real patients"
  Typical arc_shape: spike_then_build (doctor shock spike, then steady build)
  Peak moment: The shocking finding that even surprised the medical professional

PROBLEM-SOLUTION LEAD:
  pain recognition → "that's me" → hope → excitement → wanting proof
  Stages:
    1. PAIN RECOGNITION (hook): "Yes, that IS my problem"
    2. "THAT'S ME" (problem callout deepening): "They understand exactly what I'm going through"
    3. HOPE (promise introduction): "There might be a solution I haven't tried"
    4. EXCITEMENT (mechanism + elevator pitch): "This sounds genuinely different and possible"
    5. WANTING PROOF (close): "Prove this works for someone in my situation"
  Typical arc_shape: ascending_with_dip (pain dip, then ascending through hope to excitement)
  Peak moment: The "that's me" moment when the prospect feels personally seen

HYBRID LEAD:
  [Combine stages from component types]
  Stages: Merge the strongest stages from each component type
  Arc shape: Determined by dominant component
  Peak moment: Determined by which component's peak is most powerful
```

### Step 3: Customize Arc to Campaign Assets

```
TAKE the selected template and customize:

FOR each stage in the progression:
  IDENTIFY the lead component that triggers this emotional transition:
    - Which specific copy element moves the prospect from Stage N to Stage N+1?
    - Is it the hook? The problem callout? A credibility insertion? The mechanism name?
    - Map SPECIFIC upstream assets to each transition trigger

  EXAMPLE for problem-solution lead:
    Stage 1→2 transition: Triggered by problem callout (from root_cause.expression)
    Stage 2→3 transition: Triggered by promise introduction (from promise.primary_statement)
    Stage 3→4 transition: Triggered by mechanism naming (from mechanism.name + mechanism.uniqueness_claim)
    Stage 4→5 transition: Triggered by credibility insertion + attention lock

ADJUST arc based on four_element_mapping.emphasis_order:
  - Place the STRONGEST element at or near the peak moment
  - Place the WEAKEST element early (before prospect is fully engaged)
  - Ensure all 4 elements appear within the arc progression

VERIFY:
  - Every transition has a specific trigger (not just "and then they feel...")
  - The arc has minimum 4 stages (starting + at least 2 middle + ending)
  - No stage is a repeat of a previous stage (always forward motion)
  - The ending state is ALWAYS "emotionally sold, wanting proof"
```

### Step 4: Determine Arc Shape

```
SELECT arc_shape from:

  ASCENDING:
    Pattern: Steady emotional build from start to peak
    Visual: /
    Best for: Conspiracy, prophecy, statistical leads
    When to use: When each piece of information intensifies the previous
    Risk: Can feel monotonous if stages don't provide enough contrast

  ASCENDING_WITH_DIP:
    Pattern: Build → deliberate emotional dip → overcome → peak
    Visual: / \ /↑
    Best for: Story vulnerability, problem-solution leads
    When to use: When a vulnerability/pain moment creates contrast before hope
    The dip: Usually the deepest pain moment or the "I thought all hope was lost" beat
    Risk: Dip must not lose the reader — it must deepen engagement, not create despair

  SPIKE_THEN_BUILD:
    Pattern: Hook creates immediate emotional spike → settle → gradual build to peak
    Visual: ↑ . /
    Best for: Compressed discovery, doctor discovery leads
    When to use: When the hook is a shock/surprise that needs processing time
    The spike: Usually the shocking stat, doctor reaction, or discovery moment
    Risk: If the build after the spike is too slow, reader plateaus

  SLOW_BUILD_TO_PEAK:
    Pattern: Gradual, controlled escalation with the peak at the very end
    Visual: ........./↑
    Best for: Authority-flex leads
    When to use: When credibility must be established before the emotional peak
    The build: Each credential/evidence adds a small emotional increment
    Risk: Too slow and the reader drops off before reaching the peak

VALIDATE arc_shape matches lead type template:
  IF arc_shape conflicts with lead type's natural rhythm:
    FLAG for review — ensure intentional choice, not mismatch
```

### Step 5: Identify Peak Moment

```
DETERMINE the single peak emotional moment in the lead:

  The peak moment is the sentence or passage where the prospect reaches
  MAXIMUM emotional engagement BEFORE the close.

  Peak moment characteristics:
    - Highest emotional intensity in the entire lead
    - Usually occurs at 60-80% through the lead (not at the beginning or very end)
    - Creates the "THIS is what I've been looking for" feeling
    - Directly connects to the strongest E5 element
    - Often contains or immediately follows the mechanism naming

  IDENTIFY:
    peak_text_description: What is happening at the peak moment?
    peak_emotional_state: What does the prospect feel at maximum intensity?
    peak_trigger: What specific lead component causes the peak?
    peak_position: Where in the lead does the peak occur? (approximate percentage)

  VALIDATE:
    - Peak is NOT at the very start (that's the hook, not the peak)
    - Peak is NOT at the very end (the close is resolution, not peak intensity)
    - Peak connects to the elevator pitch's strongest element
    - Peak is achievable within 350-800 words
```

### Step 6: Map Transitions and Compile Arc

```
FOR each stage transition in the arc:
  DOCUMENT:
    from_state: The emotional state before transition
    to_state: The emotional state after transition
    trigger: The specific lead component causing the transition
    copy_element: The type of copy (hook, problem callout, credibility, mechanism name, etc.)
    upstream_asset: The specific data from upstream packages used
    intensity_change: increase / decrease / maintain
    approximate_word_count: How many words this transition occupies

BUILD complete arc object:
  - All stages documented
  - All transitions mapped to triggers
  - Peak moment identified and placed
  - Arc shape validated against lead type
  - Starting state and ending state confirmed

CROSS-REFERENCE with vault emotional progressions:
  COMPARE designed arc against vault_lead_intelligence.emotional_progressions
  NOTE: similarities to proven arcs (validation)
  NOTE: intentional differences (innovation)
  FLAG: Any departure from proven patterns that may be risky
```

## Quality Gates

- [ ] Starting state is "attention/curiosity" (headline-delivered, not pre-headline)
- [ ] Ending state is "emotionally sold, wanting proof" (NOT "curious" or "interested" or "intrigued")
- [ ] Minimum 4 progression stages documented (starting + 2 middle + ending minimum)
- [ ] Every stage has a specific trigger (no vague "and then they feel..." transitions)
- [ ] Every trigger maps to a concrete lead component and upstream asset
- [ ] Peak moment identified and placed at 60-80% through the lead
- [ ] Peak moment connects to the strongest E5 element from four_element_mapping
- [ ] Arc shape matches the classified lead type's natural rhythm
- [ ] No stage repeats a previous emotional state (always forward motion)
- [ ] Emotional intensity increases across the arc (with exception of intentional dips)
- [ ] Vault emotional progressions studied and compared
- [ ] If ascending_with_dip shape: dip location identified and justified
- [ ] Prospect thought documented at each stage (internal monologue clarity)
- [ ] All stages achievable within 350-800 word lead length

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| **Plateau Arc** | Prospect reaches "interested" at stage 2 and stays "interested" through stage 5 | No forward emotional motion; reader feels stuck and disengages; violates the principle that every sentence must move the prospect forward |
| **Curiosity Ending** | Arc ends at "curious to learn more" instead of "emotionally sold, wanting proof" | The lead's job is the EMOTIONAL sale; ending at curiosity means the lead failed its core purpose per E5 |
| **Emotional Whiplash** | Arc jumps from "pain" to "excitement" to "fear" to "hope" with no logical progression | Random emotional shifts feel manipulative; the prospect needs a COHERENT emotional journey |
| **Peak at Hook** | The hook is the most emotionally intense moment and everything after is lower energy | Hook captures attention; the peak should be the elevator pitch climax; premature peaking deflates the lead |
| **Missing Dip** | Story vulnerability lead has no pain/vulnerability moment — goes straight to hope | The dip creates CONTRAST that makes hope feel earned; without it, hope feels ungrounded and shallow |
| **Proof Insertion** | An emotional stage tries to "prove" something with data or evidence | The lead makes the EMOTIONAL sale; proof belongs in the campaign argument; inserting proof breaks the emotional arc |
| **Generic Stages** | Stages named "engaged" and "more engaged" and "very engaged" | Emotional states must be SPECIFIC: "shame," "identification," "hope," "desire" — not intensity levels of the same feeling |
| **No Trigger Mapping** | Stages listed without identifying what CAUSES the transition | An arc without triggers is a wish list; triggers connect the arc to actual copy components that will be built in Layer 2 |
