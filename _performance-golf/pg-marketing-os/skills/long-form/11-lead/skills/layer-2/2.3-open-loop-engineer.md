# Layer 2.3 — Open Loop Engineer

## Purpose

Design and place open loops throughout the lead — teases at compelling information that will be delivered later in the campaign. Open loops exploit the brain's hardwired need to "close" incomplete information, sustaining attention through the entire campaign argument. Per Schwartz: forward signaling bluntly tells the reader that what you are about to say is important. Per Georgi: open loops reference the tangible tease (trick, secret, 5 fruits, etc.) and create planned ambiguity — deliberately leaving the reader unsure of a vital element. The lead must contain a minimum of 2 open loops, each with an identified closure point downstream in the campaign.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Identify Open Loop Opportunities

Scan the lead content (problem callout + elevator pitch) and the upstream campaign structure for elements that can be TEASED now and CLOSED later.

**Open loop opportunity sources:**

| Source | What Can Be Teased | Where It Gets Closed |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Mechanism name | The tangible thing (ritual, trick, switch, secret) | Mechanism explanation CPB chunk |
| Root cause | The surprising real cause | Root cause reveal section of argument |
| Discovery story | What the doctor/researcher found | Discovery story section of argument |
| Counter-intuitive info | Why conventional approaches fail | "Why nothing else works" CPB chunk |
| Results | Specific dramatic outcomes | Testimonial/proof CPB chunks |
| Mechanism process | What the mechanism DOES (not how) | Mechanism explanation CPB chunk |

**Minimum requirement:** 2 open loops. Maximum recommended: 4-5 (beyond that, the lead becomes a string of teases that frustrate rather than compel).

### Step 2: Design Each Open Loop

For each open loop, construct the complete loop specification:

**Loop Component 1: The Tease Text**

The actual language that creates the open loop. Must be:
- Specific enough to create curiosity (not "something amazing")
- Vague enough to NOT close the loop (not "this enzyme called lipase")
- Tangible when possible (name a number, a thing, a type — "5 fruits", "a 5-second ritual", "a tiny molecule")
- Emotionally charged (connects to desire, fear, or curiosity)

**Loop Component 2: The Loop Type**

Classify using the five types from source teachings:

```
Type 1 — Tangible Tease:
  References a specific, concrete element without revealing what it is.
  Pattern: "a simple 5-second breakfast ritual..." / "5 delicious 'firming' fruits..."
  / "a tiny molecule in your body..." / "one weird trick..."
  Power: Creates a mental "placeholder" — the brain NEEDS to know what the thing is.

Type 2 — Discovery Tease:
  Hints at a revelation or finding without revealing what was discovered.
  Pattern: "what the doctor explained next changed everything..."
  / "what he found shocked the entire medical community..."
  / "the discovery that [prestigious institution] tried to suppress..."
  Power: The brain craves the "punchline" of the discovery.

Type 3 — Result Tease:
  Shows a dramatic result without explaining how it was achieved.
  Pattern: "within weeks, decades of fat buildup virtually erased..."
  / "his blood pressure dropped 30 points in 14 days..."
  / "she went from size 16 to size 6..."
  Power: The brain asks "HOW?" — and must keep reading to find out.

Type 4 — Counter-Intuitive Tease:
  States something surprising that violates expectations.
  Pattern: "he told them to abandon their diets and eat as many carbs as they want..."
  / "she threw out her dangerous painkillers and swore off injections..."
  / "the answer had nothing to do with exercise, diet, or willpower..."
  Power: Cognitive dissonance — the brain needs resolution.

Type 5 — Mechanism Tease:
  References what the mechanism does without explaining the process.
  Pattern: "this 'fat release switch' takes advantage of a biological loophole..."
  / "activates a little-known process in your body..."
  / "triggers the automatic burning of belly fat by the natural release of..."
  Power: Creates a "knowledge gap" — the brain senses important info is missing.
```

**Loop Component 3: Placement**

Where in the lead does this open loop appear?

| Placement Zone | Best Loop Types | Effect |
|----------------|----------------|--------|
| Within problem callout | Result tease, counter-intuitive tease | "Your problems have a solution — keep reading" |
| Opening of elevator pitch | Tangible tease, mechanism tease | "Here's the exciting thing — but I'll explain more in a moment" |
| Middle of elevator pitch | Discovery tease, counter-intuitive tease | "Something surprising is coming — stay with me" |
| After elevator pitch / before transition | Tangible tease, result tease | "The best part is coming — pay close attention" |
| Embedded in forward signaling | Any type | "In the next 3 minutes, you'll see [tease]..." |

**Loop Component 4: Closure Location**

Where in the campaign does this loop get CLOSED? Every open loop MUST have a specific downstream closure point.

```yaml
closure_locations:
  mechanism_explanation: "CPB chunk that explains HOW the mechanism works"
  root_cause_reveal: "CPB chunk that reveals the root cause"
  discovery_story: "The discovery story section in the body copy"
  proof_section: "Proof/testimonial CPB chunks"
  counter_intuitive_section: "'Why nothing else works' CPB chunk"
  tangible_reveal: "The moment the 'trick/secret/ritual' is named or described"
```

**Loop Component 5: Pull Strength Assessment**

How strong is this loop's pull — how desperately does the brain need to close it?

| Pull Strength | Score | Description |
|---------------|-------|-------------|
| Weak | 1-3 | Mildly curious but could walk away. "I wonder what that is." |
| Moderate | 4-6 | Interested and would like to know. "I want to find out." |
| Strong | 7-8 | Compelled to continue. "I NEED to know what this is." |
| Irresistible | 9-10 | Cannot stop reading. "I will not leave until I find out." |

**Minimum pull strength per loop:** 7/10

### Step 3: Apply Forward Signaling Techniques

Forward signaling explicitly TELLS the reader that important information is coming. It works alongside open loops to sustain attention.

**Forward signaling patterns:**

```
Direct urgency:
  "Pay close attention to what I'm about to share with you..."
  "In the next 3 minutes, you'll see..."
  "What I'm about to tell you next could change everything..."

Importance flagging:
  "This is vital..."
  "This is the most important thing you'll read today..."
  "What you're about to discover..."

Conditional promise:
  "If you follow what I'm about to show you..."
  "Once you see what I'm about to reveal..."
  "After you hear this, you'll never look at [topic] the same way..."
```

**Placement rule:** At least ONE forward signal must appear at the transition between the lead's elevator pitch and the campaign intro/argument. This is the "attention lock" zone.

### Step 4: Validate Loop Integrity

For each open loop, verify:

**The Premature Closure Test:**
> Does this loop accidentally give away the answer? Read the tease in isolation — does the reader have enough information to GUESS the answer without reading further?

- If YES (reader can guess) — the loop is too transparent. Add more ambiguity.
- If NO (reader cannot guess) — the loop is properly open.

**The Frustration Test:**
> Does this loop frustrate the reader instead of compelling them? Is it so vague it feels manipulative rather than intriguing?

- If frustrated — add more tangible specifics (a number, a type, a hint)
- If intrigued — the loop is properly calibrated

**The Relevance Test:**
> Does this loop connect to something the prospect actually WANTS to know? Is the eventual answer worth the wait?

- If the answer would be disappointing — the loop fails. Choose a different element to tease.
- If the answer would deliver an "aha" moment — the loop passes.

### Step 5: Score the Open Loop Array

| Dimension | Weight | Scale | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------|-------------|
| Minimum count met | 0.20 | 1-10 | Are at least 2 open loops present? |
| Pull strength average | 0.25 | 1-10 | Average pull strength across all loops |
| Type diversity | 0.15 | 1-10 | Are different loop types used (not all the same)? |
| Closure locations identified | 0.20 | 1-10 | Does every loop have a specific closure point? |
| Forward signaling present | 0.10 | 1-10 | At least one forward signal used? |
| Integrity (no premature closure) | 0.10 | 1-10 | No loops accidentally close themselves? |

**Minimum threshold:** Weighted average >= 7.0/10

## Anti-Patterns

- **The Clickbait Loop:** "What happens next will SHOCK you..." — generic, manipulative, and content-free. No tangible tease, no specific curiosity. Fix: "What the doctor explained next about this tiny molecule changed everything..." (specific, tangible, connected to content).
- **The Premature Closer:** "This 5-second breakfast ritual involves mixing these specific enzymes into your morning smoothie..." — the loop just closed itself by explaining what the ritual IS. Fix: "A simple 5-second breakfast ritual that triggers your body's natural fat-burning switch..." (teases the ritual without revealing it).
- **The Orphan Loop:** Opening a loop that never gets closed anywhere in the campaign. The prospect reads the entire piece waiting for the answer and never gets it. EVERY loop must have a closure location. If the campaign cannot close the loop, do not open it.
- **The Feature Tease:** "Wait until you see the 7 modules inside..." — this teases features, not benefits or mechanism. Features do not create compelling open loops. Fix: "Wait until you see how [mechanism] starts working in the first [timeframe]..."
- **The Loop Flood:** Placing 6-7 open loops in a 400-word lead — the reader feels jerked around rather than compelled. Maximum 4-5 loops. The lead should FLOW, not stutter from tease to tease.
- **The Irrelevant Tease:** "And there's an incredible bonus I'll tell you about later..." — the prospect does not care about bonuses during the lead. They care about their PROBLEM and the MECHANISM. Tease what matters at this stage.
