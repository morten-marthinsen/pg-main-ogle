# Layer 3.4 — Carlton Compliance Checker

**Version:** 1.0
**Layer:** 3 (Story Refinement)
**Type:** Compliance Validation (Final Refinement Pass)
**Dependencies:** Layer 2 outputs (all story sections), 3.1-sensory-detail-calibrator output, 3.2-suspense-pacing-optimizer output, 3.3-proof-integration-validator output, teaching_index (Carlton Storytelling Principles — all 9)
**Output:** carlton_compliance_report.json

---

## Purpose

Apply all nine John Carlton storytelling principles as the final refinement pass before the story moves to Layer 4 validation. This is the comprehensive quality gate that ensures the story meets the master-level standard Carlton established: concise, breathless, empathetic, conversational, reader-centric, and emotionally resonant.

Carlton's key insight: "Most people are NOT natural storytellers. They ramble, repeat themselves, butcher plots, and bombard the listener with irrelevant bullshit." This checker prevents every one of those failures. It is the last defense against a story that is technically sound (beats mapped, proof integrated, pacing optimized) but CRAFTSMANSHIP-deficient — a story that passes structural tests but fails the human test.

The Carlton Compliance Checker does not generate new content. It evaluates the refined story text against nine non-negotiable principles and produces a per-principle compliance report with specific violation locations, quality scores, and remediation directives.

## The Carlton Standard

John Carlton's storytelling principles are not suggestions. They are the result of decades of writing copy that produced millions in revenue. Each principle exists because its violation reliably kills story effectiveness.

Carlton did not theorize about storytelling. He tested it — in direct mail, in VSLs, in campaigns where the story either worked or it did not. These nine principles are extracted from what WORKED.

**The Internal Editor Test:** Carlton described developing an "inner editor" — a stern internal voice that says "Come on" and "doesn't let anything get by." This compliance checker IS that inner editor, applied systematically to every principle.

**The 17-Version Standard:** Carlton wrote 17 versions of a single letter before it was right. The first 16 versions had craft deficiencies that this checker would have caught. The compliance checker accelerates the revision process by identifying exactly what must change, so you don't need 17 versions — but you DO need to meet the same standard.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Principle 1 — Three-Line Structure (Setup, Action, Moral)

```
EVALUATE macro story structure for Carlton's three-line framework:

PRINCIPLE: "Set up (the tease of the payoff to come), Plot elements /
           action (the fulfillment of the tease), Moral (the punch line
           of the story)."

CHECK 1A: SETUP PRESENT AND CLEAR
  SCAN story opening (first 15-20% of word count):
  - Does the setup TEASE a payoff?
  - Does the reader know WHY they should keep reading?
  - Is there an implicit promise of where this is going?
  EVIDENCE: Quote the setup's payoff tease.
  GOOD: "But that very night I received a phone call that would change
        everything." (clear tease — something changed)
  BAD: Beginning with context that doesn't promise anything.
  STATUS: present / absent / weak
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 1B: ACTION / PLOT FULFILLS THE TEASE
  SCAN story middle (20-80% of word count):
  - Does the action section DELIVER on what the setup promised?
  - Is the tease fulfilled — not just eventually, but recognizably?
  - Can the reader trace a line from "the phone call that would
    change everything" to the actual change?
  EVIDENCE: Quote the fulfillment of the setup's tease.
  STATUS: fulfilled / partially_fulfilled / unfulfilled
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 1C: MORAL / PUNCHLINE PRESENT
  SCAN story ending (last 15-20% of word count):
  - Is there a clear punchline, moral, or conclusion?
  - Does the ending LAND — create a feeling of completion?
  - Carlton's standard: reader should FEEL something at the end.
  NOTE: "Moral" doesn't mean moralistic. It means the POINT
        of the story — what it all means. The "so what."
  EVIDENCE: Quote the moral/punchline.
  GOOD: "Finally, it all made sense."
  BAD: Story trails off without a clear point.
  STATUS: present / absent / weak
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_1_SCORE: average of 1A, 1B, 1C scores
PRINCIPLE_1_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
LOG: all evidence, all scores, all violations
```

### Step 2: Principle 2 — Transporting

```
EVALUATE whether the reader feels IN the world the story creates:

PRINCIPLE: "The concept of 'transporting' is critical. You're driving
           the story, and it's your responsibility to keep it on the
           road. Your reader will abandon you at the first hint you
           don't know where we're going."

CHECK 2A: WORLD ESTABLISHMENT
  SCAN the first 100 words of the story:
  - Is a WORLD established? (not just facts — a world)
  - Can the reader SEE where they are?
  - Is there at least one sensory anchor in the opening?
  CROSS-REFERENCE: 3.1 sensory calibration — transporting_achieved
  STATUS: established / partially / not_established
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 2B: SUSTAINED IMMERSION
  SCAN the full story for immersion breaks:
  - Does the reader stay IN the world throughout?
  - Are there moments where the narrative "breaks character" —
    shifting from story mode to lecture mode, data mode, or
    sales mode?
  IMMERSION BREAK MARKERS:
    - Shift from first-person narrative to third-person explanation
    - Shift from conversational tone to academic tone
    - Shift from story progression to data dump
    - Addressing the reader directly in the middle of action
      (one exception: "I know what you're thinking" is acceptable)
    - Long parenthetical asides that break the narrative frame
  FOR each immersion break found:
    LOG: location, break_type, break_text, impact_severity
  STATUS: sustained / interrupted (count breaks)
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 2C: READER IDENTIFICATION
  EVALUATE whether the reader can SEE THEMSELVES in the story:
  - Is the protagonist relatable? (prospect-mirroring elements)
  - Are the protagonist's problems recognizable to the prospect?
  - Could the reader think "that could be me" at any point?
  CROSS-REFERENCE: Layer 1 character architect — prospect_mirror_elements
  STATUS: strong_identification / moderate / weak
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 2D: CONSISTENT DIRECTION
  Carlton: "Your reader will abandon you at the first hint you don't
           know where we're going."
  EVALUATE: Does the story maintain a clear trajectory?
  - Does the reader always sense that the story is GOING somewhere?
  - Are there tangents, digressions, or sections that feel aimless?
  - Does every beat advance toward the revelation/transformation?
  FOR each aimless section:
    LOG: location, section_text, reason_aimless
  STATUS: clear_direction / mostly_clear / loses_direction
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_2_SCORE: average of 2A, 2B, 2C, 2D scores
PRINCIPLE_2_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 3: Principle 3 — Breathless Prose

```
EVALUATE whether the story creates Carlton's "breathless prose":

PRINCIPLE: "Tell your story, aiming for the kind of breathless prose
           that makes your prospect afraid to exhale, for fear of
           missing a delicious detail."

CHECK 3A: OXYGEN DEBT
  EVALUATE the overall reading experience:
  - Does the reader feel they CANNOT stop reading?
  - Is there a sense that stopping would mean missing something?
  - Is there always something ABOUT TO happen?
  CROSS-REFERENCE: 3.2 suspense pacing — breathless_prose_achieved
  TEST: At any point in the story, if the reader stopped, would they
        feel a pull to resume? Or would they feel "that was enough"?
  FLAG any "exit-comfortable" points — places where the reader
       could stop without feeling they're missing something.
  STATUS: breathless / mostly_breathless / has_exit_points
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 3B: DELICIOUS DETAILS
  Carlton specifically says "delicious detail" — not just any detail.
  EVALUATE: Are the details in the story INTERESTING?
  - Do sensory details create curiosity or engagement?
  - Or are they mundane descriptions that add nothing?
  GOOD DELICIOUS DETAIL:
    "He returned with a notepad and began to scribble down what
     looked like a recipe complete with a list of strange plants
     and herbs, most of which I've never ever heard of."
    → This detail is delicious because it creates CURIOSITY.
  BAD NON-DELICIOUS DETAIL:
    "He walked into the room wearing a blue shirt."
    → This detail is inert. It creates nothing.
  FOR each sensory detail: classify as delicious / inert
  CALCULATE: delicious_detail_ratio (should be >= 0.75)
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 3C: FORWARD MOMENTUM
  EVALUATE whether every sentence pulls toward the next:
  - Does each sentence create a reason to read the next sentence?
  - Or are there sentences that are "complete" — they finish a thought
    without opening a new one?
  CROSS-REFERENCE: 3.2 suspense pacing — reading gravity assessment
  COUNT: momentum_sentences vs. terminal_sentences
  IDEAL: >= 80% momentum sentences
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_3_SCORE: average of 3A, 3B, 3C scores
PRINCIPLE_3_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 4: Principle 4 — Excess Trimmed

```
EVALUATE whether everything unnecessary has been deleted:

PRINCIPLE: "Then, tidy it up by deleting everything that doesn't
           need to be there."

CHECK 4A: WORD-LEVEL EXCESS
  SCAN for words that can be deleted without losing meaning:
  - Unnecessary adverbs ("very", "really", "quite", "just",
    "actually", "basically", "literally" when not literal)
  - Filler words ("that" when unnecessary, "in order to" → "to",
    "the fact that" → DELETE)
  - Redundant modifiers ("free gift", "unexpected surprise",
    "completely unique")
  COUNT: excess_words_found
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 4B: SENTENCE-LEVEL EXCESS
  SCAN for sentences that can be deleted without losing narrative:
  - Sentences that restate what was already said
  - Sentences that explain what the reader already understands
  - Transition sentences that say "As I mentioned..." or
    "Going back to what I said earlier..."
  - Self-referential meta-commentary ("Now, this is the important
    part" — just MAKE it important through craft)
  COUNT: excess_sentences_found
  FOR each: LOG location, sentence_text, reason_for_excess
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 4C: SECTION-LEVEL EXCESS
  SCAN for sections or paragraphs that can be removed entirely:
  - Extended backstory that doesn't serve the main narrative
  - Tangential sub-stories that don't advance the discovery
  - Over-explained context that the reader doesn't need
  - Repeated proof elements (same point proven multiple ways
    when once is sufficient)
  COUNT: excess_sections_found
  FOR each: LOG location, section_summary, reason_for_excess,
           recommended_action (delete / compress / relocate)
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 4D: THE CONCISENESS TEST
  Carlton's standard: "Concise, memorable stories pack a punch."
  EVALUATE: Could this story be told in fewer words without
            losing its impact?
  CALCULATE:
    current_word_count: integer
    estimated_minimum_word_count: integer (if all excess trimmed)
    compression_ratio: float (estimated_minimum / current)
  IF compression_ratio < 0.80: Story has significant excess.
  IF compression_ratio > 0.95: Story is already lean.
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_4_SCORE: average of 4A, 4B, 4C, 4D scores
PRINCIPLE_4_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 5: Principle 5 — "And Here's What That Means for YOU"

```
EVALUATE whether the story connects to the reader's life:

PRINCIPLE: "Don't try any clever transitions back into your sales
           pitch. Instead, you merely say: 'And here's what that
           means for YOU...'"

CHECK 5A: SEGUE PRESENT
  SCAN the story's ending / transformation section:
  - Is there an explicit bridge from the story to the reader's life?
  - Does the story transition from "what happened to the protagonist"
    to "what this means for YOU, the reader"?
  ACCEPTABLE SEGUE FORMS:
    - "And here's what that means for you..."
    - "Now here's why this matters for YOUR [problem]..."
    - "And that's when I realized — this could help people
       just like you..."
    - "So what does this mean for someone dealing with [problem]?"
    - Any form that explicitly bridges story → reader
  UNACCEPTABLE:
    - Story ends without ever connecting to the reader
    - Story transitions to product pitch without reader connection
    - Clever/subtle transitions that the reader might miss
  STATUS: present / absent
  SEGUE_TEXT: string (quote the actual segue)
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 5B: SEGUE QUALITY
  IF segue is present:
  - Is it NATURAL? (feels like the natural end of the story)
  - Is it DIRECT? (not buried in a paragraph of other content)
  - Is it SPECIFIC? (connects to the reader's specific problem,
    not vague "this could help you too")
  GOOD: "And here's what that means for you — if you've been
        struggling with [specific problem], this is exactly why
        nothing else has worked."
  BAD: "Anyway, that's my story. Hope you found it helpful."
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 5C: CARLTON'S "COMPLETE THE THOUGHT" RULE
  Carlton: "When writing to adults, you can't assume anything.
           Adults are so numb to incoming data, they will suck up
           even a great story, absorb it, and move on... without
           coming to any conclusion whatsoever. As the copywriter,
           it's your job to complete the thought."
  EVALUATE: Does the story explicitly state its implication?
  - Does the reader know what CONCLUSION to draw?
  - Or could the reader finish the story and think "nice story"
    without connecting it to their own situation?
  STATUS: thought_completed / thought_incomplete
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_5_SCORE: average of 5A, 5B, 5C scores
PRINCIPLE_5_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 6: Principle 6 — About the Reader, Not the Writer

```
EVALUATE whether the story is about the READER, not the writer:

PRINCIPLE: "The story is about your reader, not you. Readers want
           to 'see' themselves in your story."

CHECK 6A: PROTAGONIST MIRROR
  EVALUATE whether the protagonist serves as a reader proxy:
  - Does the protagonist share the reader's problem?
  - Does the protagonist share the reader's frustration?
  - Does the protagonist share the reader's starting point?
  - Can the reader think "that sounds like me" or "that sounds
    like someone I know"?
  CROSS-REFERENCE: Layer 1 character_architect.prospect_mirror_elements
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 6B: SELF-INDULGENT CONTENT CHECK
  SCAN for content that serves the WRITER but not the READER:
  - Extended personal history irrelevant to the prospect's problem
  - Credential showcasing that goes beyond what builds trust
  - "Look how smart I am" passages
  - Inside references the prospect wouldn't understand
  - Technical jargon used to impress rather than inform
  - Stories about the writer's success that don't connect to the
    reader's potential success
  FOR each self-indulgent passage:
    LOG: location, passage_text, why_self_indulgent
  COUNT: self_indulgent_passages
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 6C: READER BENEFIT DENSITY
  COUNT how many sentences explicitly or implicitly reference
  the READER'S benefit, problem, or potential transformation.
  CALCULATE: reader_benefit_density = reader_benefit_sentences /
             total_sentences
  TARGET: >= 0.15 (at least 15% of sentences touch the reader's world)
  NOTE: In stories, this often happens through the protagonist mirror
        rather than direct address. Both count.
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_6_SCORE: average of 6A, 6B, 6C scores
PRINCIPLE_6_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 7: Principle 7 — Conversational

```
EVALUATE whether the story sounds spoken, not written:

PRINCIPLE: "Imagine talking face-to-face. Have a conversation with
           your reader. Stay in the reader's pocket."

CHECK 7A: SPOKEN QUALITY
  APPLY the read-aloud test to the full story:
  - Does the story sound natural when read aloud?
  - Could someone TELL this story verbally?
  - Or does it sound like something that was WRITTEN?
  MARKERS OF SPOKEN QUALITY:
    - Contractions used ("don't", "can't", "it's")
    - Sentence fragments ("No second portions, no desserts, no snacks.")
    - Conversational markers ("So...", "And here's the thing...",
      "Now...", "Look...")
    - Direct address ("you", "your")
    - Questions to the reader ("You know what I mean?")
    - Colloquial language appropriate to the protagonist
  MARKERS OF WRITTEN QUALITY (violations):
    - Formal connectors ("Furthermore", "Moreover", "Additionally")
    - No contractions ("do not" instead of "don't")
    - Academic vocabulary ("utilize", "facilitate", "paradigm")
    - Complex sentence structures that trip the tongue
    - Passive voice overuse
  COUNT: spoken_markers_found, written_markers_found
  RATIO: spoken_quality_ratio = spoken / (spoken + written)
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 7B: FACE-TO-FACE TEST
  Carlton's test: imagine sitting face-to-face with the prospect.
  - Would they lean in or back away?
  - Would they say "tell me more" or "OK, nice talking to you"?
  EVALUATE: At each section transition, would a listener still
            be engaged? Or would they be looking for an exit?
  FLAG any section where the face-to-face test fails.
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 7C: POCKET TEST
  Carlton: "Stay in the reader's pocket."
  EVALUATE: Does the story maintain intimacy throughout?
  - Is the tone consistent — one person talking to one person?
  - Or does it shift to "broadcast mode" (one person talking
    to an audience)?
  POCKET VIOLATIONS:
    - "Dear reader" or formal address
    - "As many of you know..." (broadcast)
    - Shifts to marketing language ("limited time offer",
      "exclusive opportunity")
    - Shifts to corporate voice
  COUNT: pocket_violations
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_7_SCORE: average of 7A, 7B, 7C scores
PRINCIPLE_7_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 8: Principle 8 — Empathy

```
EVALUATE whether the story demonstrates understanding of the
prospect's experience:

PRINCIPLE: "Put some empathy into this damn thing." (Gary Halbert
           to Carlton — Carlton's formative lesson in empathy.)
           "The story must demonstrate understanding of the prospect's
           experience."

CHECK 8A: PROBLEM RECOGNITION
  EVALUATE whether the story shows UNDERSTANDING of the prospect's pain:
  - Does the protagonist experience the SAME pain the prospect feels?
  - Is the pain described in a way the prospect would nod at?
  - Does the story avoid trivializing the problem?
  GOOD EMPATHY:
    "Given Grace's condition and state of mind, the timing
     couldn't have been worse." (reader with a sick loved one FEELS this)
    "I told her about my shitty life... I told her about how every
     girl I've ever known wanted nothing to do with me..." (reader
     with romantic frustration identifies immediately)
  BAD EMPATHY:
    "I had a minor health issue" (trivializes)
    "My wife needed to lose a few pounds" (dismissive)
  STATUS: strong_empathy / moderate_empathy / weak_or_absent
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 8B: VULNERABILITY PRESENT
  EVALUATE whether the protagonist shows VULNERABILITY:
  - Does the protagonist admit failure, fear, or weakness?
  - Is there a moment where the protagonist is NOT in control?
  - Does the story include a "cry for help" or "rock bottom" beat?
  Carlton learned from Halbert: empathy in diet copy requires a
  "special way of writing" that involves "more empathy than writers
  are used to doing." This manifests as protagonist vulnerability.
  GOOD VULNERABILITY:
    "I pleaded. 'If you know anything that can help her, please
     tell me.'" (the protagonist is desperate — the reader identifies)
  BAD: Protagonist who has all the answers from the start.
  STATUS: vulnerability_present / absent
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 8C: HIDDEN EMPATHY (not stated, demonstrated)
  Carlton's rule: "You can't just come out and say, I understand
                   how you feel. You can't do that. It has to be
                   kind of smooth and hidden and professional."
  EVALUATE: Is the empathy SHOWN, not told?
  - Does the story DEMONSTRATE understanding without saying
    "I understand how you feel"?
  - Is the empathy embedded in the protagonist's experience
    rather than stated as a claim?
  VIOLATION: Any instance of "I know how you feel" / "I understand
             your pain" / explicit empathy claims NOT grounded in
             demonstrated experience.
  STATUS: shown / told / mixed
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_8_SCORE: average of 8A, 8B, 8C scores
PRINCIPLE_8_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 9: Principle 9 — Reader Feels Something

```
EVALUATE whether the story triggers an emotional response:

PRINCIPLE: "In the end, your reader should feel something:
           Happiness, Alarm, Astonishment, or Greed."

CHECK 9A: PRIMARY EMOTION IDENTIFICATION
  IDENTIFY the primary emotion the story is designed to trigger:

  HAPPINESS: "Aww, the puppy got rescued."
    - Story creates warmth, relief, satisfaction
    - Reader feels good about the outcome
    - Typical in: transformation stories, underdog stories

  ALARM: "My God, I'm gonna keep a loaded gun by my bedside."
    - Story creates urgency, concern, protective instinct
    - Reader feels they must ACT
    - Typical in: warning stories, health danger stories

  ASTONISHMENT: "My neighbors are doing WHAT at night?"
    - Story creates surprise, shock, "I can't believe it" reaction
    - Reader's worldview is disrupted
    - Typical in: revelation stories, discovery stories

  GREED: "Hey! I want that kind of deal, too!"
    - Story creates desire, envy, "I want that for myself"
    - Reader sees results they want to replicate
    - Typical in: proof stories, transformation stories

  IDENTIFIED_EMOTION: enum[happiness, alarm, astonishment, greed, mixed]
  EMOTION_CLARITY: Is it clear WHICH emotion the story targets?
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 9B: EMOTION DELIVERY
  EVALUATE whether the emotion actually LANDS:
  - Does the reader FEEL the identified emotion?
  - Or is the emotion implied but not actually triggered?
  TEST: After reading the story, does the reader have a physiological
        response? (Even mild — a slight increase in heart rate,
        a smile, a frown, a widening of the eyes?)
  GOOD DELIVERY:
    "I felt like I wanted to cry, not because I was sad, but because
     for the first time in years I felt hope." → Reader feels hope
    "$11,300 in a single day. From one simple trade." → Reader
     feels greed/desire
  BAD DELIVERY:
    Story ends with information but no feeling. Reader thinks
    "interesting" but feels nothing.
  STATUS: emotion_delivered / partially_delivered / flat
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

CHECK 9C: PUNCHLINE IMPACT
  Carlton: the moral is the "punch line" of the story.
  EVALUATE the story's final beat:
  - Does the ending PUNCH? (create impact, land with force)
  - Or does it FADE? (trail off, lose energy, end weakly)
  GOOD PUNCHLINE:
    "Simply put, these foreign chemicals are entering your
     bloodstream and disrupting your brain... and guess what?
     The level of EDCs in Malaysia was one of the highest in the
     world — yet they had one of the LOWEST levels of overweight
     people." → Astonishment. Punchline lands.
  BAD ENDING:
    "So that's basically what happened. I hope this information
     has been helpful." → No punch. Story fades out.
  STATUS: punches / fades
  SCORE: integer[1-10]

PRINCIPLE_9_SCORE: average of 9A, 9B, 9C scores
PRINCIPLE_9_STATUS: pass if score >= 7, fail otherwise
```

### Step 10: Compile Carlton Compliance Report

```
ASSEMBLE the comprehensive compliance report:

PER-PRINCIPLE SUMMARY:
  FOR each principle (1-9):
    principle_name: string
    score: integer[1-10]
    status: enum[pass, fail]
    violations: [list of specific violations with locations]
    evidence: [quotes from story demonstrating compliance or failure]
    remediations: [specific fixes for each violation]

OVERALL COMPLIANCE:
  total_principles_passed: integer (out of 9)
  total_principles_failed: integer
  average_score: float (average of all 9 principle scores)
  weighted_average: float (weighted by principle importance)

  PRINCIPLE WEIGHTS:
    1. Three-line structure:        10% (structural foundation)
    2. Transporting:                15% (immersion is critical)
    3. Breathless prose:            15% (engagement is critical)
    4. Excess trimmed:              10% (conciseness supports all)
    5. "Means for YOU" segue:       10% (connection to reader)
    6. About reader, not writer:    10% (reader-centricity)
    7. Conversational:              10% (tone is non-negotiable)
    8. Empathy:                     10% (emotional identification)
    9. Reader feels something:      10% (emotional impact)

  COMPLIANCE STATUS:
```

## Anti-Patterns

**The Rambler:**
Carlton's primary warning: "Most people are NOT natural storytellers. They ramble, repeat themselves, butcher plots, and bombard the listener with irrelevant bullshit." A story that goes on tangents, repeats key points, or includes irrelevant backstory is rambling. The compliance checker catches this through Principle 4 (excess trimmed) and Principle 3 (breathless prose — ramblers do not create oxygen debt).

**The Clever Transitioner:**
Carlton explicitly warns: "Don't try any clever transitions back into your sales pitch." Writers who create elaborate segues from the story to the reader — metaphorical bridges, rhetorical flourishes, philosophical observations — are violating Principle 5. The fix is simple: "And here's what that means for YOU..." Direct. Unpretentious. Effective.

**The Self-Absorbed Storyteller:**
A story where the writer is showing off their craft, their vocabulary, their cleverness — rather than serving the reader. The reader does not care how good a writer you are. They care about their problem. The compliance checker catches this through Principle 6 (about the reader, not the writer).

**The Empathy Claimer:**
Writing "I understand how you feel" or "I know exactly what you're going through" without DEMONSTRATING that understanding through the protagonist's experience. Carlton learned from Halbert: empathy must be "smooth and hidden and professional" — SHOWN through the story, never claimed as a statement. The compliance checker catches this through Principle 8 (empathy shown, not told).

**The Feeble Punchline:**
Carlton warns: "He'll despise you for getting his hopes up for a good tale, if you then dash them with a feeble punch line." A story that builds tension and engagement but ends weakly — trailing off, summarizing, or failing to deliver an emotional impact — is the worst kind of craft failure. The reader invested their attention and got nothing in return. The compliance checker catches this through Principle 9 (reader feels something) and Principle 1 (moral present).

**The Technical Storyteller:**
A story that is structurally correct (beats mapped, proof present, sensory details included) but emotionally dead. The compliance checker exists precisely for this failure — a story can pass all structural tests from Layers 1 and 2 but fail the Carlton standard because it lacks the HUMAN element: empathy, vulnerability, conversational warmth, and emotional impact.
