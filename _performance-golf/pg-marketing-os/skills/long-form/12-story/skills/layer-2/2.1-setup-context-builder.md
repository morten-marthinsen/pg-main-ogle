# Layer 2.1 -- Setup & Context Builder

## Purpose

Build the story's opening section: the setup, the context, and the inciting incident that launches the narrative. This is Carlton's "Setup" -- the tease of the payoff to come. The setup must accomplish three things in the fewest possible words: (1) establish the protagonist's situation so the reader identifies with them, (2) create stakes that make the reader care about what happens next, and (3) introduce the inciting incident that propels the protagonist into the journey. Per Carlton: stories should aim for "breathless prose that makes your prospect afraid to exhale, for fear of missing a delicious detail" -- and that breathlessness must begin from the FIRST LINE. The setup does NOT explain the mechanism, does NOT deliver the discovery, and does NOT preview results. It creates the conditions under which all of those will emerge.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Classify the Setup Type from Story Classification

The setup structure varies dramatically based on the classified story type. Select the appropriate setup template:

**Setup Type Matrix:**

| Story Type | Setup Focus | Opening Move | Stakes Source |
|------------|------------|--------------|---------------|
| Standard Discovery | Protagonist's painful situation + inciting event that sends them on the Trip | Emotional state of protagonist facing a crisis | Personal/relational consequences of the problem |
| Accidental Discovery | Context of the accident/unexpected event | The unexpected event itself or its immediate aftermath | The urgency created by the accident/tragedy |
| Straight-to-Expert | How the protagonist connected with the expert/authority | Authority credentials or the desperation that led to seeking help | The protagonist's failed attempts before finding the expert |
| Proof Story / Live Experiment | Experiment framing -- who was invited, why, and the premise | "I invited X people to beta-test..." or "X months ago, we launched..." | The scale of the opportunity and the risk of missing it |
| Origin Story | The founder's personal crisis or motivation | The moment of frustration or revelation that sparked creation | Why nothing else existed to solve the problem |
| Transformation Story | The protagonist's "before" state in vivid detail | A scene from the worst moment of the problem | What the protagonist stands to lose if nothing changes |
| Warning Story | The threat or danger the protagonist becomes aware of | An alarming fact, statistic, or event | The consequences of inaction |
| Underdog / Vulnerability | The protagonist's shame, humiliation, or lowest point | A scene of vulnerability that creates empathy | The risk of permanent defeat or resignation |

### Step 2: Establish the Protagonist's Situation (Identification Layer)

The reader must see THEMSELVES in the protagonist within the first 3-5 sentences of the setup. This is the identification layer -- the moment the reader thinks "this person is like me" or "I know exactly how that feels."

**Identification techniques by protagonist role:**

```
Everyman Proxy (most common in health/relationships):
  - Show the protagonist experiencing the SAME symptoms/problems the prospect has
  - Use prospect language (from root_cause.what_they_think)
  - Make the protagonist's circumstances broadly relatable
  - Example: "Grace had tried everything... the diets, the exercise programs, the pills..."
  - Key: the protagonist's STRUGGLE must mirror the prospect's struggle

Authority Figure (common in finance/sports instruction):
  - Show the authority figure's expertise WITHOUT long credential dumps
  - Demonstrate credibility through ACTION, not claims
  - Make the authority relatable despite expertise ("even after 23 years...")
  - Example: "My name is Jack Crooks and for the last 23 years I've been hunting down..."
  - Key: authority is ESTABLISHED, not argued for

Reluctant Discoverer:
  - Show the protagonist who did NOT set out to find a solution
  - The discovery comes to THEM, not the other way around
  - Creates the "accident" feel that increases believability
  - Example: "I hated leaving her, but as it turned out, the men I would meet..."
  - Key: reluctance increases authenticity

Beta-Tester Group (proof stories):
  - Establish the group as "ordinary people just like you and me"
  - Show the variety within the group (age, background, experience)
  - Frame the test as a real experiment with real stakes
  - Example: "Most weren't rocket scientists or Harvard graduates..."
  - Key: ordinariness creates identification

Patient Zero / Protagonist Couple:
  - Show the intimate, personal stakes (marriage, health crisis, financial ruin)
  - Use sensory details from a specific, vivid scene
  - Make the couple's dynamic universally recognizable
  - Example: "There we sat, in the world renowned Dr. Wood's office..."
  - Key: personal intimacy creates emotional investment
```

**Identification requirements:**
- Protagonist's emotional starting state matches `emotional_arc.starting_state`
- At least ONE prospect mirror element from `protagonist.prospect_mirror_elements` is shown
- The protagonist's vulnerability is visible (but not over-explained)
- Reader thinks "this is about someone like ME" or "I've felt this way too"

### Step 3: Establish Stakes Without Over-Explaining

Stakes are what the protagonist stands to LOSE. Without stakes, the reader does not care what happens next. Stakes must be established quickly -- in 1-3 sentences -- and must be FELT, not merely stated.

**Stakes categories:**

| Category | What's at Risk | Emotional Weight | Example |
|----------|---------------|------------------|---------|
| Relational | Marriage, family connection, intimacy | High | "I thought she was going to leave me..." |
| Health | Physical wellbeing, life expectancy, mobility | High | "My doctor said if I didn't make a change..." |
| Financial | Savings, retirement, livelihood, debt | High | "I was breaking even -- $700 to acquire a customer that paid $700..." |
| Identity | Self-worth, masculinity/femininity, social status | Very High | "I didn't even feel like a man anymore..." |
| Opportunity | A rare chance that may not come again | Medium-High | "This sub-niche is so tiny... we can't risk spoiling the potential gains..." |
| Legacy | What they leave behind, their children's future | High | "I had a wife, I had three kids. I quit my job..." |

**Over-explanation test:**
The setup establishes THAT stakes exist, not WHY they exist. If the setup contains:
- Extended backstory explaining the history of the problem -- CUT IT
- Detailed medical/scientific explanation of the condition -- CUT IT
- Long description of failed attempts (more than 1 sentence) -- CUT IT
- Analysis of why the situation is bad -- CUT IT

**Carlton's trim test:** "Delete everything that doesn't need to be there." If removing a sentence doesn't change the reader's understanding of the stakes, the sentence should not be there.

### Step 4: Construct the Inciting Incident

The inciting incident is the moment that LAUNCHES the protagonist into the journey. Without it, there is no story -- just a situation. The inciting incident is the "Trip" in discovery stories, the "experiment launch" in proof stories.

**Inciting incident types by story format:**

```
Discovery Stories (Standard):
  Pattern: An external event forces the protagonist into a new situation
  where the discovery becomes possible.
  Examples:
    - "But that very night I received a phone call that would change
      everything... I'd been asked to lead an educational project in Malaysia."
    - "So that night, on a total whim, I spent almost every last dollar
      I had on a plane ticket..."
    - "I asked him if he knew anything... 'If you know anything that
      can help her, please tell me.'"
  Requirements:
    - Must feel UNPLANNED or RELUCTANT (not a strategic search)
    - Must create a physical or emotional displacement
    - Must open the door to an encounter the protagonist didn't expect

Discovery Stories (Accidental):
  Pattern: An accident, tragedy, or unexpected event directly leads to
  the discovery -- the discovery is a byproduct, not the goal.
  Examples:
    - "But out of the ashes of this tragedy, gave birth to a
      revolutionary breakthrough..."
    - "It was in that hot, dank-smelling hut in the middle of Africa
      where a small elderly man came into my life..."
  Requirements:
    - The accident must be genuinely unplanned
    - The discovery must seem like fate or divine intervention
    - The protagonist must NOT have been looking for the mechanism

Straight-to-Expert:
  Pattern: A connection (friend, colleague, referral) puts the
  protagonist directly in front of the expert who holds the answer.
  Examples:
    - "Fortunately enough, a celebrity friend of mine connected me to
      the sleep specialist and anti-aging physician to the stars..."
    - "And by the time I stumbled upon Dr. David's website, I was
      pretty desperate..."
  Requirements:
    - The connection must feel organic, not contrived
    - The expert's credibility must be briefly established
    - The protagonist's desperation justifies seeking extreme help

Proof Story / Live Experiment:
  Pattern: A deliberate decision to test a hypothesis with real people
  and real money/effort -- the experiment IS the inciting incident.
  Examples:
    - "Just 18 months ago, I invited 487 people to BETA-test a newly
      discovered sub-niche..."
    - "And after working like a couple of whipped mules for months,
      my son John and I rolled out our first pick on February 1, 2007."
  Requirements:
    - Must state the SCALE of the experiment (number of participants)
    - Must establish WHEN it began (specific date)
    - Must frame it as a real test with real stakes ("REAL people,
      with REAL money")
    - Must create the question: "So what happened?"
```

### Step 5: Apply Carlton's Breathless Prose Standard

Review the complete setup and apply Carlton's breathless prose standard. The reader must feel compelled to continue from the very first sentence.

**Breathless prose checklist:**

| Principle | Test | Fix if Failing |
|-----------|------|----------------|
| Afraid to exhale | Would the reader pause to check their phone mid-sentence? | Increase urgency, add a cliffhanger element |
| Delicious detail | Does each sentence add a vivid, specific detail that earns its place? | Replace generic descriptions with specific, sensory ones |
| Transported | Does the reader feel they are IN the scene, not observing it? | Add one sensory detail (sight, sound, smell, temperature) |
| Concise | Could any sentence be removed without losing the setup's power? | Delete it. Carlton: "trim the excess" |
| Conversational | Does it sound like someone TELLING a story, not WRITING one? | Read it aloud. If it sounds like an essay, rewrite it |
| Reader-centric | Does the reader see THEMSELVES in the story? | Strengthen the identification element |

**Breathless prose techniques:**
- Short sentences after long ones for rhythm variation
- Sentence fragments for emphasis ("Humiliating.")
- Ellipses for pacing and suspense ("And then... something happened.")
- Direct address to the reader when natural ("I know what you're thinking...")
- Present-tense feelings in past-tense narratives ("I still remember the feeling...")

### Step 6: Verify Setup Does NOT Over-Explain or Prematurely Reveal

The setup ESTABLISHES the conditions for the story. It does NOT:
- Explain the mechanism or how it works
- Reveal the discovery or the secret
- Preview the results or transformation
- Explain the root cause (that comes later in the story or argument)
- Tell the reader what the story will prove (let the story prove itself)
- Repeat what the lead already teased (expand, don't echo)

**Lead-Story Boundary Check:**
The lead TEASED the discovery story briefly (per Georgi). The setup must EXPAND on that tease without merely repeating it. Compare `lead_package.discovery_story_teased` with the setup text -- if they cover the same ground with the same language, the setup has failed to expand.

| Lead Tease (what the lead did) | Setup Expansion (what 2.1 must do) |
|-------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| "A rogue Ivy League doctor stumbled upon..." | Now we MEET the protagonist, feel their pain, and understand WHY they were in a position to stumble upon anything |
| "487 people beta-tested..." | Now we learn WHO these people were, HOW the experiment was set up, and WHAT was at stake |
| "A simple discovery in Malaysia..." | Now we're IN Malaysia, smelling the food, meeting Ashraf, feeling the protagonist's desperation |

### Step 7: Score the Setup Section

| Dimension | Weight | Scale | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------|-------------|
| Protagonist identification | 0.25 | 1-10 | Does the reader see themselves in the protagonist? |
| Stakes established | 0.20 | 1-10 | Are the stakes clear and emotionally felt? |
| Inciting incident power | 0.20 | 1-10 | Does the inciting incident create forward momentum? |
| Breathless prose quality | 0.20 | 1-10 | Is the prose concise, vivid, and compelling? |
| Over-explanation absence | 0.15 | 1-10 | Does the setup avoid explaining too much? |

**Minimum threshold:** Weighted average >= 7.0/10

## Anti-Patterns

- **The Backstory Dump:** "John was born in 1952 in a small town in Ohio. He grew up on a farm, went to college at Ohio State, married his high school sweetheart in 1975, and worked in insurance for 30 years before retiring in 2005. In 2006, he started noticing some changes in his health..." -- nobody cares about the biography. Start at the moment of CRISIS. Fix: "John stared at the test results, his hands shaking. The numbers didn't lie -- and neither did the look on his doctor's face."

- **The Mechanism Preview:** "What John didn't know yet -- but was about to discover -- was that a tiny molecule called leptin was controlling his entire metabolism, and that certain endocrine-disrupting chemicals were blocking it..." -- this reveals the mechanism IN the setup. The setup creates the conditions for discovery; it doesn't preview the discovery. Fix: Remove entirely. The mechanism revelation belongs in 2.3.

- **The Emotionless Experiment:** "We conducted a 12-month beta test with 487 participants in the FOREX currency sub-niche to evaluate potential returns." -- this reads like a research paper, not a story. Fix: "Just 18 months ago, I invited 487 people to BETA-test a newly discovered sub-niche... and on February 1, 2007, this small group of ordinary investors, following my exact instructions, had a chance to make more than $497 per minute."

- **The Lead Echo:** Setup repeats exactly what the lead already said. "As I mentioned earlier, a Harvard-trained doctor discovered a breakthrough..." -- this is not expansion, it's repetition. Fix: Move PAST the tease into new territory. Show the scene, introduce the characters, create the world the lead only hinted at.

- **The Contrived Inciting Incident:** "One day, John decided to research natural health remedies online and found a website about ancient herbs." -- there is no tension, no displacement, no sense of the unexpected. Fix: "But that very night I received a phone call that would change everything. Not that I knew it at the time..."

- **The Over-Explained Stakes:** "John was worried because high blood pressure is the leading cause of heart disease, which kills 647,000 Americans per year according to the CDC, and his family had a history of cardiovascular disease going back three generations..." -- this is a health report, not a story setup. Fix: "His doctor's words kept echoing: 'If you don't make a change, you won't see your grandkids graduate.'"

## Setup Templates by Story Type

### Template A: Standard Discovery Setup

```
[Protagonist emotional state -- 1-2 sentences showing the pain]
[The inciting incident -- external event that displaces protagonist]
[Brief stakes -- what's at risk if nothing changes]
[Forward momentum -- phrase that propels into the next section]
```

### Template B: Proof Story / Live Experiment Setup

```
[Experiment framing -- "X months ago, I invited Y people to test..."]
[Context of the opportunity -- what was being tested and why]
[First result tease -- the initial data point that hooks the reader]
[Scale and stakes -- the size of the potential and the exclusivity frame]
```

### Template C: Straight-to-Expert Setup

```
[Protagonist's desperation or failed attempts -- 1-2 sentences]
[The connection to the expert -- how they found Yoda]
[Brief expert credibility -- 1 sentence of credential]
[The expert's first surprising statement -- creates forward momentum]
```

### Template D: Vulnerability / Shame Setup

```
[A specific scene at the lowest point -- vivid, sensory, intimate]
[The emotional state -- shame, humiliation, despair]
[The stakes -- what is being lost or destroyed]
[The glimmer -- something that opens the door to possibility]
```
