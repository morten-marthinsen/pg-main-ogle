## PURPOSE

Writes the future pacing section and constructs the binary choice setup that hands off to Skill 14 (close). Future pacing creates TWO vivid futures — the positive future with the product and the negative future without it. The binary choice setup (Principle 8) collapses a complex purchase decision into a simple either/or: take action and transform, or do nothing and continue suffering.

11 of 12 TIER1 controls use a binary choice close. This microskill constructs the setup — the two vivid futures and the framing that positions the purchase decision as a binary fork. Skill 14 executes the final close language; this microskill delivers the emotional runway that makes the close feel inevitable.

---

## IDENTITY

**This microskill IS:**
- A future pacing writer that creates vivid positive and negative futures
- A binary choice constructor that frames the decision as two paths, no middle ground
- An emotional peak-delivery system for steps 12-13 of the emotional architecture
- The handoff builder that packages everything Skill 14 needs for the close

**This microskill is NOT:**
- A close writer (that is Skill 14 — this skill builds TO the close)
- A guarantee or scarcity writer (that is 3.2 — those sections precede this)
- A general future-pacing generator — these futures must be SPECIFIC to this product and mechanism
- A motivational speaker — the futures must be concrete, not aspirational platitudes

## PROCESS

### Step 1: Gather Future Pacing Inputs

1. Collect the specific elements that make futures VIVID:
   - Product name (for positive future)
   - Mechanism name (what powers the transformation)
   - Villain name (what causes the negative future)
   - Root cause name (what perpetuates the problem)
   - Guarantee branded name (for safety reference in positive future)
   - Scarcity claim (for urgency in negative future)
   - Niche-specific outcomes (what "success" looks like in this market)
   - Niche-specific suffering (what "failure" looks like in this market)

2. The futures must be SPECIFIC to the product and niche:
   - Generic: "Imagine a better life" → UNACCEPTABLE
   - Specific: "Imagine stepping on the scale 30 days from now and seeing a number you haven't seen since your twenties" → ACCEPTABLE
   - Specific: "Imagine stepping up to that 180-yard par 3, pulling out one club, and hitting a towering shot that lands softly on the green" → ACCEPTABLE

### Step 2: Write the Positive Future

3. Write the positive future — what life looks like WITH the product/mechanism activated:

**Structure:**
```
Time frame 1 (immediate — days 1-7):
  - First noticeable change (specific, sensory, believable)
  - Emotional reaction to first change

Time frame 2 (short-term — weeks 2-4):
  - Meaningful progress (tied to core problem resolution)
  - Social validation (others notice)

Time frame 3 (medium-term — months 1-3):
  - Full transformation (the promised outcome realized)
  - Life changes that flow FROM the transformation
  - Identity shift ("you're not just [better at X] — you're someone who [identity]")
```

4. Writing rules for positive future:
   - Use SENSORY language (see, feel, hear, touch)
   - Use SPECIFIC details (numbers, scenarios, people)
   - Use PRESENT TENSE ("you step on the scale" not "you will step on the scale")
   - Use SECOND PERSON ("you" — put the reader in the scene)
   - Include at least ONE social moment (someone else notices or reacts)
   - Include at least ONE emotional moment (how the reader FEELS)
   - Do NOT use generic aspirational language ("live your best life," "reach your potential")

5. TIER1 positive future examples:

**Resurge:**
"Imagine waking up tomorrow feeling genuinely refreshed for the first time in years. Stepping on the scale and seeing the number drop — not because of a crash diet, but because your body spent the night doing what it was designed to do. Within weeks, friends are asking what you've changed. The mirror starts showing you someone you remember — someone you missed."

**PSS:**
"Picture yourself stepping up to the ball on the first tee. No anxiety. No swing thoughts cluttering your mind. Just a smooth, natural motion — the club swinging like a pendulum — and the ball launching off the face with that sound you know means pure contact. Your playing partners look at each other. 'What happened to YOUR swing?'"

**Montezuma:**
"Day three. You feel something you haven't felt in years — that stirring. That confidence. By the end of the first week, the change is undeniable. And on that Saturday morning when your wife reaches for you and you're READY — that's the moment everything changes."

### Step 3: Write the Negative Future

6. Write the negative future — what continues WITHOUT action:

**Structure:**
```
Present state (what's happening RIGHT NOW):
  - The problem as it exists today (specific, painful)
  - What the reader is currently doing about it (nothing, or failing solutions)

Near future (weeks/months ahead):
  - The problem WORSENING (because the root cause isn't addressed)
  - The villain continuing to operate (mechanism not activated)
  - Specific consequences of inaction

Emotional weight:
  - How the reader FEELS knowing they had the chance and didn't take it
  - The regret of returning to the same problem tomorrow
```

7. Writing rules for negative future:
   - Tie to the ROOT CAUSE (not just symptoms)
   - Reference the VILLAIN (the thing causing the problem)
   - Use SPECIFIC consequences (not vague "things will get worse")
   - Do NOT be cruel or shaming — be HONEST about what inaction means
   - Frame as CONTINUATION of current state, not punishment
   - Include the "you KNOW" moment: "Deep down, you know what happens if nothing changes"

8. TIER1 negative future examples:

**Resurge:**
"Or... you can close this page and go back to the way things have been. The shallow sleep. The fatigue. Watching the number on the scale creep higher no matter what you try. Knowing that every night, your body is aging faster than it should — because the root cause was never addressed."

**357:**
"Or you can keep struggling with clubs that weren't designed for your swing. Keep topping fairway woods. Keep watching your playing partners outdrive you by 30 yards. Keep wondering why your scores never improve despite all the lessons and practice."

**BBW:**
"Or you can do nothing. Close this page. Go back to the same patterns, the same financial ceiling, the same feeling that opportunities are somehow passing you by. While your beta-dominant brain wave keeps your hippocampus suppressed — and the wealth-generation neural pathways stay dormant."

### Step 4: Construct the Binary Choice

9. Frame the decision as exactly TWO paths — no middle ground:

**Pattern: "You have two choices right now..."**

```
"Right now, you're standing at a fork in the road.

Choice A: [Take action — specific action, access the mechanism, begin transformation]
  - [Positive outcome 1]
  - [Positive outcome 2]
  - [Positive outcome 3]
  - Protected by [Guarantee Branded Name]

Choice B: [Do nothing — close this page, continue as-is]
  - [Negative consequence 1 — tied to villain/root cause]
  - [Negative consequence 2]
  - [The problem continues because nothing changes]

There is no third option. The [mechanism/villain] doesn't pause while you think about it. Every day you wait is another day [negative consequence]."
```

10. TIER1 binary choice patterns:

**Resurge:**
"You have two paths. Path one: Try Resurge tonight. Fall asleep deeper. Wake up lighter. Watch the weight melt away while your body regenerates. Risk nothing with our 60-day guarantee. Path two: Do nothing. Continue the shallow sleep cycle. Continue the frustration. Continue watching your health decline. The choice is yours — but Shallow Sleep Syndrome doesn't take nights off."

Or you can ignore everything you've just learned about your hippocampus, your theta waves, and the neural pathways that separate the wealthy from everyone else."

11. Binary choice requirements:
    - EXACTLY two options (never three, never "several")
    - Choice A includes: action verb + product name + guarantee reference
    - Choice B includes: inaction + villain/root cause continuation + emotional weight
    - No middle ground offered ("take some time to think about it" is NEVER an option)
    - The implication: delay IS Choice B (not a temporary pause)

### Step 5: Add Scarcity Reinforcement (If Present)

12. If scarcity was established in 3.2, reinforce it within the binary choice:
    - "But remember — [scarcity claim]. This opportunity may not be here tomorrow."
    - "The [quantity/pricing/window] I mentioned won't last."
    - Brief, not belabored — 1-2 sentences maximum

### Step 6: Add Guarantee Reinforcement

13. Reference the guarantee one final time in the context of Choice A:
    - "And remember — you're protected by the [Guarantee Branded Name]."
    - "You risk nothing. [Guarantee summary in one sentence]."
    - This is the FINAL safety net before the prospect decides

## FAILURE MODES

### Failure Mode 1: Generic Future Pacing
- **Symptom:** "Imagine waking up feeling great and having the life you've always wanted"
- **Cause:** Futures written without product-specific or niche-specific details
- **Prevention:** Step 2 requires sensory language, specific details, and time frames
- **Recovery:** Rewrite with product-specific outcomes, niche scenarios, and sensory details

### Failure Mode 2: Three or More Options Offered
- **Symptom:** "You could try it, think about it, or come back later"
- **Cause:** Binary structure not enforced
- **Prevention:** Step 4 requires exactly 2 options; delay IS Choice B
- **Recovery:** Collapse to exactly 2: action vs. inaction

### Failure Mode 3: Negative Future is Cruel
- **Symptom:** Reader feels attacked or shamed — defensive reaction
- **Cause:** Negative future written as punishment instead of honest consequence
- **Prevention:** Step 3 writing rules require honest framing, not shaming
- **Recovery:** Soften to "this is what continues" rather than "this is what you deserve"

### Failure Mode 4: Handoff Missing Critical Fields
- **Symptom:** Skill 14 cannot execute close because binary choice text or guarantee reference missing
- **Cause:** Handoff fields not fully populated
- **Prevention:** Step 7 populates all handoff fields; Step 8 verifies completeness
- **Recovery:** Populate missing fields from upstream confirmed data
