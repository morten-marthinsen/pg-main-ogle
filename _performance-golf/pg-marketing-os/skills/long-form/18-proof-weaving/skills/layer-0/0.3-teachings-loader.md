## CORE TEACHINGS: PROOF WRITING PRINCIPLES

### Clayton Makepeace: Proof Maximization

**The Proof Waterfall Principle:**
> "Every claim you make creates a debt. Proof is how you pay it off. The bigger the claim, the bigger the proof required."

**Proof Hierarchy:**
1. **Third-Party Validation** (strongest) — Studies, institutions, media
2. **Social Proof** — Testimonials, customer counts, sales figures
3. **Demonstration** — Specific outcomes with numbers
4. **Logic** — If-then reasoning that leads to obvious conclusion
5. **Authority** — Credentials and expertise of source

**Claim-Proof Pairing Rule:**
> "Never let a claim stand alone for more than 2-3 sentences before introducing supporting proof."

---

### Gary Halbert: Testimonial Structure

**The "Real Person" Formula:**
```
[First Name] [Last Name], [City], [State]

"[Their words in quotes — conversational, specific, emotional]"
```

**Why this works:**
- Full name = accountability
- Location = relatable neighbor
- Quotes = authentic voice

**Halbert's Testimonial Selection Criteria:**
1. Specific results (numbers, timeframes)
2. Emotional language (feeling words)
3. Variety of demographics
4. Different benefits highlighted

---

### Eugene Schwartz: Proof Progression

**Market Sophistication → Proof Requirements:**

| Stage | Proof Need | Proof Type Emphasis |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| Stage 1 | Minimal | Simple claim sufficient |
| Stage 2 | Moderate | Basic testimonials work |
| Stage 3 | Strong | Multiple proof types needed |
| Stage 4 | Heavy | Mechanism + demonstration required |
| Stage 5 | Maximum | Novel proof angle essential |

**Schwartz's "Proof Intensification":**
> At each sophistication level, proof must be MORE specific, MORE dramatic, MORE differentiated than competitors.

---

### John Carlton: Story-Proof Integration

**The Carlton Proof Checkpoint:**
> "Every story must contain at least one 'aha' moment where the reader thinks: 'That could be me.'"

**Transformation Proof Structure:**
1. **Before State** — Relatable suffering (specific details)
2. **Trigger Event** — What changed (discovery, meeting expert)
3. **After State** — Specific outcomes (not vague improvement)
4. **Timeline** — When transformation occurred

**Carlton's Anti-Proof Warning:**
> "Vague proof is worse than no proof. 'I feel better' kills credibility. 'I dropped 23 pounds in 6 weeks and my doctor took me off blood pressure medication' creates belief."

---

### Stefan Georgi: RMBC Proof Placement

**Proof Placement Formula:**
- **R**oot Cause Section: Logic proof (why nothing else worked)
- **M**echanism Section: Demonstration proof (how it works)
- **B**elief Section: Testimonial proof (others achieved results)
- **C**lose Section: Risk reversal + callback proof

**RMBC Proof Density Targets:**
- Root Cause: 1-2 proof elements
- Mechanism: 3-4 proof elements (heaviest)
- Belief/Story: 2-3 proof elements
- Close: 2 proof elements (callbacks to earlier)

---

### David Ogilvy: Credibility Markers

**Ogilvy's Specificity Rule:**
> "The more specific your claim, the more believable it becomes. '27% improvement' is more credible than 'significant improvement.'"

**Credibility Amplifiers:**
- Exact numbers (not rounded)
- Named sources (not "studies show")
- Specific timeframes (not "quickly")
- Named locations (not "a clinic")

## PROOF TYPE TEACHINGS

### Testimonial Best Practices

**DO:**
- Use full names with locations
- Include specific outcomes with numbers
- Show variety in demographics/situations
- Let their voice sound authentic (imperfect grammar OK)

**DON'T:**
- Use generic praise ("It's amazing!")
- Stack too many at once (testimonial fatigue)
- Use obviously fabricated-sounding language
- Place testimonials without transitions

### Study Citation Best Practices

**DO:**
- Name the institution
- Specify the finding with numbers
- Frame emotionally (what it MEANS)
- Connect to the mechanism being discussed

**DON'T:**
- Use academic citation format (dry)
- Reference obscure journals (no credibility)
- Present findings without emotional context
- Over-cite (diminishing returns)

### Demonstration Best Practices

**DO:**
- Use specific percentages and timeframes
- Compare to alternatives (superiority)
- Show real-world application
- Make the demonstration visual if possible

**DON'T:**
- Use vague improvement language
- Miss comparison opportunities
- Present without context (so what?)
- Forget the emotional payoff
