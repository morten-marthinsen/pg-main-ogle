# Skill 13.2.1: Problem Agitation & False Belief Writer

**Version:** 1.0
**Skill:** 10 — Root Cause Narrative
**Layer:** 2 (Draft Generation)
**Position:** First writer — opens the root cause narrative with symptom identification and false belief installation
**Type:** Copy Generation
**Dependencies:** narrative_sequence_map.json (from 1.4), emotional_arc_design.json (from 1.3), communication_type_classification.json (from 1.1), checkpoint_response.json (from 0.4), upstream_data.json (from 0.1)
**Output:** phases_1_2_draft.json

---

## PURPOSE

Write Phase 1 (Problem Agitation / Symptom Identification) and Phase 2 (False Belief Installation) — the opening of the root cause narrative. Phase 1 names every symptom the reader experiences so precisely that they feel deeply understood. Phase 2 articulates what they currently believe about the cause of those symptoms with such accuracy that they feel the writer is reading their mind. Together, these phases create the psychological setup for the root cause reveal: the reader is nodding "yes, that's me" and simultaneously held captive by a false belief about to be shattered.

---

## IDENTITY

**This microskill IS:**
- A copy writer that generates Phase 1 and Phase 2 prose text
- A symptom articulator that uses the prospect's own language to name their experience
- A false belief installer that precisely states what the reader currently believes is causing their problem
- A setup builder that creates the contrast necessary for the reveal in Phase 4

**This microskill is NOT:**
- A root cause revealer — it NEVER reveals the root cause; it builds the platform for the reveal
- A solution presenter — no mechanism, product, or solution appears here
- An authority builder (that is Phase 3 in 2.2) — credibility comes later
- A free-form creative writer — it follows the classified communication type, emotional arc, and word count budget

## PROCESS

### Step 1: Extract Symptom Data

```
GATHER symptom data from all available sources:

FROM confirmed_root_cause.three_part_structure.what_they_think:
  EXTRACT the false beliefs that imply symptoms
  EXAMPLE: "Bad diet, lack of exercise, slow metabolism"
    → Implied symptoms: weight gain, low energy, bloating, fatigue

FROM upstream_data (if deep research available):
  EXTRACT customer language patterns about symptoms
  EXTRACT exact phrases prospects use to describe their experience
  EXTRACT symptom frequency data (most universal → most specific)

FROM copy_blocks.false_belief_statement (if available):
  EXTRACT pre-written symptom language as starting point

COMPILE symptom_list:
  - RANK symptoms from most universal to most specific
  - Universal symptoms go first (highest nodding potential)
  - Specific symptoms go later (deepens the "you're talking about ME" feeling)
  - Minimum: 4 symptoms named
  - Target: 8-12 symptoms for health; 4-6 for finance/golf

EXAMPLE symptom lists by niche:
  Health/gut: bloating, gas, brain fog, fatigue, sugar cravings, weight gain,
              joint pain, skin issues, digestive discomfort, poor sleep,
              afternoon energy crash, food sensitivities
  Golf: slice, inconsistency, lost distance, anxiety over the ball,
        frustration on the range, scores not improving despite lessons
  Finance: stagnant portfolio, eroded savings, missed opportunities,
           confusion about which strategy works, fear of retirement shortfall
```

### Step 2: Write Phase 1 — Problem Agitation

```
APPLY communication_type writing parameters:

  IF scientific_explanation:
    TONE: Clinical naming of symptoms, precise, listing
    EXAMPLE PATTERN: "If you're experiencing [symptom], [symptom], or [symptom]...
                      you're not alone. And you're not imagining it."

  IF direct_confrontation:
    TONE: Aggressive symptom calling, confrontational
    EXAMPLE PATTERN: "Your [symptom] isn't getting better. Your [symptom] is getting
                      worse. And every [solution] you've tried hasn't done a damn thing."

  IF conspiracy_reveal:
    TONE: Knowing, insider, sympathetic
    EXAMPLE PATTERN: "You've noticed the [symptom]. The [symptom]. The [symptom] that
                      just won't go away no matter what you do. There's a reason for that."

  IF not_your_fault:
    TONE: Empathetic, validating, gentle
    EXAMPLE PATTERN: "The [symptom] you've been struggling with... the [symptom] that
                      keeps you up at night... it's not because of anything you've done wrong."

  IF data_presentation:
    TONE: Factual, statistical, authoritative
    EXAMPLE PATTERN: "[X]% of Americans over 40 experience [symptom]. [Y]% report
                      [symptom]. And [Z]% say [symptom] is affecting their daily life."

DRAFT Phase 1 prose:
  OPEN with the entry_transition pattern from the sequence map
  SEQUENCE symptoms from universal → specific
  USE the prospect's own vocabulary (not clinical terms unless scientific_explanation type)
  HIT the emotional target from the arc design (e.g., "frustrated recognition")
  END with a pivot that suggests these symptoms are connected

  WORD COUNT: Match phase_1.word_count_target (tolerance: +/- 15%)

CRITICAL RULES for Phase 1:
  - DO NOT explain WHY the symptoms exist (that comes in Phase 4)
  - DO NOT introduce the root cause or even hint at it
  - DO NOT offer solutions or hope yet
  - DO name symptoms the reader experiences in THEIR language
  - DO make the reader feel deeply seen and understood
  - DO create the sense that these symptoms are connected (not random)
```

### Step 3: Write Phase 2 — False Belief Installation

```
FROM confirmed_root_cause.three_part_structure.what_they_think:
  EXTRACT the false belief(s) to articulate

FROM copy_blocks.false_belief_statement (if available):
  USE as starting point, adapt to communication type voice

DRAFT Phase 2 prose:

  THE CORE TECHNIQUE:
  Articulate what the reader currently believes SO precisely that they feel
  the writer is reading their mind. This is NOT mocking or condescending —
  it is empathetic mirroring of their worldview.

  PATTERN STRUCTURES:

  Pattern A — "You think..." enumeration:
    "You think it's because of [false cause 1].
     You've been told it's [false cause 2].
     Maybe you even believe it's [false cause 3].
     And you'd be wrong about ALL of it."

  Pattern B — "You've tried..." accumulation:
    "You've tried [solution 1]. You've tried [solution 2].
     You've even tried [solution 3].
     And NOTHING has worked — not really.
     Because every one of those approaches targets [false cause],
     when the real problem is something entirely different."

  Pattern C — "They told you..." externalization:
    "Your doctor told you [false advice 1].
     The magazines said [false advice 2].
     The internet convinced you [false advice 3].
     They were all wrong. And it's not your fault for believing them."

  Pattern D — "Everyone knows..." inversion setup:
    "Everyone knows the key to [result] is [false belief].
     It's common sense. It's what the experts say.
     And it's the exact opposite of the truth."

  SELECT pattern based on:
    - communication_type: direct_confrontation → Pattern A or D
    - communication_type: not_your_fault → Pattern C
    - communication_type: conspiracy_reveal → Pattern C
    - communication_type: scientific_explanation → Pattern B
    - communication_type: opposite_of_truth → Pattern D

  APPLY emotional target from arc design for Phase 2
  (typically: "misled frustration" or "false security challenged")

  END with a transition hook into authority bridge:
    - Suggest that someone has seen through this false belief
    - Create anticipation without revealing the truth
    - Example: "But one person saw through the lie. And what he discovered
               about the REAL cause changes everything..."

  WORD COUNT: Match phase_2.word_count_target (tolerance: +/- 15%)

CRITICAL RULES for Phase 2:
  - DO state the false belief with empathetic precision
  - DO make the reader feel understood, not stupid
  - DO set up the contrast for the reveal (false belief must be clear enough
    to be shattered in Phase 4)
  - DO NOT reveal the root cause — only articulate the false belief
  - DO NOT blame the reader for holding this belief
  - DO NOT use condescending language ("you mistakenly thought...")
  - DO end with a hook that creates anticipation for Phase 3
```

### Step 4: Adjust for Format Variation

```
IF format_variation == "vsl_sequence":
  - Longer, more conversational prose
  - Can use sentence fragments for emphasis
  - Repeat key symptom phrases for oral rhythm
  - Phase 1 and Phase 2 clearly separated

IF format_variation == "magalog_sequence":
  - Tighter prose, sub-header between Phase 1 and Phase 2
  - Phase 1 may use bullet points for symptom list
  - Phase 2 in paragraph form
  - Bold key phrases

IF format_variation == "advertorial_sequence":
  - COMBINE Phase 1 and Phase 2 into one editorial opening
  - News voice: "Millions of Americans experience [symptoms], and most believe
    [false belief]. But new research suggests they may be completely wrong."
  - Journalistic distance maintained

IF format_variation == "email_sequence":
  - Phase 1 may be its own email (subject line references most universal symptom)
  - Phase 2 opens the next email or second half of first email
  - Each email needs its own micro-hook
```

### Step 5: Quality Self-Check

```
BEFORE outputting, VERIFY:

  □ Phase 1 names at least 4 symptoms
  □ Symptoms use prospect's vocabulary, not jargon
  □ Symptoms sequence from universal → specific
  □ Phase 1 emotional target hit (check against arc design)
  □ NO root cause revealed or hinted at in Phase 1
  □ NO solutions, hope, or mechanism mentioned in Phase 1

  □ Phase 2 articulates the false belief with precision
  □ False belief matches what_they_think from three-part structure
  □ Phase 2 emotional target hit (check against arc design)
  □ NO blame placed on the reader for holding the false belief
  □ Phase 2 ends with anticipation hook for Phase 3
  □ NO root cause revealed in Phase 2

  □ Word counts within 15% of targets
  □ Communication type voice consistent throughout
  □ No generic/AI-default language ("imagine," "discover," "unlock")
  □ Prose reads naturally when spoken aloud (for VSL format)

  IF any check fails: REVISE before outputting
```

## QUALITY CRITERIA

1. **Reader feels understood** — the symptom list must feel like a mirror of the reader's experience, not a clinical checklist
2. **False belief precisely articulated** — the reader must think "yes, that IS what I believe" before Phase 4 shatters it
3. **No premature revelation** — zero hints of the root cause, mechanism, solution, or product
4. **Emotional targets hit** — Phase 1 hits its arc target (e.g., frustrated recognition); Phase 2 hits its target (e.g., misled frustration)
5. **Word counts within tolerance** — both phases within 15% of sequence map targets
6. **Voice matches communication type** — scientific explanation sounds clinical; direct confrontation sounds aggressive; not_your_fault sounds empathetic
7. **Transition hook present** — Phase 2 must end with a line that makes the reader NEED to keep reading into Phase 3

---

## FAILURE MODES

| Failure | Severity | Detection | Handling |
|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| Root cause revealed in Phase 1 or 2 | CRITICAL | Any mention of the actual root cause | Remove immediately; Phase 1-2 must ONLY name symptoms and false beliefs |
| Fewer than 4 symptoms named | MODERATE | Symptom count < 4 | Add more symptoms from research data or niche knowledge |
| False belief is vague | HIGH | Reader cannot identify what they believe | Rewrite with specific language matching what_they_think |
| Reader blamed for false belief | HIGH | Language implies reader is stupid or lazy | Rewrite with empathetic framing — "you were told," not "you thought wrong" |
| No transition hook | MODERATE | Phase 2 ends without anticipation | Add hook line connecting to authority/reveal |
| Generic symptoms used | MODERATE | Symptoms are not niche-specific | Replace with niche-accurate, prospect-vocabulary symptoms |
| Word count severely off | LOW | More than 30% over/under target | Edit to bring within tolerance |

## EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Gundry — Scientific Explanation Type

```
PHASE 1 — PROBLEM AGITATION (200 words):

"If you've been dealing with unexplained bloating after meals... if brain fog
hits you every afternoon like clockwork... if you've watched your waistline
expand despite eating what you thought were healthy foods...

You're not alone.

Gas. Digestive discomfort. Sugar cravings you can't shake. Fatigue that
coffee can't fix. Joint pain that showed up out of nowhere. Skin that's
lost its glow.

Maybe you've even noticed your memory isn't as sharp as it used to be.
Or that your energy crashes around 2 or 3 PM every single day.

These aren't random symptoms. They're not just 'getting older.' And they're
not in your head.

They're connected. Every single one of them traces back to the same place.
And it's a place no one has told you to look."

Symptoms named: [bloating, brain fog, weight gain, gas, digestive discomfort,
  sugar cravings, fatigue, joint pain, skin issues, memory decline, energy crash]
Emotional target: "frustrated recognition" — MEDIUM intensity


PHASE 2 — FALSE BELIEF INSTALLATION (100 words):

"You've been told the problem is your diet — that you need to eat less
and move more. That it's about calories in, calories out. That whole grains,
lean proteins, and plenty of fruits and vegetables are the answer.

Maybe you've tried low-fat. Maybe you've tried keto. Maybe you've tried
counting every calorie and logging every meal.

And it didn't work. Not really. Not permanently.

Because every one of those approaches is built on the same flawed assumption
about what's actually causing the damage.

But one physician — with credentials that would make your doctor's head
spin — saw through the entire charade..."

False belief articulated: "Bad diet, calories, lack of exercise cause symptoms"
Pattern used: Pattern B — "You've tried..." accumulation
Anticipation hook: "But one physician... saw through the entire charade..."
```

### Example 2: Haney — Direct Confrontation Type

```
PHASE 1 (175 words):

"Your slice isn't getting better. You know it. I know it.

You step up to the tee and that familiar knot tightens in your stomach.
You know what's coming. The ball starts left, then sweeps right — into
the trees, into the water, into the rough. Again.

Your handicap hasn't dropped in years. Your playing partners have stopped
asking for your secret. Your range sessions feel like punishment, not practice.

You've lost distance. You've lost consistency. You've lost the joy of
the game you used to love.

And the worst part? You've spent hundreds — maybe thousands — on lessons,
training aids, and YouTube tips that promised to fix it."

PHASE 2 (100 words):

"You believe your slice is caused by swinging over the top. Or a weak grip.
Or poor shoulder rotation. Or standing too close to the ball.

That's what Golf Digest told you. That's what your club pro told you.
That's what every YouTube instructor with a camera and a polo shirt told you.

Grip changes. Stance adjustments. Backswing drills. Follow-through exercises.

IT'S A LOT OF BULL.

And one man — the coach who fixed Tiger Woods' swing — is about to tell
you exactly why..."

False belief: "Grip, stance, backswing mechanics cause the slice"
Pattern used: Pattern A — "You think..." (confrontational variant)
```

### Example 3: Stansberry — Data Presentation Type

```
PHASE 1 (150 words):

"Your retirement savings aren't growing the way they promised. The 401(k)
that was supposed to fund your golden years looks more like a piggy bank
with a leak.

Market volatility has erased gains you waited years to build. Inflation
has quietly eaten the purchasing power of every dollar you saved. And the
'safe' investments your advisor recommended are returning less than the
cost of living increase.

You feel it every time you check your portfolio. Something is fundamentally
wrong."

PHASE 2 (90 words):

"You've been told the answer is diversification. Dollar-cost averaging.
Index funds. 'Stay the course' and 'time in the market beats timing the
market.'

You followed the rules. You did everything right.

And your wealth is still being eroded — silently, systematically, every
single year.

Because every mainstream financial strategy is built on one assumption
that hasn't been true since 1971.

One financial analyst — with a track record of predicting every major
market event of the last two decades — knows exactly what it is..."

False belief: "Diversification and standard investment strategies protect wealth"
```
