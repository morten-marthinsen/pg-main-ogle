## Headline

"The new Rolls-Royce is designed to be owner-driven. No chauffeur required."

## Context & Background

At the time, Rolls-Royce was known for being a high-class car often driven by a chauffeur. In this ad, Rolls-Royce sought to bridge a feeling of luxury and status with the idea of the car being "owner-driven" and available for casual, everyday use. Mentioning that "no chauffeur is required" frames the car as one that is worthy of a chauffeur, but shouldn't need one.

Ogilvy follows this lead with a list of 17 remarkable facts about the car -- this same body copy was used in many of his other ads including the famous "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" ad.

## Key Specimen Value

- Repositioning strategy: taking a perceived barrier (need for chauffeur) and turning it into an invitation
- "Worthy of a chauffeur, but doesn't need one" -- implicit status elevation
- 17 numbered facts format -- scannable, authoritative, proof-stacking
- Same body copy, completely different positioning through headline alone

The new Rolls-Royce is designed to be owner-driven. No chauffeur required.

**Subhead:**

The Rolls-Royce is eighteen inches shorter than the largest domestic cars. With power steering, power brakes and an automatic gear-shift, it is very easy to drive and park.

**Body Copy:**

What makes Rolls-Royce the best car in the world?

"There is really no magic about it," says an eminent Rolls-Royce engineer.

"Such details as these:"

1. Every Rolls-Royce engine is run for seven hours at full throttle before installation, and each car is test-driven for hundreds of miles over varying road surfaces.

2. The Rolls-Royce is guaranteed for three years. This warranty is twelve times longer than the warranty on most domestic cars.

3. The Rolls-Royce is guaranteed for three years. This guarantee—it costs far less than fourteen coats of finishing paint go on.

4. There is no metal-to-metal contact between the body of the car and the chassis frame—except for the speedometer drive. The entire body is insulated and under-sealed.

5. At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise comes from the electric clock. The silence of the engine is uncanny. Writes the Technical Editor of *THE MOTOR*: "... three mufflers tune out sound frequencies—acoustically."

6. The finished car spends a week in the final test-shop, being fine-tuned. Here it is subjected to 98 separate ordeals. For example, the engineers use a stethoscope to listen for axle-whine.

7. The Rolls-Royce radiator has never been changed, except that when Sir Henry Royce died in 1933 the monogram RR was changed from red to black.

8. By moving a switch on the steering column, you can adjust the shock absorbers to suit road conditions—at any speed.

9. Auxiliary switch deflects the rear window, by housing a network of 1360 invisible wires in the glass. There are two separate ventilating systems, so that you can ride in comfort with all the windows closed. Air conditioning is optional.

10. The seats are upholstered with eight hides of English leather—enough to make 128 pairs of soft shoes.

11. A picnic table, veneered in French walnut, slides out from under the dash. Two more swing out behind the front seats.

12. You can get such optional extras as an *Espresso* coffee-making machine, a dictating machine, a bed, hot and cold water for washing, an electric razor.

**Jet Engines and the Future**

Certain airlines have chosen Rolls-Royce jet engines for their Boeing 707's and Douglas DC-8's. Rolls-Royce pop-up jets in the Vulcan and Victor bombers. The Handley-Page *Herald* 27 and the Vickers *Viscount*.

Rolls-Royce engines power more than half the turboprop and propjet airlines supplied to an order for world airlines.

Rolls-Royce now employs 42,000 people and the company's engineering experience also covers marine jet and gas turbines, as well as marine jet and jet engines, as well as gasoline engines for many other applications.

The huge research and development resources of the company are now at work on many projects for the future, including nuclear and rocket propulsion.

**ROLLS-ROYCE AND BENTLEY**
