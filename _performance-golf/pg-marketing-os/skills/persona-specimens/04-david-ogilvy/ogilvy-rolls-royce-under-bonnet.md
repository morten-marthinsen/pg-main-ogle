## Headline

"What's under the bonnet of a Rolls-Royce?"

## Context & Background

By far, this was the longest ad Ogilvy did for Rolls-Royce. The first three pages are from a three-page spread that ran in The New Yorker in 1959. A full page ad with the same copy ran in the Chicago Daily Tribune that same year. They had to break the copy down into 5 columns and minimize the font-size to fit it all.

What made this ad longer is that Ogilvy didn't necessarily create new, different copy, but assembled copy from multiple ads into one. This one was the most curiosity-driven, due to the question-based headline. Like his "A Guide To Rolls-Royce Comfort and Graces" headline, this one is very non-direct and comes across like an article.

Overall, this is an excellent example of how Ogilvy reformatted and repurposed his copy, which worked very well.

## Key Specimen Value

- Question headline drives curiosity
- Longest ad in the campaign -- 3-page spread
- Copy repurposing: assembled from multiple prior ads into one comprehensive piece
- Editorial disguise: reads like a magazine article, not an advertisement
- Demonstrates that long copy works when the content is fascinating
