**How to write travel advertising**

**1. Identify the "springs of action."** People travel for many reasons. Before you write an advertisement, find out which of these reasons appeal most to your potential customers.

Research conducted for the United States Travel Service revealed the primary reason people from different countries visit the U.S. The Irish and English really want to see "This ain't my momma's country!" research came in useful to us. The French want to visit Les Grands Magasins. Japanese visitors want to visit a university. The Germans want to visit Disneyland. We used all this in our advertising for the United States, positioning it as "Visit America — It's a Whole Different World!" ... and all that this implies.

Puerto Rican research even went so far as to identify separate types of vacation — "quiet relaxation" and "exciting exotic experience." Prospective travelers come in as many varieties as travelers themselves.

**2. Make your advertising deliver a specific promise.** Ogilvy & Mather conducted a study of 1500 travel advertisements. We found that those which promised a "benefit" — something specific to the traveler — had their readership scores highest.

Most travel advertising simply promised done with 20% prices. It listed new sauces. Effective advertising promises something specific.

**3. Give your advertising a big idea.** Take a look at these two advertisements.

Both sold travel to the same country and both appeared in similar publications. One was tremendously effective (received four times the coupon response of the other). Both were interesting. Both were full of factual information about that travel product, but one had a "big idea."

Experiment! This difference is a factor of four between two advertisements which are both "interesting" and both "well-written."

**4. Don't shy away from long copy.** One of our most successful early travel advertisements for the Government of Puerto Rico used over 1000 words. It's a myth that readers won't read long copy. If you make the information interesting and give it a pay-off for itself that turns on their attention and self-interest, they will.

Good advertising involves specific promises, and long copy offers you the opportunity to give more specifics. More specifics give you more opportunity to appeal to the motivations we're talking about.

The one thing that matters are nations about many things —

from the quality of the drinking water to "how safe will I be?" Use long copy to answer those concerns and doubts that all tourists have.

**The real secret of success**

If you can start writing about your country or tourist area in a way that sounds fresh, you may have a real breakthrough. For American Express, we devised a new travel advertising campaign that broke new ground. Instead of showing the sights of faraway places, as most travel advertisers do, we concentrated on what happens to travelers when they get there. Research showed that most tourists want to find themselves in a place that is lush, exotic, totally different. We captured this in a campaign for American Express in France. All of the headlines included specific names and places. In France, we used a photograph of a man eating in a restaurant. Research showed it was the most effective.

**5. Give your product a first person voice.** Let the destination itself speak. We wrote an unusual campaign for the Bahamas that let the islands tell their own story. Instead of an advertising voice, changing tourist themes from year to year meant the advertising felt authentic.

**6. "Store-door" advertising**

This new technique, now being used by many tourist advertisers, uses direct response in combination with regular travel promotion. For an island destination, a "store-door" ad offered the fulfillment of a brochure on the island's attractions.

The same week, Ogilvy & Mather people in London and Paris were getting inquiries from readers who responded to our "store-door" ads for the same destination. The headline said: "New American Express gives you something that money has never been able to buy."

That same week, Ogilvy & Mather people in London sent brochures, maps, and detailed information to those who responded.

**7. Bargains are still irresistible.** An Editors Poll in a recent issue of Travel & Leisure reports that saving money was half featured. Air travel carriers have long since proved this.

The one travel ad that pulled coupons from all over? "Winter style days, bath round-trip airfare included."

**How to generate more coupon returns**

**8. Make your coupon larger.** Use the biggest coupon allowed. Travel advertisers know the coupon works.

Most tourists use the coupon in the first of the publications listed on the coupon. However, use all the names. One of our clients recently redesigned the top of four different American Express Travel Division coupons. With minor word changes, coupon requests increased by 25%.
