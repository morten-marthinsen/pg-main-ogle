## OCR Extracted Body Copy (2026-02-15)

**Photo caption (top right):**

*The Rolls-Royce Silver Cloud II—$14,895 P.O.E.*

**Headline:**

You can adjust the shock absorbers to the changing road by flicking a switch on the new Rolls-Royce and Bentley

**Subhead:**

A brain-box that is polished with ground oat husks; cadmium-plated cotter pins; a walnut-trimmed interior that unites utter silence with the luxurious smell of leather—such are the tremendous trifles that make the Rolls-Royce so coveted at $14,895. Guaranteed for three years—12 times as long as most domestic cars.

**Body Copy:**

The Rolls-Royce and its fraternal twin, the Bentley, are designed for people who are determined to live well while in transit. There are few reasonable desires which the owner cannot satisfy by pressing a button, pulling a lever, or flicking a switch.

For example, if the road surface is not to your liking, you can, in effect, change it. A switch on the steering column lets you adjust the shock absorbers to any driving condition. You harden or soften the ride at will.

"Rough roads pass almost unnoticed," writes Gordon Wilkins, motoring correspondent of *The Observer*, "for they neither shock the occupants nor annul their ease with distressing tire noises."

If the noise from other cars distresses you, you can close the windows and still get fresh air through an efficient ventilating system. Within your Rolls-Royce, blissful silence reigns. You can converse in discreet whispers.

Three walnut picnic tables are among the graces of the Rolls-Royce and Bentley. One slides out from the dash—an ideal place for a road map. Two others fold down behind the front seats. They make fine work surfaces upon which the children can rest their coloring books to keep them quietly amused on long trips.

Each of the front seats is individually adjustable. The driver can set his seat to the most comfortable position for a combined range of 14 possible adjustments—for himself and his companion reaches the exact bias of the road while his companion reaches the exact position she prefers.

Much of the interior is surfaced with French walnut which has been hand-polished by English craftsmen. Notes are made on the bur and burl of the walnut used in each car, and are filed under your serial number. If a portion of the wood should ever be damaged, it can be matched flawlessly in grain and finish.

How you see the rear window defroster. Or, rather, you do not see it. It consists of a heating element of 1,360 nearly invisible wires moulded into the glass—finer than a human hair. At the flick of a switch, mist and frost are cleared electrically.

The gas cap cannot be opened without the driver's consent. You unlock it electrically from a button on the dash. And you should never run out of gas. A green light on the instrument panel grows more reproachful as you consume the last three gallons.

Your luggage rides here in carpeted seclusion. It can never be soiled by the spare wheel, which occupies a separate compartment under the luggage. You can test the air pressure in the spare through a special port without removing the tire.

There are two illuminated vanity mirrors in niches in the rear quarters of the Rolls-Royce. Ladies may therefore perfect their make-up in privacy before leaving the car.

Under a hutch in the wheel compartment you will find an assortment of cadmium-plated tools and replacement bulbs. They set in a fitted case of orange velvet—further evidence of Rolls-Royce's obsession with peace and quiet.

Corrosion is held at bay by a plating of chromium on every nut, bolt, washer and cotter pin. Even the tail pipe is chromed.

A dictating machine, an *Espresso* coffee maker, an electric razor, a bed, a telephone, and water for making are available as optional extras—for creature comforts.

The basic construction of the Rolls-Royce is, of course, its magnificent riding qualities. From a 100 m.p.h. cruising speed, it has a bonus of safety of ride. But the $790/year car is definitely more fun.

The power-assisted steering is, according to Robert Glenton in the *Sunday Express*, "as delicate as the play of a pool cue in a glass of champagne..." The power brakes will halt the car squarely from 100 m.p.h., without a tremor. And there are three independent brake linkages—two hydraulic and one mechanical. In the unlikely event that one brake should fail, the others automatically keep the car under perfect control.

Here are the only differences between the Rolls-Royce and the Bentley—their radiator design. In all other respects they are identical motor cars. The Bentley costs $300 less because its radiator is simpler to make.

If you desire the rewarding experience of driving a Rolls-Royce or a Bentley, write Rolls-Royce, Inc., 30 Rockefeller Plaza, New York 20, Circle 5-1144, or telephone to one of the dealers you will find on the adjacent page.
