## Context & Background

This was a two page ad that ran in the New Yorker in 1960. Of all the ads in Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce campaign, this one probably does the best job of flying under the radar and coming across as an editorial. "Guide To..." is a great headline starter for those into native advertising.

The ad is also a powerful example of how to sell luxury. What Ogilvy is primarily discussing in this ad are tiny, minute details and little-known features that make the car special and unique. It's these extras and non-necessities that the rich and wealthy splurge on beyond the core qualities of the car like its look and build.

This ad (and the entire campaign) is a must-study for those selling custom-made or specially assembled items.

## Key Specimen Value

- "Guide to" headline -- pure native advertising/editorial disguise
- Selling luxury through tiny details and non-necessities
- The premium is justified by the extras, not the basics
- Two-page New Yorker spread -- significant real estate for copy-heavy approach

Three walnut picnic tables are among the graces of the Rolls-Royce and Bentley. One slides out from the dash—an ideal place for a road map. Two others fold down behind the front seats.
