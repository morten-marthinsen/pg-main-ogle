# E2 Subject Line Specimen Library
## Source: Ben Settle Corpus (2,681 emails, 2011-2018)

**Purpose:** Self-contained pattern DNA for the E2 Subject Line Engine. Every category includes its formula template, emotional triggers, curated specimens (best-in-class), and niche adaptation examples. Load this file during Layer 0 or Layer 1 of E2 execution.

**Corpus Stats:**
- 2,681 total emails | 2,263 unique subject lines | ~85 reused 3+ times
- Average word count: 7.3 words | Sweet spot: 4-9 words (70% of all SLs)
- Dominant emotional recipe: Greed + Ego + Curiosity (33% of all SLs)
- Top 3 openers: "The" (15.5%), "How" (14.6%), "Why" (8.8%) = 39% of all SLs
- Statements >> Questions. Declarative authority, not uncertain questioning.

### Category A: "How To" Framework (258 SLs -- 11.4%)
**Formula:** `How to [verb] [unexpected/edgy object]`
**Triggers:** Curiosity + Greed (A1), Greed + Relief (A2), Curiosity + Humor (A3), Social Proof + Curiosity (A4)

**What makes it work:** The "How to" frame is NOT tutorial. It works because Ben pairs the utility promise with a bizarre, paradoxical, or crude object that creates a double-take. The juxtaposition between "helpful framework" and "WTF object" is the engine.

### Category E: Profanity & Edgy Language Overlay (130 SLs -- 5.7%)
**Formula:** Not a structural formula -- a TONAL OVERLAY applied across all categories.
**Triggers:** Pattern interrupt, Humor, Tribal bonding

**What makes it work:** The profanity is NEVER gratuitous. The crude word IS the hook that makes an otherwise-standard business lesson impossible to ignore. The function is pattern interrupt in the inbox. High-frequency words: turd (11x), crap (15x), hell (25+), ass/azz (20+), balls (8x), fart (8x).

#### Specimens
1. Stick out in the inbox like a fart in study hall (but in a good way)
2. Why copywriters are almost as useless as tits on a bull
3. How to make your list happier than a pig in pewp
4. When you feel a tingle in your balls, you're about to get kicked in the nuts
5. Don't whiz on the electric fence
6. Brilliant copy won't sell a turd
7. A turd on video is still a turd
8. Pubic hair on the bar of soap

**Key rule:** The crude word must SERVE a point. It is the hook that makes the business lesson unforgettable. Remove the crude word and the insight disappears -- that's the test.

#### Niche Adaptations
- Health: Reduce to PG-13. Use "crap," "hell," mild irreverence. Avoid sexual/scatological.
- Golf: Mild crude works well. "The swing tip that's full of crap" / "Don't let some hack pee on your scorecard."
- Finance: Use "hell" and contrarian aggression. Avoid scatological. "Why that stock tip is a steaming pile."

### Category R: Command / Imperative (53 SLs -- 2.3%)
**Formula:** `Don't [common action]` / `Stop [thing they're doing]` / `[Strong verb] [unexpected object]`
**Triggers:** Authority + Curiosity (the command creates immediate tension -- WHY should I obey?)

**What makes it work:** Direct orders create instant engagement because they trigger the "why" reflex. "Don't die before reading this" -- the absurdity of the command forces the open. "Stop watching porn" -- the shock of the accusation forces the open. Commands work because they bypass the reader's decision to engage; they CREATE the engagement through the authority of the imperative.

## SECTION 5: STRUCTURAL & TONAL INTELLIGENCE

### Word Count Zones
| Zone | Word Count | Use Case | Examples |
|------|-----------|----------|----------|
| Mystery | 2-4 words | Authority, concept naming, vagueness as hook | "The attitude" / "Dead ad walking" |
| Sweet Spot | 5-8 words | Most email types, highest volume zone | "How to profit from hate mail" |
| Full Concept | 9-12 words | How-to with qualifiers, paradox setups | "How to sell to people who hate your guts" |
| Story/Simile | 13+ words | When you need the full mental picture | "Stick out in the inbox like a fart in study hall (but in a good way)" |

### Punctuation Rules
- **Default to NO punctuation.** Statements, not questions. Authority, not uncertainty.
- **Quotation marks (8.6%):** For scare quotes, coined terms, or reader-voice framing. "Will the $20 Gene Schwartz product teach me anything new?"
- **Question marks (2.3%):** Rarely used. Reserve for "Who wins the fight" pattern.
- **Exclamation marks (1.1%):** Almost never. Ben conveys urgency through WORD CHOICE, not punctuation.
- **Ellipsis (1.2%):** Open loops, trailing thoughts. Use sparingly.
- **Parentheses (1.5%):** Self-aware asides: "(yes, seriously)" / "(but in a good way)"

### The Edginess Calibration Filter
Transform any generic subject line into a Ben Settle subject line with one substitution:

| Generic | Ben Settle Version |
|---------|-------------------|
| "How to stand out" | "How to stick out like a fart in study hall" |

| "Client management" | "The case for rudely ignoring clients" |
| "Increase your income" | "How to make your list happier than a pig in pewp" |

**The test:** Ask "How would I say this if I was slightly drunk at a bar telling a buddy?" That's the register.
