"Crap! did I need a reservation?"

But alas, no.

You see, the owner doesn't want *everyone* as a customer.

And, as he said:

"I just put those on the tables

Riff-raff being anyone he doesn't want to serve.

After sitting down, a man and a woman came in with two loud, obnoxious kids. Long story short:

The owner shoos them out and says all the tables are reserved.

Then, he says:

"You're welcome" to the rest of us.

## Structural Analysis

1. **Story opener with disclaimer frame** — "Following is a true story" is the explicit ST marker. The disclaimer about changed names adds intrigue and frames it as something worth telling. The "anti-professional teachings" tease plants the lesson seed before the story begins.

2. **Scene-setting with sensory detail** — "a classy wine bar," "the owner personally cooks your meal on the spot," "the first thing you see on all the tables is a little reserved sign." Settle uses just enough detail to make the scene vivid without over-describing.

3. **Dialogue as the story's engine** — Three direct quotes from the wine bar owner carry the narrative: "I just put those on the tables to keep the riff-raff out," the explanation about kids, and "You're welcome." Each quote reveals character and advances the lesson.

4. **The "small" anecdote principle** — This is not a grand narrative. It is a personal observation from a wine bar visit. The smallness makes it relatable and prevents reader skepticism — anyone could have been there.

5. **Explicit lesson marker** — "Moral of the story?" is the exact extraction phrase the ST body type calls for. It creates a clean break between narrative and takeaway.

6. **Lesson stated in 2 sentences** — "This guy runs his business the way HE wants to. He serves ONLY the customers he wants." No elaboration, no over-explaining. The story did the work.

7. **Self-connection bridge** — "It's the way I run my business as well" links the third-party story back to Settle's own brand, making the lesson personal.

8. **Soft product bridge** — "If this way of doing biz'nid appeals to you, then my Email Players newsletter could be a good fit for you." No hard sell. The qualifier "Maybe. It really just depends." is classic Settle — screening the reader even in the CTA.

9. **Pop culture callback** — "Seinfeld's Soup Nazi (the Wine Nazi?)" anchors the lesson in shared cultural knowledge and reinforces the subject line.

10. **CTA with thematic echo** — "Pour yourself a full glass of the details here" ties the CTA language back to the wine bar setting, creating a closed loop between story and sell.

### Key Techniques
- **Third-party observation story** — Settle is the observer, not the protagonist. Removes self-promotion smell.
- **Dialogue-driven narrative** — Quotes carry the story; description is minimal.
- **"Moral of the story?" explicit extraction** — Cleanest lesson marker in the ST body type.
- **Qualifying CTA** — "Maybe. It really just depends." screens reader even while selling.
- **Thematic CTA language** — "Pour yourself a full glass" echoes the wine bar setting.
