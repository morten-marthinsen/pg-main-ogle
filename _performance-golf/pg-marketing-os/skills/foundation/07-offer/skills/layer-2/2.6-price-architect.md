## Purpose

Design the complete price architecture — not just the price, but the entire price EXPERIENCE: how price is anchored, revealed, framed, justified, and made to feel like a steal. This skill applies Hormozi's pricing principles (charge premium, maximize the value-price gap) and Todd Brown's price presentation sequence (anchor → discount → actual → terms → savings).

---

## The Principle

**"Price is never the issue. Value relative to price is the issue. The price architect's job is to make the actual price feel small against the perceived value."**

---

## Hormozi Pricing Principles

```
1. Charge premium — higher price = higher perceived value, better clients, better results
2. Virtuous cycle: Higher price → more investment → better engagement → better results → more proof
3. Never compete on price. Differentiate on value.
4. The gap between cost and price IS the value. Maximize the gap.
5. Those who pay the most, pay the most attention.
```

## Design Process

### Step 1: Establish the Value Baseline

```
FROM value_stack.json:
  grand_total_stated_value: $[amount]
  core_product_value: $[amount]
  total_bonus_value: $[amount]

FROM bonus_designs.json:
  Individual bonus values: [$amounts]

FROM guarantee_architecture.json:
  Guarantee type and duration (affects perceived risk)

CALCULATE:
  total_perceived_value = grand_total_stated_value
  This becomes the ANCHOR for all price presentation
```

### Step 2: Determine Actual Price

```
PRICE DETERMINATION FACTORS:

FACTOR 1: VALUE-TO-PRICE RATIO TARGET
  Minimum target: 10x value-to-price
  Ideal target: 15-20x value-to-price
  Example: If stated value = $5,000, price should be $250-$500

FACTOR 2: MARKET CEILING AWARENESS
  FROM upstream_data → competitor_landscape
  What do competitors charge for similar offers?
  NOTE: This is awareness, NOT benchmarking
  Hormozi principle: Don't compete on price — but know the range

FACTOR 3: PROSPECT CAPACITY
  FROM upstream_data → demographic_profile + market_psychology
  What can the target audience afford?
  What price creates "stretch but not break" psychology?
  NOTE: Stretch = investment mentality = better engagement

FACTOR 4: OUTCOME VALUE
  What is the RESULT worth to the prospect?
  If the result saves $10,000/year → a $997 price is 10x ROI
  If the result saves 10 hours/week → quantify hourly value
  Price should be a fraction of the outcome value

FACTOR 5: BUSINESS MODEL REQUIREMENTS
  What price supports the business model?
  Customer acquisition cost, fulfillment cost, profit margin
  Note: This is business-side, not prospect-facing

SYNTHESIZE actual_price with justification
```

### Step 3: Design the Anchor Strategy

```
PRICE ANCHORING:
  The prospect must FEEL the gap between value and price.
  Anchoring creates this feeling.

ANCHOR TYPE 1: VALUE STACK ANCHOR
  "Total value: $4,997. Today: $297."
  Most common. Uses the total stated value as anchor.
  Requirement: Stated values must be credible

ANCHOR TYPE 2: ALTERNATIVE COST ANCHOR
  "Private coaching at this level costs $500/hour × 12 sessions = $6,000."
  Uses the cost of achieving the same result another way.
  Requirement: Alternative must be real and comparable

ANCHOR TYPE 3: OUTCOME VALUE ANCHOR
  "If this saves you 10 hours per week at $50/hour, that's $26,000/year."
  Uses the dollar value of the result.
  Requirement: Outcome must be quantifiable

ANCHOR TYPE 4: COMPONENT ANCHOR
  "Module 1 alone is worth more than the entire investment."
  Uses a single high-value component.
  Requirement: That component must genuinely be worth the price

SELECT primary anchor type with justification
OPTIONALLY layer a secondary anchor for reinforcement
```

### Step 4: Design Payment Structure

```
PAYMENT OPTIONS:

OPTION A: SINGLE PAYMENT
  One upfront payment
  Pros: Clean, immediate revenue, no payment failures
  Cons: Higher barrier to entry
  Best for: < $500 offers, buyers with means

OPTION B: INSTALLMENT PLAN
  2-6 payments over time
  Pros: Lower entry barrier, higher total collection possible
  Cons: Payment failures, cash flow delay
  Best for: $200-$2000 range
  NOTE: Total of installments often exceeds single payment price
    Example: 1 pay of $297 OR 3 pays of $117 ($351 total)
    This compensates for payment risk and rewards decisive action

OPTION C: TRIAL OFFER
  "$1 today, then $[X] in [Y] days"
  Pros: Maximum conversion, low barrier
  Cons: High refund rate, compliance concerns
  Best for: Subscription models, high-confidence products

OPTION D: PAY-IN-FULL + PAYMENT PLAN
  Offer both: "One payment of $297 (save $54) OR 3 monthly payments of $117"
  Pros: Captures both buyer types
  Cons: Slightly more complex presentation

SELECT payment structure(s) with reasoning
DESIGN specific terms for each option
```

### Step 5: Design Price Presentation (Todd Brown Sequence)

```
FOLLOW Todd Brown's price presentation sequence:

STEP 1: TOTAL VALUE STATEMENT
  "With everything you get today — [list components + bonuses] —
   [Product Name] has a total value of $[ANCHOR PRICE]."

STEP 2: VALUE DEMONSTRATION ("If all it did was...")
  (This is scripted by 2.7, but price architecture must account for it)
  3 rounds of "if all it did was [benefit], would it be worth $[anchor]?"

STEP 3: DISCOUNT REVEAL
  "But today, when you [grab/enroll in] [Product Name],
   it isn't going to cost you $[anchor]."

STEP 4: INTERMEDIATE ANCHOR (optional)
  "It's not even going to cost you $[mid-point]."
  Only use if the jump from anchor to actual is dramatic enough

STEP 5: ACTUAL PRICE REVEAL
  "Instead, today you get everything for just $[ACTUAL PRICE]."

STEP 6: PAYMENT TERMS
  "That's just [payment terms description]."
  If installments: "Just [X] easy payments of $[Y]."

STEP 7: SAVINGS STATEMENT
  "That's [X]% off — a savings of over $[savings amount]."

SCRIPT the entire price presentation sequence
```

### Step 6: Design Price Justification Narrative

```
WHY does the prospect believe this price is fair?

JUSTIFICATION APPROACHES:

APPROACH 1: COST OF INACTION
  "What is it costing you to NOT solve this?"
  Quantify the ongoing cost of the problem
  Make the price feel like an investment, not an expense

APPROACH 2: COMPARISON TO ALTERNATIVES
  "You could hire a coach at $[X]/hour..."
  "You could spend $[X] on courses that don't work..."
  "You could waste another [time] trying to figure it out alone..."
  Make the price feel like a shortcut, not a cost

APPROACH 3: ROI FRAMING
  "If this delivers [outcome], what's that worth to you?"
  "Members report [X] in value within the first [timeframe]."
  Make the price feel like it pays for itself

APPROACH 4: INVESTMENT FRAMING
  "This isn't an expense — it's an investment in [transformation]."
  "The most successful people invest in themselves."
  Make the price feel like a smart decision

SELECT 1-2 justification approaches
WRITE narrative for each
```

## Quality Criteria

1. Value-to-price ratio ≥ 10x (total stated value / actual price)
2. Anchor strategy uses credible, defensible amounts
3. Payment structure offers at least one option that reduces barrier
4. Price presentation follows Todd Brown 7-step sequence
5. Price justification narrative makes the price feel like an investment
6. Comparison anchors are real alternatives (not fabricated)
7. Savings statement is mathematically accurate
8. Price is positioned relative to market (aware of competitors, not copied from them)

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Do NOT set price based solely on competitor pricing (race to the bottom)
- Do NOT anchor with values that strain credibility ($50,000 value for a PDF)
- Do NOT skip the value demonstration step (price without context is always "too expensive")
- Do NOT offer only installments with no full-pay option (reduces perceived value)
- Do NOT present price without first establishing anchor (number feels arbitrary without anchor)
- Do NOT forget the savings math — if anchor is $2,997 and price is $297, CALCULATE and STATE the savings
