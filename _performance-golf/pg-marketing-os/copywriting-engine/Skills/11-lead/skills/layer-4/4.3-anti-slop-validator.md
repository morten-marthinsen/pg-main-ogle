# Layer 4.3 — Anti-Slop Validator

**Version:** 1.0
**Layer:** 4 (Validation & Assembly)
**Type:** Language Quality Gate
**Dependencies:** full draft lead text (from Layer 3 output), teaching_index.georgi_formula (from 0.3), teaching_index.e5_framework (from 0.3), anti-slop-standards (from QualityProtocol)
**Output:** anti_slop_validation.json

---

## Purpose

Detect generic language, over-explanation, feature-dumping, marketing buzzwords, and "copywriter mode" writing in the lead. The lead is the first 350-800 words a prospect reads. If these words sound like AI-generated slop, marketing boilerplate, or a copywriter trying too hard, the prospect clicks away before the emotional sale is made. This validator applies both the general anti-slop standards AND lead-specific slop categories that address the unique failure modes of campaign leads.

---

## Primary Persona

**Sarah A. Conco — Client Protection Specialist**
Reads the lead from the client's perspective: would a business owner be embarrassed if this language represented their brand? Does it sound like a real human wrote it, or like a template was filled in? Zero tolerance for language that damages credibility.

## Secondary Persona

**Dr. Richard Stern — Skeptical Academic**
Reads with a scalpel. Every vague claim, every unsupported superlative, every phrase that substitutes intensity for substance gets flagged. If the language cannot survive scrutiny, it fails.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Scan for Lead-Specific Slop Categories

```
The lead has UNIQUE slop failure modes beyond general copywriting slop.
These are the most damaging because they violate the lead's core function.

CATEGORY 1: OVER-EXPLANATION
  DEFINITION: Explaining HOW the mechanism works in the lead
  WHY IT'S SLOP: The lead TEASES the mechanism — explanation belongs in the campaign argument
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL (automatic FAIL)

  SCAN FOR:
    - Sentences explaining the mechanism's process or science
    - "How it works" language ("the way this works is...", "the science behind...")
    - Step-by-step descriptions of the mechanism's function
    - Causal chains explaining WHY the mechanism produces results
    - Technical terminology used to explain (not just to intrigue)

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "This enzyme inhibits the production of cortisol by blocking
               the HPA axis at the hypothalamic level, which prevents..."
    ACCEPTABLE: "a little-known enzyme that essentially 'switches off' your
                body's stress response at the source"

CATEGORY 2: FEATURE-DUMPING
  DEFINITION: Listing product features instead of mechanism benefits
  WHY IT'S SLOP: The lead is about the mechanism and promise, not the product
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL (automatic FAIL)

  SCAN FOR:
    - Product feature lists ("includes X, Y, and Z")
    - Technical specifications
    - Ingredient lists presented as features
    - "Comes with..." / "Features..." / "Includes..." language
    - Module or lesson descriptions
    - Format/delivery details (video, PDF, app, etc.)

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "This program includes 12 video modules, a companion
               workbook, and weekly coaching calls..."
    ACCEPTABLE: "a breakthrough method that targets the real root cause"

CATEGORY 3: DISCOVERY STORY BLOAT
  DEFINITION: Going too deep into the backstory in the lead
  WHY IT'S SLOP: The lead briefly TEASES the discovery story — the full story belongs in the body
  SEVERITY: HIGH (requires remediation)

  SCAN FOR:
    - Discovery narrative exceeding 2-3 sentences
    - Detailed chronological accounts ("First he went to... then he discovered...")
    - Character backstory that delays the mechanism tease
    - Origin stories that consume more than 15% of lead word count

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "Dr. Williams spent 27 years in the Amazon rainforest,
               studying with indigenous healers. His first breakthrough came
               in 1997 when a Yanomami elder showed him a rare plant..."
               [continues for 150 words]
    ACCEPTABLE: "what a rogue Harvard physician discovered during an
                expedition that his colleagues called 'career suicide'"

CATEGORY 4: MARKETING BUZZWORDS
  DEFINITION: Using empty superlatives and marketing cliches without specifics
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Buzzwords signal "marketing material" — prospect's defenses go up
  SEVERITY: MODERATE (flag and remediate)

  SCAN FOR:
    - "Revolutionary" / "Breakthrough" / "Game-changing" (without specific context)
    - "Cutting-edge" / "State-of-the-art" / "Next-generation"
    - "Transform your life" / "Change everything" (without mechanism anchor)
    - "Unlock your potential" / "Unleash the power" / "Harness the secret"
    - "Groundbreaking discovery" / "Paradigm shift" / "Quantum leap"
    - "Synergy" / "Optimize" / "Leverage" (used generically)

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "This revolutionary, game-changing breakthrough will
               transform your life forever"
    ACCEPTABLE: "a method so different from mainstream advice that most
                doctors refuse to believe it until they see the data"

CATEGORY 5: GENERIC PROBLEM CALLOUTS
  DEFINITION: Using tired, template-style problem identification
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Generic callouts fail the Clemens vagueness test — they don't trigger personal identification
  SEVERITY: MODERATE (flag and remediate)

  SCAN FOR:
    - "Are you tired of..." / "Do you struggle with..." / "Have you ever..."
    - "If you're like most people..." / "You're not alone..."
    - "Imagine if..." (as an opener — overused)
    - "What if I told you..." / "What if there was a way to..."
    - "Fed up with..." / "Sick and tired of..."
    - "Frustrated by..." (as a standalone opener without specificity)

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "Are you tired of struggling with your weight? Do you feel
               frustrated every time you step on the scale?"
    ACCEPTABLE: "That number on the scale — the one you dread every
                Monday morning — isn't your fault."

CATEGORY 6: GENERIC BENEFITS
  DEFINITION: Vague benefit language that could apply to any product
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Benefits must be connected to the specific mechanism — generic benefits build no desire
  SEVERITY: MODERATE (flag and remediate)

  SCAN FOR:
    - "Amazing results" / "Incredible results" / "Life-changing results"
    - "Feel great" / "Look great" / "Feel like a new person"
    - "Live your best life" / "Reach your full potential"
    - "More energy" / "Better sleep" / "Less stress" (without mechanism connection)
    - Any benefit statement that works equally well for a competitor's product

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "You'll feel amazing, have more energy, and finally
               live the life you deserve"
    ACCEPTABLE: "within days, that brain fog you've accepted as normal
                starts to lift — because the inflammation blocking your
                neural pathways is finally being addressed"

CATEGORY 7: PROOF LEAKAGE
  DEFINITION: Including studies, statistics, or evidence as proof in the lead
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Proof belongs in the campaign argument — the lead makes the EMOTIONAL sale
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL (automatic FAIL)

  SCAN FOR:
    - Study citations ("a 2019 study published in...")
    - Statistical evidence used to prove claims ("94% of participants...")
    - Detailed testimonials as evidence ("John from Texas lost 47 lbs...")
    - Research summaries ("researchers at Harvard found...")
    - Before/after data deployed as proof

  NOTE: DISTINGUISH from acceptable usage:
    ACCEPTABLE as hook: "437% returns" (used as attention device, not proof)
    VIOLATION as proof: "In a controlled study, the method produced 437% returns,
                        proving that it outperforms traditional approaches"

    ACCEPTABLE as credibility: "developed by a Stanford-trained physician"
    VIOLATION as proof: "as verified by a team of Stanford researchers who
                        published their findings in the New England Journal of Medicine"

CATEGORY 8: PRODUCT REVELATION
  DEFINITION: Naming or describing the product in the lead
  WHY IT'S SLOP: The lead is about the mechanism and promise — the product comes in the offer
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL (automatic FAIL)

  SCAN FOR:
    - Product name mentioned directly
    - Product format described ("this supplement," "this online course," "this app")
    - Pricing or offer language
    - Call-to-action language ("order now," "click below," "get started")
    - Package or delivery descriptions

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "It's called NeuroMax Pro — a daily supplement that contains..."
    ACCEPTABLE: "a specific compound that targets the inflammation
                at the root of the problem"
```

### Step 2: Scan for General Slop Patterns

```
BEYOND lead-specific categories, scan for general language slop:

FILLER OPENERS:
  - "It's important to note that..."
  - "In today's fast-paced world..."
  - "At the end of the day..."
  - "It goes without saying..."
  - "As we all know..."
  - "In the realm of..."
  - "Here's the thing..."

HEDGE WORDS (unless genuinely uncertain):
  - "perhaps" / "maybe" / "possibly"
  - "it seems" / "it appears"
  - "one might argue" / "arguably"
  - "it could be said" / "in some ways"

EMPTY INTENSIFIERS:
  - "very" (replace with specific word)
  - "really" (delete or dimensionalize)
  - "extremely" / "incredibly" (quantify instead)
  - "absolutely" / "totally" (let the fact speak)

AI TELLTALE PHRASES:
  - "Let's dive in..." / "Without further ado..."
  - "In conclusion..." / "Moving forward..."
  - "It's worth mentioning..." / "Needless to say..."
  - "seamlessly" / "effortlessly" (magic words hiding complexity)
  - "harness" / "unlock" / "leverage" (when used generically)
  - "delve into" / "embark on" / "navigate the landscape"
  - "comprehensive" / "robust" / "holistic" (corporate AI hallmarks)

PASSIVE VOICE (check ratio):
  - Count sentences in active voice vs. passive
  - THRESHOLD: >= 80% active voice
  - FLAG each passive construction

ABSTRACT NOUN DENSITY:
  - Count abstract nouns per sentence
  - "optimization," "implementation," "functionality," "paradigm"
  - THRESHOLD: <= 1 abstract noun per sentence on average
```

### Step 3: Log Every Violation

```
FOR each violation found:

  RECORD:
    - violation_id: string (e.g., "SLOP-001")
    - location: string (sentence number or quote from lead)
    - text: string (the exact violating text)
    - category: enum[over_explanation, feature_dumping, discovery_story_bloat,
                     marketing_buzzwords, generic_problem_callouts,
                     generic_benefits, proof_leakage, product_revelation,
                     filler_opener, hedge_word, empty_intensifier,
                     ai_telltale, passive_voice, abstract_noun]
    - severity: enum[CRITICAL, HIGH, MODERATE, LOW]
    - remediation: string (specific fix — not "make it better")

  CRITICAL violations (automatic FAIL):
    - over_explanation
    - feature_dumping
    - proof_leakage
    - product_revelation

  HIGH violations (require remediation before PASS):
    - discovery_story_bloat
    - ai_telltale (multiple instances)

  MODERATE violations (flag for improvement):
    - marketing_buzzwords
    - generic_problem_callouts
    - generic_benefits
    - filler_opener
    - hedge_word
    - empty_intensifier

  LOW violations (note but do not fail):
    - single passive voice instance
    - single abstract noun above threshold
```

### Step 4: Calculate Slop Density

```
slop_density = total_violations / (word_count / 100)

RATING:
  0.0–0.5 → EXCELLENT (clean, professional copy)
  0.6–1.0 → ACCEPTABLE (minor cleanup)
  1.1–2.0 → WEAK (systematic revision needed)
  2.1+    → FAILING (fundamental language problem)

CATEGORY DISTRIBUTION:
  COUNT violations per category
  IDENTIFY if one category dominates (systemic issue vs. scattered)
```

### Step 5: Apply the Read-Aloud Test

```
SIMULATE reading the full lead aloud:

LISTEN FOR:
  - Phrases that feel awkward spoken (flag)
  - Sentences that require re-reading to parse (flag)
  - Transitions that feel robotic (flag)
  - Language that sounds "written" not "spoken" (flag)
  - Any moment where a listener would say "what?" (flag)
  - Marketing voice that sounds like a commercial, not a conversation

THE CONVERSATIONAL BAR:
  "Could this be spoken by a knowledgeable friend explaining
   something exciting they just discovered — or does it sound
   like a marketing department wrote it?"

  IF marketing voice → additional violations logged
  IF conversational voice → PASS this check
```

### Step 6: Determine Gate Result

```
GATE DECISION:

  IF any CRITICAL violation exists → FAIL
  IF slop_density > 2.0 → FAIL
  IF more than 3 HIGH violations → FAIL

  IF zero CRITICAL AND zero HIGH AND slop_density <= 0.5 → PASS
  IF zero CRITICAL AND <= 1 HIGH AND slop_density <= 1.0 → PASS_WITH_WARNINGS
  IF zero CRITICAL AND 2-3 HIGH AND slop_density <= 2.0 → REVISE

  PASS: Ship as-is
  PASS_WITH_WARNINGS: Minor cleanup recommended, not blocking
  REVISE: Must fix before assembly (does not block other validators)
  FAIL: Must fix before assembly (blocks final assembly)
```

## Quality Gates

- [ ] All 8 lead-specific slop categories scanned
- [ ] All general slop patterns scanned (filler, hedge, intensifiers, AI telltales)
- [ ] Every violation logged with location, exact text, category, severity, and specific remediation
- [ ] CRITICAL violations correctly trigger automatic FAIL
- [ ] Slop density calculated using (violations / word_count per 100)
- [ ] Active voice ratio computed (threshold: >= 80%)
- [ ] Abstract noun density computed (threshold: <= 1 per sentence average)
- [ ] Read-aloud test applied as final conversational quality check
- [ ] Category distribution computed to identify systemic vs. scattered issues
- [ ] Remediation notes are SPECIFIC ("replace X with Y") not vague ("make it better")

---

## Anti-Patterns

- **Severity deflation:** Marking over-explanation as MODERATE instead of CRITICAL because "it's only one sentence" — even one sentence of mechanism explanation in the lead is a structural violation
- **Acceptable-use confusion:** Failing to distinguish between a statistic used as a hook device (acceptable) and a statistic used as proof (violation) — context determines the category
- **Style-over-substance scoring:** Passing copy that sounds smooth but is full of empty intensifiers and marketing buzzwords — fluent slop is still slop
- **Generic remediation:** Writing "improve this section" instead of "replace 'revolutionary breakthrough' with a specific claim about what makes the mechanism different from [competitor approach]"
- **Ignoring the conversational bar:** Passing copy that reads well on paper but sounds artificial spoken aloud — the lead must sound like a conversation, not a brochure
- **Credibility vs. proof confusion:** Flagging brief credibility insertions ("developed by a Harvard physician") as proof leakage — credibility MENTIONS are allowed; proof DEPLOYMENTS are not
