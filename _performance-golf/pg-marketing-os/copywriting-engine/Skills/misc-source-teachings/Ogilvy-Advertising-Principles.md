## CORE PHILOSOPHY

> "The consumer isn't a moron; she's your wife. You insult her intelligence if you assume that a mere slogan and a few vapid adjectives will persuade her to buy anything."

> "I do not regard advertising as entertainment or an art form, but as a medium of information. When I write an advertisement, I don't want you to tell me that you find it 'creative.' I want you to find it so interesting that you buy the product."

## THE HEADLINE DOCTRINE

### The 80/20 Rule of Headlines

> "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."

### Ogilvy's Favorite Headline

> "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in the New Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."

**Why it works:**
- No adjectives - pure factual claim
- Specific detail (60 mph, electric clock)
- Implies quality through concrete observation
- Found in actual technical documentation (he spent 3 weeks researching)
- Creates vivid mental image

### Headline Principles

1. **Specificity over superlatives** — Facts fascinate, adjectives bore
2. **The "ticket on the meat"** — Headlines must flag down specific prospects
3. **Write 20 headlines minimum** — Never select without testing
4. **Test before choosing** — Use split-runs and colleague opinions
5. **Never tell the whole story** — Create reason to read more

## OGILVY'S 10 WRITING RULES (1982 Internal Memo)

From an internal memo titled "How to Write" sent to all Ogilvy & Mather employees:

### The Rules

1. **Read the Roman-Raphaelson book on writing. Read it three times.**
   - "Writing That Works" - absorb excellent voice, tone, style
   - Good writing is the mark of good reading habits

2. **Write the way you talk. Naturally.**
   - Down-to-earth, relaxed tone
   - Good writing is everyday and casual

3. **Use short words, short sentences, short paragraphs.**
   - Reading takes energy - limited resource
   - Good writing spits it out

4. **Never use jargon words like reconceptualize, demassification, attitudinally, judgmentally. They are hallmarks of a pretentious ass.**
   - Big words make writers sound pretentious
   - Risk confusing readers and disconnecting them
   - Good writing is straightforward

5. **Never write more than two pages on any subject.**
   - Never write more than necessary
   - If you can shorten without losing meaning, do it
   - Good writing is light reading

6. **Check your quotations.**
   - Writers must be honest and trustworthy
   - Double-check facts - readers rely on you
   - Good writing is honest

7. **Never send a letter or a memo on the day you write it. Read it aloud the next morning—and then edit it.**
   - Writing isn't perfect on first try
   - Sleep on it, return with fresh eyes
   - Good writing is clear

8. **If it is something important, get a colleague to improve it.**
   - Your writing speaks for you long after you part with it
   - Every word counts toward credibility
   - Good writing needs a trusty editor

9. **Before you send your letter or memo, make sure it is crystal clear what you want the recipient to do.**
   - Every piece needs a goal
   - Nobody wants to read a dead-end message
   - Good writing has a purpose

10. **If you want ACTION, don't write. Go and tell the guy what you want.**
    - Nothing beats face-to-face interaction
    - Emotions influence decisions
    - Sometimes it takes more than good writing

## THE 7 OGILVY LAWS

### Law 1: Research Beats Opinions

> "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals."

- Ogilvy didn't guess; he researched
- When working on food brands, visited kitchens to watch people cook
- Hunt for small truths and real habits
- Speak the customer's language - trust is instant when they see their own thoughts reflected

### Law 2: The Power of One Big Promise

- Every great ad makes ONE clear promise
- The brain prefers simple goals and fast decisions
- One promise is a lighthouse; ten features create fog
- Example: Dove's "One-quarter moisturizing cream" - single promise that set product apart for decades

### Law 3: Headlines Do Most of the Work

> "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy."

- If your headline fails, your ad fails
- Email subject line, YouTube thumbnail, ad header = all headlines
- Two triggers: clarity and curiosity
- Write 20 headlines before you pick one

### Law 4: Specifics Sell (The Concreteness Effect)

> "Vague claims slide off the brain like water off a duck's back. Specifics stick."

- Don't say "great service" — say "243 support tickets answered in under 5 minutes last week"
- Adding numbers, dates, and names drops doubt fast
- Specifics act like street names on a map - without them, the customer feels lost
- The Rolls-Royce headline: found in technical reports, not invented

### Law 5: Show, Don't Just Tell

- Pictures beat paragraphs, especially in the first 3 seconds
- Visuals must tell a story or lend authority
- Open with "show and tell" - show problem being solved immediately
- The image must sell the product, not just decorate the page

### Law 6: Long Copy Works for Complex Sales

> "The more informative your advertising, the more persuasive it will be."

- People read what interests them
- Long copy necessary when product is expensive or complex
- Buyers switch to slow thinking when money is involved
- They want details on price, time, and trust

### Law 7: Consistency and Testing Compounds Success

> "Never stop testing, and your advertising will never stop improving."

> "The most important word in the vocabulary of advertising is TEST."

- Every ad should contribute to long-term brand image
- Consistency builds memory; testing drives performance
- Test relentlessly - new headline this week, new image next week
- Keep the winner, drop the loser

## OGILVY'S 12 COPYWRITING HABITS

From a 1955 letter where Ogilvy described his personal process:

1. **Never write in the office** — Too many interruptions. All writing at home.

2. **Study the precedents** — Look at every ad for competing products during the past 20 years.

3. **Helpless without research material** — "The more 'motivational' the better."

4. **Write out problem definition and purpose** — Go no further until client accepts.

5. **Before writing, list every conceivable fact and selling idea** — Organize and relate to research and copy platform.

6. **Write 20 alternative headlines** — Never select final headline without asking others' opinions.

7. **Entirely without ideas when starting** — Gets bad-tempered. Growls at wife.

8. **Terrified of producing a lousy advertisement** — Throws away first 20 attempts.

9. **If all else fails** — "I drink half a bottle of rum and play a Handel oratorio on the gramophone. This generally produces an uncontrollable gush of copy."

10. **Next morning, edit the gush** — Get up early.

11. **Have secretary type a draft** — "I cannot type, which is very inconvenient."

12. **Self-edit aggressively** — "I am a lousy copywriter, but I am a good editor. After four or five editings, it looks good enough to show the client."

### The Key Insight

> "I am a lousy copywriter, but I am a good editor."

Great copy comes from relentless editing, not natural talent.

## SIGNATURE CAMPAIGNS & TECHNIQUES

### The Hathaway Man (Eye Patch)

- Created "story appeal" with a mysterious eye patch on a model
- Turned ordinary shirt ads into intriguing narratives
- Demonstrated: A visual hook creates curiosity that headlines alone cannot

### Rolls-Royce "Electric Clock"

- Three weeks of research before writing
- Found the detail in technical documentation
- No adjectives, just irrefutable fact
- Specificity creates believability

### Schweppes Commander Whitehead

- Used real company executive as spokesperson
- Built brand character through consistent personality
- Long-running campaign that compounded brand equity

### Dove "1/4 Moisturizing Cream"

- Single promise, repeated consistently
- Set product apart for decades
- Demonstration of "one big promise" principle

## ARENA APPLICATION NOTES

### When to Invoke Ogilvy Perspective

1. **Credibility Building** — When claims need factual support and authority
2. **Headline Evaluation** — When assessing attention-grabbing power with substance
3. **Research Grounding** — When checking if copy reflects actual customer language
4. **Clarity Assessment** — When evaluating if message respects reader intelligence
5. **Specificity Checking** — When vague claims need concrete proof points

### Ogilvy Judging Lens

When evaluating candidates through Ogilvy's lens, check:

- **Credibility Score (0-10):** Does this feel trustworthy to an intelligent reader?
- **Specificity Score (0-10):** Are claims backed by concrete facts, numbers, details?
- **Clarity Score (0-10):** Is the message immediately clear?
- **Research Grounding:** Does this reflect actual customer language and concerns?
- **Headline Quality:** Does the headline work as "80 cents of the dollar"?
- **Intelligence Respect:** Does this treat the reader as smart (not a moron)?

### Key Distinction from Other Personas

Ogilvy is uniquely **credibility-first and research-grounded**. While Halbert emphasizes entertainment and Makepeace emphasizes flow, Ogilvy demands that every claim be:
1. Based on actual research
2. Expressed in specific, factual terms
3. Respectful of reader intelligence
4. Clear and honest

> "The consumer isn't a moron; she's your wife."

This standard makes Ogilvy essential for sophisticated audiences and markets where trust is paramount.
