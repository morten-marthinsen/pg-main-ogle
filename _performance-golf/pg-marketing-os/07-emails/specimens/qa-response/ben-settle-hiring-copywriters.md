## Full Email Text

"Email Players" subscriber Chris Orzechowski askeths:

=====

Hey Ben,

I'm an Email Players subscriber and I don't usually ask a lot of
questions, but there was something I wanted to get your opinion on.

Before I ask my question let me just say - I've been following your
advice from your newsletter and podcast and I've started to make
money as an email copywriter. I really think things started taking
off for me when I became a subscriber. I guess it's because I
finally had some skin in the game. So thanks for that.

Anyway

Question:

There was something that stuck out to me. You said that if you
would ever go back to traditional freelancing you would do emails
only. You said you'd have a few clients that paid you like 3-5k and
5-10% of sales every month just to write emails.

Now, obviously you personally would charge a lot more being that
you're on the top of the mountain.

My question is what kind of companies should I target if I wanted
to work out a deal like that? Maybe this is a stupid question - I
dont know.

I was thinking 7 or 8 figure info publishers and ecommerce
businesses. I feel like they would need emails the most and would
probably recognize their need for it.

Obviously every business could benefit from using email, but in
your own experience what do you think would be the best kind of
businesses to target for these bigger freelance email retainer
deals?

=====

<!-- [BODY — Ben's response + expanded argument] -->
Answer:

Target companies *already* aggressively using emails.

I am not a big fan of going after companies and trying to
"convince" them why direct marketing, copywriting, email marketing,
etc is a good investment and please hire me, pwetty pwease with
sugar on top.

Nah-boo.

Go after companies *already* hiring writers.

They're already sending lots of mail, running ads, paying for it,
testing, and know their numbers. Chances are they're hungry for
copywriters, especially good ones who can add another percentage or
two to their response.

<!-- [BRIDGE — Universal principle + product connection] -->
This goes for selling most anything.

Do thou what Jesus did:

Go to those in need of a physician (so to speak).

Not the already healthy ones.

Way easier selling aspirin to someone suffering from a migraine
than trying to convince someone without pain why they might need
aspirin the future.

## Structural Analysis

1. **Named subscriber with future significance** — Chris Orzechowski went on to become a well-known email copywriter. Ben publishes the question from a then-unknown subscriber, which retrospectively validates the advice. (This is accidental but instructive: featuring real subscribers by name creates a network effect.)

2. **Extended question display** — the subscriber's question runs 160+ words. Ben lets it breathe. This is the "let them talk" variant of QA: the longer the question, the more the reader identifies with the asker's situation. Many QA emails truncate too quickly.

3. **Embedded testimonial in the question** — "I've started to make money as an email copywriter. I really think things started taking off for me when I became a subscriber." The subscriber's own preamble does selling work Ben never has to do himself.

4. **"Askeths" — archaic verb conjugation** — a signature Settle move: injecting mock-formal language for comedic personality. One word signals this is not a boring FAQ.

5. **Single-sentence answer** — "Target companies *already* aggressively using emails." The answer is one line. After 160 words of question, the 8-word answer creates dramatic contrast. The asterisks around "already" add emphasis that would be italics in rendering.

6. **Negative framing reinforces the positive** — "I am not a big fan of going after companies and trying to 'convince' them" — stating what NOT to do before restating what TO do. The "pwetty pwease with sugar on top" mockery makes the wrong approach feel pathetic.

7. **Principle elevation** — "This goes for selling most anything." One sentence transitions from specific (targeting email clients) to universal (targeting anyone already buying). The QA body type's extraction move: specific question in, universal principle out.

8. **Biblical analogy** — "Do thou what Jesus did: Go to those in need of a physician" — unexpected reference that elevates a tactical tip into timeless wisdom. The parenthetical "(so to speak)" prevents it from feeling preachy.

9. **Aspirin/migraine analogy** — the closing analogy is vivid, specific, and instantly understood. "Selling aspirin to someone suffering from a migraine" vs. "convince someone without pain why they might need aspirin the future." This is the principle in picture form: the reader will remember the image long after they forget the email.

10. **Minimal CTA** — just two lines. No urgency, no deadline, no tease of next issue. The content was so strong and the principle so clean that Ben lets the email end on the lesson. The CTA is almost an afterthought — which paradoxically makes it feel more trustworthy. Not every QA email needs a hard bridge.
