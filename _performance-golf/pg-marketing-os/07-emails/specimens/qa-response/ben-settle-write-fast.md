"Email Players" subscriber Alejandro C. asks about fast writing:

"Do you now what I'd pay for? A crash course on getting faster my email writing efforts.
It often takes me up to an hour to get an email done: editing and sending included. Sometimes more."

<!-- [BODY — Ben's response + expanded argument] -->
My command is your wish.

Checky:

I once read an interview with Neil Gaiman who is easily one of the world's most respected and prolific writers (comic books, novels, short stories, screenplays, etc). He said his high school guidance counselor asked him what he wanted to be when he grew up. Neil told him he wanted to write American comic books. When he realized school was all but useless for this, he decided to start working in publishing to get experience writing. So he got on the phone, and by the end of the day sold two newspaper articles he hadn't yet written.

Then, he said:

(Paraphrased)

"It's good for any would-be writer to write a couple hundred thousands words under tight deadlines"

More:

At the end of 2010 (the week between Christmas and New Years) I made a goal to be client-free by the end of 2011. The first thing I tried was creating a small info publishing business based heavily on SEO that required me writing over 1,000 ezine articles, 400 or so unique blog posts, and about 90 or so email auto-responder messages. Plus, I was writing all the emails and copy on retainer for a biz-opp company, I was also writing my own daily emails, writing my old monthly Crypto Marketing Newsletter, and some other things.

For a month and a half I barely slept.

I wrote morning, noon, and night.

And, while that business flopped (Google decided to slap article directories right when sales were starting to come in — Yay!), on a scale of 1-10 my writing speed went to 12.

<!-- [BRIDGE — Universal principle + product connection] -->
Thus, the free secret to fast writing:

Writing lots of words under intense deadline pressure.

It ain't secksy, but it's got teeth.

Just like everything I teach in "Email Players", not-so-coincidentally.

## Structural Analysis

1. **Attribution format** — "Email Players subscriber Alejandro C. asks about fast writing:" — names the subscriber, identifies them as a paying customer (social proof), and frames the topic in a single line before displaying the question.

2. **Question display is relatable** — "It often takes me up to an hour to get an email done" — nearly every reader has felt this pain. The question selection is strategic: it surfaces a universal struggle.

3. **Inverted cliche opener** — "My command is your wish" (flipping "your wish is my command") — a micro-personality injection that signals irreverence before the actual content begins.

4. **Dual-anecdote structure** — First anecdote (Neil Gaiman) provides borrowed authority. Second anecdote (Ben's own SEO sprint) provides personal proof. Together they triangulate the same principle from two independent sources.

5. **Credential embedding for authority figure** — "easily one of the world's most respected and prolific writers (comic books, novels, short stories, screenplays, etc)" — parenthetical credential list establishes why Gaiman's opinion matters, done in one breath.

6. **Specific numbers create credibility** — "over 1,000 ezine articles, 400 or so unique blog posts, and about 90 or so email auto-responder messages" — the granularity makes the story unchallengeable. Vague claims invite skepticism; specific ones invite belief.

7. **Vulnerability through failure** — "while that business flopped (Google decided to slap article directories)" — admitting the venture failed makes the lesson MORE credible, not less. The principle survived the failure of the vehicle.

8. **Escalating rhythm in the personal story** — three short sentences build momentum: "For a month and a half I barely slept. / I wrote morning, noon, and night. / And, while that business flopped..."

9. **Principle extraction is blunt** — "Writing lots of words under intense deadline pressure." One sentence. No hedging. No qualifiers. This is the QA body type at its cleanest: question asked, principle delivered.

10. **Bridge is a single analogy** — "It ain't secksy, but it's got teeth. Just like everything I teach in 'Email Players', not-so-coincidentally." The bridge from principle to product is exactly two sentences. No elaborate transition needed because the content already demonstrated the value.
