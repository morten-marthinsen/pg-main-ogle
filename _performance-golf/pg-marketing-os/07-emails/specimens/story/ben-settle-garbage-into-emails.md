## Full Email Text

<!-- [OPENING — Story opener with challenge setup] -->
Once upon a time, I was challenged to turn anything into an email.

On the spot.

Without time to think, plan, or even so much as ponder.

<!-- [BODY — Story narrative with dialogue, specific details, demonstration] -->
The copywriter I was hanging with and I were at a McDonalds at the time and they said, "okay smart ass… turn that piece of trash on the ground into an email for this client!" This person's client had a salon mall, and his emails were selling hair stylists on renting a space there.

Amused at the challenge, I said:

"Easy, write an email about how you were in McDonalds and saw some trash on the ground, which made you think twice about eating there. And it's the same with salons. You might be shocked how much salon business is lost by something as simple as a piece of trash tucked away in a corner. At our salon mall we have cleanliness standards… yada yada yada…"

Anyway, you get the idea.

Very easy to do, if you train your mind for it.

<!-- [BRIDGE — Second challenge story + product connection] -->
And guess what:

I recently wrote an email for a Golf business I am a partner in where I was given a similar challenge to turn something (a disturbing, taboo topic, of sorts) that, at a glance, has nothing whatsoever to do with golf, golfers, or even swinging a stick, on the spot, under the same "pressure."

It's not run yet, so I can't say how well it'll do.

But, it's in the back of the August "Email Players" issue.

And, just reading it can give you an idea of how to do this for any market, using ideas that are bold, original, and that your market hasn't seen before (certainly not from the gaggle of swipers laying in wait who haven't had an original thought since conception). If you're wise enough to subscribe before it goes to the printer in time to get it… or if you are already subscribed… don't copy it obviously.

Only dumbos do that.

Instead, let it influence your thinking.

Expand your mind.

And, get you thinking of email at a deeper level than anyone you compete against — which is not that hard to do anymore.

## Structural Analysis

1. **"Once upon a time" story marker** — Classic fairy-tale opener immediately signals ST body type. The ironic contrast between fairy-tale language and a McDonalds challenge creates Settle's signature irreverent tone.

2. **Staccato pacing in opening** — Three short fragments after the opener: "On the spot." / "Without time to think, plan, or even so much as ponder." Each fragment escalates the stakes. This is pure pacing craft — the reader feels the time pressure.

3. **Dialogue as story engine** — "okay smart ass... turn that piece of trash on the ground into an email for this client!" The dialogue is informal, profane, and real. It sounds like two copywriters actually talking. No cleaned-up, professional-sounding quotes.

4. **Demonstration-as-proof** — Settle doesn't just claim he can turn anything into an email. He demonstrates it live inside the story, improvising the trash-to-salon email on the spot. The reader watches the skill in action. The story IS the proof.

5. **Specific context for credibility** — "This person's client had a salon mall, and his emails were selling hair stylists on renting a space there." The specificity (salon mall, hair stylists, renting space) makes the scenario feel real and eliminates the "this sounds made up" objection.

6. **Throwaway lesson** — "Very easy to do, if you train your mind for it." This is the implicit lesson — not dressed up, not belabored. One sentence. The story already demonstrated the principle; this sentence just names it.

7. **"And guess what:" bridge marker** — This is one of Settle's primary ST bridge phrases, signaling the transition from story to product. It creates a "there's more to this" curiosity loop.

8. **Second story as product tease** — The golf email challenge mirrors the first story but withholds the details. "A disturbing, taboo topic, of sorts" creates curiosity. "It's in the back of the August Email Players issue" turns the curiosity into a purchase driver.

9. **Honesty about unproven results** — "It's not run yet, so I can't say how well it'll do." This admission of uncertainty is counter-intuitive in a sales email but massively increases credibility. Settle sells the LEARNING value, not the result.

10. **Anti-swiper positioning** — "certainly not from the gaggle of swipers laying in wait who haven't had an original thought since conception" and "don't copy it obviously. Only dumbos do that." These lines position Email Players subscribers as thinkers, not copiers. The reader self-selects into the "smart" camp by buying.

11. **Elevated reframe in close** — "get you thinking of email at a deeper level than anyone you compete against" — elevates the product from tactical ("get email templates") to strategic ("think differently about email"). The dash qualifier "which is not that hard to do anymore" adds competitive motivation.

### Key Techniques
- **Demonstration-as-proof** — The story shows the skill in action rather than claiming it.
- **Parallel story structure** — First challenge (McDonalds) demonstrated openly; second challenge (golf) withheld as product tease.
- **"And guess what:" explicit bridge marker** — Clean transition from narrative to sell.
- **Honesty about unproven results** — Builds credibility by admitting what he doesn't know.
- **Anti-swiper tribe positioning** — Makes buying an identity statement, not a transaction.
- **Staccato pacing** — Short fragments create urgency and energy in the opening.
