Once upon a time I got this amusing opt-out:


====

Dude I have no idea what this letter is about nor do I care do not send me anymore emails or scam letters anymore I am not interested. THAN OR NOW!!!!!!!! LOOSE MY EMAIL ADDRESS. ........DO NOT SEND ME ANYTHING ELSE. EVER!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!

<!-- [BODY — Forensic investigation: tracing the troll's origin] -->

The lad sounds a tad too tightly wound for his own good.

Usually I just delete these.

But, in this case, I wanted to track where he came from.

And, sure enough, he came from Pay-per-click where we don't use double opt in. Plus, he used a free yahoo address. That probably means he joined just to "get the free stuff" (i.e. the bribe to join my list), then probably just checked his email again for the first time after almost a month, saw a few dozen emails from me piled up, and proceeded to go into full-on nutzo mode.

<!-- [BODY — Irony layering: who's actually spamming whom?] -->

It's ironic, too.

Why?

Because emails like the above are the real "spam."

Guy opts into my list willingly and on his own volition, then sends me unwanted shrieking complaints (some of it in ALL CAPS which means he's serious) about getting emails he asked me to send him.

Even more ironic:

I not only say "you'll be getting daily promotional emails" at the opt-in box (clearly, so the Google AdWords powers-that-be see it) but you also have to check a box acknowledging you read the terms before my form lets you opt-in, and the first email with the bribe link outright says I mail a lot, etc and how to opt-out if that's going to give someone acid reflux.

<!-- [BRIDGE — Lesson extraction + tactical advice] -->

Anyway, I ain't mad at the guy..

It's probably not his fault he's stupid.

But if you're going to email using my system, best brace for similar emails.

Especially if you use paid traffic and single opt-in.

(If you don't occasionally get these, you ain't trying hard enough, Bub.)

## Structural Analysis

### Body Type Pattern: NR (Negative Response)
1. **Troll Display (lines 1-8):** ALL-CAPS unsubscribe rant with excessive exclamation marks and a spelling error ("LOOSE" for "LOSE") — the formatting of the complaint itself is the comedy; Ben doesn't need to editorialize much
2. **Forensic Investigation (lines 10-16):** Rather than mocking directly, Ben traces the subscriber's origin: PPC traffic, no double opt-in, free Yahoo address, joined for the freebie, disappeared for a month, returned to a full inbox. This detective work transforms random rage into an explainable, predictable pattern
3. **Irony Reversal (lines 18-28):** Ben flips the "spam" accusation — the troll's unsolicited, unwanted shrieking complaint is the actual spam, sent to someone who never asked for it. Then stacks a second irony: the opt-in page explicitly says "daily promotional emails," requires a checkbox acknowledgment, and the first email repeats the frequency warning
4. **Compassionate Dismissal + Tactical Lesson (lines 30-36):** "It's probably not his fault he's stupid" — the title line lands here with mock sympathy. Then pivots to a real tactical lesson: if you use paid traffic and single opt-in, expect these emails. The parenthetical "(If you don't occasionally get these, you ain't trying hard enough, Bub)" reframes hate mail as a success indicator
5. **Minimal CTA (lines 38-42):** Clean, unadorned link — no pitch setup needed because the entire email IS the pitch for Ben's email system

### Key Techniques
- **Forensic structure as demolition** — instead of emotional response, Ben does cold analysis: traces origin, reconstructs behavior, explains the pattern. This is more devastating than mockery because it reduces the troll to a predictable data point
- **Double irony stacking** — first irony (troll's complaint is the real spam) + second irony (explicit disclosure was already provided at every stage). Each layer makes the troll look more unreasonable
- **Troll-as-inevitability framing** — positions hate mail not as something to fear but as a natural byproduct of aggressive email marketing with paid traffic. This normalizes it for the reader and removes the emotional sting
- **Success-indicator reframe** — "(If you don't occasionally get these, you ain't trying hard enough, Bub)" transforms the negative into a benchmark metric. Readers who receive similar complaints should feel validated, not discouraged
- **Mock sympathy as punchline** — "It's probably not his fault he's stupid" lands perfectly because the forensic analysis that precedes it actually IS sympathetic in structure (explaining WHY the person reacted this way), making the line both funny and oddly generous
- **Implicit system selling** — the entire email demonstrates the Ben Settle email approach (aggressive, daily, polarizing, paid traffic) without once describing or pitching it. The Email Players link at the end needs no setup because the reader just watched the system in action
- **Tactical specificity** — "Pay-per-click... single opt-in... free yahoo address" gives the reader a concrete subscriber profile to watch for, making the lesson immediately actionable
