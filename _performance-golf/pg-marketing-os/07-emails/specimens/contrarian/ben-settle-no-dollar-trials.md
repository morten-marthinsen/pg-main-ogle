## Full Email Text

<!-- [OPENING -- Reader question quoted as the conventional wisdom setup] -->
The question that lops into the cue every now and then:

"Ben, have you ever thought
about offering a $1 trial into your
email players continuity?"


For about .0007 seconds I considered it.

<!-- [BODY -- Two-part dismissal: logical + analogical] -->
First, you already get a free trial.

When you opt in to my list, you get the first issue (in pdf, of course, not print). So a $1 trial is kinda irrelevant at that point. Especially since I pretty much only pitch "Email Players" 95% of the time to my list anyway.

Secondly:

I don't like the price shopper mentality $1 trials attract.

It's a great offer for software, etc.

But not something like "Email Players".

I'm no interested in offering a $1 trial to my high quality content than Rolls Royce is in offering its high end customers a $1 trial. Like with Rolls Royce, you get a test drive (i.e. the opt in issue I give away). And while on that drive, you can go ye forth and use my test drive to make all the sales you want with.

(And many people do.)

If you apply it and it doesn't work, then I just saved you some dough.

<!-- [BRIDGE -- Value vs price shoppers framework] -->
Bottom line?

I want value shoppers not price shoppers.

Value shoppers play to win.

Price shoppers play to not lose -- dipping their pinky toes in the water so they can decide if they want to stick with it vs chasing after some other get rych quick BSO that catches their eye.

<!-- [CTA -- Clean, no-fanfare link] -->
All right, that's it for today.

"Email Players" subscription info here:

http://www.EmailPlayers.com

Ben Settle

## Structural Analysis
- **Conventional Wisdom Challenged:** "$1 trial offers are smart business because they lower the barrier to entry and get more people in the door" -- the widely-accepted SaaS/continuity pricing tactic applied to information products.
- **Reframe Device:** Luxury brand analogy -- Rolls Royce doesn't offer $1 trials; it offers test drives. The analogy elevates the product category from "subscription to try" to "luxury purchase to commit to." The secondary reframe is the buyer psychology split: $1 trials attract price shoppers who "play to not lose" rather than value shoppers who "play to win."
- **Lesson Extraction:** $1 trials attract the wrong buyer psychology -- people who are hedging, not committing. A free sample (the opt-in issue) already serves the try-before-you-buy function. The $1 trial adds nothing except lower-quality subscribers.
- **Bridge Type:** Framework bridge -- "I want value shoppers not price shoppers" creates a dichotomy that the reader self-sorts into. Nobody wants to be a price shopper who "dips their pinky toes in." The framework itself does the selling by making the reader identify as the kind of person who commits.
- **Key Structural Move:** The Rolls Royce analogy does double duty -- it dismantles the $1 trial objection AND elevates the product's perceived value in the same breath. The reader walks away thinking Email Players is in the Rolls Royce category, not the SaaS-trial category. Positioning through analogy rather than direct claim.
