To top it off, I followed your "last day affiliate promo" strategy from EP Issue #74, and brought in $22,500 in the final 24 hours - which was more than ANY other day in the entire 18 days we promoted the product.

Pretty amazing, compared to other clients I've written packages for who expect to receive hate mail on a daily basis ;-)

Anyways, I hope this warms your cold, black little heart.

## Structural Analysis

1. **Sensory metaphor in attribution** — "gets a whiff of the secrets inside the newsletter, implements it, and shows what an ultra-successful email campaign smells like" — Ben extends the subject line's olfactory metaphor through the setup. This is the "themed attribution" variant where the setup line carries the email's creative concept.

2. **Case study testimonial subtype** — Unlike emotional/gratitude testimonials, this one reads like a campaign debrief: timeline (18 days), cadence (2x daily), revenue range ($1k-$7k daily), peak ($22,500 final day), list size (under 6,000). The subscriber provides enough data to make the testimonial function as implicit proof of the method.

3. **Specific issue reference** — "I followed your 'last day affiliate promo' strategy from EP Issue #74" — this does two things: (a) proves the subscriber is a real, long-term reader who can cite specific issues, and (b) makes the product feel like a library of discrete, implementable strategies rather than generic advice.

4. **Counterintuitive result** — "We didn't get a single complaint on the last day (even though I mailed 8 or 9 times)." This directly contradicts what most readers believe about email frequency. The subscriber even frames it as correcting Ben ("Only thing you were wrong about..."), which adds authenticity — a testimonial that pushes back on the guru feels more real than pure praise.

5. **Causal attribution** — "I really do believe that years of sending daily emails with the clear voice/personality created such a deep enough bond with our audience over time" — the subscriber doesn't just report results; he explains WHY the method worked. This transforms the testimonial from proof into education.

6. **List size anchor** — "All this from a list of under 6,000 subscribers." One line that handles the "I don't have a big list" objection. It also makes the per-subscriber revenue calculable (~$15-20/subscriber over 18 days), which is extraordinary.

7. **Ben's voice in the testimonial's framing** — The subscriber writes in a casual, Ben-like voice ("tomfoolery you're always ranting about," "warms your cold, black little heart"). This meta-demonstrates that reading Ben's emails trains you to write like Ben — which is itself a selling point.

8. **Two-sentence bridge** — "Sheesh. It's almost like this stuff works." — deadpan understatement after $22,500-in-24-hours. The sarcasm IS the commentary. No lesson extraction needed.

9. **Immediate deadline** — "I'm sending it to the printer tomorrow." — the tightest possible urgency. Not "soon," not "next week." Tomorrow.

10. **Key technique: The "case study" testimonial that embeds operational data.** When the subscriber provides campaign timelines, daily revenue ranges, frequency experiments, and list sizes, the testimonial becomes both proof AND tutorial. The reader thinks "I could do this" because they can see the exact inputs and outputs. This is the highest-conversion TM variant because it collapses the gap between "believe" and "implement."
