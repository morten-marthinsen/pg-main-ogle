1. The late Jim Camp (called the world's "most feared negotiator", and for good reason, *everything* he taught can make you more sales, and in a way people love to buy from)

Then, you have to write more copy to deal with those objections.

Not so with my email methods.

I'm not saying you'll get zero objections. But, they're way fewer than following the standard ways of writing copy everyone has been indoctrinated with over the years.

It's simple.

It works like crazy.

And, it's easily one of the most profitable lessons I've ever taught.

## Structural Analysis
- **List Type:** Annotated authority/resource list (curated recommendations with commentary)
- **List Count:** 4 items (plus a 2-item preamble list)
- **List Device:** Numbered (1-4), with extended parenthetical annotations per item
- **Opening Hook:** Contrarian premise -- "I don't study copywriters anymore." The subject line's quotation marks around "email" signal that these are NOT email teachers, creating curiosity about who they actually are.
- **Bridge Type:** Synthesis bridge -- extracts the shared principle from all 4 list items (they sell without creating objections), then connects that principle directly to what the current newsletter issue teaches. This is not a generic "buy my stuff" bridge; it logically extends the list's insight into a product benefit.
- **Key Structural Move:** The nested list structure. The email opens with a 2-item meta-list (salesmen, biographies), narrows to the first category, then delivers the 4-item main list. This creates a "zooming in" effect -- broad category to specific recommendations -- that makes the reader feel like they are getting progressively deeper access to Settle's personal knowledge hierarchy. The parenthetical annotations do heavy lifting: each one is a mini-fascination that could stand alone as its own email hook.
