**Headline:** How to make successful television commercials

**Byline:** by Ogilvy & Mather

Ogilvy & Mather has placed every $900,000,000 in television advertising. We have produced thousands of successful television campaigns for our clients in the United States and abroad. We have been studying what makes some of these campaigns more profitable than others. Here are 18 tips that we have learned.

**P**eople don't watch television in order to see commercials. The commercial must attract the viewer's attention. Yet few packaged-goods are more interesting than the television programs that surround the commercials.

What follows are some ways you can get the viewer to watch your commercial instead of going to the refrigerator.

Your most important decision. What you say is more important than how you say it. Nothing is more important than what is in the commercial.

Should you position Dove as a product for dry skin? Or as a product which cleans deep down in your pores? Should you position Dove as a product which creates sensitive skin?

The techniques that work

**The brand image.** Once you have determined the most effective positioning, you must stick to it. All the great commercials owe their greatness to a clear positioning, executed with consistency over many years.

You must find a way to stand out from the 30,000 other commercials run on television each year in the United States.

The best commercials contain groups of people talking to each other about your product and how well it works.

You would have to expect to eat a lot of dull meals if the television campaign for Mercedes-Benz Bacon were merely an announcer reading copy.

Here are some techniques that we have found to be more successful than others:

**1. Problem-solution.** Television permits you to make dramatic demonstrations of the solutions to people's problems, not just important, but as important as ever after all these years.

**2. Humor.** Because of the growing skepticism of commercial claims, more and more people now accept humorous messages. If the humor is related to the selling idea, it can be very effective.

**3.** "Slice of Life." The "Slice of Life" technique is the most successful technique in television advertising, representing about one out of every three commercials on the air. The name "Slice of Life" has become the trade name for commercials showing real people in real-life situations. People identify with them.

**"Merrill Lynch is bullish on America."**

**4.** A continuing presenter. If you are asking people to buy your product, not just to applaud your commercial, it helps to use a presenter, and to use that presenter in every commercial. Four years ago Ogilvy & Mather discovered the man in New York.

**5.** The continuing character or characters groups or cartoons or puppets become uniquely identified with your product. The Hathaway man is a famous example.

**6.** The problem of attention. Television permits you to make dramatic demonstrations of solutions to people's problems.

**7.** Unless there is a real difference product quality, the brand with the most consistent, most likeable brand personality will get the largest market share.

**8.** "Slice of Life." The single most popular format for television. As Ann Tolbert, the former head of the Ogilvy & Mather Television department, says, "If done right, it can be one of the most effective television formats."

**9. Avoid the irrelevant.** Don't start your commercial with an attention-getting but irrelevant gimmick.

Ogilvy & Mather uses a Clutter Reel — a simulated batch of commercials — to test whether your commercial can hold attention in the environment in which it will be shown.

Questions from a Clutter test are measured for recall, persuasion, ability to communicate the key idea, and overall consumer reaction.

**10.** The opening. The first five seconds of a commercial are critical. Avoid slow openings and extended build-ups. You have very little time to attract attention.

**11.** Music. Music is emotional shorthand. It establishes the mood more quickly than any other device. Music helps to trigger recall of a commercial.

**Demonstrations: how to get more for your money**

On the other hand, even the best demonstration can be boring unless it is made memorable. It is a fact of life that demonstrations are the most effective technique for many product categories. It is for this reason that we have studied successful television demonstrations more closely than other kinds of commercials. Here are some of the things we have found.

Mr. Whipple has proven to be one of the most successful commercial demonstrations on television.

**12.** The key visual. The more visual your commercial, the more effective it will be. "Slice of Life" technique is largely verbal. No matter what technique you use, a good visual is the most important element.

**13.** A new word about length. Until recently, a 30-second commercial was the standard unit in television. Now many advertisers are experimenting with 15-second spots. Tests show that a 15-second commercial has 60 to 75 percent of the communication power of a 30-second spot. Since a 15-second commercial costs about half as much as a 30-second commercial, you can run it more often and get much more visibility.

**How to test your commercials**

Before going on the air with a finished commercial, Ogilvy & Mather has developed techniques for testing commercials at every stage of production.

1. The storyboard. Ogilvy & Mather measures storyboard effectiveness, in both finished commercials and in rough form, against norms for comparable categories.

2. The "animatic." We have found that "animatics" — film on videotape versions of storyboards — can save both time and money. Making an animatic costs you as much as a rough commercial, but it can deliver better results. Your research can be based on a single exposure and cost less than a finished commercial.

3. Clutter tests. Questions from a Clutter test are measured for recall, persuasion, the key idea, and overall consumer reaction.

**The commercial is only as good as the product**

In the product itself you have the best demonstration of all. If the product sells, it can only be because the product really delivers on the promise the advertising makes.

The commercial is only as good as the product. No amount of advertising can sell a product that doesn't work.
