For over four years Ogilvy & Mather has been using a technique that reveals the relationship between advertising expenditure, market share and profit.

This technique uses a mathematical model developed by the Hendry Corporation, an independent group of management consultants.

Ogilvy & Mather is the only advertising agency licensed to use this tool.

The model can be used with equal validity for many products in many categories of consumer goods and services.

The information needed for the model is data that is readily available in your company — or data that you can readily assemble. It does not require elaborate market research, or a computer. It can be obtained quickly, and without great expense.

The technique is new. Some of our clients have used it to plan their advertising budgets.

The varying uses of the theory are recounted briefly in this report. We also provide some specific suggestions that might help you clarify your own thinking about advertising.

**What is the optimum spending level for advertising?**

Analysis for one of our clients showed that doubling his advertising would result in a 5% increase in market share and would produce a significant increase in profit.

**How much of your share of market do you owe to advertising?**

This is a question that seldom gets asked, but the answer should be known, especially for a major part of their share of market. But for some companies, the figure would be doubtful. For example, even if one leading cosmetics company were to cease advertising completely, its share of market would be only 6 percent in the absence of advertising.

On the other hand, there are situations in which advertising expenditure truly may represent a corporate investment. Whether the margins were high enough to make this a profitable exercise depends on the brand and the marketplace. To find out more, you have only to find out in dollars and cents what you are currently paying for your share of market.

**How can you best allocate your total advertising dollars among different brands or among different sizes of the same brand?**

Almost all manufacturers with a number of products determine advertising budgets by taking a percentage of sales. The effect of this is to spend heavily on brands which already have a big or growing share on some brands and less on others. This often means spending too much on your big brands — and too little on brands that could, given a little more investment, grow in share and profits. The model enables you to estimate when this is the case — and how to improve your profits.

**How can you best allocate your advertising budget among several brands?**

In the same way that brand shares can be attributed to advertising so can the advertising of individual brands. Budgets for advertising can also vary from product to product.

For one client, our analysis showed that one of our clients could realize $500,000 in additional profit by reallocating his advertising budget among brands.

The analysis showed him how to distribute his advertising spending far more efficiently. He was equally productive in each of his marketing efforts.
