## Analysis (from SwipeFile.com)

This vintage ad by David Ogilvy, titled "Confessions of a Magazine Reader," is a brilliant example of how to turn admiration into persuasion. Ogilvy doesn't just promote Reader's Digest -- he uses storytelling, credibility, and human insight to sell it without sounding like a salesman. It's subtle, intelligent marketing at its best.

### Why This Ad Still Works Today

- Ogilvy opens with personal authenticity -- he actually reads 34 magazines a month.
- He praises Reader's Digest by highlighting its values, not its features.
- His tone feels like a friendly recommendation, not a pitch.
- Every paragraph builds credibility and warmth, ending with a memorable personal endorsement.

## Key Specimen Value

- Personal testimony format: Ogilvy as the testimonial subject
- Admiration-as-persuasion: genuine enthusiasm is more convincing than any technique
- "Confessions" framing -- echoes his book title, creates intimacy
- Values-based selling: what the magazine stands for, not what it contains
- 34 magazines a month -- specific number creates credibility

**CONFESSIONS OF A MAGAZINE READER**
*by David Ogilvy*

[Ogilvy opens by stating he reads 34 magazines a month and explaining his relationship with Reader's Digest]

Some highbrows may look down on The Digest, charging it with superficiality and over-simplification. There is some justice in this charge — you can learn more about the Congo in Foreign Affairs Quarterly, or more about Abraham Lincoln in Carl Sandburg's book. But I seldom read a highbrow magazine without wishing that a Digest editor had worked his will upon it — to make it more readable. Digest articles are never long-winded. Never obscure. Never boring.

[Ogilvy discusses Reader's Digest crusades against cigarettes, billboards, boxing, and pornography]

[Ogilvy closes with personal endorsement of the magazine]
