## Headline

"Ellerton Jette owns 279 Hathaway Shirts"

## Context & Background

When it comes to Ogilvy's Hathaway ads, the first thing most people think of is the "Hathaway man with the eye patch". And in the 50s and 60s, most every one of their ads had that character. However, this one, published just a year after the original "The Man In the Hathaway Shirt" ad, featured the founder of Hathaway, Ellerton Jette.

If they mentioned he was the founder right away, the headline wouldn't have as much juice. But because we don't know who the man is initially, we're left wondering why he owns so many shirts.

The idea went very well with the intention of the ad, which was to sell different colored shirts. Note the polarizing view they take on white shirts in the middle of the body copy. Taking a more extreme stance made it a little bit controversial (exciting) and helped support their pitch for buying a variety of colored shirts.

## Key Specimen Value

- Specific number in headline (279) creates irresistible curiosity
- Delayed reveal: doesn't mention he's the founder until body copy
- Controversial opinion embedded: attacks white shirts to sell colored ones
- Social proof through founder's personal behavior: "if the maker owns 279, the product must be extraordinary"

## OCR Extracted Body Copy (2026-02-15)

**Ellerton Jette owns 279 Hathaway Shirts**

ELLERTON JETTE owns two hundred and seventy-nine HATHAWAY shirts, of all kinds. And 47 Hathaway ties as well. What makes this interesting is that almost all the shirts in Jette's personal wardrobe were made from exotic fabrics. You never saw a man who has more contempt for white shirts. A white shirt, says Jette, is about as personal as a paper napkin. He'd rather wear a sack than a white shirt. This may be going too far by afternoon; it gets sold at the collar and cuff. This feeling about white shirts has a profound effect on the shirts he makes. For Ellerton Jette, you see, is the President of the Hathaway Shirt Company.

Wearing a white shirt at the office is like wearing a uniform—a painful abdication of individuality. Clothes tell the world not only if the wearer is a person of taste, but also something about his personality. A man who has no desire to look exactly like everybody else in the room will turn instinctively to Hathaway. For no other shirtmaker puts as much imagination and creative intelligence into its designs. Not even the most expensive custom shirtmakers spend so much on making them. But yet the shirts sell at moderate prices—at fine stores everywhere.

Classic stripes, batistes, checks, plain colors—as much more interesting to make up every morning. To make every morning to be so practical! So much better today. Next time you set in the market for shirts, ask the salesman to show you a selection of Hathaway shirts—really the most distinguished shirts—really the most distinctive shirts in America. In New York, telephone MUrray Hill 6-4338.
