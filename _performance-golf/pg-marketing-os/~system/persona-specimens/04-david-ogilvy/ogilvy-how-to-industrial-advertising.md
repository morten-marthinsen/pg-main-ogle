## OCR Extracted Body Copy (2026-02-15)

Ogilvy & Mather has created industrial advertising for over 200 of our clients in 30 countries.

Here, with all the dogmatism of brevity, are some of the ways we have learned to do this more effectively.

**1. Use your best people.** Industrial advertising is a more difficult discipline than consumer advertising — there are more "reasons why" involved. Yet most agencies delegate it to their junior people. This results in more mediocre advertising than is necessary, wasting hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Ogilvy & Mather puts some of its best people to work on industrial accounts, and we attract good people who enjoy the stimulus of learning about complex products. Industrial advertising is more demanding than consumer advertising.

**2. Prove your case.** Advertising with a simple message — well proved — is preferred by industrial prospects. Hard-nosed buyers, now-or-lose business men, respond to industrial advertising which proves its case.

"Unique selling propositions" work remarkably well in industrial advertising. Boredom, they tell us, is the main reason buyers stop reading an industrial advertisement. Boredom is the natural enemy of good advertising.

"Chatsworth's 545,000 Barrel Cistern" — how a more-than-usual headline drew more than 300 responses.

"It Took L'Oeuf de Canard, Ecole Witzig, Marbella, and 41 others to Prove It." An important case history, directly responsible for a big increase in sales.

"Savings up to 16%." The headline is specific — it tells the reader what the advertiser has to offer. Dinner can also be reasonably effective. To demonstrate an advantage, one of our clients showed that two leading competitors had tested in their own labs supporting our product's superiority.

"$30,000 to $150,000 a year extra profit." A manufacturer was sure he could offer companies that used his equipment extra profits. His advertisements declared: "$30,000 to $150,000 a year extra profit." As a result, the company went from fifth to first in the field — and his weight was like ten times its normal weight. This ad drew 2,000 responses.

**3. Create a new use for a new material.** Great new industrial products — polymers, ceramics and composites — are being developed and marketed in the chemical and process industries. Many companies who make these materials turn to Ogilvy & Mather.

Ogilvy & Mather helped du Pont introduce the first commercial, mass produced polycarbonate plastics in the packaging industry — for polypropylene film for food packaging. The market grew rapidly.

We helped Shell Chemical Co. create awareness of packages that could be made of polypropylene.

We helped sell Dow Corning's new silicone, which was used primarily by women to coat their faces. It was usually used by a manufacturer who became a good example of industrial advertising at its best.

If you have developed a new material, make it available to **all** your product groups. Otherwise your privacy — or your laboratory director's self-interest — may be blocking innovation.

Ogilvy & Mather developed an overall format for presenting the newest information about these high-technology materials. Then created a "new use" for Corning, a recipe, nationwide symposium.

Here are some additional principles for presenting products. Here's an unusual campaign.

**"Set Yer an Expert on the Uncommon Called by This Cryptastic Quiz"**

Reprinted by a Shell engineer with a sense of humor, this unusual ad attracted a record number of inquiries.

**Ogilvy & Mather became a common source about long copy**

Ogilvy & Mather has pioneered some important research about business advertising. Not the least of this research shows that the vast majority of the top-scoring ads in industrial marketing have long copy.

*Here is a bad plan and the copy is all over the map:* (a) There is more selling going on in most industrial copy than most advertising people believe; copy actually tends to get read more thoroughly than consumer copy.

(b) If you write a 50- or 100-line ad, you can be sure that nobody can run off half the copy. Even if they do, you are no worse off for having tried. If your ad is interesting, you will be read.

(c) In our experience, industrial advertising no matter how long or short the copy, likes picture-caption format — illustrations with informative captions underneath. This is a significant format.

(d) Finally, read readers respond to long copy — not just general-interest readers. Industrial prospects responsible for spending large

**4. Make your advertisement a product guide** or compendium. Over the past six years, we and Shell Chemical have worked together to create a specialized editorial business-type of principle and visual that makes this kind of advertising possible.

**"Brass Sheet-Making"**

Murray Corporation recently found that advertising space placed on ads of many of our agricultural clients, including Shell Chemical, was especially effective. We found that editorial advertising of this kind is the most effective way to alert those few readers who actually need a product.

Here are some examples of effective how-to:
"Lighting Pole, It's a Way to Cut Concrete Costs by 20%"
"The Uncommon Uses of Uncommon Corks"

Our order resulting from this advertisement was five times greater than we expected to get from the entire campaign. The same day the ad appeared.

For Shell Chemical, Ogilvy & Mather used a file of applications for a product — polybutylene pipe. Each application was then photographed and became the only illustration in an advertisement that helped treble inquiries.

**5. Create for a new material.** Given a choice to what many advertisers believe, price does not come first in the minds of most industrial buyers. They put quality and performance first.

A new material can improve the competitive same price increase immediately; even a high price can be an advantage, because it implies quality.

**How to get more for your money**

Research to find the most efficient print media positions for industrial ads is limited. But McGraw-Hill 600 research shows that one thing is certain: the average industrial page gets high readership even in a mediocre position.

Each study tested about 40 items of industrial advertising. We now have results covering 20 different McGraw-Hill publications and 50 studies.

**6. Think visuals.** Don't use spreads, for any reason. Our research shows that spreads attract no more readers than single pages, so you're paying twice to point the claim to exact hundreds. Spreads produce lots of wasted space and lots of wasted money.

If possible, split-run. Full pages with smaller units are more cost-effective.

Make sure all newspaper advertisements for full bleed. Bleed ads are worth every penny.

Make sure of newspaper advertisements for full bleed rates and color rates, too. Color ads attract significantly more readers.
