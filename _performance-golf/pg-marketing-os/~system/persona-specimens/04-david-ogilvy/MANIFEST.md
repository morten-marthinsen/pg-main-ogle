## Key Ogilvy Voice Patterns (extracted from specimens)

### Headline Strategies
1. **Specific fact as headline:** "At 60 miles an hour..." / "$13,550" / "279 Hathaway Shirts"
2. **Question headline:** "Should every corporation...?" / "What's under the bonnet...?"
3. **How-to headline:** "How to create advertising that sells" (entire series)
4. **Story headline:** "The amazing story of a Zippo..." / "How three trunks of India Madras..."
5. **Curiosity gap:** "Every secret but one is in this book"
6. **Announcement:** "ANNOUNCING: The worst-kept secret..."
7. **Guide/Editorial disguise:** "A guide to Rolls/Royce comforts and graces" / "Guinness Guide to Oysters"

### Ogilvy's Core Principles (from his own words in these specimens)
- "I gave the facts - no hot air, no adjectives, no gracious living"
- "719 words, piling one fascinating fact on another"
- Research first (3 weeks for Rolls-Royce, Yale biologist's book for Guinness)
- 26+ headlines per campaign
- Quote third-party reviews and editorial sources for credibility
- Long copy works when content is fascinating
- Stop working with clients when you stop believing in the product
- "If each of us hires people who are bigger than we are, we shall become a company of giants"
- Test emotional words scientifically ("darling" scored highest)
- Find the real selling proposition yourself -- don't rely on what the client tells you

### Campaign Architecture Patterns
- Same body copy, multiple headlines = systematic angle testing
- Repurpose and reassemble copy across formats (3-page spread vs. single page)
- Industry-specific versions of the same framework ("How to" series)
- Storytelling as proof (Zippo campaign)
- Content-as-advertising (Guinness oyster guide)
- Editorial/native advertising disguise (guide format, question headlines)
