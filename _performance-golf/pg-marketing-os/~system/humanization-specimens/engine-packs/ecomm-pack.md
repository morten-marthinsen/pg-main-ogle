## SPECIMEN A — Specific Referents Over Vague Language (H-04, Gold)

Study the STRUCTURE of this passage. E-comm copy must be specific and concrete — product pages that use vague language ("it," "things," "what") lose credibility. This specimen shows how to replace narration with direct audience engagement using specific, visualizable language.

> "I took lessons for years, but nothing worked."
>
> Seriously, can you imagine spending years with multiple instructors — and then having your improvement VANISH the moment you walk off the range?

**Structural qualities to absorb:** Direct questions that pull the reader INTO the experience. Specific, concrete verbs (VANISH) instead of generic descriptions. Conversational intensifiers ("Seriously") that signal genuine empathy. Vocal stress at the emotional peak.

---

## SPECIMEN B — Single Declarative Over Parallel Lists (H-02, Gold)

Study the STRUCTURE of this passage. E-comm feature copy often degrades into parallel lists ("Not X. Not Y. Z."). This specimen shows how one confident statement does more work than three fragments.

> And the results were PERMANENT.
>
> That's right — when we checked back months later — Harry's improvement had held.

**Structural qualities to absorb:** One word (PERMANENT) with vocal stress does what three sentences tried to do. "That's right" as a conversational confirmation — invites the reader in. No structural scaffolding around a word that's strong enough to stand alone.

## SPECIMEN C — Escalating Clause Over Parallel Structure (H-03, Candidate)

Study the STRUCTURE of this passage. Product pages often list what a product ISN'T in parallel structure. This specimen shows how to collapse a list into an escalating clause that builds naturally.

> Contrary to popular belief, it's not lack of talent, age or even discipline causing this problem.

**Structural qualities to absorb:** Four parallel sentences collapsed into one. "Or even discipline" creates escalation — the reader feels the list building rather than watching it repeat. "Contrary to popular belief" opens with a frame-break that earns attention.
