# Microskill 2.5: Authority Proof Drafter

**Layer:** 2 (Drafting)
**Purpose:** Write "living proof" authority copy that establishes credibility through personal stake

---

## CORE PRINCIPLE

Authority proof goes beyond credentials. The most powerful authority proof shows that the expert has PERSONAL STAKE in the solution — they use it themselves, they've bet their reputation on it, they've put their own family on it. This transforms credibility from "I have letters after my name" to "I believe this so deeply I stake my life on it."

---

## AUTHORITY PROOF TYPES

### Type 1: The Credentials Stack

**Standard credential establishment:**

```
"Dr. Stephen Sinatra is a board-certified cardiologist (F.A.C.C.), a certified nutrition specialist, and a certified anti-aging specialist.

For over 35 years, he's practiced integrative cardiology, combining the best of conventional medicine with natural approaches.

He's authored 17 books on heart health. Been featured on Dr. Oz, The Today Show, and CNN.

But here's what really matters: he USES this formula himself. Every single day."
```

**Structure:**
- Core credential (board certified)
- Specialty positioning (integrative)
- Social proof (books, media)
- Personal stake transition

---

### Type 2: The Living Proof Frame (Sears Style)

**Expert as the proof:**

```
"I'm 67 years old. I work 12-hour days. I travel constantly.

My energy? Higher than it was at 40.
My mental clarity? Sharp as ever.
My heart numbers? Better than most 35-year-olds.

I'm not just recommending this formula. I'm living proof it works.

And I've been taking it every day for the past 15 years."
```

**Structure:**
- Age/activity level
- Personal outcomes
- Specific metrics
- Duration of personal use
- "Living proof" positioning

---

### Type 3: The Personal Stake Declaration (Stansberry Style)

**"My family" positioning:**

```
"This isn't theoretical for me.

This is what I'm doing to protect MY family.

When I look at my children, when I think about their future, I don't have the luxury of being wrong.

Every recommendation I make, I make knowing that my own family's security depends on the same strategies."
```

**Structure:**
- Personal context
- Family stake declaration
- Emotional weight
- Skin-in-the-game proof

---

### Type 4: The Insider Authority

**"I know what they don't tell you" positioning:**

```
"As a board-certified cardiologist, I've seen behind the curtain.

I know what the drug companies don't want you to know.
I know what your doctor wasn't taught in medical school.
I know what the establishment would prefer stayed buried.

After 35 years of practice, I've learned that the real solutions often come from outside the mainstream medical system."
```

**Structure:**
- Credential establishment
- Insider knowledge claim
- Villain implication
- Alternative positioning

---

### Type 5: The Track Record Authority

**Past predictions/results prove current credibility:**

```
"In 2007, I warned readers about the coming financial crisis. They thought I was crazy.

In 2008, the crisis hit — exactly as I predicted.

In 2011, I recommended a certain investment strategy. Those who followed it saw returns of 847%.

I don't tell you this to brag. I tell you this so you understand: when I say something is coming, I have a track record of being right."
```

**Structure:**
- Past prediction/recommendation
- Outcome validation
- Specific results
- Track record → current credibility transfer

---

### Type 6: The Authority Chain (Sinatra Style)

**Stack multiple authorities:**

```
"This isn't just my opinion.

Dr. James Roberts, former head of cardiology at Yale, uses this formula with his patients.

Dr. Michael Chen, who trained under me, recommends it to everyone he sees.

The Journal of the American College of Cardiology published a paper on this exact approach.

When experts who stake their reputations on results all agree — that's not coincidence. That's consensus."
```

**Structure:**
- Lead authority position
- Secondary authority validation
- Tertiary authority validation
- Institutional backing
- Consensus conclusion

## AUTHORITY WRITING RULES

### Rule 1: Credentials Must Be Specific

**WEAK:** "A leading doctor recommends..."
**STRONG:** "Board-certified cardiologist Dr. Stephen Sinatra, F.A.C.C...."

### Rule 2: Credentials Alone Aren't Enough

**After establishing credentials, ALWAYS add:**
- Personal stake ("I use this myself")
- Track record ("I've been recommending this for 15 years")
- Emotional investment ("My family depends on this")

### Rule 3: Humanize the Authority

**Don't just list qualifications — show the person:**
- Their age and vitality
- Their daily life
- Their struggles and discoveries
- Their family concerns

### Rule 4: Connect Authority to Reader

**Bridge from "expert" to "guide for you":**
- "And now I want to share this with you..."
- "That's why I created this formula..."
- "This is exactly what I'd recommend to my own family..."

## AUTHORITY INTENSIFICATION TECHNIQUES

### Technique 1: The Insider Rebel

```
"I'll probably get in trouble for telling you this.

The medical establishment doesn't like doctors who go against the orthodoxy.

But after 35 years of watching patients suffer on approaches that don't work, I can't stay silent anymore."
```

### Technique 2: The "At First I Was Skeptical Too"

```
"When I first heard about this compound, I dismissed it. Too simple. Can't possibly work.

Then I looked at the research. Then I tested it on myself. Then I started recommending it to patients.

I haven't stopped since."
```

### Technique 3: The "What I Tell My Own Family"

```
"Want to know the real test of whether I believe in something?

It's not whether I recommend it to patients. It's whether I give it to MY family.

My wife takes this. My children take this. My grandchildren take this.

That's how much I believe in what I'm sharing with you."
```
