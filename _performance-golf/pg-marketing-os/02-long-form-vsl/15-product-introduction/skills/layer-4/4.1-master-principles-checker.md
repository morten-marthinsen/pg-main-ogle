---

## PURPOSE

Validates all 8 master principles from the TIER1 analysis against the complete product introduction draft. These 8 principles are the non-negotiable laws of elite product introductions — derived from deep analysis of 12 proven controls. Every principle must PASS. Zero tolerance. A single principle failure means the product introduction has a structural flaw that undermines its effectiveness.

This is NOT a style check or a preference assessment. These are structural requirements that distinguish elite product introductions from average ones. All 12 TIER1 controls follow all 8 principles. A product introduction that violates even one principle is operating below the standard.

---

## IDENTITY

**This microskill IS:**
- A binary pass/fail validator for 8 non-negotiable structural principles
- A zero-tolerance enforcement system — ALL 8 must pass
- An evidence-based checker that cites specific text locations for each judgment
- A remediation advisor that suggests fixes for any failing principle

**This microskill is NOT:**
- A style guide or preference checker
- A quality scorer (that is handled in assembly)
- A copywriter or editor — it validates, not rewrites
- A partial-credit grader — each principle is pass or fail, nothing in between

## PROCESS

### Principle 1: PRODUCT IS NEVER THE HERO

**Standard:** The mechanism is the hero. The product is the delivery vehicle. The copy sells the mechanism; the product is how the prospect accesses it.

1. Scan ALL draft sections for hero positioning:
   - Does the product reveal frame the product as "the solution" or as "how you access the mechanism"?
   - Does the component reveal explain components in terms of the MECHANISM or in terms of the PRODUCT?
   - Does the value stack sell the PRODUCT or the OUTCOME (mechanism activation)?
   - Does the future pacing describe life with the MECHANISM activated, or life with the PRODUCT?

2. Evidence check:
   - PASS if: Product consistently framed as vehicle/delivery system for mechanism
   - FAIL if: Product treated as the innovation itself, with mechanism as supporting detail
   - Look for: "With [Product Name], you get access to [mechanism]" → PASS
   - Look for: "[Product Name] is the breakthrough that changes everything" → FAIL (product is hero)

3. TIER1 reference:
   - BBW: Product is "the first Theta-based sound frequency" (mechanism is Theta/hippocampus — product delivers it)
   - Resurge: Product is "the world's first anti-aging nutritional protocol" (mechanism is deep sleep/HGH — product activates it)
   - Total Restore: Product is "a formula to repair your gut barrier" (mechanism is gut barrier/lectin defense — product executes it)

### Principle 2: WITHHOLDING CREATES VALUE

**Standard:** Product name timing is appropriate. In story-driven VSLs, the name is withheld until 60-75% of the copy to build desire. In advertorial/news formats, immediate naming is acceptable.

4. Check naming timing against format:
   - If story-driven VSL: Product name should NOT appear before the bridge moment
   - If advertorial/news: Product name may appear early
   - If challenge: Product name may appear early

5. Evidence check:
   - PASS if: Naming timing matches format (late for story, immediate for news/challenge)
   - FAIL if: Name appears prematurely in story format (before mechanism belief is established)
   - Note: If naming_timing was "immediate" AND format is story-VSL → FAIL

### Principle 3: THE BRIDGE IS THE MOST DANGEROUS POINT

**Standard:** The bridge moment does not feel like a sales pitch. The transition from mechanism education to product introduction is invisible or nearly so.

6. Read the bridge_text with "pitch detector" mindset:
   - At any point, does a reader think "they're trying to sell me something"?
   - Is there commercial language in the bridge? (buy, order, offer, deal, limited)
   - Does the tonal register shift abruptly?
   - Could a reader miss the "seam" between mechanism narrative and product section?

7. Evidence check:
   - PASS if: Bridge feels like natural story/education continuation, no commercial language, no tonal shift
   - FAIL if: Bridge contains commercial language, tonal register shifts abruptly, or "now I'm selling" feeling detected
   - Specific banned language in bridge: buy, purchase, order, offer, deal, discount, limited time, act now

### Principle 4: COMPONENTS ARE PROOF, NOT FEATURES

**Standard:** Each component/ingredient/step carries its own mini-proof package. Components demonstrate WHY the mechanism works, not just WHAT the product contains.

8. Scan each component section in component_reveal_draft:
   - Does each component have at least one proof element? (clinical, authority, statistic, comparison, dosage specificity)
   - Does each component explain WHY it works (mechanism connection) or just WHAT it is (feature description)?

9. Evidence check:
   - PASS if: Every component carries proof; descriptions explain mechanism connection
   - FAIL if: Any component is a bare feature description without proof
   - "Contains L-glutamine" → FAIL (feature only)
   - "Contains L-glutamine at 213mg — the exact dosage shown to repair intestinal permeability" → PASS (proof + mechanism)

10. Count: How many components have proof? If <70% → FAIL. If >=70% → check quality. If 100% → PASS.

### Principle 5: VALUE BEFORE PRICE

**Standard:** Value is fully established before any price number appears. The sequence is ALWAYS: build value → anchor high → reveal low → per-day breakdown. Never reverse.

11. Verify section ordering:
    - Does the value stack (bonuses + authority) appear BEFORE the first price number?
    - Does the component reveal (which establishes product value) appear before price?
    - Is the first dollar amount the reader encounters an ANCHOR (high) or the ACTUAL price?

12. Evidence check:
    - PASS if: Value fully established, anchoring present, first number is anchor not actual price
    - FAIL if: Actual price appears before value is established, or no anchoring precedes price
    - Exception: Authority-play (no bonuses) still requires component value + anchoring before price

### Principle 6: GUARANTEES ARE NAMED AND BRANDED

**Standard:** Generic "money-back guarantee" is replaced with a branded guarantee name that becomes a selling point. The guarantee is a FEATURE, not a policy.

13. Check the guarantee section:
    - Is there a specific branded name? (not just "60-day money-back guarantee")
    - Is the name memorable and product-specific?
    - Does the guarantee section read as a feature (confident, proud) or a policy (legal, cautious)?

14. Evidence check:
    - PASS if: Guarantee has a branded name AND reads as a feature
    - FAIL if: Guarantee uses generic language without branding OR reads as legal boilerplate
    - "You're protected by our 60-day money-back guarantee" → FAIL (generic)
    - "You're protected by the Triple-Back-Guarantee" → PASS (branded)
    - "You're covered by the Empty Jar Guarantee — use the entire jar and still get a full refund" → PASS (branded + feature)

### Principle 7: SCARCITY MUST BE JUSTIFIED

**Standard:** Every scarcity claim is backed by a REASON. Naked urgency without justification is never used.

15. Check the scarcity section (if present):
    - Is there a stated reason for the scarcity? (ingredient sourcing, manufacturing limits, price increases, suppression, real-world supply)
    - Does the reason appear IN the copy (not just in metadata)?
    - Is the reason specific and credible?

16. Evidence check:
    - PASS if: Scarcity has stated justification in copy OR no scarcity is used
    - FAIL if: Scarcity claim exists without justification
    - "Limited time offer — act now!" → FAIL (no reason)
    - "Due to the difficulty of sourcing these rare ingredients, we can only produce 500 bottles per batch" → PASS (justified)
    - No scarcity section at all → PASS (absence is acceptable; unjustified presence is not)

### Principle 8: THE CLOSE IS A BINARY CHOICE

**Standard:** The prospect faces exactly two vivid futures. 11 of 12 TIER1 controls use binary choice. No middle ground, no "think about it" option.

17. Check the binary choice setup:
    - Are there exactly 2 options? (action vs. inaction)
    - Is Choice A specific? (includes product name, guarantee reference, action verb)
    - Is Choice B specific? (includes villain/root cause continuation, emotional consequence)
    - Is there NO middle ground? (no "take your time," "come back later," "think about it")
    - Is delay framed as Choice B? (not taking action NOW = choosing inaction)

18. Evidence check:
    - PASS if: Binary choice present with 2 specific, vivid options and no middle ground
    - FAIL if: More than 2 options, vague options, or middle ground offered
    - FAIL if: Future pacing is generic ("imagine a better life") instead of specific

## FAILURE MODES

### Failure Mode 1: Principle Failure Not Detected
- **Symptom:** Final output violates a principle — quality degraded
- **Cause:** Checker not thorough enough in scanning text
- **Prevention:** Each principle has specific text-scanning requirements and evidence citation
- **Recovery:** Re-run checker with more granular text analysis

### Failure Mode 2: Partial Pass Treated as Full Pass
- **Symptom:** Principle 4 passes with only 60% of components having proof
- **Cause:** Threshold too lenient
- **Prevention:** Principle 4 requires >=70% for pass; 100% is target
- **Recovery:** Flag components without proof for remediation

### Failure Mode 3: Soft Fail Not Escalated
- **Symptom:** "Minor" principle violation allowed to pass
- **Cause:** Checker treats some principles as "soft" requirements
- **Prevention:** ALL 8 are HARD requirements — zero tolerance
- **Recovery:** Fail the check; route to remediation

## EXAMPLES

### Example 1: All 8 Pass (Elite Control Standard)

```
Principle 1: PASS — Product framed as "how you access the mechanism" throughout
  Evidence: "Total Restore brings all sixteen gut-protecting ingredients together in one capsule"
Principle 2: PASS — Name withheld until 70% (story-driven VSL format)
  Evidence: First name mention at bridge moment after mechanism education
Principle 3: PASS — Bridge uses accessibility architecture, zero commercial language
  Evidence: "Not everyone has time to read a book..." — no buy/order/offer language
Principle 4: PASS — 16/16 components have proof packages (clinical, authority, dosage)
  Evidence: Each prong has clinical data or authority endorsement
Principle 5: PASS — Three-prong defense + authority established before $49.95 price
  Evidence: First number is anchor ($297), not actual price
Principle 6: PASS — "Total Restore Promise" — branded, feature-framed
  Evidence: "You're protected by what I call the Total Restore Promise"
Principle 7: PASS — "First-time customers, 24 hours, or until supplies run out"
  Evidence: Justified by production economics
Principle 8: PASS — Binary choice with specific positive and negative futures
  Evidence: "Path one: Try Total Restore... Path two: Continue the damage..."

Summary: 8/8 PASS. ALL PRINCIPLES MET.
```

### Example 2: 2 Failures Detected (Hypothetical Draft)

```
Principle 3: FAIL — Bridge contains "this special offer"
  Evidence: "That's why I want to tell you about this special offer..."
  Remediation: Remove "special offer" language. Rewrite: "That's why I spent two years developing a formula..."

Principle 6: FAIL — Guarantee is "60-day money-back guarantee" (generic)
  Evidence: "You're covered by our 60-day money-back guarantee"
  Remediation: Brand the guarantee. Suggest: "The Deep Sleep Promise" or "The Total Restoration Guarantee"

Summary: 6/8 PASS, 2/8 FAIL. PRINCIPLES NOT MET.
Failing: [Principle 3, Principle 6]

```
