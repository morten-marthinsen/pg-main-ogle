# Layer 3.1 — Sensory Detail Calibrator

**Version:** 1.0
**Layer:** 3 (Story Refinement)
**Type:** Sensory Immersion Calibration
**Dependencies:** Layer 2 outputs (setup_context, discovery_sequence, mechanism_revelation, transformation_payoff), teaching_index (Carlton Storytelling Principles — "transporting")
**Output:** sensory_calibration_report.json

---

## Purpose

Calibrate sensory details throughout the story for believability and immersion. The goal is Carlton's "transporting" — the reader must feel they are IN the world the story creates. They should be temporarily inside the protagonist's experience, not watching from the outside.

Sensory calibration operates on a razor's edge: too few details and the reader never enters the story world — the narrative remains abstract and distant. Too many details and the reader's credulity strains, pacing collapses, and the prose feels overwrought. The calibration principle is: **enough detail to create immersion, not so much that it strains believability or slows the pace.**

Carlton's standard: "Readers want to 'see' themselves in your story, that they're temporarily 'in' the world you create with your words." The sensory detail calibrator ensures the world is vivid enough to enter but never so decorated that it becomes implausible or exhausting.

## The Calibration Principle

Sensory details in persuasive storytelling serve a different function than in literary fiction. In fiction, sensory language creates atmosphere. In persuasive copy, sensory language creates BELIEF. The reader who can see, hear, and feel the protagonist's experience is a reader who believes the experience happened.

**The Believability Spectrum:**

Mundane details are automatically believed. Extraordinary details require anchoring.

```
AUTOMATIC BELIEF ←————————————————————→ REQUIRES ANCHORING

"It was hot."         "The humidity was    "The rare Amazonian orchid
                       oppressive."         released its bioluminescent
                                            pollen into the jungle air."
```

**Calibration Rule:** For every extraordinary sensory detail, provide at least one mundane anchor. The mundane detail tells the reader "this is real life" — and the extraordinary detail rides on that credibility.

**The Carlton Standard — Good vs. Bad:**

- BAD: "The sun was setting over the Malaysian jungle as the humid tropical air wrapped around my body like a wet blanket, the sounds of exotic birds filling the canopy above while the rich scent of decaying vegetation mixed with frangipani blossoms..." (overwrought — sounds like a travel novel, not a real person telling a story)
- GOOD: "It was hot, dank-smelling, in the middle of Africa." (enough to place the reader — and it sounds like how a real person would describe it)
- GOOD: "After my grueling 42 hours of traveling, I'm introduced to the firefighters I would be training, a group of 22 men from all over Malaysia." (the "42 hours" is the sensory anchor — the reader FEELS the exhaustion)

The best sensory details are ones a real person would actually notice and mention when telling this story aloud.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Inventory All Sensory Details in Story Draft

```
PARSE the full story draft and TAG every sensory detail by channel:

SENSORY CHANNELS:

  VISUAL (V): What the protagonist SEES
    - Physical descriptions of people, places, objects
    - Colors, shapes, lighting, movement
    - Facial expressions, body language
    - Environmental details (landscape, rooms, weather)
    EXAMPLES:
      "a group of 22 men from all over Malaysia" (V — visual, people)
      "didn't have an ounce of fat on them" (V — physical appearance)
      "plates of cakes, fried food and candy bars" (V — objects)

  AUDITORY (A): What the protagonist HEARS
    - Dialogue (reported speech)
    - Environmental sounds
    - Silence (deliberate absence of sound)
    - Internal monologue presented as heard thought
    EXAMPLES:
      "'The secret you've been searching for,' he said with a glint" (A — dialogue)
      "I heard an unexpected noise through my wall" (A — environmental)

  KINESTHETIC (K): What the protagonist FEELS physically
    - Temperature, texture, pressure
    - Physical sensations (pain, exhaustion, relief)
    - Movement and spatial awareness
    - Bodily states (hunger, tension, relaxation)
    EXAMPLES:
      "grueling 42 hours of traveling" (K — exhaustion)
      "It was hot" (K — temperature)
      "I felt like I wanted to cry" (K — physical emotional response)

  OLFACTORY/GUSTATORY (O/G): What the protagonist SMELLS or TASTES
    - Ambient smells
    - Food/drink descriptions
    - Chemical or natural scents
    - Taste experiences
    EXAMPLES:
      "dank-smelling" (O — ambient smell)
      "delicious plates of cakes" (G — implied taste)

  EMOTIONAL/INTERNAL (E): What the protagonist FEELS emotionally
    - Named emotions
    - Internal reactions
    - Thought processes
    - Realizations and shifts
    EXAMPLES:
      "I hated leaving her" (E — emotional state)
      "for the first time in years I felt hope" (E — emotional shift)
      "I felt like I wanted to cry, not because I was sad" (E — emotional)

FOR each sensory detail found:
  LOG: location (section + sentence), channel, detail_text, detail_type
  CLASSIFY intensity:
    LIGHT: Single word or phrase ("it was hot")
    MODERATE: Full clause or sentence ("the humidity wrapped around me")
    HEAVY: Multiple sentences or extended description (paragraph-level scene painting)

LOG: total_details_count, details_per_channel, details_per_section,
     intensity_distribution
```

### Step 2: Assess Channel Balance

```
EVALUATE the distribution of sensory channels across the story:

IDEAL CHANNEL DISTRIBUTION (persuasive storytelling):
  Visual (V): 30-40% — the backbone; readers think in pictures
  Kinesthetic (K): 20-30% — physical sensation creates empathy
  Emotional (E): 20-30% — internal state drives identification
  Auditory (A): 10-15% — dialogue and sound create texture
  Olfactory/Gustatory (O/G): 0-10% — use sparingly, only when relevant

CALCULATE actual distribution:
  visual_percentage: float
  kinesthetic_percentage: float
  emotional_percentage: float
  auditory_percentage: float
  olfactory_gustatory_percentage: float

FLAG imbalances:
  - Visual > 50% → "Story is over-described visually. Reader sees but
    doesn't FEEL. Add kinesthetic or emotional details."
  - Emotional > 40% → "Story is too internalized. Reader is told how
    to feel instead of experiencing it. Show through action and
    sensory detail, don't tell through emotion labels."
  - Kinesthetic < 10% → "Story lacks physical grounding. Add body
    sensations — exhaustion, heat, tension, relief — to anchor
    the reader in the protagonist's physical experience."
  - Olfactory/Gustatory > 15% → "Excessive smell/taste detail.
    Unless the story is specifically about food, cooking, or
    chemical exposure, these channels should be minimal."
  - Auditory < 5% AND story contains dialogue → "Dialogue present
    but no auditory texture. Add how things were SAID, not just
    what was said."

CHANNEL BALANCE SCORE: integer[1-10]
  10 = perfect distribution across channels, natural and immersive
  7-9 = minor imbalance, easily adjusted
  4-6 = noticeable dominance of one channel, systematic rebalancing needed
  1-3 = story relies on single channel, major revision required
```

### Step 3: Audit Detail Density Per Section

```
EVALUATE sensory detail density for each story section:

FOR each section (setup, discovery_sequence, mechanism_revelation,
                  transformation_payoff):

  CALCULATE:
    word_count: integer
    detail_count: integer
    density_ratio: details_per_100_words

  IDEAL DENSITY BY SECTION:

  SETUP / CONTEXT:
    Target density: 3-5 details per 100 words
    PURPOSE: Establish the world quickly. Place the reader.
    RULE: One strong establishing detail is worth five weak ones.
    GOOD: "It was hot, dank-smelling, in the middle of Africa."
          (3 details in 10 words = highly efficient world-building)
    BAD: Three paragraphs describing the airport, the flight,
         the taxi ride, the hotel room... (density too spread out)

  DISCOVERY / EXPERIMENT SEQUENCE:
    Target density: 2-4 details per 100 words
    PURPOSE: Details serve the suspense, not the scenery.
    RULE: Sensory details in this section should create TENSION
          or CURIOSITY, not atmosphere.
    GOOD: "I heard an unexpected noise through my wall in her room..."
          (auditory detail that creates curiosity)
    BAD: Extended description of the hostel's architecture
         (irrelevant to suspense)

  MECHANISM REVELATION:
    Target density: 1-3 details per 100 words
    PURPOSE: This section is about INFORMATION, not sensation.
    RULE: Minimal sensory detail — the revelation itself IS the
          experience. Too much sensory language here dilutes the
          impact of the revelation.
    GOOD: "Ashraf smiled, patted me on the shoulder" (one visual/
          kinesthetic beat before the reveal)
    BAD: A paragraph describing Ashraf's clothing, the room's
         lighting, the temperature... (delays the reveal)

  TRANSFORMATION / PAYOFF:
    Target density: 3-6 details per 100 words
    PURPOSE: This is where the reader FEELS the result. Maximum
             sensory engagement to make the transformation REAL.
    RULE: Results must be experiential, not clinical. The reader
          should FEEL the transformation, not read a data table.
    GOOD: "I stepped on the scale and couldn't believe it —
           14 pounds gone in 3 weeks. My pants were literally
           falling off me."
    BAD: "Subject experienced a 14.3% reduction in body mass
          index over 21 days."

  FLAG sections that deviate more than 50% from target density:
    Over-dense: "Section [X] has [Y] details per 100 words —
                 target is [Z]. Strip excess to maintain pace."
    Under-dense: "Section [X] has [Y] details per 100 words —
                  target is [Z]. Add sensory anchors to maintain
                  immersion."

SECTION DENSITY SCORE: integer[1-10] (per section)
```

### Step 4: Apply Believability Spectrum Test

```
FOR each sensory detail, assess its position on the believability spectrum:

BELIEVABILITY CLASSIFICATION:

  MUNDANE (automatic belief):
    - Common physical sensations ("it was hot", "I was exhausted")
    - Universal experiences ("I couldn't sleep that night")
    - Simple observations ("there were 22 men")
    - Basic emotional states ("I was scared", "I felt hopeful")
    BELIEVABILITY: 100% — reader accepts without question

  ORDINARY-SPECIFIC (high belief):
    - Specific but plausible details ("42 hours of traveling")
    - Named places and real locations ("a tiny Malaysian island
      called Langkawi")
    - Plausible character descriptions ("six guys in their mid-fifties")
    - Detailed but realistic observations ("they would eat their
      plate of food, but then never eat again for the rest of the day")
    BELIEVABILITY: 90-100% — specificity enhances credibility

  EXTRAORDINARY-ANCHORED (moderate belief):
    - Unusual claims grounded in mundane context
    - Surprising observations with plausible explanation
    - Exotic details anchored by ordinary framing
    BELIEVABILITY: 70-90% — believable IF anchored properly
    TEST: Is the extraordinary detail preceded or followed by
          a mundane anchor? If yes → anchored. If no → floating.

  EXTRAORDINARY-FLOATING (low belief):
    - Exotic details without mundane anchoring
    - Sensational descriptions that sound invented
    - Purple prose that no real person would speak
    - Details that serve the writer's style, not the story's truth
    BELIEVABILITY: 30-70% — reader's skepticism activates
    EXAMPLES:
      "The ancient temple's walls seemed to whisper secrets of
       forgotten civilizations as moonlight danced through the
       cracks in the stone..."
      → This is fiction writing, not persuasive storytelling.
         No real person describes their experience this way.

  UNBELIEVABLE (credulity failure):
    - Details that contradict common experience
    - Impossibly precise sensory recall from years ago
    - Purple prose that obviously serves the writer, not the story
    - Sensory descriptions that no human would process simultaneously
    BELIEVABILITY: 0-30% — reader disengages
    EXAMPLES:
      "I could smell the exact combination of jasmine and
       sandalwood that I'd smelled exactly 7 years earlier in
       my grandmother's garden in Tuscany..."
      → Nobody remembers smells with this precision. This
         destroys believability.

FOR each detail classified as EXTRAORDINARY-FLOATING or UNBELIEVABLE:
  LOG: location, detail_text, classification, believability_score
  GENERATE anchoring remediation:
    TECHNIQUE 1 — Precede with mundane detail:
      BEFORE: "The exotic herbs released their intoxicating fragrance"
      AFTER: "The stuff smelled weird — like nothing I'd ever come across
              at the drugstore back home"

    TECHNIQUE 2 — Use conversational framing:
      BEFORE: "The ancient remedy revealed its secrets to me"
      AFTER: "I had no idea what I was looking at, but something told
              me this was different"

    TECHNIQUE 3 — Add self-aware qualifier:
      BEFORE: "The sunset painted the sky in impossible colors"
      AFTER: "I know this sounds like something out of a movie, but
              the sky that evening was unreal"

    TECHNIQUE 4 — Replace with how a real person would say it:
      BEFORE: "The crystalline waters of the Mediterranean sparkled
               like a thousand diamonds"
      AFTER: "The water was so clear you could see the bottom"

BELIEVABILITY SCORE: integer[1-10]
  10 = every detail passes the "would a real person say this?" test
  7-9 = minor floating details, easily anchored
  4-6 = multiple unanchored extraordinary details
  1-3 = story reads like fiction, not testimony
```

### Step 5: Apply the "Would You Say This?" Test

```
FOR each sensory detail, apply Carlton's conversational standard:

THE TEST: If the protagonist were telling this story to a friend
          at a bar, would they include this detail?

  PASS: Details a real person would mention
    - "It was hot as hell over there"
    - "This guy — mid-fifties, not an ounce of fat on him"
    - "I heard her through the wall every night"
    - "I nearly cried when I read the research"
    - "42 hours of traveling — I was wrecked"

  FAIL: Details a real person would NOT mention
    - Precise color descriptions of clothing
    - Atmospheric descriptions of weather patterns
    - Extended landscape descriptions
    - Metaphorical sensory language ("the air tasted like freedom")
    - Simultaneous multi-channel descriptions ("I saw the light
      while hearing the birds while feeling the warmth while
      smelling the flowers...")

  BORDER CASES: Details that depend on context
    - Food descriptions → PASS in health/diet stories
    - Physical sensation details → PASS in health/performance stories
    - Environmental details → PASS if they create tension or stakes
    - Sound details → PASS if they advance the plot

FOR each FAIL:
  LOG: location, detail_text, reason_for_fail
  GENERATE conversational replacement or recommend deletion

CONVERSATIONAL AUTHENTICITY SCORE: integer[1-10]
  10 = every detail sounds like real human speech
  7-9 = occasional literary flourish, easily stripped
  4-6 = noticeable "written" quality to sensory language
  1-3 = reads like a novel, not a person telling a story
```

### Step 6: Check Pacing Impact of Sensory Details

```
EVALUATE whether sensory details are helping or hurting the story's pace:

FOR each story section:

  IDENTIFY sensory detail clusters (3+ details within 50 words):

  PACING IMPACT ASSESSMENT:
    ACCELERATING: Detail creates tension or urgency
      "I heard the noise again. Louder this time." → pace quickens
    NEUTRAL: Detail provides necessary context without slowing
      "It was late, the hostel was quiet." → pace maintained
    DECELERATING: Detail slows momentum without adding tension
      "The room was small, with peeling blue wallpaper, a single
       window overlooking the courtyard..." → pace drags

  FLAG all DECELERATING details that appear:
    - Before a revelation (delays the payoff)
    - During a tension sequence (breaks suspense)
    - In the mechanism revelation section (dilutes the information)
    - Consecutively (two or more decelerating details in a row)

  PACING REMEDIATION:
    TECHNIQUE 1 — Delete: If the detail adds no tension, curiosity,
                  or world-building, remove it entirely.
    TECHNIQUE 2 — Compress: Reduce multi-sentence description to
                  a single phrase.
      BEFORE: "The office was large and well-appointed, with dark
               wood paneling on the walls, a massive oak desk in
               the center, and floor-to-ceiling bookshelves."
      AFTER: "His office looked like it cost more than my house."
    TECHNIQUE 3 — Relocate: Move the detail to a lower-tension
                  section where deceleration is acceptable.
    TECHNIQUE 4 — Convert: Transform atmosphere detail into
                  tension detail by adding emotional charge.
      BEFORE: "The lab was cold and sterile."
      AFTER: "The lab felt cold — the kind of cold that makes
              you wonder what they're keeping preserved in here."

PACING IMPACT SCORE: integer[1-10]
  10 = every sensory detail serves the pace — accelerating or neutral
  7-9 = minor decelerating details, easily fixed
  4-6 = multiple pace-killing descriptions
  1-3 = sensory detail is actively destroying the story's momentum
```

### Step 7: Generate Calibrated Story Text with Annotations

```
PRODUCE the calibrated story text:

1. APPLY all remediations from Steps 4-6:
   - Anchor floating extraordinary details
   - Replace "written" details with conversational equivalents
   - Compress or delete pace-killing descriptions
   - Rebalance channel distribution if flagged

2. ANNOTATE the calibrated text:
   FOR each sensory detail in the final text:
     [V] = visual  [A] = auditory  [K] = kinesthetic
     [O] = olfactory  [G] = gustatory  [E] = emotional
     [LIGHT] [MOD] [HEAVY] = intensity level

   EXAMPLE:
     "It was hot [K-LIGHT], dank-smelling [O-LIGHT], in the
      middle of Africa [V-LIGHT]."
     → 3 channels, all light intensity = efficient world-building

3. PRODUCE section-by-section calibration summary:
   FOR each section:
     - Original detail count vs. calibrated detail count
     - Details added, removed, modified
     - Channel distribution before/after
     - Density ratio before/after
     - Believability score before/after

4. PRODUCE the overall calibration verdict:
   - Total details in calibrated text
   - Channel balance assessment
   - Believability assessment
   - Pacing impact assessment
   - Conversational authenticity assessment
   - Carlton "transporting" achieved: boolean
```

### Step 8: Calculate Composite Sensory Calibration Score

```
CALCULATE the composite sensory calibration score:

  channel_balance: integer[1-10]          (from Step 2)
  section_density: integer[1-10]          (from Step 3, average across sections)
  believability: integer[1-10]            (from Step 4)
  conversational_authenticity: integer[1-10] (from Step 5)
  pacing_impact: integer[1-10]            (from Step 6)

  sensory_calibration_composite = weighted average:
    believability: 30% weight (most critical — unbelievable details
                               destroy the story's persuasive function)
    conversational_authenticity: 25% weight (details must sound spoken)
    pacing_impact: 20% weight (details must not kill momentum)
    channel_balance: 15% weight (immersion requires multi-channel)
    section_density: 10% weight (right amount in right places)

  GATE THRESHOLD: sensory_calibration_composite >= 7.0

  IF composite < 7.0:
    STATUS = FAIL
    Identify top 3 improvement areas by lowest sub-scores
    Prioritize remediations that move the composite above 7.0

  CARLTON TRANSPORTING ASSESSMENT:
    IF believability >= 7 AND conversational_authenticity >= 7
       AND pacing_impact >= 7:
      transporting_achieved = true
    ELSE:
      transporting_achieved = false
      transporting_failure_reason = [specific reason]
```

## Anti-Patterns

**The Travel Writer:**
Describing the story's setting as if writing a Lonely Planet guide — "The lush Malaysian jungle stretched endlessly beneath a canopy of ancient dipterocarp trees, their leaves filtering the equatorial sunlight into dappled patterns on the forest floor." Nobody in a persuasive story talks like this. One sentence — "It was hot, in the middle of Malaysia" — does more for immersion than a paragraph of National Geographic prose.

**The Emotion Teller:**
Naming every emotion instead of showing it through sensory experience. "I felt devastated. I was heartbroken. I experienced profound sadness." vs. "I sat in the car for twenty minutes before I could turn the key." The second version lets the reader FEEL the devastation through physical detail. Telling emotions is lazy; showing through sensation is craft.

**The Simultaneous Sensor:**
Describing what the protagonist saw, heard, felt, smelled, and tasted all in the same paragraph. Real humans do not process five sensory channels simultaneously when recounting an experience. They remember one or two dominant impressions. Multi-channel sensory dumps read as fabricated.

**The Precision Recaller:**
Including impossibly precise details from memories that should be fuzzy — "I remember the exact shade of burnt sienna on the clay tiles, the way the third floorboard from the left creaked at 440Hz..." Human memory is impressionistic, not photographic. Over-precise recall signals fiction, not testimony.

**The Atmospheric Filler:**
Using sensory details as word count padding — adding weather, lighting, and ambient descriptions that serve no narrative function. Every sensory detail must either (1) establish the world, (2) create tension, (3) build character, or (4) make the transformation feel real. If it does none of these, delete it.

**The Purple Metaphor:**
Replacing direct sensory language with literary metaphors — "The truth hit me like a freight train of enlightenment, shattering the glass ceiling of my ignorance." Real people do not speak in extended metaphors. Simple, direct sensory language is always more believable than decorated literary language.
