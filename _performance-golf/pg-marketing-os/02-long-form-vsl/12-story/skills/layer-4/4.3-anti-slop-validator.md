# Layer 4.3 — Anti-Slop Validator (Story)

**Version:** 1.0
**Layer:** 4 (Validation & Assembly)
**Type:** Language & Narrative Quality Gate
**Dependencies:** full draft story text (from Layer 3 output), story_classification (from 1.1), story_beats (from 1.2), carlton_compliance (from 3.4), sensory_detail_calibration (from 3.1), teaching_index (from 0.3), anti-slop-standards (from QualityProtocol)
**Output:** anti_slop_validation.json

---

## Purpose

Detect generic, cliched, unbelievable, or lazy story elements in the engineered campaign story. Stories are the HIGHEST-RISK zone for slop in copywriting. Where the lead is 350-800 words of compressed teasing, the story is an extended narrative that offers dozens of opportunities for generic characters, cliched discoveries, unbelievable details, convenient coincidences, exposition dumps, stock dialogue, and emotional flatness. A single slop violation in a story can shatter the suspension of disbelief that the entire narrative depends on. This validator applies 12 story-specific slop categories with a zero-tolerance policy -- any violation requires remediation before the story can be assembled.

---

## Primary Persona

**Sarah A. Conco -- Client Protection Specialist**
Reads the story from the client's perspective: would a business owner be embarrassed if this narrative represented their brand? Does it sound like a real person's experience, or like a fill-in-the-template story generator produced it? Zero tolerance for narrative elements that damage credibility or trigger the reader's "this is fake" detector.

## Secondary Persona

**Dr. Richard Stern -- Skeptical Academic**
Reads with a scalpel. Every generic protagonist, every cliched transition, every unbelievable sensory detail, every convenient coincidence, every exposition dump gets flagged. If a reader would roll their eyes, wince, or mentally check out -- it fails.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Scan for Story-Specific Slop Categories

```
Stories have UNIQUE slop failure modes that do not apply to leads, arguments, or offers.
These are the 12 story-specific slop categories. Each violation shatters narrative
believability and must be remediated.

CATEGORY 1: GENERIC PROTAGONIST
  DEFINITION: A protagonist described with vague, template-style characteristics
  WHY IT'S SLOP: A generic protagonist cannot create identification. The prospect
                  cannot see themselves in "John, a regular guy who just wanted to
                  feel better." They CAN see themselves in a person with SPECIFIC,
                  relatable details.
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL

  SCAN FOR:
    - "[Name] was a regular/ordinary/average [person/man/woman] who..."
    - Protagonist described only by age and occupation with no distinguishing detail
    - Protagonist whose problem is stated generically ("struggled with his weight")
    - No specific habits, fears, embarrassments, or distinguishing traits
    - Protagonist who could be swapped for any other character without story impact

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "John was a 52-year-old accountant from Ohio who struggled with
               his weight. He'd tried everything but nothing worked."
    ACCEPTABLE: "The morning John's daughter refused to sit next to him on the
                 plane -- that was the moment the diet apps and gym memberships
                 stopped being about health and became about whether his own
                 kid was ashamed of him."

CATEGORY 2: CLICHED DISCOVERY
  DEFINITION: Using overused transitional phrases for key story moments
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Cliches signal template writing. The reader has encountered these
                  phrases hundreds of times and they trigger "sales copy" detection.
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL

  SCAN FOR:
    - "One day, everything changed..."
    - "Little did he know, his life was about to change forever..."
    - "That's when he stumbled upon / discovered / came across..."
    - "What happened next changed everything..."
    - "And then it hit him like a ton of bricks..."
    - "He couldn't believe his eyes when..."
    - "That's when the lightbulb went off..."
    - "His jaw dropped when he realized..."
    - "Nothing could have prepared him for what he was about to learn..."
    - "And that's when everything clicked into place..."

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "One day, everything changed when Dr. Smith stumbled upon
               a little-known research paper."
    ACCEPTABLE: "It was the third rejection letter in a month. Dr. Smith was
                 about to archive the whole project when a footnote in a 1987
                 Japanese paper -- one that no English-language journal had ever
                 cited -- stopped him cold."

CATEGORY 3: UNBELIEVABLE DETAILS
  DEFINITION: Sensory or contextual details that don't match the setting or strain credulity
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Unbelievable details break immersion. The reader exits the story world
                  and thinks "that didn't happen" or "that's not how that works."
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Sensory details that contradict the established setting
      (e.g., "crisp autumn air" in a scene set in tropical Malaysia)
    - Emotional responses that don't match the situation
      (e.g., protagonist is calm during what should be a crisis)
    - Technical details that are factually wrong
    - Cultural details that don't match the location
    - Time inconsistencies (morning vs. evening confusion)
    - Physical impossibilities presented as fact
    - Details so specific they feel fabricated rather than recalled
      (e.g., "at exactly 3:17 PM on a Tuesday" -- too precise without reason)

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "As he walked through the ancient Balinese temple, the sound of
               church bells echoed across the valley" (wrong cultural detail)
    ACCEPTABLE: "The gamelan music from the temple ceremony carried across the
                 rice paddies -- tinny and distant, competing with the motorcycle
                 traffic on the road below"

CATEGORY 4: CONVENIENT COINCIDENCES
  DEFINITION: Too many lucky breaks or serendipitous events without earned progression
  WHY IT'S SLOP: When every obstacle is solved by coincidence, the story feels
                  manufactured rather than lived. Real discoveries involve struggle,
                  dead ends, and persistence -- not a chain of lucky breaks.
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Protagonist "happens to" meet exactly the right person at the right time
      more than once (one serendipitous meeting is acceptable -- a chain is not)
    - Problems that resolve themselves without protagonist action
    - Information that appears exactly when needed without the protagonist seeking it
    - Multiple "lucky" events in sequence without setbacks between them
    - The expert/yoda character appearing without a plausible reason for the encounter
    - Results that appear immediately with no adjustment period

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "He happened to sit next to a world-renowned biochemist on the plane,
               who happened to be carrying a research paper about the exact enzyme
               he'd been wondering about."
    ACCEPTABLE: "He'd emailed 43 researchers. Forty said no. Two never replied.
                 The forty-third -- a retired endocrinologist in Kyoto who'd
                 published one paper on the topic in 1991 -- wrote back in
                 broken English: 'Come see me. I will show you.'"

CATEGORY 5: EXPOSITION DUMPS
  DEFINITION: Breaking from narrative into explanation mode
  WHY IT'S SLOP: When the story stops being a story and becomes a lecture, the reader
                  exits the narrative and re-enters analytical mode. The spell is broken.
                  Explanation should emerge FROM the story, not interrupt it.
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL

  SCAN FOR:
    - Paragraphs that shift from narrative tense to explanatory tense
    - "Here's what you need to understand..." / "The science behind this is..."
    - Long parenthetical explanations within story action
    - The narrator stepping out of the story to explain a concept
    - Information delivered in textbook format within a narrative context
    - Carlton violation: failure to maintain "breathless prose"

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "Dr. Chen explained the mechanism. [The way it works is that
               cortisol levels are regulated by the HPA axis, which consists of
               the hypothalamus, pituitary gland, and adrenal cortex. When
               stress is chronic, the negative feedback loop becomes...]"
    ACCEPTABLE: "'Your body is attacking itself,' Dr. Chen said, pointing to a
                 chart that looked like a battlefield. 'This enzyme -- this ONE
                 enzyme -- is the commander ordering the attack. Kill the
                 commander, the battle stops.'"

CATEGORY 6: STOCK CHARACTERS
  DEFINITION: Characters built from archetype templates without individual personality
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Stock characters are immediately recognizable as literary devices
                  rather than real people. The Yoda who speaks in fortune-cookie
                  wisdom, the supportive spouse who says "I believe in you," the
                  skeptical colleague who later admits they were wrong -- these are
                  character costumes, not characters.
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Yoda/guide character who speaks exclusively in profound wisdom
    - Supportive spouse/friend with no personality beyond "supportive"
    - Villain/antagonist who is cartoonishly evil or dismissive
    - Skeptic character who exists only to be proven wrong
    - Beta-tester who is indistinguishable from other beta-testers
    - Any character who serves only one function with no distinguishing traits
    - Characters who speak in perfectly formed paragraphs of wisdom

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "'My friend,' said the old professor, stroking his white beard,
               'the secret to health is not found in modern medicine. It is found
               in the wisdom of the ancients, passed down through generations.'"
    ACCEPTABLE: "'Look, I almost didn't tell you this,' Tanaka said, pulling out
                 a drawer full of yellowed papers. 'My department head would have
                 me fired. But these results -- ' he stabbed a finger at a column
                 of numbers -- 'these results are real, and someone needs to see them.'"

CATEGORY 7: ARTIFICIAL DIALOGUE
  DEFINITION: Dialogue that sounds scripted, overly formal, or like a sales pitch
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Real people don't speak in complete, perfectly structured sentences
                  that conveniently deliver exactly the information the reader needs.
                  Artificial dialogue is the fastest way to break story immersion.
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL

  SCAN FOR:
    - Characters delivering exposition through dialogue
      ("As you know, the enzyme responsible for cortisol regulation...")
    - Dialogue that sounds like a brochure ("This ancient method has been
       used for centuries to promote optimal health and wellness")
    - Characters asking convenient questions to set up explanations
      ("But doctor, how exactly does this enzyme work?")
    - Dialogue with no personality markers (every character sounds the same)
    - Unrealistically articulate speech about complex topics
    - Characters who never use contractions, fragments, or colloquialisms
    - Dialogue that includes product benefits in unnaturally explicit form

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "'My friend, I will now reveal to you the ancient secret that has
                been hidden from Western medicine for centuries,' said the healer."
    ACCEPTABLE: "'You want to know why you're tired?' She didn't wait for an answer.
                 'It's not what you're eating. It's not your sleep. It's this.'
                 She held up an X-ray that made no sense to me."

CATEGORY 8: RESULT INFLATION
  DEFINITION: Claims or narrative results that strain credulity
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Inflated results trigger the prospect's skepticism defense.
                  Better to understate and be believed than overstate and be dismissed.
                  The Makepeace transparency paradox: admitting limitations BUILDS
                  credibility; perfection DESTROYS it.
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Perfect results with no caveats or variability
    - Transformation that happens unrealistically fast
    - Results that exceed what the mechanism could plausibly produce
    - "100% success rate" type claims without transparency
    - Every beta-tester/participant succeeding with no failures
    - Emotional transformations that are too dramatic (complete 180-degree life changes)
    - Before/after contrasts that are too extreme to be credible

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "Every single person in the trial lost at least 30 pounds
               within the first month, with zero side effects."
    ACCEPTABLE: "Out of 487 participants, 411 saw measurable results within
                 60 days. 23 saw no change. And 3 -- I'll be honest -- reported
                 mild stomach discomfort in the first week that subsided by day 10."

CATEGORY 9: MISSING STAKES
  DEFINITION: Nothing at risk for the protagonist
  WHY IT'S SLOP: Without stakes, there is no tension. Without tension, there is
                  no story. Without story, there is no emotional proof. The protagonist
                  must have something to LOSE -- their health, their relationship,
                  their dignity, their financial security, their reputation.
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Protagonist embarks on the journey without clear personal consequences
    - No "what happens if this doesn't work" articulated or implied
    - Protagonist has nothing invested in the outcome
    - Discovery/experiment has no risk of failure
    - The journey feels like a pleasant excursion rather than a desperate search
    - No emotional vulnerability -- protagonist is detached from the outcome

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "Curious about the method, Dr. Williams decided to investigate further
               to see if the research held up."
    ACCEPTABLE: "Dr. Williams had six months left on his grant. His last three
                 papers had been rejected. If this compound didn't work -- if
                 this was another dead end -- he'd be teaching freshman bio at
                 a community college by spring."

CATEGORY 10: EMOTIONAL FLATNESS
  DEFINITION: Telling emotions instead of showing them
  WHY IT'S SLOP: "I was sad" is information. "I sat in the parking lot for forty
                   minutes because I couldn't walk into the gym knowing everyone
                   would look at me" is experience. Emotional flatness robs the
                   story of its transportation power.
  SEVERITY: CRITICAL

  SCAN FOR:
    - Emotion labels without behavioral evidence
      ("He was excited" / "She felt hopeful" / "I was devastated")
    - Emotional transitions that skip the experience
      ("He went from hopeless to hopeful")
    - Abstract emotional language ("a wave of emotion washed over him")
    - Missing physical/behavioral manifestations of emotion
    - Characters ANNOUNCING their feelings rather than SHOWING them
    - Carlton violation: reader should FEEL something -- they can't FEEL
      "he was excited." They CAN feel the behavior that shows excitement.

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "When John saw the results, he was overwhelmed with joy.
               He felt like his life was finally turning around."
    ACCEPTABLE: "John read the number three times. Then he read it again.
                 He walked into the kitchen, set the paper on the counter,
                 and called his wife. He couldn't get past 'I have to tell
                 you something' before his voice cracked."

CATEGORY 11: TEMPLATE-OBVIOUS STRUCTURE
  DEFINITION: The reader can see the formula behind the story
  WHY IT'S SLOP: When the reader recognizes the template, they exit the story
                  and start analyzing the sales technique. The architecture must
                  be invisible. The reader should experience a STORY, not a
                  FRAMEWORK with content filled in.
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Transitions that feel formulaic ("But that's not all..." / "And then,
       something incredible happened..." / "But here's where it gets interesting...")
    - Beat sequence that perfectly matches a template with no organic variation
    - Story structure so predictable the reader knows what comes next at every point
    - "And here's the best part..." used as a structural crutch rather than a natural beat
    - Section breaks that align with obvious template sections
    - The story feeling like a Mad Libs exercise (protagonist name, location,
       mechanism name, result number filled into a standard template)

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: "Step 1: John discovered the problem. Step 2: He found the expert.
               Step 3: The expert revealed the solution. Step 4: He tried it.
               Step 5: It worked. And here's the best part..."
    ACCEPTABLE: The template IS used (every story has structure) but the narrative
                flows so naturally that the reader experiences events, not sections.
                The architecture is invisible. Transitions are organic, not announced.

CATEGORY 12: ANACHRONISMS AND LOGICAL HOLES
  DEFINITION: Timeline errors, contradictions, or logical impossibilities in the narrative
  WHY IT'S SLOP: These break the reader's trust in the story's factual basis.
                  If the story gets basic facts wrong, the reader questions everything
                  else -- including the mechanism and results.
  SEVERITY: HIGH

  SCAN FOR:
    - Timeline contradictions (event A happens before event B, but later it's reversed)
    - Character knowledge inconsistencies (character knows something they shouldn't yet)
    - Geographic impossibilities (locations that don't match the described journey)
    - Technology/product anachronisms (referencing things that didn't exist at the stated time)
    - Cause/effect violations (result appears before the mechanism is applied)
    - Math errors in data cascades (percentages that don't add up)
    - Character age/career inconsistencies
    - Weather/season inconsistencies with stated dates

  EXAMPLES:
    VIOLATION: Story set in 2005 but protagonist "searched on his smartphone"
               (iPhones launched in 2007)
    ACCEPTABLE: Timeline, geography, technology, and cause/effect chain are
                internally consistent and factually plausible
```

### Step 2: Scan for General Language Slop

```
BEYOND story-specific categories, scan for general copywriting slop:

FILLER OPENERS:
  - "It's important to note that..."
  - "In today's fast-paced world..."
  - "As we all know..."
  - "Here's the thing..."
  - "Let me tell you about..."

EMPTY INTENSIFIERS:
  - "very" / "really" / "extremely" / "incredibly"
  - "absolutely" / "totally" / "completely"
  - THRESHOLD: Zero tolerance in story -- every word must earn its place

AI TELLTALE PHRASES:
  - "Let's dive in..." / "Without further ado..."
  - "seamlessly" / "effortlessly"
  - "harness" / "unlock" / "leverage" (generic usage)
  - "delve into" / "embark on a journey"
  - "comprehensive" / "robust" / "holistic"
  - "navigate" / "landscape" / "paradigm"

PASSIVE VOICE:
  - Count active vs. passive sentences
  - THRESHOLD: >= 85% active voice (stories require even higher active voice than leads)
  - Stories are ACTION -- passive voice kills momentum

ADVERB DEPENDENCY:
  - Flag adverbs used in place of strong verbs
  - "He walked quickly" → "He sprinted" / "He darted"
  - "She said quietly" → "She whispered" / "She murmured"
  - THRESHOLD: <= 1 unnecessary adverb per 200 words
```

### Step 3: Log Every Violation

```
FOR each violation found:

  RECORD:
    - violation_id: string (e.g., "STORY-SLOP-001")
    - location: string (exact passage -- quoted text from the story)
    - category: enum[generic_protagonist, cliched_discovery, unbelievable_details,
                     convenient_coincidences, exposition_dumps, stock_characters,
                     artificial_dialogue, result_inflation, missing_stakes,
                     emotional_flatness, template_obvious, anachronism_logical_hole,
                     filler_opener, empty_intensifier, ai_telltale,
                     passive_voice, adverb_dependency]
    - severity: enum[CRITICAL, HIGH, MODERATE, LOW]
    - specific_fix: string (EXACT suggested replacement or revision -- not "make it better")

  SEVERITY MAPPING:
    CRITICAL (automatic FAIL -- zero tolerance):
      - generic_protagonist
      - cliched_discovery
      - exposition_dumps
      - artificial_dialogue
      - emotional_flatness

    HIGH (require remediation before PASS):
      - unbelievable_details
      - convenient_coincidences
      - stock_characters
      - result_inflation
      - missing_stakes
      - template_obvious
      - anachronism_logical_hole

    MODERATE (flag for improvement):
      - filler_opener
      - empty_intensifier
      - ai_telltale
      - adverb_dependency

    LOW (note but do not fail):
      - isolated passive voice
```

### Step 4: Calculate Slop Density

```
slop_density = total_violations / (word_count / 100)

RATING:
  0.0     → PRISTINE (zero violations -- exceptional)
  0.1-0.3 → EXCELLENT (minor issues only)
  0.4-0.8 → ACCEPTABLE (some cleanup needed)
  0.9-1.5 → WEAK (systematic revision needed)
  1.6+    → FAILING (fundamental narrative quality problem)

CATEGORY DISTRIBUTION:
  COUNT violations per category
  IDENTIFY if one category dominates (systemic issue vs. scattered)
  IDENTIFY if critical categories have any violations (automatic FAIL)
```

### Step 5: Apply the "Real Person's Story" Test

```
THE NARRATIVE AUTHENTICITY TEST:

Read the full story one final time with this question:

  "Could this story have been told by a REAL PERSON describing a REAL
   EXPERIENCE to a friend over coffee -- complete with the specific details,
   emotional texture, and conversational rhythms of genuine recollection?

   Or does it sound like a COPYWRITER wrote a STORY designed to SELL?"

This is a spectrum assessment:

  AUTHENTIC: The story feels recalled from memory -- specific, slightly imperfect,
             emotionally textured, with the rhythm of genuine experience
  CRAFTED_AUTHENTIC: The story is clearly crafted but maintains authenticity --
                      the reader knows it's written but doesn't feel manipulated
  CRAFTED_OBVIOUS: The story is competently written but the craft is visible --
                    the reader can see the framework behind the narrative
  TEMPLATE: The story feels like a template with details filled in --
            the reader recognizes the formula immediately
  ARTIFICIAL: The story reads as obviously manufactured -- the reader
              feels insulted by the attempt at narrative manipulation

RECORD: narrative_authenticity: enum[AUTHENTIC, CRAFTED_AUTHENTIC, CRAFTED_OBVIOUS,
                                     TEMPLATE, ARTIFICIAL]

GATE RELEVANCE:
  AUTHENTIC or CRAFTED_AUTHENTIC → contributes to PASS
  CRAFTED_OBVIOUS → contributes to PASS_WITH_WARNINGS
  TEMPLATE → contributes to REVISE
  ARTIFICIAL → contributes to FAIL
```

### Step 6: Determine Gate Result

```
GATE DECISION:

  IF any CRITICAL violation exists → FAIL
  IF narrative_authenticity = ARTIFICIAL → FAIL
  IF slop_density >= 1.6 → FAIL
  IF more than 3 HIGH violations → FAIL

  IF zero CRITICAL AND zero HIGH AND slop_density <= 0.3
     AND narrative_authenticity in [AUTHENTIC, CRAFTED_AUTHENTIC] → PASS
  IF zero CRITICAL AND <= 1 HIGH AND slop_density <= 0.8
     AND narrative_authenticity in [AUTHENTIC, CRAFTED_AUTHENTIC, CRAFTED_OBVIOUS] → PASS_WITH_WARNINGS
  IF zero CRITICAL AND 2-3 HIGH AND slop_density <= 1.5 → REVISE
  ELSE → FAIL

  PASS: Ship as-is
  PASS_WITH_WARNINGS: Minor cleanup recommended, not blocking
  REVISE: Must fix before assembly (does not block other validators)
  FAIL: Must fix before assembly (blocks final assembly)
```

- [ ] All general language slop patterns scanned (filler, intensifiers, AI telltales, passive voice, adverbs)

- [ ] Zero-tolerance policy enforced: any CRITICAL violation = FAIL regardless of overall density

## Anti-Patterns

- **Severity deflation:** Marking exposition dumps as MODERATE because they are "brief" -- even a single sentence of exposition breaking the narrative is a CRITICAL violation in story context. Stories MUST maintain narrative tense.
- **Generic fix generation:** Writing "make the protagonist more specific" instead of "Replace 'John was a regular guy who struggled with his weight' with a specific scene showing John's weight affecting a specific life moment -- e.g., a moment of shame, a physical limitation, or a relationship consequence"
- **Cliche blindness:** Missing cliched transitions because they are so common they feel natural -- "everything changed when..." is a cliche even though it appears in thousands of sales letters. Its ubiquity is precisely the problem.
- **Believability calibration failure:** Flagging specific details as "too specific" when specificity is actually a strength -- the validator must distinguish between fabricated-feeling specificity ("at exactly 3:17 PM") and authentic-feeling specificity ("the third Tuesday in March, right after my kid's soccer practice")
- **Template defense:** Not flagging template-obvious structure because "all stories follow a structure" -- the STRUCTURE is expected; the VISIBILITY of the structure is the violation. The reader should experience events, not sections.
- **Result inflation tolerance:** Accepting inflated results because "the mechanism really is that effective" -- even if the mechanism IS effective, the STORY must present results with the Makepeace transparency paradox (acknowledging variability) to maintain credibility
- **Emotional flatness confusion:** Accepting intense LANGUAGE ("he was absolutely devastated, completely overwhelmed") as showing emotion -- intense language about emotions is still TELLING. Showing requires behavioral detail.

## Handoff

Pass `anti_slop_validation` to:
- **4.5-final-story-assembler.md** (required -- feeds anti_slop status and violation count into story-package.json)
- **STORY-AGENT.md** (Gate 4 evaluation -- status must be PASS or PASS_WITH_WARNINGS, zero CRITICAL violations)
- If FAIL → remediation routes based on violation categories:
  - Generic protagonist → back to **1.3-character-architect.md** (redesign protagonist with specificity)
  - Cliched discovery → back to **2.2-discovery-sequence-builder.md** (rewrite discovery beats)
