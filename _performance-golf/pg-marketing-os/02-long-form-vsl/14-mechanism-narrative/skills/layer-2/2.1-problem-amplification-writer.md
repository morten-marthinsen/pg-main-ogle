# Skill 14 — Microskill 2.1: Problem Amplification Writer

**Skill:** 11 — Mechanism Narrative
**Microskill:** 2.1 — Problem Amplification Writer
**Version:** 1.0
**Layer:** 2 (Draft Generation)
**Position:** First writer in Layer 2
**Type:** Prose Generation — Phase 1
**Dependencies:** 1.4-explanation-sequence-mapper (phase_1 spec), 1.3-emotional-arc-designer (phase_1 emotional target), 1.2-framing-pattern-selector (phase_1 frame), 0.4-checkpoint (confirmed state)
**Output:** phase-1-draft.json

---

## PURPOSE

Writes Phase 1 of the mechanism narrative: Problem Amplification. The prospect must feel the FULL WEIGHT of the problem before receiving the mechanism solution. This phase escalates the problem beyond what Skill 10's root cause narrative established — the reader already knows the root cause, but now they must feel how BAD that root cause really is. The amplification creates emotional urgency that makes the mechanism reveal feel like rescue, not education.

---

## IDENTITY

**What this IS:**
- A prose generator that writes the first section of mechanism narrative copy
- An emotional escalation engine that intensifies the problem before the solution arrives
- A continuity writer that picks up exactly where Skill 10's root cause narrative ended

**What this is NOT:**
- Not the mechanism reveal — Phase 1 does NOT name or introduce the mechanism
- Not the root cause narrative — that was Skill 10's job; this ESCALATES, not establishes
- Not a fear-mongering exercise — amplification is strategic, not gratuitous
- Not a standalone piece — must connect seamlessly to Skill 10's ending and Phase 2's bridge

## PROCESS

### Step 1: Determine Amplification Technique

```
BASED ON narrative type and emotional arc, SELECT amplification technique:

TECHNIQUE A: Fear Escalation
  Use When: Arc = fear_education_hope, or problem involves health/safety/mortality
  Pattern: Make the problem feel LETHAL, URGENT, or PERSONALLY PRESENT
  Key Moves:
    - Name specific physical consequences
    - Introduce time dimension ("right now, as you read this...")
    - Personalize ("lurking inside YOUR body")
    - Escalate from inconvenience to threat
  TIER1 Example (LA-3):
    "There's a lethal problem lurking deep inside your belly...
    And chances are, you have NO idea it's there."
    — Gundry escalates from "digestive discomfort" to "lethal problem"
    — Personalization: "inside YOUR belly"
    — Time urgency: "right now"
  Word Efficiency: 100-300 words

TECHNIQUE B: Shame Amplification
  Use When: Arc = shame_vindication_dignity, or problem involves identity/self-worth
  Pattern: Name the shame explicitly — what the reader is too embarrassed to say
  Key Moves:
    - Name the specific shame scenario
    - Validate that the feeling is real and painful
    - Show how it affects identity, relationships, daily life
    - Make the shame feel undeserved (setup for vindication)
  TIER1 Example (Montezuma):
    "The debilitating humiliation and shame of not being able
    to perform... of seeing the disappointment in her eyes...
    of making excuses to avoid intimacy..."
    — Names specific shame scenarios
    — Validates the pain
    — Shows relationship impact
  Word Efficiency: 100-250 words

TECHNIQUE C: Frustration Validation
  Use When: Arc = frustration_revelation_confidence, or problem involves failed solutions
  Pattern: List everything the reader has tried and validate that it should have worked
  Key Moves:
    - Name specific failed approaches by name
    - Acknowledge effort ("You did everything right")
    - Validate confusion ("How is it possible that nothing worked?")
    - Escalate frustration to "the system is broken, not you"
  TIER1 Example (DIM):
    "You've tried the diets. You've tried the exercise programs.
    You've cut the calories, counted the macros, sweated through
    the workouts... and your hormones are STILL out of control."
    — Lists specific attempts
    — Validates effort
    — Shows continued failure despite effort
  Word Efficiency: 150-350 words

TECHNIQUE D: Identity Wounding
  Use When: Arc involves identity, or narrative type = identity_driven
  Pattern: Show how the problem defines the reader's self-concept
  Key Moves:
    - Name the identity label
    - Show how others see them through the problem
    - Connect the problem to who they BELIEVE they are
    - Create distance between current identity and desired identity
  TIER1 Example (Superbrain):
    "The Boy With The Broken Brain. That's what they called me.
    And for years, I believed them."
    — Identity label assigned by others
    — Internalized as self-belief
    — Creates emotional wound to heal later
  Word Efficiency: 100-200 words

TECHNIQUE E: Quiz/Elimination
  Use When: Arc = curiosity_aha_urgency, or narrative type = paradigm_shift
  Pattern: Systematically eliminate EVERY conventional explanation
  Key Moves:
    - Present conventional wisdom as quiz questions
    - Show each one is wrong or incomplete
    - Eliminate until nothing is left — creating vacuum for mechanism
    - Make the reader feel their entire understanding was wrong
  TIER1 Example (BBW):
    "Is it your work ethic? No — you work harder than most.
    Your intelligence? No — you're smart enough.
    Your education? No — plenty of dropouts are millionaires.
    Your connections? No — many started with nothing.
    Then WHAT is it?"
    — Systematic elimination of every belief
    — Creates vacuum
    — Reader has no answer left — mechanism fills void
  Word Efficiency: 200-500 words
```

### Step 2: Draft the Amplification Copy

```
WRITE Phase 1 prose following these rules:

OPENING SENTENCE:
  - Must connect to Skill 10's ending emotional state
  - Must feel like a natural continuation, not a new section
  - Must intensify, not repeat, the root cause narrative

  PATTERN: "But [root cause] is only the beginning of the problem..."
  PATTERN: "And what [villain] has done to your [body/mind/life] goes
    even deeper than you realize..."
  PATTERN: "Here's what makes this so urgent..."

BODY OF AMPLIFICATION:
  - Deploy the selected amplification technique
  - Use the assigned framing pattern signal words
  - Hit the emotional intensity target from 1.3
  - Stay within word budget from 1.4

  STRUCTURAL PATTERN:
    1. Open with escalation statement (1-2 sentences)
    2. Provide specific, concrete details of the amplified problem (3-8 sentences)
    3. Personalize — connect to reader's specific experience (2-4 sentences)
    4. Culminate — reach peak problem intensity (1-2 sentences)
    5. Create vacuum — the reader NEEDS an answer (1 sentence)

CRITICAL RULES:
  - DO NOT name the mechanism — that is Phase 3
  - DO NOT offer hope — that comes in Phase 3+
  - DO NOT repeat Skill 10's root cause verbatim — ESCALATE it
  - DO NOT use generic fear language — be specific, concrete, visceral
  - DO reference the villain established in Skill 10
  - DO make the problem feel worse than the reader initially thought
  - DO create urgent need for a solution

CLOSING SENTENCE:
  - Must create a natural lead-in to Phase 2 (Root Cause Bridge)
  - Must leave the reader in the peak amplified emotional state
  - Must implicitly ask "Is there any hope?" without stating it

  PATTERN: "Until recently, there was no answer..."
  PATTERN: "For decades, nobody understood WHY..."
  PATTERN: "But what if everything you've been told was wrong?"
```

### Step 3: Apply Frame-Specific Language

```
USING assigned frame from sequence map:

IF frame == "hidden_truth":
  Language patterns: "What nobody told you..." / "The truth is..."
  / "Hidden in plain sight..." / "For years, this was overlooked..."
  Apply: Problem was always worse than anyone admitted

IF frame == "suppressed_secret":
  Language patterns: "What they don't want you to know..."
  / "Deliberately kept from..." / "While you suffered..."
  Apply: Problem was ALLOWED to persist by powerful interests

IF frame == "scientific_breakthrough":
  Language patterns: "Until recently, science had no explanation..."
  / "For decades, researchers struggled to understand..."
  Apply: Problem was genuinely unsolvable until new research

IF frame == "competitor_debunking":
  Language patterns: "Everything you tried was based on a lie..."
  / "The entire industry sold you the wrong answer..."
  Apply: Problem persisted because the dominant solution was wrong

IF frame == "personal_transformation":
  Language patterns: "I know exactly how you feel..."
  / "I lived with this for [X] years..."
  Apply: Problem is deeply personal, not abstract

IF frame == "ancient_wisdom_validated":
  Language patterns: "For centuries, this suffering was considered
    inevitable..." / "Modern medicine abandoned what the ancients knew..."
  Apply: Problem persisted because modern approaches ignored ancient wisdom

IF frame == "engineering_innovation":
  Language patterns: "The tools available simply weren't designed to
    address this..." / "No one had built a solution for THIS..."
  Apply: Problem required engineering that didn't exist
```

### Step 4: Verify Phase 1 Draft Quality

```
CHECK 1 — Emotional Escalation:
  Does the problem feel WORSE after reading Phase 1 than after Skill 10?
  If NOT: Rewrite with more specific, visceral details
  PASS threshold: Clear escalation from root cause to amplified problem

CHECK 2 — Mechanism Concealment:
  Does Phase 1 name or hint at the mechanism?
  If YES: Remove ALL mechanism references — Phase 1 is problem-only
  PASS threshold: Zero mechanism references

CHECK 3 — Word Budget:
  Is draft within 10% of allocated word budget?
  If OVER: Trim least impactful sentences
  If UNDER: Add specificity or personalization

CHECK 4 — Continuity:
  Does opening connect to Skill 10's ending?
  Does closing create vacuum for Phase 2 bridge?
  PASS threshold: No jarring transitions at either end

CHECK 5 — Specificity:
  Does amplification use concrete, specific details (not vague fear)?
  PASS threshold: At least 3 specific, concrete problem details

CHECK 6 — Villain Reference:
  Does Phase 1 reference the villain from Skill 16?
  PASS threshold: At least 1 villain callback (builds continuity)
```

## QUALITY CRITERIA

1. **Problem feels worse** — reader's perception of problem severity increases after Phase 1
2. **Mechanism hidden** — zero references to mechanism name, type, or solution
3. **Villain present** — at least one callback to Skill 10's villain
4. **Specific, not vague** — concrete details, named scenarios, visceral language
5. **Word budget met** — within 10% of allocation
6. **Emotional target hit** — Phase 1 achieves the intensity specified by 1.3
7. **Continuity maintained** — connects seamlessly to Skill 10's end and Phase 2's start

---

## FAILURE MODES

| Failure | Symptom | Resolution |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Mechanism leaked | Phase 1 names or hints at solution | Remove all solution language, rewrite closing |
| Generic fear language | "Things could get worse" without specifics | Replace with concrete, named consequences |
| Repeats Skill 10 | Phase 1 restates root cause without escalation | Rewrite to ESCALATE, not repeat |
| Too long | Over word budget by > 15% | Cut least emotional sentences, preserve specificity |
| Too short | Under budget by > 20% | Add personalization layer or additional consequence scenarios |
| Hope introduced early | Phrases like "but there is good news" | Remove all hope signals — Phase 1 is problem-only |
| Emotional flat line | No escalation from entry to target | Restructure: start lower, build to peak |
