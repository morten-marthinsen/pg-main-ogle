**Output:** phase-3-draft.json

---

## PURPOSE

Writes Phase 3 of the mechanism narrative: the dramatic moment where the mechanism is NAMED and introduced to the reader. This is the single most memorable moment in the entire mechanism narrative. The name must land with weight, create immediate conceptual understanding, and be memorable enough that the reader can recall and repeat it. This phase uses one of 5 naming architectures to deliver the mechanism name with maximum dramatic impact.

---

## IDENTITY

**What this IS:**
- A dramatic reveal writer that introduces the mechanism name at the peak emotional moment
- A naming architecture executor that follows specific naming patterns
- The creator of the mechanism's "brand moment" — the single phrase or sentence the reader remembers

**What this is NOT:**
- Not the full explanation — that is Phase 4 (2.3)
- Not a slow introduction — the naming moment is sharp, specific, and dramatic
- Not a product introduction — the mechanism is not the product (that bridge comes later)

## PROCESS

### Step 1: Select Naming Architecture

```
LOAD 5 naming architectures from tier1_index:

ARCHITECTURE 1: metaphorical_rename
  Pattern: Scientific term → vivid everyday metaphor name
  Structure: "[Scientific thing] — or as researchers call it, [metaphor name]"
  OR: "Think of it as [metaphor name] — a [plain description]"
  Examples:
    - AMPK → "The Master Switch" / "The Longevity Enzyme"
    - Gut lining → "Your body's Secret Service"
  Best For: Complex biological mechanisms needing immediate accessibility
  Naming Rules:
    - Name must be 2-4 words
    - Must create instant visual image
    - Must be specific (not generic like "the key" or "the secret")
    - Must be repeatable in conversation
  TIER1 Pattern (Gundry/LA-3):
    "Scientists call it AMPK, but think of it as the master switch
    inside every single one of your cells. When this switch is ON,
    your body burns fat, fights inflammation, and repairs itself.
    When it's OFF... everything starts breaking down."

ARCHITECTURE 2: proprietary_branded
  Pattern: Invented name that signals ownership and uniqueness
  Structure: "We call it [Branded Name] — the first [category] to [unique claim]"
  Examples:
    - "Square Face Technology" (SF1)
    - "Firming Flavonoids" (Montezuma)
  Best For: Engineered or compound-based mechanisms with unique specifications
  Naming Rules:
    - Name must sound proprietary (not generic)
    - Should imply engineering, design, or formulation
    - Must be distinct from any competitor naming
    - Capitalization signals brand status
  TIER1 Pattern (SF1):
    "Square Face Technology. It's the single biggest innovation in
    driver design in the last 20 years. And until now, no major
    manufacturer would touch it."

ARCHITECTURE 3: scientific_label
  Pattern: Full scientific name + immediate plain-language translation
  Structure: "[Full scientific name] — or in plain English, [what it does]"
  Examples:
    - "AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) — think of it as your body's
      built-in fat-burning switch"
    - "DIM (diindolylmethane) — a natural compound that rebalances
      your hormones at the cellular level"
  Best For: When scientific credibility is the primary frame
  Naming Rules:
    - Include the full scientific term (builds credibility)
    - Immediately follow with accessible translation (prevents confusion)
    - Translation must be one sentence, concrete
    - Scientific name should be pronounced/spelled for the reader
  TIER1 Pattern (DIM):
    "It's called DIM — diindolylmethane. Don't worry about the name.
    Just know that it's a natural metabolite found in cruciferous
    vegetables that your body needs to process and balance hormones."

ARCHITECTURE 4: villain_contrast
  Pattern: Mechanism name gains meaning by contrast with villain
  Structure: "[Villain name] keeps you trapped. [Mechanism name] sets you free."
  OR: "While [villain] holds you in [state], [mechanism] activates [desired state]"
  Examples:
    - "Billionaire Brain Wave" vs. "Slave Wave" (BBW)
    - "Theta" vs. "Beta" (same campaign, different layer)
  Best For: Paradigm shift narratives, binary frameworks
  Naming Rules:
    - BOTH names must be introduced (villain name + mechanism name)
    - Contrast must be BINARY — one or the other, no middle ground
    - Names must be emotionally loaded (not neutral)
    - Villain name should be established FIRST, mechanism name as the counter
  TIER1 Pattern (BBW):
    "Scientists discovered there are two types of brain waves that
    determine your financial destiny. The first — called the Beta
    Wave — is what they call 'The Slave Wave.' It keeps you trapped
    in a cycle of hard work and financial struggle. But the second
    wave... the Theta Wave... is what they call 'The Billionaire
    Brain Wave.' It's the frequency that activates your brain's
    natural ability to attract wealth."

ARCHITECTURE 5: historical_origin
  Pattern: Name derived from place, person, or time of discovery
  Structure: "In [place/time], [person] discovered [mechanism] — what locals call [name]"
  Examples:
    - "Montezuma's Secret" (geographic + historical)
    - "Danish Rosehips" (origin-labeled compound)
  Best For: Discovery narratives, ancient wisdom frames
  Naming Rules:
    - Origin must be SPECIFIC (not "an ancient civilization" — name it)
    - Historical connection must feel authentic, not forced
    - Name must carry the weight of its origin (exotic, prestigious, ancient)
    - If person-named, the person must be credible or legendary
  TIER1 Pattern (Montezuma):
    "The locals called it 'Montezuma's Secret' — a specific type of
    flavonoid found only in the cocoa grown in this remote village.
    For centuries, the men there had been consuming it daily. And
    the results were... remarkable."

SELECT architecture based on naming_approach from 1.1:
  IF naming_approach includes "metaphorical_rename" → Architecture 1
  IF naming_approach includes "proprietary_branded" → Architecture 2
  IF naming_approach includes "scientific_label" → Architecture 3
  IF naming_approach includes "villain_contrast" → Architecture 4
  IF naming_approach includes "historical_origin" → Architecture 5
  IF naming_approach == "any" → Select based on mechanism_type and e_level
```

### Step 2: Design the Buildup Sequence

```
THE NAMING MOMENT HAS 4 BEATS:

BEAT 1: Deliberate Buildup (30-40% of Phase 3 words)
  Purpose: Create anticipation — reader EXPECTS something important is coming
  Techniques:
    - Promise language: "What they found changes everything..."
    - Tension holders: "And it comes down to a single..."
    - Anticipation markers: "It has a name. And once you hear it,
      you'll never see [problem] the same way."
  Rules:
    - Do NOT name the mechanism yet
    - Do NOT rush — buildup creates the drama
    - Each sentence should increase anticipation
    - The reader should be leaning forward

BEAT 2: Contextual Framing (15-25% of Phase 3 words)
  Purpose: Position the mechanism in its proper context BEFORE naming
  Techniques:
    - Category placement: "It's an enzyme..." / "It's a frequency..."
    - Discovery context: "Found in..." / "Identified by..."
    - Significance signal: "The single most important..."
  Rules:
    - Provide just enough context for the name to land with meaning
    - Do NOT over-explain (that is Phase 4)
    - Frame must match the selected architecture

BEAT 3: The Name Drop (5-10% of Phase 3 words — a single sentence or phrase)
  Purpose: The actual moment the mechanism name is spoken
  Techniques:
    - Short sentence: "They call it AMPK."
    - Reveal structure: "It's called [name]."
    - Dramatic isolation: Put the name in its own sentence or even its own line
  Rules:
    - The name must stand ALONE — not buried in a complex sentence
    - The sentence containing the name should be the SHORTEST in the phase
    - If VSL: this is a pause moment — visual emphasis
    - If print: this can be bold, offset, or in a callout

BEAT 4: Immediate Positioning Statement (20-30% of Phase 3 words)
  Purpose: Tell the reader what the mechanism MEANS immediately after naming
  Techniques:
    - Function statement: "[Name] is what controls [key function]"
    - Contrast statement: "When [name] is active, [good]. When it's not, [bad]."
    - Significance statement: "[Name] is the reason [big claim]"
  Rules:
    - Must follow the name within 1-2 sentences
    - Must be concrete, not abstract
    - Must create desire to learn more (Phase 4)
    - Must NOT be the full explanation — just the positioning
```

### Step 3: Draft the Phase 3 Copy

```
WRITE Phase 3 prose combining the 4 beats:

STRUCTURAL TEMPLATE:
  [BEAT 1 — Buildup: 2-5 sentences of rising anticipation]
  [BEAT 2 — Context: 1-3 sentences of framing]
  [BEAT 3 — Name Drop: 1 sentence, short, dramatic]
  [BEAT 4 — Positioning: 2-4 sentences of immediate meaning]

VOICE AND TONE:
  - The naming moment should feel like a GIFT being unwrapped
  - Tone shifts from tension (Beat 1) to revelation (Beat 3) to clarity (Beat 4)
  - The name should feel inevitable — like this was always the answer
  - Avoid breathless hype — let the drama come from the structure, not adjectives

ARCHITECTURE-SPECIFIC DRAFTING:

  IF metaphorical_rename:
    Beat 1: Build to the concept (the "switch," the "enzyme," the "agent")
    Beat 2: Frame the scientific reality
    Beat 3: "Scientists call it [scientific name]. But think of it as [metaphor name]."
    Beat 4: What the metaphor means in practical terms

  IF proprietary_branded:
    Beat 1: Build to the innovation ("no one had ever engineered...")
    Beat 2: Frame the engineering challenge
    Beat 3: "[Branded Name]."
    Beat 4: What makes it proprietary and why that matters

  IF scientific_label:
    Beat 1: Build to the discovery
    Beat 2: Frame the research context
    Beat 3: "It's called [Full Scientific Name]."
    Beat 4: "In plain terms, it [function]. Don't worry about the name —
      just know that [significance]."

  IF villain_contrast:
    Beat 1: Build to the binary ("there are two forces at work...")
    Beat 2: Introduce villain name FIRST (reader knows the bad one)
    Beat 3: "But there's another wave. They call it [Mechanism Name]."
    Beat 4: Binary contrast — villain = bad state, mechanism = desired state

  IF historical_origin:
    Beat 1: Build through the story ("in a remote village..." / "centuries ago...")
    Beat 2: The discovery moment in the story
    Beat 3: "The locals called it [Name]." / "[Person] named it [Name]."
    Beat 4: What modern science revealed about this ancient knowledge
```

### Step 4: Verify Naming Memorability

```
CHECK the mechanism name against MEMORABILITY criteria:

  SYLLABLE COUNT: 2-4 syllables ideal
    "AMPK" — 4 letters, spoken as initialism = PASS
    "Square Face Technology" — multi-word but vivid = PASS
    "Firming Flavonoids" — alliterative, 6 syllables = PASS
    "AMP-activated protein kinase" — too long alone, needs metaphor rename = NEEDS PAIR

  CONCRETE IMAGERY: Does the name create a visual?
    "Master Switch" — YES (physical switch)
    "Billionaire Brain Wave" — YES (wave + wealth)
    "Danish Rosehips" — YES (geographic + botanical)
    "DIM" — NO alone (needs plain-language pairing) = NEEDS PAIR

  EMOTIONAL LOADING: Does the name carry emotional weight?
    "Slave Wave" — YES (negative, powerful)
    "Longevity Enzyme" — YES (aspiration)
    "Montezuma's Secret" — YES (mystery + history)

  REPEATABILITY: Can someone tell a friend about this easily?
    Test: "I learned about this thing called ___. It's ___."
    If the name fills the blank naturally = PASS
    If explanation needed to even say the name = NEEDS simplification

  IF memorability check fails on 2+ criteria:
    FLAG: "Mechanism name may not be memorable enough.
      Consider adding a metaphorical rename layer."
    DO NOT change the name (confirmed in checkpoint)
    DO add a metaphorical rename alongside the scientific name
```

### Step 5: Verify Phase 3 Quality

```
CHECK 1 — Drama:
  Does the naming moment feel dramatic, not flat?
  Is Beat 3 the shortest, most impactful sentence?
  PASS: Name stands alone with weight

CHECK 2 — Architecture Followed:
  Does the draft follow the selected naming architecture pattern?
  PASS: Architecture recognizable in structure

CHECK 3 — Name Memorable:
  Memorability check from Step 4 passes?
  PASS: Name meets 3+ of 4 criteria

CHECK 4 — Not Over-Explained:
  Does Phase 3 stop at positioning, not full explanation?
  Phase 4 must have material to work with
  PASS: Reader understands WHAT but wants to know HOW

CHECK 5 — Emotional Target:
  Does the naming moment achieve the emotional shift from 1.3?
  PASS: Reader feels the designated phase 3 emotion

CHECK 6 — Word Budget:
  Within 10% of allocation?
  PASS: Within range
```

## QUALITY CRITERIA

1. **Name introduced with drama** — Beat 3 is the most impactful single sentence
2. **Architecture followed** — the naming pattern matches the selected architecture
3. **Name is memorable** — passes memorability check (syllable, imagery, emotion, repeatability)
4. **Not over-explained** — positions the mechanism without exhausting the explanation
5. **Buildup creates anticipation** — reader expects something important before the name drops
6. **Positioning is concrete** — reader knows WHAT the mechanism does, not just its name
7. **Word budget met** — within 10% of allocation

---

## FAILURE MODES

| Failure | Symptom | Resolution |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Name buried in long sentence | Beat 3 is not the shortest sentence | Isolate the name into its own short sentence |
| No buildup — name appears too early | Reader isn't prepared for the reveal | Add 2-3 anticipation sentences before Beat 3 |
| Name is not memorable | Fails 2+ memorability criteria | Add metaphorical rename alongside scientific name |
| Over-explained | Phase 3 contains full mechanism explanation | Cut to positioning only, move detail to Phase 4 |
| Architecture mismatch | Draft doesn't follow selected architecture | Rewrite following architecture template |
| Flat emotional beat | Naming moment feels informational, not dramatic | Restructure buildup to peak at Beat 3 |
| Name sounds generic | "The Solution" / "The Answer" / "The Method" | Use specific, concrete naming per architecture |

## EXAMPLES

### Example 1: LA-3 / AMPK — Metaphorical Rename

```
Architecture: metaphorical_rename
Word Budget: 204 words

Draft:
  [Beat 1 — Buildup]:
  "And it all comes down to a single molecule. One tiny enzyme
  that exists inside every cell of your body. An enzyme that
  scientists have spent decades studying — and that most doctors
  have never heard of.

  [Beat 2 — Context]:
  It was first identified by researchers at one of the most
  prestigious medical institutions in the world. And what they
  found about this enzyme rocked the scientific community.

  [Beat 3 — Name Drop]:
  They call it AMPK.

  [Beat 4 — Positioning]:
  Think of AMPK as a master switch inside every one of your cells.
  When AMPK is switched ON, your body burns fat for fuel, fights
  inflammation, and repairs damaged cells. Your metabolism hums.
  Your energy soars. Your body acts YOUNG.

  But when AMPK is switched OFF — which happens naturally as you
  age and is ACCELERATED by the modern diet — everything goes
  in reverse. Fat accumulates. Inflammation spreads. Cells
  break down faster than they can repair.

  And here's what nobody told you: after age 30, your AMPK
  activity starts declining. Every year. Silently."

Naming Reference:
  primary_name: "AMPK"
  secondary_name: "master switch"
  shorthand: "AMPK/the master switch"
```
