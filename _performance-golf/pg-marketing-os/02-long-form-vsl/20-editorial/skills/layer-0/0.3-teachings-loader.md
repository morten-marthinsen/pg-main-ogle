## THE 6 EXPERT LENSES

### 1. Clayton Makepeace — Flow & Momentum

**Core Philosophy:** Copy should flow like a river, sweeping the reader along without friction.

**Editorial Principles:**

1. **Transitions are bridges** — They carry the reader from one thought to the next. Bad transitions are potholes.

2. **Singular voice** — Copy should sound like ONE person speaking. Any shift in voice breaks the spell.

3. **Momentum is everything** — Every paragraph should create forward pull. If momentum dies, the reader dies.

4. **Repetition builds belief** — Key phrases appearing 5-10+ times throughout creates subconscious conviction.

5. **Pacing variation** — Long paragraphs followed by short punches. Never monotonous rhythm.

**What Makepeace Would Flag:**
- Jarring transitions between sections
- Voice shifts mid-copy
- Paragraphs that kill momentum
- Missing repetition of key themes
- Monotonous rhythm patterns

---

### 2. Gary Halbert — Entertainment & Engagement

**Core Philosophy:** Boring is the cardinal sin. Copy must entertain to persuade.

**Editorial Principles:**

1. **Personality creates connection** — Generic copy is forgettable. Distinctive voice is memorable.

2. **Make them forget they're being sold** — If it FEELS like copy, it's not good copy.

3. **Hook power is everything** — First sentence must grab. If they don't read the first, they won't read the rest.

4. **Stories trump claims** — A good story is more persuasive than a list of benefits.

5. **Would they share this?** — If the copy isn't interesting enough to mention to someone else, it's not interesting enough.

**What Halbert Would Flag:**
- Boring, generic openings
- Lack of personality or voice
- Copy that FEELS like copy
- Weak or forgettable stories
- Nothing "shareable" or memorable

---

### 3. Eugene Schwartz — Sophistication & Awareness

**Core Philosophy:** You cannot create desire. You can only identify and channel existing desire.

**Editorial Principles:**

1. **Awareness spectrum determines approach:**
   - Most Aware: Just name the product
   - Product Aware: Prove your product is best
   - Solution Aware: Show how your product delivers
   - Problem Aware: Agitate problem, then reveal solution
   - Unaware: Start with shared worldview/story

2. **Sophistication determines claim boldness:**
   - Stage 1: Direct claim works
   - Stage 2: Enlarged claim
   - Stage 3: Mechanism differentiation
   - Stage 4: Improved mechanism
   - Stage 5: Pure identification/story

3. **Headline carries 80% of the weight** — Get it right or nothing else matters.

4. **Channel existing desire** — Find what they already want and connect it to your offer.

**What Schwartz Would Flag:**
- Awareness mismatch (too salesy for aware, too educational for unaware)
- Claims too bold/weak for sophistication level
- Headline not calibrated to market
- Creating desire instead of channeling it

---

### 4. David Ogilvy — Credibility & Clarity

**Core Philosophy:** The consumer isn't a moron. She's your wife.

**Editorial Principles:**

1. **Specifics are more believable than generalities** — "47% increase" beats "significant improvement"

2. **Clarity trumps cleverness** — If they don't understand it, they won't buy it.

3. **Facts, not adjectives** — "A quiet car" is weak. "At 60mph, the loudest noise is the clock" is powerful.

4. **Elegance serves persuasion** — Well-crafted copy signals competence. Sloppy copy signals scam.

5. **Research before writing** — Every claim should be defensible.

**What Ogilvy Would Flag:**
- Vague, unsubstantiated claims
- Clever-but-confusing language
- Adjective-heavy, fact-light copy
- Sloppy grammar or presentation
- Undefensible claims

---

### 5. Craig Clemens — Mechanism Architecture & Scientific Positioning

**Core Philosophy:** The mechanism is the hero. Make it feel scientifically advanced yet accessible.

**Editorial Principles:**

1. **Disruptive promise** — Opening should INVERT expected belief, not just promise benefit.

2. **Binary reframe** — "What they think vs. What's really true" must be explicit.

3. **External failure attribution** — Reader failure is SYSTEM failure, not personal failure. Vindication, not shame.

4. **Graspable metaphor** — Complex biology must be explained in 12-year-old language. ("Master switch," "grape vs. raisin")

5. **Branded mechanism** — Mechanism needs a NAMED anchor phrase. ("Leaky Gut," "AMPK Master Switch")

6. **Institutional proof priority** — Science-first, testimonials-second. Lead with UCLA, not "Mary from Ohio."

7. **Specific credential stacking** — UNUSUAL and SPECIFIC credentials. "Inventor of [device]" beats "board-certified."

8. **Fear-forward framing** — Open with DANGER before desire. Create urgency through threat.

9. **Complex mechanism + Simple solution** — Sophisticated biology, trivial execution. ("Just take one capsule")

10. **Paradigm-shift language** — "This changes everything" framing, not incremental improvement.

**What Clemens Would Flag:**
- Mechanism not positioned as hero
- Generic credentials
- Testimonials before science
- Reader shamed for past failure
- Mechanism not named/branded
- No graspable metaphor
- Benefit-forward instead of fear-forward
- Complex solution matching complex mechanism

---

### 6. Dan Kennedy — Direct Response Fundamentals

**Core Philosophy:** The offer is king. Make it impossible to say no.

**Editorial Principles:**

1. **Offer clarity is non-negotiable** — They must know EXACTLY what they get.

2. **Risk reversal strength** — All risk on seller. Guarantee should make NOT buying feel risky.

3. **Urgency must be believable** — Manufactured urgency is worse than no urgency.

4. **Checkout prevention** — Anticipate and address every objection before the close.

5. **Price framing** — Anchor high, reveal low. Make the price feel like a steal.

6. **Clear next step** — CTA must be crystal clear. No confusion about what to do.

7. **Reasons to act NOW** — Give them real, believable reasons why waiting is a mistake.

**What Kennedy Would Flag:**
- Unclear or confusing offer
- Risk still on buyer
- Fake/manufactured urgency
- Unanswered objections
- Poor price positioning
- Weak or confusing CTA
- No compelling reason to act now
