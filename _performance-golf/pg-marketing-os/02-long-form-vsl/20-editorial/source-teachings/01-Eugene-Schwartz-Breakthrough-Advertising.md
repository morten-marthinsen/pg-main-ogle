# Eugene Schwartz - Breakthrough Advertising

## Source Overview
- **Author:** Eugene M. Schwartz
- **Published:** 1966
- **Status:** Seminal text in direct response copywriting
- **Relevance to Mechanisms:** Foundational - introduced the concept of mechanism sophistication in advertising

---

## Core Philosophy: Mass Desire

> "Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product. This is the copywriter's task: not to create this mass desire--but to channel and direct it."

**Key Insight:** Advertising doesn't create desire; it channels what already exists. The mechanism becomes the vehicle for channeling that existing desire into belief in your specific solution.

---

## The Five Levels of Market Sophistication

Schwartz identified that markets evolve through predictable stages, and the role of mechanism changes dramatically at each stage:

### Stage 1: First to Market
- **Approach:** Direct claim
- **Mechanism Role:** Not needed
- **Example:** "Lose weight now"
- **Key Rule:** Be simple and direct. Don't get in your own way with complexity.

### Stage 2: Competition Emerges
- **Approach:** Expanded/amplified claim
- **Mechanism Role:** Still minimal
- **Example:** "Lose 3 pounds in 7 days"
- **Key Rule:** Drive the direct claim to its absolute limit.

### Stage 3: Introduce a Unique Mechanism
- **Approach:** Mechanism-backed claim
- **Mechanism Role:** CRITICAL - This is where mechanisms become essential
- **Example:** "Take this pill that blocks the absorption of fat in your intestines, and you lose weight in seven days or less."
- **Key Rule:** Consumers are skeptical of exaggerated claims. You need a NEW MECHANISM: a new way to make the old promise work.

> "A new mechanism is a new way of making an old promise work. It's a different process, a unique tool, or a fresh chance for a new possibility of success where sadness has resulted before."

### Stage 4: Enlarge or Replace the Mechanism
- **Approach:** Enhanced or new mechanism
- **Mechanism Role:** Must be bigger, better, or different
- **Key Rule:** Your competitors have mechanisms too. Either enlarge your mechanism (make it more impressive) OR find a new one entirely.

### Stage 5: Identification and Emotional Connection
- **Approach:** Pure identification
- **Mechanism Role:** Transcended - focus shifts to identity
- **Key Rule:** Return to the prospect. Name their dominant thoughts. Enter the conversation in their mind. Offer dramatic statements that verbalize what they're feeling.

## The Mechanization Technique

Schwartz outlined "Mechanization" as one of the key techniques for breakthrough copy, specifically teaching "How to Verbally Prove That Your Product Does What You Claim."

### Three Stages of Verbal Proof:

**Stage One: Name the Mechanism**
- Give it a memorable, specific name
- Make it tangible and real

**Stage Two: Describe the Mechanism**
- Explain how it works
- Reveal the inner workings
- Build understanding and trust

**Stage Three: Feature the Mechanism**
- Make it the star of the ad
- For high sophistication markets, put the mechanism in the headline
- Shift emphasis from WHAT the product does to HOW it works

### Example Application:
- **Before (Promise-Based):** "Lose 3 kg in two hours now"
- **After (Mechanism-Based):** "Get fat to float out of your body"

## The Five States of Customer Awareness

While distinct from market sophistication, awareness levels determine HOW you present your mechanism:

### 1. Most Aware
- Customer knows product, knows what it does, knows they want it
- Headline needs only product name and a bargain price
- Mechanism role: Not featured prominently

### 2. Product-Aware
- Prospect isn't fully aware of all product does
- Needs convincing of performance level
- Mechanism role: Supports claims

### 3. Solution-Aware
- Knows desired result exists
- Doesn't know your product delivers it
- Mechanism role: Bridges solution to your specific product

### 4. Problem-Aware
- Knows they have a problem
- Doesn't know solutions exist
- Mechanism role: Introduces new possibility

### 5. Unaware
- No knowledge of problem or solution
- Mechanism role: Part of story/education framework

## Key Quotes for Mechanism Creation

> "What this market needs now is a new device to make all these old claims become fresh and believable to them again. In other words, A NEW MECHANISM: a new way to make the old promise work."

> "The elaboration is concentrated on the mechanism, rather than on the promise."

> "Customers often need a tangible reason to believe a product works. Highlighting a patented formula, revolutionary technology, or specific process can set your offering apart."

---

## Application Framework for Mechanism Creation

Based on Schwartz's teachings:

1. **Diagnose Market Sophistication Level**
   - How many similar products have they been told about before?
   - What promises have already been made?
   - What mechanisms are already in play?

2. **Determine Mechanism Strategy**
   - Stage 1-2: Lead with promise, mechanism supports
   - Stage 3: Introduce mechanism as primary differentiator
   - Stage 4: Enlarge existing or create new mechanism
   - Stage 5: Transcend mechanism, lead with identification

3. **Execute Mechanization Technique**
   - Name it (memorable, specific)
   - Describe it (how it works)
   - Feature it (make it the star when appropriate)

4. **Match to Awareness Level**
   - Adjust mechanism presentation based on prospect's current knowledge

---

## Why This Matters for Modern Mechanism Creation

Schwartz's framework provides the strategic foundation for understanding WHEN and HOW to deploy mechanisms. In 2024/2025, most markets are at Stage 3-4 sophistication, meaning:

- Direct claims alone no longer work
- Mechanisms are not optional--they're required
- The mechanism must feel new and different
- Eventually, even mechanisms become commoditized (Stage 4-5)

The copywriter who masters market sophistication diagnosis and mechanism deployment has a significant competitive advantage.
