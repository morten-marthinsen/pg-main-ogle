## Purpose

Apply Craig Clemens' Vagueness Principle across the entire lead. Ensure problem callouts are appropriately vague (reader fills in their own experience) while results and mechanism teases are appropriately specific and vivid. The Clemens Rule governs the inverse relationship between problem specificity and result specificity: the less specific the problem, the more the reader self-identifies; the more specific the result, the more the reader desires it.

## The Clemens Rule

The core calibration principle:

**PROBLEMS — Keep Vague:**
The reader must fill in their OWN experience. Vague problem language triggers personal memory retrieval — the prospect supplies the emotional weight.

- CORRECT: "Have you ever seen a girl you wanted to meet... but WUSSED OUT?"
- WRONG: "Have you ever seen a hot girl with all the right curves, striking blue eyes, at a store in the mall?"

Why wrong: Over-specific problem descriptions NARROW the audience. The reader who saw a brunette at the gym does NOT relate to the blonde at the mall. Vague descriptions let EVERY reader insert their own version.

**RESULTS — Make Vivid and Specific:**
Results must paint a picture the prospect can taste. Specificity here AMPLIFIES desire because the reader is now projecting themselves INTO a detailed future.

- CORRECT: "Your phone buzzes every night with women wondering what you're doing... your buddies want to know your secret... you walk into a room and feel eyes on you"
- WRONG: "You'll have more success with women" / "Your dating life will improve"

Why wrong: Vague results create NO emotional pull. The prospect cannot desire what they cannot picture.

**MECHANISM TEASES — Specific Names and Tangible Details:**
Mechanism teases need specific naming and tangible details to create curiosity gaps and credibility signals.

- CORRECT: "5 delicious 'firming' fruits" / "a simple 5-second breakfast ritual" / "a forgotten Thai herb called Kaempferia Parviflora"
- WRONG: "certain foods" / "a morning routine" / "a natural ingredient"

Why wrong: Vague mechanism teases create NO curiosity gap. Specific naming signals insider knowledge and creates an information gap the prospect needs to close.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Segment Lead Text by Function

```
PARSE the full lead draft into functional segments:

SEGMENT TYPES:
  PROBLEM_TEXT: All sentences describing the prospect's current problem,
                pain, frustration, or undesirable situation
  RESULT_TEXT:  All sentences describing desired outcomes, benefits,
                future states, or what becomes possible
  MECHANISM_TEASE_TEXT: All sentences naming, hinting at, or teasing
                       the mechanism — names, ingredients, methods
  NEUTRAL_TEXT: Transitions, attention commands, credibility statements,
                structural connectors

TAG each sentence with its segment type.
LOG: total_sentences, sentences_per_type
```

### Step 2: Audit Problem Text for Over-Specificity

```
FOR each PROBLEM_TEXT sentence:

  SCAN for over-specific markers:

  PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION EXCESS:
    - Named body parts with visual detail ("your flabby arms", "your protruding belly")
    - Specific appearance attributes ("your thinning gray hair")
    → SHOULD BE: "that part of your body you're embarrassed about"
    → SHOULD BE: "looking in the mirror and not recognizing yourself"

  SCENARIO OVER-SPECIFICATION:
    - Named locations ("at the grocery store", "at your office")
    - Named people ("your boss", "your mother-in-law")
    - Specific times ("last Tuesday", "on your 50th birthday")
    → SHOULD BE: Unnamed situations the reader fills in
    → SHOULD BE: "ever been in a situation where..."

  DEMOGRAPHIC NARROWING:
    - Age-specific ("if you're over 50")
    - Gender-specific when unnecessary
    - Income-specific when unnecessary
    → FLAG unless deliberately targeting a specific demographic

  EMOTIONAL OVER-SPECIFICATION:
    - Naming the exact emotion instead of the situation
    - "You felt humiliated" vs. "You know that feeling..."
    → SHOULD BE: Describe the SITUATION, let the reader NAME their own emotion

  FOR each flagged item:
    LOG: location, flagged_text, violation_type, remediation_suggestion
    CLASSIFY severity: minor (slightly narrowing) | major (significantly narrowing)
```

### Step 3: Audit Result Text for Under-Specificity

```
FOR each RESULT_TEXT sentence:

  SCAN for under-specific markers:

  VAGUE OUTCOME LANGUAGE:
    - "improve your health" → SHOULD BE: "wake up at 6am feeling 25 again"
    - "make more money" → SHOULD BE: "check your account and see an extra $3,247"
    - "feel better" → SHOULD BE: "that surge of energy at 3pm when everyone else crashes"
    - "have better relationships" → SHOULD BE: "your spouse looks at you the way
       they did on your wedding day"

  MISSING SENSORY DETAILS:
    - No visual imagery (what does the result LOOK like?)
    - No kinesthetic imagery (what does it FEEL like?)
    - No social proof imagery (what do OTHERS notice/say?)
    → Each result should include at least 1 sensory channel

  MISSING TEMPORAL SPECIFICITY:
    - "eventually" / "over time" / "gradually"
    → SHOULD BE: "within 7 days" / "by next Tuesday" / "before your next check-up"

  MISSING SOCIAL DIMENSION:
    - Results described only in first person
    → ADD: What others notice, say, ask about
    → "Your friends start asking what you've been doing differently"

  FOR each flagged item:
    LOG: location, flagged_text, violation_type, remediation_suggestion
    CLASSIFY severity: minor (somewhat vague) | major (creates no desire)
```

### Step 4: Audit Mechanism Teases for Specificity

```
FOR each MECHANISM_TEASE_TEXT sentence:

  VERIFY specific naming is present:

  NAMED MECHANISM:
    - The mechanism must be referred to by its SPECIFIC NAME
    - NOT "a new method" or "this approach" or "a solution"
    - YES: "the Cortisol Reset Protocol" / "the 5-Second Thyroid Fix"
    → FLAG any generic reference to the mechanism

  TANGIBLE TEASE DETAILS:
    - Numbers: "5 fruits" / "3-step" / "60-second"
    - Adjectives that create curiosity: "forbidden" / "forgotten" / "delicious"
    - Origin markers: "Thai herb" / "Japanese technique" / "Harvard-discovered"
    → FLAG any tease that lacks tangible detail

  CURIOSITY GAP VERIFICATION:
    - Does the tease create a specific information gap?
    - "A forgotten Thai herb" — YES (which herb? why forgotten?)
    - "A natural ingredient" — NO (too generic, no gap)
    → FLAG teases that fail to create a specific curiosity gap

  FOR each flagged item:
    LOG: location, flagged_text, violation_type, remediation_suggestion
    CLASSIFY severity: minor (weak tease) | major (no curiosity gap)
```

### Step 5: Generate Remediation for Flagged Items

```
FOR each flagged item across all three audits:

  GENERATE specific remediation:

  PROBLEM OVER-SPECIFICITY REMEDIATION:
    TECHNIQUE 1 — Remove visual details, keep emotional situation:
      BEFORE: "staring at your sagging belly in the bathroom mirror"
      AFTER:  "that moment in front of the mirror when you realize
               something has to change"

    TECHNIQUE 2 — Replace named scenario with universal pattern:
      BEFORE: "sitting at your desk at 3pm, unable to focus on the spreadsheet"
      AFTER:  "hitting that wall in the afternoon where your brain just... stops"

    TECHNIQUE 3 — Use "you know that feeling" framing:
      BEFORE: "feeling embarrassed at the beach in your swimsuit"
      AFTER:  "you know that feeling when you want to enjoy the moment
               but you're too busy thinking about how you look?"

  RESULT UNDER-SPECIFICITY REMEDIATION:
    TECHNIQUE 1 — Add sensory channel:
      BEFORE: "you'll have more energy"
      AFTER:  "you spring out of bed at 6am — no alarm needed — feeling
               like you could run a mile before breakfast"

    TECHNIQUE 2 — Add social dimension:
      BEFORE: "you'll look younger"
      AFTER:  "your coworkers start asking if you went on vacation...
               your spouse can't stop staring"

    TECHNIQUE 3 — Add temporal anchor:
      BEFORE: "your health will improve"
      AFTER:  "within the first 72 hours, you'll notice the brain fog
               lifting... by day 7, your pants feel different...
               by day 30, people are asking what your secret is"

  MECHANISM TEASE UNDER-SPECIFICITY REMEDIATION:
    TECHNIQUE 1 — Add number or quantity:
      BEFORE: "a natural method"
      AFTER:  "a simple 5-second ritual"

    TECHNIQUE 2 — Add origin or discovery marker:
      BEFORE: "an ingredient"
      AFTER:  "a high-altitude Himalayan root that ancient monks used
               for mental clarity"

    TECHNIQUE 3 — Add curiosity adjective:
      BEFORE: "a protein"
      AFTER:  "a 'banned' peptide that Big Pharma tried to suppress"

  RECORD: original_text, remediated_text, technique_used
```

### Step 6: Score Vagueness Calibration

```
CALCULATE section scores:

problems_appropriately_vague:
  total_problem_sentences: integer
  flagged_over_specific: integer
  pass_rate: float (flagged / total)
  status: PASS if pass_rate >= 0.85, FAIL otherwise

results_appropriately_specific:
  total_result_sentences: integer
  flagged_under_specific: integer
  pass_rate: float (1 - flagged / total)
  status: PASS if pass_rate >= 0.85, FAIL otherwise

mechanism_teases_specific:
  total_tease_sentences: integer
  flagged_under_specific: integer
  pass_rate: float (1 - flagged / total)
  status: PASS if pass_rate >= 0.85, FAIL otherwise

overall_calibration:
  status: PASS if all three sections PASS
  status: FAIL if any section FAILS
```

## Anti-Patterns

**The Over-Painted Problem:**
Writing problem callouts that read like a novel — "You wake up at 3am, lying in your king-size bed, staring at the ceiling fan spinning slowly, feeling the weight of your 237-pound body pressing into the mattress." This narrows the audience to one person. Strip the details; keep the feeling.

**The Lazy Result:**
Writing results that sound like a press release — "Experience improved outcomes across all metrics." This creates zero emotional pull. Results must be cinematic — the reader needs to SEE the result, FEEL it in their body, and NOTICE others reacting to it.

**The Generic Tease:**
Referring to the mechanism as "this method" or "the solution" instead of by its specific name. Generic teases signal that the copywriter has nothing genuinely new to offer. Specificity signals insider knowledge.

**The Reversed Clemens:**
Making problems specific and results vague — the exact OPPOSITE of the Clemens Rule. This is the most common failure pattern. It narrows the audience at the problem (fewer people identify) and creates no desire at the result (nobody wants vague improvement).

**The Number Dump:**
Over-correcting result vagueness by stuffing every result sentence with statistics — "47% improvement in 3.2 days across 127 biomarkers." Results should be EXPERIENTIAL, not clinical. Numbers support but do not replace vivid imagery.
