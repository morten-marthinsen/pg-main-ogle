---

## Purpose

Craft the opening hook sentence — the first 5-15 words the prospect reads after the headline. The hook must use a specific opening device and create an irresistible pull to continue reading. It is the single highest-leverage sentence in the entire campaign: if the hook fails, nothing downstream matters. The hook must continue the emotional momentum of the headline while introducing the lead's trajectory.

---

## Persona Deployment

**Primary Persona:** The Legendary Copywriter — Master Copy Composite
Brings decades of direct response intuition. Every word in the hook is chosen for maximum stopping power. Thinks in terms of "what makes the eye freeze on the page" and "what makes the finger stop scrolling."

**Secondary Persona:** Jake Torres — Viral Content Architect
Brings modern attention dynamics. Understands how hooks function in digital environments where attention is measured in fractions of a second. Tests hooks against scroll-stopping benchmarks from social and paid media.

## Execution Protocol

### Step 1: Define Opening Device Specifications

```
LOAD lead_type_classification.opening_device

DEVICE SPECIFICATIONS:

  shocking_stat:
    Structure: A specific, startling number or percentage as the opening element
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Real data point, unexpected magnitude, immediate relevance
    Example: "In the last 12 months, his readers collected gains of 437%..."
    Power source: Specificity + unexpected magnitude

  pattern_interrupt:
    Structure: A statement that breaks the expected pattern of the niche
    Word count: 5-12 words
    Requirements: Subverts assumption, creates cognitive dissonance
    Example: "I'm probably the worst investor in America."
    Power source: Contradiction + identification

  authority_flex:
    Structure: A credential or institutional reference that demands attention
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Genuine authority, recognizable institution, implies insider access
    Example: "A former White House Doctor has just gone public with..."
    Power source: Institutional credibility + insider implication

  doctor_shock:
    Structure: Medical authority encountering something surprising or unprecedented
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Medical credential + unexpected finding or reaction
    Example: "When Dr. [Name] first saw the lab results, he called his team in immediately."
    Power source: Authority + surprise + urgency

  threat_frame:
    Structure: A named threat, villain, or danger introduced immediately
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Specific threat named, consequences implied, affects the reader
    Example: "On October 15th, the biggest financial betrayal in American history will..."
    Power source: Named threat + personal stakes + specificity

  rhetorical_question:
    Structure: A question that surfaces a latent concern the prospect already has
    Word count: 5-12 words
    Requirements: Must surface existing concern (not create new one), answerable with "yes"
    Example: "Have you noticed your energy crashing every afternoon around 2 PM?"
    Power source: Self-identification + "that's me" recognition

  problem_callout:
    Structure: Direct identification of the prospect's core pain or frustration
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Specific enough to resonate, vague enough for self-projection (Clemens)
    Example: "If you've ever felt frustrated watching your savings shrink while..."
    Power source: Pain recognition + "someone understands me"

  curiosity_gap:
    Structure: Incomplete information that demands resolution — an open loop in sentence one
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Specific enough to intrigue, incomplete enough to compel continuation
    Example: "There's a 5-second morning ritual that explains why some people never..."
    Power source: Specific detail + missing resolution

  future_pace:
    Structure: A vivid picture of what becomes possible — the transformed state
    Word count: 8-15 words
    Requirements: Sensory-rich, emotionally charged, achievable-feeling
    Example: "Imagine waking up tomorrow morning and stepping on the scale to see..."
    Power source: Desire activation + vivid visualization
```

### Step 2: Generate Hook Variations

```
GENERATE minimum 4 hook variations using different opening devices:

  VARIATION 1: Primary device (from lead_type_classification.opening_device)
    - Use the classified device type
    - Build from strongest upstream asset for this device
    - Target 8-12 words

  VARIATION 2: Alternative device from same lead type's viable devices
    - Select a secondary device compatible with the classified lead type
    - Different angle on the same core promise/mechanism
    - Target 5-10 words

  VARIATION 3: Vault-inspired variation
    - Study vault_lead_intelligence.hook_catalog for hooks in similar niches
    - Adapt structure (NOT copy content) to this campaign's assets
    - Target 8-15 words

  VARIATION 4: Contrarian device
    - Use a device type NOT typical for the classified lead type
    - Test whether an unexpected approach creates stronger attention capture
    - Target 5-12 words

FOR each variation:
  CONNECT to headline: Hook must continue or build on headline's promise/emotion
  EMBED mechanism foreshadowing: Hint at the mechanism without naming or explaining it
  ACTIVATE emotion: Trigger the first emotional beat of the lead's arc
  MAINTAIN specificity: Include at least one specific detail (number, name, timeframe)

OPTIONAL: Generate additional variations if initial 4 are below threshold
```

### Step 3: Score Each Hook Variation

```
FOR each hook variation, score on 5 dimensions:

  ATTENTION_CAPTURE (weight: 0.30):
    10: Physically impossible to not read the next sentence
    8-9: Extremely compelling — creates immediate "need to know"
    6-7: Good hook but doesn't stop the scroll
    4-5: Reads like an opening sentence, not a hook
    1-3: Generic opening that could be from any campaign

  HEADLINE_CONTINUATION (weight: 0.20):
    10: Seamlessly builds on headline momentum — feels like same conversation
    8-9: Clear connection to headline's promise and emotional direction
    6-7: Related to headline but doesn't continue its momentum
    4-5: Weak connection — feels like a different conversation
    1-3: Contradicts or ignores headline entirely

  MECHANISM_FORESHADOWING (weight: 0.20):
    10: Reader senses something specific and new is about to be revealed
    8-9: Creates anticipation for the mechanism without naming it
    6-7: Hints at "something" but too vague to build anticipation
    4-5: No sense that a mechanism exists
    1-3: Either names the mechanism outright or has zero foreshadowing

  EMOTIONAL_PULL (weight: 0.15):
    10: Activates the exact emotional driver for this lead type
    8-9: Strong emotional resonance — reader feels something
    6-7: Mild emotional engagement
    4-5: Emotionally neutral — reads as information not feeling
    1-3: Emotionally flat or off-target

  SPECIFICITY (weight: 0.15):
    10: Contains specific number, name, timeframe, or detail that grounds it
    8-9: One strong specific element present
    6-7: Semi-specific — "a doctor" vs. "Dr. James Caldwell of Johns Hopkins"
    4-5: Vague — could apply to any product in this niche
    1-3: Completely generic — no specific detail

CALCULATE weighted_score for each variation:
  weighted_score = (attention * 0.30) + (headline_cont * 0.20) +
                   (mechanism_foreshadow * 0.20) + (emotional_pull * 0.15) +
                   (specificity * 0.15)

MINIMUM THRESHOLD: 8.0/10 weighted score for primary selection
```

### Step 4: Select Primary Hook

```
SORT variations by weighted_score DESC

SELECT primary_hook:
  - Highest weighted_score
  - Must score >= 8.0 weighted
  - Must score >= 8 on attention_capture (non-negotiable floor)
  - Must score >= 7 on headline_continuation (must connect)

IF no variation meets threshold:
  GENERATE 2 additional variations targeting weak dimensions
  RE-SCORE and RE-SELECT

SELECT backup_hook:
  - Second highest scoring variation
  - Must use a DIFFERENT device type from primary
  - For A/B testing or fallback

DOCUMENT selection_rationale:
  Why this hook over the runner-up
  Which dimension gave it the edge
  How it connects to the classified lead type
```

### Step 5: Verify Hook-to-Lead Continuity

```
VALIDATE that selected hook:
  1. Continues the headline's emotional trajectory (not a restart)
  2. Sets up the lead type's expected progression
     - Statistical hook → leads to performance data cascade
     - Story hook → leads to vulnerability/identification moment
     - Authority hook → leads to credential revelation
     - Problem hook → leads to pain recognition cascade
  3. Does NOT:
     - Name the mechanism (tease only)
     - Make the full promise (that's the elevator pitch's job)
     - Introduce the product
     - Start explaining anything
  4. Creates forward momentum — impossible to stop after reading it
```

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| **Generic Opener** | "If you're looking for a way to..." or "What if there was a solution..." | Zero stopping power; reads like every other campaign in the niche; scroll-past guaranteed |
| **Mechanism Reveal** | Hook names or explains the mechanism in the first sentence | Violates Georgi DON'T rule; eliminates curiosity before the lead begins |
| **Promise Front-Load** | Full promise stated in the hook — nothing left to build toward | The elevator pitch builds to the promise; the hook creates the pull TO the pitch |
| **Headline Repeat** | Hook restates the headline in slightly different words | No forward momentum; reader feels they already got the information |
| **Emotional Mismatch** | Headline is hopeful/aspirational, hook is fear-based/threatening | Breaks emotional continuity; reader feels whiplash and disengages |
| **Vague Curiosity** | "Scientists have discovered something amazing..." | No specificity to anchor attention; feels like clickbait, not a real hook |
| **Over-Specified** | Hook contains so much detail it reads as a paragraph, not a sentence | Hooks must be punchy (5-15 words); density over length |
| **Question Without Tension** | "Did you know that exercise is important for health?" | Rhetorical questions must surface latent CONCERN, not state the obvious |
