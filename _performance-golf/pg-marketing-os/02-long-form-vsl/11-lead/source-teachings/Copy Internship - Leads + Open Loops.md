# Stefan Lead Formula \- Credit Stefan Georgi

\<\<Random Copywriting Knowledge-Bomb \+ A Checklist Below\>\>

One of the biggest mistakes I see in a lot of sales letters is people trying to do too much with their lead. The reality is that the structure of a good lead should be pretty formulaic, and it should also have a pretty definitive end point, where you’re transitioning into the next phase of your sales copy. What a lot of people do however is start trying to explain things too early. They explain how the solution works, why old solutions didn’t work, start talking about their story, and otherwise getting off topic. For anyone writing copy (or employing copywriters) I think it’s important to remember: the ONLY purpose of your lead is to call out your prospective customer, catch their attention, then draw them in so they keep reading/watching.

Also a quick note: this principle can be applied to pretty much all writing that’s intended to sell. For say a 250 word eComm description, it’s the same. The first few sentences should be DRAWING THE READER IN. That’s it. Don’t start explaining a damn thing until they’re engaged with you.

Otherwise…it’s like going fishing and repeatedly reeling in the bait before you’ve had a bite.

Do:

\- Call Out Problem(s)

\- Promise Solution to Problem(s)

\- Hint at Unique Mechanism Behind the Solution

\- Hint at Surprising Counter-Intuitive Information (Why Traditional Solution Doesn’t work. Or how there’s a REAL cause of the problem they’re unaware of)

\- Briefly insert credibility/authority elements

\- Briefly Tease Discovery Story. 

\- Briefly Call Out and Address Skepticism

Don’t: 

\-Explain how the unique mechanism of the NEW solution works

\- Explain in detail why old solutions don’t work 

\- Go deeply into the discovery story

\- Explain what the solution (product) is

If anyone wants to add anything here, or thinks I’ve forgotten something, just comment and let me know

# Open Loops

**Schwartz Review:**  
Forward Signaling: “You say bluntly to your reader that what you’re about to say is important.” “This is a vital point.” “Pay attention to this next point.” I also use this to “sell” them on listening to a science section.

Suspense \- Planned ambiguity. Deliberately leaving your reader unsure of some vital element of your thought flow.

- An open loop is essentially a tease at a compelling thing you’re going to tell someone later  
- It’s the same psychology as to why TV shows leave a key piece of information missing at the end of a show. A cliffhanger. You get a piece of the suspenseful plot twist, but you won't see the character who the plot twist refers to. Or you’ll only see the look on the character’s face, without seeing what happens. Or you’ll see a character doing something unexpected, or “out of character” suggesting their real role or intention isn’t quite what it seems.  
- We are hard-wired to HATE not getting all the key pieces of information. Your brain has a powerful urge to “close” the loop and get resolution and closure.  
- Story about Eric / Drama  
- Story about this fucking Harry Potter game.  
- I use open loops whenever they make sense. You want to remind your customer of that key piece of information they haven’t got yet  
- Most of my “open loops” have to do with the “tangible tease” in my intro… a trick, secret, 5 fruits, tangible thing, etc....
