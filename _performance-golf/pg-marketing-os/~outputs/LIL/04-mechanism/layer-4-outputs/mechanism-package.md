## 2. MECHANISM EXPLANATION

### Core Explanation (one paragraph)

Toddlers don't learn movement from words — they learn from what they can see and touch. That's why coaching kids at this age is so hard. The See & Swing System teaches golf the same way a shape sorter teaches geometry: through the design of the equipment itself. Yellow ball on the clubhead matches white ball on the ground — that's alignment. Stance markers show where to stand — that's setup. The club stands upright so they walk in and grab it — that's the starting position. Every piece teaches one thing through a visual or physical cue. No instructions, no coaching, no corrections. The equipment teaches. Your kid discovers. You just play.

## 12. SOURCE CITATIONS — DEVELOPMENTAL CLAIMS

All developmental, brain, and learning claims in this mechanism package have been fact-checked against peer-reviewed research and institutional sources. This section provides traceable citations for each claim so that no copy goes to market without verifiable backing.

### Claim-by-Claim Verification

**CLAIM: "Toddlers don't learn movement from words — they learn from what they can see and touch."**
- **Status:** SUPPORTED
- **Basis:** Piaget's preoperational stage (ages 2-7) establishes that children at this stage rely on perception, intuition, and sensory interaction — not abstract verbal reasoning. Children "need to explore, interact, and experiment to gain information and understand their world." A 2020 PLoS ONE study found the observational-imitative method was "the most effective instruction method when participants have no experience with the sport action to be performed."
- **Sources:**
  - Piaget, J. (1951). *Play, Dreams and Imitation in Childhood*. London: Routledge.
  - NCBI StatPearls — Cognitive Development: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537095/
  - Pignato, S. et al. (2020). "Learning new sport actions: Pilot study to investigate the imitative and the verbal instructive teaching methods in motor education." *PLoS ONE*. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7428179/

**CLAIM: "A toddler's brain isn't built to convert words into movement."**
- **Status:** SUPPORTED (softened from original "can't")
- **Basis:** Research on motor imagery shows children cannot fully engage with motor imagery (converting verbal instruction into mental rehearsal of movement) until ages 5-10. Children in the preoperational stage "cannot yet understand adult logic or mentally manipulate information." A study comparing verbal instruction vs. demonstration for novel motor skills found children scored higher on imitation-based tasks than verbal instruction tasks.
- **Sources:**
  - Waterman, A.H. et al. (2020). "Comparing motor imagery and verbal rehearsal strategies in children's ability to follow spoken instructions." *Journal of Experimental Child Psychology*. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022096520304872
  - Lumen Learning — Piaget's Preoperational Stage: https://courses.lumenlearning.com/suny-lifespandevelopment/chapter/piagets-preoperational-stage-of-cognitive-development/
  - Frontiers in Psychology (2020). Motor skill learning in children: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.00002/full

**CLAIM: "That's developmental science, not opinion."**
- **Status:** SUPPORTED
- **Basis:** Piaget's preoperational stage framework has been replicated and validated for nearly 80 years across thousands of studies. The AAP's 2018 clinical report "The Power of Play" independently confirms that play-based, hands-on learning is the developmentally appropriate approach for young children.
- **Sources:**
  - Yogman, M. et al. (2018). "The Power of Play: A Pediatric Role in Enhancing Development in Young Children." *Pediatrics*, 142(3). https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/142/3/e20182058/38649/
  - Piaget, J. (1946). *La formation du symbole chez l'enfant*. Neuchâtel: Delachaux et Niestlé. (English: 1951)

**CLAIM: Shape sorter analogy — "the same way they learn a shape sorter"**
- **Status:** SUPPORTED (as analogy)
- **Basis:** This is positioned as an analogy, not a factual claim. The underlying principle — preoperational children learn through sensory interaction with objects — is core Piaget. Shape sorters are a canonical example of object-mediated discovery learning in developmental psychology. The AAP confirms "play is brain building" and "a central part of healthy child development."
- **Sources:**
  - AAP (2018). "The Power of Play." *Pediatrics*. (cited above)
  - Simply Psychology — Preoperational Stage: https://www.simplypsychology.org/preoperational.html

**CLAIM: "That's not theory — it's how the brain works at this age."**
- **Status:** APPROPRIATE FOR VOICE REGISTER
- **Basis:** Piaget's framework IS theory, but one of the most extensively validated in developmental psychology (~80 years of replication). In Grant's conversational voice, "not theory" means "not speculation" — which is accurate. This language should not be used in formal/scientific contexts, but is appropriate for the mechanism's intended voice register (Grant at the range, talking to another parent).

**CLAIM: Piaget developmental psychology — "nearly 80 years"**
- **Status:** ACCURATE
- **Basis:** Jean Piaget (1896-1980) first published the preoperational stage framework in 1946. From 2026, that is 80 years.
- **Sources:**
  - Britannica — Jean Piaget: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Piaget
  - NCBI StatPearls — Stages of Development: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448206/

### Downstream Warning: "90% Brain Development by Age 5"

This claim does NOT appear in this mechanism package. If it is used in any downstream skill (promise, body copy, ads, e-comm), it MUST be reframed:

- **What the data actually says:** A child's brain reaches ~90% of its adult **physical size** by age 5-6. This refers to brain volume, NOT functional development, learning capacity, or cognitive ability.
- **Brain weight "is not a very informative index of its maturation."** Functional maturation (synaptic pruning, myelination) continues well into adolescence.
- **Safe reframe if needed:** "A child's brain reaches 90% of its adult size by age 5 — that's how fast things are developing" (describes growth rate, not learning completion).
- **Sources:**
  - PMC — Brain Development During Preschool Years: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3511633/
  - PMC — Early Brain Development and Public Health: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11526699/
  - The Human Safety Net: https://www.thehumansafetynet.org/gho/stories-and-news/90-Scientific-studies-show-child-s-brain-reaches-90-of-adult-size-between-0-6-years
