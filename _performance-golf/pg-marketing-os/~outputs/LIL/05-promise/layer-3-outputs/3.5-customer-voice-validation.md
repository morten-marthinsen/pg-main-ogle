# 3.5 Customer Voice Validation — Lil' Legends

## Purpose

Validate that the primary promise, campaign thesis, and supporting promises sound like how golf parents actually talk — not like marketer copy or AI output. Promises that use customer language resonate. Promises that use marketer language get scrolled past.

## Primary Promise — Voice Validation

> "Seven words: 'Match the yellow ball to the white ball.' The See & Swing System handles the rest — alignment, stance, setup — all through shapes and colors. No lesson. Just a little legend who won't stop swinging. I'm his golf buddy now."

### Dimension 1: Vocabulary Match (Score: 9/10)

**Marketer jargon found:** ZERO
- No proprietary, revolutionary, cutting-edge, or any marketer language
- "See & Swing System" is a proper noun (mechanism name), not jargon

**Customer vocabulary overlap:**
- "won't stop swinging" — direct match to customer success language ("doesn't want to stop" / "ALL.DAY.EVERY.DAY") [EXP2-H-017]
- "golf buddy" — direct match to customer desire language ("something we can do together") reframed
- "no lesson" — resonates with parent frustration about trying to teach and failing
- "shapes and colors" — natural parent language (how they describe toddler activities)
- "yellow ball" / "white ball" — concrete, visual, the way parents describe things to each other

**Assessment:** Sounds like a dad talking, not a marketer writing. Every word earns its place in natural speech.

### Dimension 2: Emotional Resonance (Score: 9/10)

**Emotion frame:** Hope + pride + relief
- "I'm his golf buddy now" — maps directly to the parental aspiration: "something we can do together," "proud dad"
- "won't stop swinging" — matches the success emotion parents describe: "obsessed," "doesn't want to stop," "asking to go"
- "No lesson" — relief emotion. Every parent who's tried to teach their toddler something and watched them walk away feels this.
- "Seven words" — wonder/discovery emotion. The simplicity creates an "is it really that easy?" response.

**Intensity calibration:** CORRECT — warm pride, not ecstatic hype. Matches Soul.md "warm, proud enthusiasm" register. Not over-the-top, not understated.

### Dimension 3: Problem Framing (Score: 8/10)

**Customer problem language match:**
- The promise doesn't explicitly state the problem (that's root cause territory). Instead, it solves the problem implicitly: "No lesson" = you don't have to coach them. "Golf buddy now" = you WEREN'T before.
- The implicit problem framing matches customer experience: parents who tried and failed to teach their toddler golf.

**Acknowledges failed solutions:** Implicitly — "No lesson" implies previous attempts required lessons. Not explicit, but the promise is a solution statement, not a problem statement. This is correct positioning for a primary promise.

**Gap:** Could be stronger if it explicitly named the frustration. But this is deliberate — hope market framing means we lead with the solution, not the problem. The root cause copy blocks handle problem naming.

### Dimension 4: Specificity Match (Score: 10/10)

**Numbers they would use:** "Seven words" — parents don't count words, but the specificity of "seven" makes it feel like a real thing someone actually counted, not marketing math.

**Details that feel familiar:**
- "Match the yellow ball to the white ball" — this is the kind of specific instruction a parent would actually say to a 2-year-old. It sounds real because it IS the actual instruction.
- "Alignment, stance, setup" — golf-specific but accessible. Golf parents know these terms. Non-golf parents can still follow.

## Campaign Thesis — Voice Validation

> "The only toddler golf system where the equipment teaches and your little legend can just play — the See & Swing System makes you their golf buddy, not their golf coach."

### Voice Score: 8/10

**Vocabulary:** Clean — no jargon. "Equipment teaches" is plain language. "Golf buddy, not golf coach" uses natural framing.

**Emotional resonance:** "Your little legend can just play" — "just play" is exactly how parents frame the dream. "Keep it fun," "make it fun," "something we can do together" — play IS the desire.

**Slight markdown:** "The only toddler golf system" is slightly more formal/marketing than how a parent would say it. A parent would say "the only one that actually teaches" or "nothing else does this." However, this is a campaign thesis — it's meant to be a positioning statement, not conversational copy. Acceptable formality for this function.
