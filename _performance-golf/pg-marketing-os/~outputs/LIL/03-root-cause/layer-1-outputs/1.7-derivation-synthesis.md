## Step 2: Score Candidates on Four Dimensions

| Dimension | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Truth** | 30% | Is this actually the real root cause? Grounded in research? |
| **Revelation** | 25% | Will the audience find this surprising/new? |
| **Mechanism Fit** | 25% | Can LIL's mechanism solve it? |
| **Provability** | 20% | Can we demonstrate it credibly? |

```
SCORING MATRIX:
                        Truth   Revel.  Mech Fit  Provable  WEIGHTED
A: Teaching Method       9       8        10        9        9.00
B: Silent Equipment      8       9         10       8        8.75
C: Impossible 1st Exp    9       7         8        7        7.90
D: Adapted vs. Designed  8       8         9        8        8.25
E: Parent Trap           7       7         9        6        7.25
```

**Scoring Rationale:**

### Candidate A: Teaching Method (9.00)
- **Truth (9):** Highest truth score. Piaget developmental psychology directly proves verbal instruction doesn't work for ages 2-5. The research data (1,169 quotes) overwhelmingly confirms parents default to verbal coaching and it fails. This IS the root cause.
- **Revelation (8):** Most parents haven't framed it this way. They know verbal instruction doesn't work, but they attribute it to the child's attention span, not to a developmental impossibility. Revelation comes from reframing "my kid won't listen" as "your kid CAN'T process words yet."
- **Mechanism Fit (10):** Perfect match. LIL's 4 mechanisms each replace a specific verbal instruction with a visual/tactile design cue. Focal Sphere = "match yellow to white" replaces "square the clubface." Walk-In Frame replaces "stand here." Skills Mat replaces "put your feet here."
- **Provability (9):** MEC-13 knockout ("match yellow to white") is a 7-word proof. Piaget is established science. Product is "visually self-evident" (Soul.md). Can SHOW this working with minimal demonstration.

### Candidate B: Silent Equipment (8.75)
- **Truth (8):** True but slightly metaphorical — equipment doesn't literally "speak." The underlying truth (no product teaches through design) is accurate. Slightly less grounded in direct research language than Candidate A.
- **Revelation (9):** Highest revelation score. The idea that equipment SHOULD teach is genuinely non-obvious. Parents think of equipment as objects and teachers as people. Reframing equipment as "the teacher" is a true insight.
- **Mechanism Fit (10):** Perfect match (same as A — LIL's mechanism IS equipment that teaches).
- **Provability (8):** Slightly harder to PROVE equipment "should" teach than to prove verbal instruction doesn't work. Requires an additional logical step.

### Candidate C: Impossible First Experience (7.90)
- **Truth (9):** Highly true — research confirms children quit after 2-3 swings, and equipment guarantees failure. The "lost interest" misattribution is one of the strongest patterns (30+ quotes).
- **Revelation (7):** Lower revelation — many parents already suspect the equipment caused the problem. The insight ("your kid didn't lose interest — the equipment guaranteed failure") is validating but less surprising than A or B.
- **Mechanism Fit (8):** Good match but less direct. LIL creates a SUCCESSFUL first experience, but the root cause frames the problem as "impossible experience" rather than "wrong teaching method." Mechanism addresses it but the framing is more about the symptom (failed experience) than the cause (wrong learning model).
- **Provability (7):** Harder to prove "impossible first experience" without before/after video. Pre-launch constraint hits this candidate hardest.

### Candidate D: Adapted vs. Designed (8.25)
- **Truth (8):** True at the market level — all competitors did adapt rather than design from scratch. But "adapted vs. designed" is more of a category observation than a root cause. It describes WHAT happened (wrong approach) more than WHY it fails (wrong learning model).
- **Revelation (8):** Good revelation — the "adapted from adult" framing is clear and communicable. Parents intuitively sense this but haven't articulated it.
- **Mechanism Fit (9):** Strong — LIL is the "designed from scratch" answer. But mechanism fit is slightly indirect because the root cause is about design PHILOSOPHY rather than a specific failure point.
- **Provability (8):** Provable through competitive contrast (DAT-19, 16-competitor map). Clear visual: show adult club → show scaled-down version → show LIL designed from toddler cognition.

### Candidate E: Parent Trap (7.25)
- **Truth (7):** True but describes a SYMPTOM of the root cause (the coaching dilemma), not the root cause itself. The push/pull dilemma exists BECAUSE equipment doesn't teach — it's downstream.
- **Revelation (7):** Parents recognize this dilemma immediately but it's not truly new to them. They live it. Less "aha" than A or B.
- **Mechanism Fit (9):** Strong — LIL eliminates the dilemma by making equipment the teacher.
- **Provability (6):** Hardest to prove. The coaching dilemma is subjective. Requires parent to recognize themselves in it rather than being demonstrated.

## Step 4: Three-Part Root Cause Structure

### Candidate A (Primary)

```
THE THREE-PART ROOT CAUSE:

1. WHAT THEY THINK THE PROBLEM IS:
   "The clubs are wrong — too heavy, too flimsy, wrong size. Or my kid
   just doesn't have the attention span for golf yet. Or I'm not a good
   enough teacher."

2. WHAT THE REAL PROBLEM IS:
   "Every toddler golf product was designed around how ADULTS learn golf
   — verbal instruction, conscious practice, technique correction. But
   toddlers ages 2-5 don't learn through words. They learn through what
   they can see and touch. The equipment needs to teach through its
   design — shapes, colors, physical cues — not through a parent trying
   to explain golf to a 3-year-old."

3. WHY NOTHING HAS WORKED:
   "Every solution — lighter clubs, better clubs, more coaching, less
   coaching, waiting until older — fails because they're all operating
   within the same framework: adults teach golf through words, so let's
   simplify the words for kids. But you can't simplify your way out of a
   developmental impossibility. A 3-year-old cannot convert 'keep your
   head down' into a physical action, no matter how gently you say it.
   Until the EQUIPMENT teaches through design instead of relying on
   verbal instruction, nothing will work."
```

### Candidate B (Runner-Up)

```
THE THREE-PART ROOT CAUSE:

1. WHAT THEY THINK THE PROBLEM IS:
   "I haven't found the right clubs yet — the right weight, the right
   size, the right brand."

2. WHAT THE REAL PROBLEM IS:
   "Every toddler golf club on the market is a silent object. It doesn't
   teach anything. The child picks it up and has zero information about
   what to do with it. The only teaching tool available is a parent
   talking — and toddlers can't learn through talking."

3. WHY NOTHING HAS WORKED:
   "You keep switching products, but every product is equally silent. None
   of them show the child what to do through their design. So every new
   set creates the same result: random swings, no contact, frustration,
   walkaway. The problem isn't finding the right club — it's finding a
   club that actually TEACHES."
```

### Candidate C (Alternate)

```
THE THREE-PART ROOT CAUSE:

1. WHAT THEY THINK THE PROBLEM IS:
   "My kid lost interest in golf. They're just not into it."

2. WHAT THE REAL PROBLEM IS:
   "Your kid didn't lose interest — every product on the market creates
   an impossible first experience. Wrong weight, wrong geometry, no
   visual guidance. Within 2-3 swings, your child's brain decided
   'golf = frustration.' They're not uninterested — they were set up
   to fail."

3. WHY NOTHING HAS WORKED:
   "Each new set of clubs creates the same impossible first experience
   because they all follow the same design: adult equipment made smaller.
   Your child keeps getting handed equipment that guarantees failure, and
   each failure reinforces 'golf = bad.' The interest was never lost —
   it was never given a chance to form."
```

### Candidate D (Alternate)

```
THE THREE-PART ROOT CAUSE:

1. WHAT THEY THINK THE PROBLEM IS:
   "I need better-quality clubs designed for kids."

2. WHAT THE REAL PROBLEM IS:
   "Every 'kid's club' on the market was ADAPTED from adult golf — shrunk
   down, made lighter, painted bright colors. None was DESIGNED from
   scratch for how a toddler's brain and body actually work. Adapting
   preserves every wrong assumption. Designing from scratch means starting
   with the question: 'How does a 3-year-old learn a physical skill?'"

3. WHY NOTHING HAS WORKED:
   "Switching brands doesn't help because every brand follows the same
   approach: start with an adult club and adapt it for kids. You're not
   choosing between different solutions — you're choosing between
   different versions of the same mistake."
```

### Candidate E (Alternate)

```
THE THREE-PART ROOT CAUSE:

1. WHAT THEY THINK THE PROBLEM IS:
   "I'm either overcoaching or undercoaching — I can't find the balance."

2. WHAT THE REAL PROBLEM IS:
   "The push/pull dilemma only exists because the equipment doesn't teach.
   If the club itself showed the child what to do through visual cues,
   you wouldn't need to choose between coaching and stepping back. You
   could just play alongside your kid."

3. WHY NOTHING HAS WORKED:
   "You've been trying to solve a teaching problem with better TALKING —
   more instruction, less instruction, different instruction. But the
   real solution is equipment that removes the need for instruction
   entirely."
```
