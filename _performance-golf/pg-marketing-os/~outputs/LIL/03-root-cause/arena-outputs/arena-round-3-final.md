```
HALBERT R3 FINAL:

Anchor Phrase: "Seven Words"

Your kid didn't quit golf. They quit the experience.

Here's what I mean: you got the clubs. You got out there.
You said "swing like this" — and three swings later, they
were done. Chasing the dog. Picking dandelions. Anything
but golf.

And you thought — maybe they're just not into it.

Wrong.

Every kids' golf club on the market was built around one
assumption: that someone would be TELLING the kid what to do.
The plastic ones. The metal ones. The expensive ones. All of
them — designed for coaching.

But a 3-year-old's brain doesn't learn from being told. It
learns from seeing. Touching. Matching. Discovering.

That's the same way a shape sorter works. Nobody teaches a
toddler geometry — the toy teaches through its design. The
kid picks it up, plays with it, and figures it out.

So we asked: what if golf equipment worked like a shape sorter?

Here's the answer — and this is the part you'll remember:

"Match the yellow ball to the white ball."

Seven words. That's the whole golf lesson.

Your toddler sees the yellow ball on the clubhead. Sees the
white ball on the ground. Matches them. And they're aligned
— without you saying a word.

Stance markers show where to stand. The club stands upright
so they walk in and grab it. Every piece teaches through what
a toddler's brain actually understands.

The plastic ones broke because they were toys.
The metal ones failed because they were too heavy.
The lessons didn't stick because words don't work yet.

This works because the equipment does the teaching.

And you? You don't coach. You don't correct.

You just play alongside your kid.

Seven words to start. A lifetime of golf together.
```

```
MAKEPEACE R3 FINAL:

Anchor Phrase: "The Design Does the Teaching"

You've tried the backyard sessions. The little clubs. The
"hold it like this" and "swing through" and watching them
wander off after three tries.

Every golf parent has lived that moment. And every golf

Maybe next year.

So what does their brain respond to?

The same thing it responds to with a shape sorter. Shapes.
Colors. Visual patterns they can match and discover through
play. Nobody teaches a toddler how a shape sorter works —
the design does the teaching.

Now imagine golf equipment designed the same way.

A yellow ball built into the clubhead. A white ball on the
ground. Your toddler sees the match. Makes the match. And
they're aligned — stance, grip, face angle — without a
single word of instruction.

Stance markers show where to stand. The club stands upright
so they walk right in. The mat creates a station where
everything teaches through what they can see and touch.

The whole system comes down to seven words:

"Match the yellow ball to the white ball."

That's it. The design does the teaching. Play does the rest.

And you — the parent who thought you needed to be a coach —
you get the thing you actually wanted all along.

To just play golf with your kid.
```
