**Quote 1:**

**Source:** Clark, J. E. (2007). On the problem of motor skill development. *JOPERD, 78*(5), 39-44. https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ795574.pdf

**Quote 2:**

**Source:** Dr. Jane Clark, University of Maryland, interviews and published work on fundamental motor skills.

**Relevance to LIL:** Directly supports the Lil' Legends premise — children need properly designed equipment and structured practice (Skills Mat) to develop motor skills. Golf skills won't develop from passive exposure.

## Category 3: Motor Skill / Pediatric Researchers

### Expert: Pellegrini & Smith (Landmark Research)
**Credentials:** A.D. Pellegrini (University of Minnesota) and P.K. Smith (Goldsmiths, University of London). Leading researchers on play and physical development.
**Credibility Tier:** 5/5 — Published in *Child Development* (top-tier journal)

**Quote (paraphrased from findings):**
> "Exercise play peaks during the preschool years (ages 2-5) and serves critical strength, endurance, and cognitive training functions."

**Source:** Pellegrini, A. D., & Smith, P. K. (1998). Physical activity play: The nature and function of a neglected aspect of play. *Child Development, 69*(3), 577-598.

**Relevance to LIL:** The preschool years are THE window for exercise play — and golf with Lil' Legends is structured exercise play designed for exactly this developmental window.

---

### Expert: Vygotsky (Scaffolding Theory)
**Credentials:** Lev Vygotsky — foundational developmental psychologist. *Mind in Society* is one of the most cited works in educational psychology.
**Credibility Tier:** 5/5 — Foundational academic authority

**Key concept:**
> Children learn complex skills when environmental scaffolding supports them in the "zone of proximal development" — the gap between what they can do alone and what they can do with help.

**Source:** Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). *Mind in society: The development of higher psychological processes*. Harvard University Press.

**Relevance to LIL:** The Skills Mat IS environmental scaffolding — it provides physical guides (stance markers, swing path, putting track) that support the child in their zone of proximal development. The child can't learn golf from verbal instruction, but CAN learn from physical environmental cues.

---

### Expert: Joan Vickers (Quiet Eye Research)
**Credentials:** Professor, University of Calgary. Pioneer of "Quiet Eye" research in sports vision and motor performance.
**Credibility Tier:** 5/5 — Published researcher, foundational sports science

**Key finding:**
> Athletes who fixate their gaze on a specific target point for longer ("quiet eye") perform significantly better in all aiming tasks — from golf putting to basketball free throws.

**Source:** Vickers, J. N. (2007). *Perception, cognition, and decision training: The quiet eye in action*. Human Kinetics.

**Relevance to LIL:** The Focal Sphere is designed to create a natural "quiet eye" focus point for toddlers. By giving them a colored sphere to look at, LIL builds proper gaze behavior from the earliest possible age — before they can even understand the concept of "keep your eye on the ball."
