# 1.1 Pattern Analyzer — Lil' Legends Big Idea

## Big Idea Pattern Analysis

### Pattern 1: Mechanism-Simplicity
**Pattern:** The mechanism is SO simple that its simplicity becomes the Big Idea
**Vault examples:** iPod "1,000 songs in your pocket" / Dyson "The vacuum that doesn't lose suction"
**LIL application:** "Seven words. That's the whole golf lesson." — The mechanism's simplicity (one sentence replaces coaching) IS the surprise.
**Strength:** 9/10 — Self-demonstrating. The proof IS the pitch. No persuasion gap.
**Risk:** Could feel reductive — parents may wonder "is that all there is?"

### Pattern 2: Category Bridge
**Pattern:** A trusted principle from one domain applied to an unexpected domain
**Vault examples:** "Montessori for movement" / "Peloton for cycling" (SoulCycle for home)
**LIL application:** "A shape sorter for golf." — Every parent knows shape sorters work. Applying that principle to golf is the surprise.
**Strength:** 8/10 — Instant comprehension via familiar analogy. Schema distance is natural (SD 5-6).
**Risk:** Could feel gimmicky if overworked. Must be bridge, not headline.

### Pattern 3: Role Inversion
**Pattern:** Redefines who does what in the expected relationship
**Vault examples:** Montessori "Follow the child" / "Patients teaching doctors"
**LIL application:** "Golf buddy, not golf coach." — Parent's role changes from instructor to companion. The equipment takes the teaching role.
**Strength:** 8/10 — Taps deep parental desire (play, don't work). Identity-level engagement.
**Risk:** Could feel like permission marketing rather than a Big Idea. Needs mechanism backing.

### Pattern 4: Mission/Legacy
**Pattern:** The product is a vehicle for passing something forward across generations
**Vault examples:** Patagonia "Don't buy this jacket" / TOMS Shoes "One for One"
**LIL application:** "My dad gave me this game. Now I'm giving it to the next generation." — Grant's authentic story becomes the campaign spine.
**Strength:** 7/10 — Emotionally authentic (Grant's actual story). But less commercially differentiated.
**Risk:** Emotion without mechanism. Every competitor could claim "pass down the game." Needs mechanism to differentiate.

### Pattern 5: Industry Reframe
**Pattern:** Names what the whole industry got wrong, positioning the product as the correction
**Vault examples:** "Doctors have been treating the symptom, not the cause" / Dollar Shave Club "Why pay for shave tech you don't need?"
**LIL application:** "Every kids' golf club was designed for coaching. But toddlers can't learn from coaching." — Makes the entire category wrong, not just cheap products.
**Strength:** 8/10 — Creates "of course!" moment. Explains past failures. High NR potential.
**Risk:** Must stay hope-framed. Cannot feel like attacking competitors or blaming parents.

| Pattern | Best Big Idea Type | Secondary Type | FSSIT Anchor |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|-------------|
| Mechanism-Simplicity | MECHANISM | DISCOVERY | FSSIT-1 ("You don't have to teach them") |
| Category Bridge | MECHANISM | DISCOVERY | FSSIT-3 ("Shape sorter principle") |
| Role Inversion | IDENTITY | MECHANISM | FSSIT-5 ("What if your only job was to play alongside them?") |

| Industry Reframe | MECHANISM | -- | FSSIT-2 ("Designed for coaching, not discovery") |

## Key Insight

The strongest patterns all converge on MECHANISM as the primary Big Idea type, with IDENTITY as a strong secondary. This aligns with Schwartz Stage 2->3 (mechanism differentiation needed) and the competitive landscape (zero competitors have a named teaching mechanism).

The question is not WHETHER the mechanism is central — it is. The question is WHICH LENS frames the mechanism:
- Simplicity lens ("seven words")
- Analogy lens ("shape sorter")
- Identity lens ("golf buddy")
- Industry lens ("designed for coaching, not discovery")
- Legacy lens ("my dad gave me this game")

These are not competing Big Ideas — they are competing FRAMINGS of the same strategic synthesis. The concept generation phase will determine which framing creates the most powerful Big Idea.
