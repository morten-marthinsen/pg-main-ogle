## Step 3: The Hidden Mechanisms

### Hidden Layer 1: Structural (Equipment Architecture)
**The putter's center of gravity sits behind the shaft axis, creating a persistent gravitational force that drifts the face open during the stroke. This force is invisible to the golfer, varies with stroke speed and length, and requires continuous hand compensation to counteract.**

This is the PRIMARY hidden mechanism. The golfer cannot see the CG. They cannot feel the gravitational force as a discrete variable (they feel it as "the putter fighting them" but attribute it to their own technique). And they have never been told it exists as a design problem rather than a design feature.

### Hidden Layer 2: Environmental/Industry (Normalization of the Flaw)
**The putter industry has called this gravitational face drift "toe flow" or "toe hang" and marketed it as a DESIGN FEATURE that should be "matched to your stroke arc." This reframing turned a bug into a feature and prevented the market from recognizing it as the root cause of their putting problems.**

This is the SECONDARY hidden mechanism. It explains why the hidden layer has remained hidden: the industry actively normalized the flaw. "Toe flow" sounds intentional and beneficial. "Face drift" sounds like what it is: a problem.

### Hidden Layer 3: Temporal (The Pressure Amplification Loop)
**The face drift problem is not constant -- it AMPLIFIES under pressure. When adrenaline and anxiety increase grip pressure, the golfer's hand compensation becomes more forceful and more erratic. The same putter that "worked fine" on the practice green becomes a different instrument under competitive conditions. This creates a temporal hidden layer: the problem is manageable in low-stakes situations but devastating in high-stakes moments when it matters most.**
