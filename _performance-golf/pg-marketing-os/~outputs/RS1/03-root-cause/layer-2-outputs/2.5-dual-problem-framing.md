## Niche Fit Assessment

**Niche:** Golf / Premium Equipment
**Dual Problem fit for golf:** POSSIBLE but not primary

Dual Problem framing works when two genuinely distinct problems compound each other (see ONE Wedge: Mental Equation + Design Problem). For RS1, the root cause is fundamentally SINGULAR -- rearward CG causes face drift. The dual dimension (physics flaw + self-blame) is more accurately expressed as "the cause and the belief about the cause" rather than two independent problems.

However, there IS a legitimate dual structure in the approved A+B concept:
- **Problem 1 (A):** The putter's physics are wrong (weight behind shaft → face drift)
- **Problem 2 (B):** The golfer's belief system is wrong ("it's my fault, not the putter's")

These compound each other: the belief system prevents the golfer from identifying the physics problem, and the physics problem reinforces the belief system (every failure confirms "I can't putt").

## Step 3: Compounding Effect

```
Problem 1 alone: The putter drifts open. A skilled golfer could theoretically
compensate -- and many do, most of the time. On relaxed strokes, the miss is small.

Problem 2 alone: A wrong belief about root cause would be correctable if someone
showed them the truth. But the truth is invisible without the right demo.

TOGETHER:
The physics flaw creates misses → the belief system blames the golfer →
the golfer works harder on the wrong variables → the physics flaw persists →
more misses → deeper self-blame → more wrong solutions.

The compounding creates a LOOP:
Miss → "It's my fault" → Practice stroke → Miss again (physics unchanged) →
"I'm really bad at putting" → Try new putter (same physics) → Honeymoon →
Regression → "Nothing works" → Deeper self-blame → Repeat.
```

## Step 4: Complete Dual Problem Expression

### Variant A: The Two Problems You Have

```
Here's what nobody has told you:

You don't have one putting problem. You have two.
And they've been compounding each other for years.

Problem One: The physics.
Virtually every putter ever made has its weight behind the shaft.
That weight creates a gravitational force that drifts the face open
during the stroke. Your hands have to compensate on every putt.
Under pressure, that compensation fails.

Problem Two: The belief.
Golf taught you that the putter is a neutral tool.
That if you're missing, the problem is your stroke, your nerves,
your practice habits. "It's the archer, not the arrow."

Here's how they compound:

Problem One causes misses. Problem Two makes you blame yourself.
So you work harder on your stroke -- which doesn't fix the physics.
The misses continue. The self-blame deepens.
And the loop tightens.

Every lesson you took addressed Problem Two.
No lesson you took addressed Problem One.
Because nobody was looking at where the weight was.

Fixing just one doesn't work.
Fixing the physics without changing the belief leaves you skeptical.
Changing the belief without fixing the physics leaves you frustrated.

You need both at once:
A putter where the weight is in the right place.
And the understanding that your stroke was fine all along.
```
