## 1. Language DO's — Use These Words and Phrases

### Distance Language (how golfers talk about yards)
- "lost [X] yards" — specific numbers, always
- "hit it farther" / "hit it longer" — simple, direct
- "carry distance" — informed golfers use this; it's more precise than "total distance"
- "off the tee" — the distance conversation is almost always about driver distance
- "I used to hit it [X], now I hit it [Y]" — the before/after frame they already use
- "extra 5-10 yards" — realistic gains they'd believe; 50-yard claims would be rejected
- "leaving yards on the table" — implies recoverable loss, not permanent decline

### Price/Value Language
- "not worth it" / "isn't worth the money" — the value judgment they make
- "half the price" / "fraction of the cost" — how they describe savings
- "I switched from [brand] and couldn't tell the difference" — the conversion story
- "I don't need to pay $55/dozen" — defiant statement of independence
- "$25-30 range" / "under $35" — the acceptable price zone
- "fair price" — what they want (not "cheap" or "budget" — FAIR)

### Feel Language
- "soft feel" / "soft off the putter" — feel is primarily about putting, not driving
- "clicky" (negative) vs "solid" (positive) — impact sound matters
- "mushy" (negative) — too soft is as bad as too hard
- "nice off the face" — the positive feel descriptor
- "compression" — informed golfers use this; it signals they've done research

### Ball Choice Language
- "I play [ball name]" — not "I use" — it's identity language
- "my go-to" / "my gamer" — the ball they've committed to
- "I switched from [X] to [Y]" — switching is an event, a decision worth narrating
- "for my swing speed" — swing speed is how they contextualize ball choice
- "I've tried / I tested / I compared" — the experimentation language
- "found my ball" — the language of resolution after searching

### Aging/Decline Language (handle with care)
- "I've lost [X] yards in the last [Y] years" — specific and measured
- "not what it used to be" — understated, not melodramatic
- "the body just doesn't cooperate" — external attribution (body is the villain, not them)
- "I'm [age] and..." — age always comes first as context-setting
- "still love the game" — the defiance beneath the decline
- DO NOT: "you're getting older" / "as you age" / "seniors" — these are labels they resist

## 2. Language DON'Ts — Never Use These

### Corporate/Marketing Language They Reject
- "revolutionary" / "game-changing" / "breakthrough" — dead buzzwords
- "premium performance" — every ball claims this; it means nothing
- "tour-level" — they know they're not tour players and resent the implication they should aspire to be
- "state-of-the-art" / "cutting-edge" — engineering jargon that creates distance
- "proprietary technology" — they don't care about your patents; they care about their yards
- "USGA approved" — NEVER. Use "conforms to the rules of golf"

### Condescending Language
- "for the average golfer" — no one self-identifies as "average"
- "you don't need a tour ball" — sounds like "you're not good enough"
- "for recreational golfers" — implies they're not serious
- "easy to hit" — implies their current ball is too hard for them
- "forgiving" — implies they make mistakes (even if they do)

### Overhype Language
- "CRUSH it" / "BOMB it" / "LAUNCH it" — infomercial energy
- "30 extra yards!" — unbelievable claims trigger immediate skepticism
- "the BEST ball ever made" — unprovable superlative
- "you'll never play another ball" — absolute claims they can argue with
- "guaranteed improvement" — risky and unsubstantiated

### Technical Jargon (unless earned)
- "COR" / "coefficient of restitution" — engineer language
- "spin axis management" — no one talks like this
- "optimized launch conditions" — translates to "hits farther" — just say that
- "thermoplastic urethane" — material science lecture
- "dimple turbulence model" — they don't care how the dimples work; they care where the ball lands

## 3. Voice of Customer — Emotional Registers

### Register 1: Frustration (PAIN bucket)
**When they're frustrated, they:**
- Use specific numbers and timelines ("lost 50 yards since hitting 70")
- Compare to their former selves ("Me at 30: 280. Me at 67: 210.")
- Express disbelief at the speed of decline ("I haven't really felt like I'm getting older but fuck me")
- Frame it as unfair ("it's not fair to play with a team as I just can't hold my own")

**Copy implication:** Mirror their specificity. Use numbers. Don't tell them they're frustrated — show them you understand by reflecting their exact experience.

### Register 2: Defiance (HOPE + some PAIN)
**When they're defiant, they:**
- Declare independence from premium brands ("I'll be damned if I'll ever pay $3-5 for a premium golf ball")
- Report switching with pride ("switched from Pro V1 to Maxfli Tour X and couldn't tell the difference")
- Challenge industry norms ("the entire golf ball industry is a scam")
- Assert they're still in the game ("I'm 68, crazy fit, etc.")

**Copy implication:** Honor their independence. iON+ should feel like a decision they made, not a product you sold them. The Soul.md voice ("knowledgeable golf buddy") is perfect — discovery, not pitch.

### Register 3: Seeking (SOLUTIONS_TRIED + ROOT_CAUSE)
**When they're actively searching, they:**
- Ask specific questions ("what ball for 93mph swing speed?")
- Share detailed experiment results ("tried both for 5 rounds each, here's what I found")
- Look for data ("my launch monitor shows...")
- Seek peer validation ("has anyone else switched from X to Y?")

**Copy implication:** Give them the data they're already looking for. Robot testing results, specific yardage comparisons, swing speed ranges. They're READY to be convinced — they just need credible proof.

### Register 4: Resentment (VILLAIN)
**When they're resentful, they:**
- Attack the industry broadly ("golf industry price gouging")
- Call out specific pricing ("new ProV balls are $55 a dozen")
- Express cynicism about brand claims ("I thought Vice is a marketing company?")
- Feel exploited by their own loyalty ("stop wasting your money — you're not good enough for prov1s")

**Copy implication:** Validate the resentment without amplifying it. iON+ is the answer to the problem, not another chapter in the complaint. "We built this ball because we were frustrated too" lands better than "the industry is ripping you off."
