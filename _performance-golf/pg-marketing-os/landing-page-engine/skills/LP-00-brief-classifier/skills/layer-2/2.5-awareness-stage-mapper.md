# 2.5 — Awareness Stage Mapper

**Skill:** LP-00-brief-classifier
**Position:** Layer 2, Microskill 5
**Purpose:** Map the Schwartz awareness stage (1-5) based on traffic source, audience sophistication, and product category norms. The awareness stage directly governs headline approach and section sequence in downstream architecture skills. Must explicitly cite evidence from the brief for stage selection — no hallucinated awareness stages.
**Effort Level:** medium
**Dependencies:** `brief-load.md` (Layer 0), `traffic-temp.md` (2.4), `audience-profile.md` (2.1 — sophistication dimension), `classification-signals.md` (Layer 1 — S4 signal)
**Output:** `awareness-map.md`

## Execution Logic

**Step 1: Review the Schwartz awareness model.**

```
Stage 1 — UNAWARE:
  The prospect does not know they have the problem.
  Headline approach: Curiosity, warning, pattern interrupt, story lead.
  Example: "What 3 Million Americans Do Before Bed That Doctors Are Now Warning About"

Stage 2 — PROBLEM-AWARE:
  Knows they have the problem, doesn't know a solution category exists.
  Headline approach: Problem acknowledgment + revelation promise.
  Example: "If You're Over 50 and Your Joints Ache Every Morning, Here's Why"

Stage 3 — SOLUTION-AWARE:
  Knows solutions exist in this category, doesn't know your specific product.
  Headline approach: Mechanism differentiation, "unlike other X" framing.
  Example: "The New Joint Compound That Works Differently Than Glucosamine"

Stage 4 — PRODUCT-AWARE:
  Knows your product, hasn't bought yet.
  Headline approach: Direct benefit, offer, reason-to-act-now.
  Example: "Try [Product Name] Risk-Free for 60 Days — Here's What Happens"

Stage 5 — MOST AWARE:
  Knows your product and brand well. Repeat visitor or existing customer.
  Headline approach: New version, deal, loyalty, urgency.
  Example: "New Formula — 3X Stronger. Existing Customers Save 40%"
```

**Step 2: Gather evidence for stage assignment.**
Collect all signals that indicate awareness stage:
- Traffic source (cold sources → lower stages, warm/hot → higher stages)
- Brief language about audience ("they don't know about...", "looking for alternatives to...")
- Product category norms (new category = lower stage, mature market = higher)
- Campaign context (cold acquisition vs. retargeting vs. winback)
- Competitor presence (if audience is comparing = Stage 3-4)

**Step 3: Assign stage with explicit evidence.**
Select ONE primary stage. For each piece of evidence, cite the brief directly:
- "Brief states traffic is Facebook cold targeting" → supports Stage 1-2
- "Brief describes audience as 'currently using glucosamine'" → supports Stage 3
- "Brief mentions retargeting campaign" → supports Stage 4-5

**Step 4: Map stage to headline direction and page structure guidance.**
Each stage has specific downstream implications for LP-03 (Above-Fold Architecture) and LP-04 (Section Sequence):
- Stages 1-2: Lead with story/curiosity, delay product reveal, heavy education needed
- Stage 3: Lead with mechanism differentiation, moderate education, competitive positioning
- Stages 4-5: Lead with offer/benefit, minimal education, proof and CTA forward

**Step 5: Cross-validate against traffic temperature (from 2.4).**
Confirm alignment. Flag any contradiction with explanation.

## Quality Criteria
- Stage assigned as a single integer 1-5 with full name
- Minimum 3 evidence points cited from the brief — not from general knowledge
- Cross-validation against traffic temperature performed
- Headline direction maps correctly to the assigned stage
- No awareness stage hallucination — every assignment backed by cited evidence
- Confidence level reflects actual certainty of the evidence

## Failure Modes
- Brief provides no awareness indicators → Infer from traffic source and product category norms, set confidence to `low`, flag `needs_validation`
- Evidence supports two adjacent stages equally (e.g., Stage 2-3) → Select the lower stage (more conservative — more education never hurts), note the ambiguity
- Traffic temperature conflicts with awareness stage → Flag the conflict explicitly, do not silently reconcile
