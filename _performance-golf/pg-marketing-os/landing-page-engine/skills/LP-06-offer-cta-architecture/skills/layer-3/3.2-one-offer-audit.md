## The -266% Rule

**Source:** KlientBoost research — multiple offers on a single page reduce conversions by up to 266%.

**Why it happens:** When visitors face multiple offers, they experience decision paralysis (Hick's Law). Instead of choosing between "buy" and "don't buy" (binary), they must choose between "buy A," "buy B," "buy neither," or "think about it later." The cognitive load of comparison replaces the emotional momentum of conviction.

**What counts as ONE offer:**
- ONE product in multiple quantities (1-pack, 3-pack, 6-pack) = ONE offer
- ONE product with subscription option (one-time or subscribe) = ONE offer
- ONE product with trial entry (free trial -> paid) = ONE offer
- ONE product with a bonus stack = ONE offer

**What counts as MULTIPLE offers (VIOLATION):**
- Two DIFFERENT products competing for attention
- "Also available: [other product]" recommendations above the close
- CTAs pointing to different checkout URLs or different products
- "Choose your plan" with fundamentally different services (not just quantities)
- Related product recommendations that compete with the primary purchase decision

## The 7-Point One-Offer Audit

### Point 1: Single Product Verification
**Question:** Is there exactly ONE product or service being offered for purchase?
**PASS:** Yes — one product, potentially in multiple quantities/formats
**FAIL:** No — multiple distinct products are presented as purchase options

### Point 2: Unified CTA Destination
**Question:** Do ALL CTAs on the page point to the same conversion action (same checkout, same cart, same form)?
**PASS:** Yes — all CTAs lead to the same destination
**FAIL:** No — some CTAs go to Product A checkout, others to Product B checkout

### Point 3: No Competing Product Recommendations
**Question:** Does the page avoid "also consider," "frequently bought together" (with different products), or "you might also like" sections that present alternatives to the primary offer?
**PASS:** No competing recommendations present, OR "frequently bought together" section adds items TO the existing cart (not replacing)
**FAIL:** Competing product recommendations present that could redirect purchase intent

### Point 4: No Multiple Checkout Paths
**Question:** Is there only ONE path to checkout from this page?
**PASS:** Yes — one add-to-cart flow, one checkout sequence
**FAIL:** No — multiple distinct checkout entry points for different products

### Point 5: Bundle Tiers = Same Product
**Question:** If bundle tiers exist (1-pack, 3-pack, subscribe), are they all quantity/format variations of the SAME product?
**PASS:** Yes — tiers are quantities of one product, OR no tiers exist
**FAIL:** No — tiers are actually different products masquerading as quantity options

### Point 6: Value Stack = Supplements to Core Offer
**Question:** Do bonus items in the value stack supplement the core offer rather than competing with it?
**PASS:** Yes — bonuses enhance the core product (guides, access, tools that relate to the main product)
**FAIL:** No — "bonuses" are actually separate products that could be purchased independently and distract from the core offer

### Point 7: Urgency Applies to ONE Thing
**Question:** Does the urgency/scarcity messaging apply to the SAME offer that all CTAs point to?
**PASS:** Yes — urgency is about the single offer, OR no urgency present
**FAIL:** No — urgency messaging references a different offer or product than the primary CTAs

---

## Execution Logic

**Step 1:** Run all 7 audit points. For each, document evidence and result.

**Step 2:** Calculate overall result:
- All 7 PASS -> One-Offer Audit: **PASS**
- Any 1+ FAIL -> One-Offer Audit: **FAIL**

**Step 3:** If FAIL:
- Identify the specific violation(s)
- Prescribe the exact fix for each violation
- The fix ALWAYS involves REMOVING the competing offer, NOT "explaining it better"
- GATE_3 is BLOCKED until all violations are resolved

**Step 4:** Document edge cases considered:
- Subscription vs. one-time purchase of same product -> ONE offer (PASS)
- Bundle tiers of same product -> ONE offer (PASS)
- Upsell AFTER checkout (not on the landing page) -> ONE offer (PASS — upsell is post-purchase)
- "Frequently bought together" that ADDS to cart -> Acceptable if clearly supplementary
- "Choose between Plan A and Plan B" with different features/products -> MULTIPLE offers (FAIL)

## Quality Criteria
- All 7 audit points explicitly evaluated with evidence
- Result is binary PASS or FAIL — no "conditional pass," no "partial pass"
- Edge cases documented
- If FAIL: specific fixes prescribed (always removal of competing element)
- Conversion impact note included as reminder of stakes

## Failure Modes
- Offer structure genuinely ambiguous (is it one product or two?) -> When in doubt, rule it ONE offer if: same brand, same checkout, same category. Rule it MULTIPLE offers if: different categories, different checkouts, or the visitor must choose BETWEEN them rather than buying one thing.
- Client designed the page with multiple offers intentionally -> Document the -266% penalty. Recommend splitting into separate pages. "Each offer deserves its own page with its own persuasion architecture. Combining them on one page reduces conversions for both." Flag for human review.
- "Frequently bought together" section exists -> Acceptable ONLY if it ADDS items to the cart alongside the primary product (supplementary), NOT if it presents an alternative purchase (competitive).
