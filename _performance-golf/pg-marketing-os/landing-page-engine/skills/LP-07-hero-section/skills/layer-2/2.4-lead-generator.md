## Pre-Generation Protocol

**Before writing a single word of the lead:**

1. **Read lead specimens verbatim** from `specimens-loaded.md`. Note: what does the FIRST sentence accomplish? How does it earn the right to the second sentence?

2. **Read pain quotes** from copy assets. Identify 2–3 phrases the audience uses that will be mirrored in the lead.

3. **State the three planned loops** from `lead-type-selected.md`. These are the gaps you will CREATE but NOT FILL in the lead.

4. **Write the lead type instruction** in your own words before generating: "I am writing a [Lead Type] lead. It must: [specific behaviors for this lead type]."

5. **State what will NOT appear in the lead:**
   - The mechanism name will be teased but not named (unless it's a revelation lead where naming = opening the loop)
   - The root cause will be hinted but not explained
   - The solution will be previewed but not revealed
   - The price will not appear

## Lead Type Execution Guides

### Type 1: Story Lead Execution

**Beat sequence:**
1. **Scene drop** (20–50 words): Drop reader into a specific, vivid moment. No setup, no preamble. We are IN the scene.
   - The who: A named/described person (doctor, researcher, patient, founder, "a woman named [Name]")
   - The where: Specific location (lab, clinic, 3 AM at a desk, small town in [country])
   - The what: A specific action or discovery happening

2. **Stakes established** (50–100 words): Why does this moment matter? What was at stake? Reader must feel the weight.

3. **Loop 1 opened** (30–50 words): The story arrives at a decision point or discovery — then STOPS. "What happened next would change everything."

4. **Bridge to reader** (50–100 words): Connect the story to the reader. "If you've ever [experience matching audience pain]..."

5. **Loop 2 opened** (30–50 words): Tease the revelation without revealing it. "There was a reason [subject] had [problem]. And it wasn't what anyone expected."

6. **Identification moment** (50–100 words): Reader sees themselves in the story. Specific pain language mirroring audience vocabulary.

7. **Loop 3 + forward momentum** (50–100 words): "In a moment, I'll show you [teased promise]. But first, you need to understand [bridge to next section]."

**Total: 300–600 words for standard page**

### Type 2: Promise Lead Execution

**Beat sequence:**
1. **Promise statement** (15–30 words): State the core benefit directly and specifically. Not vague. Not hedged.
2. **Audience qualifier** (20–30 words): Who this is for + who this is NOT for (self-selection)
3. **Loop 1 — The How Loop** (20–30 words): "What makes this possible is something called [tease without naming]..."
4. **Credibility anchor** (30–50 words): One specific proof signal (person, study, statistic — not vague)
5. **Failed solutions acknowledgment** (50–100 words): "You may have tried [X]. Here's why it didn't work — and why this is different."
6. **Loop 2 — The Why Loop** (20–30 words): "The reason most [solutions] fail is because they address [symptom], not [tease root cause]..."
7. **Loop 3 — The Proof Loop** (20–30 words): "Before this page is over, you'll see [teased proof]."
8. **Transition forward** (20–30 words): Read on signal.

**Total: 250–500 words for standard page**

### Type 3: Probing Question Lead Execution

**Beat sequence:**
1. **Question 1** (10–20 words): First yes-question. Highly specific to primary pain.
2. **Question 2** (10–20 words): Second yes-question. Escalates specificity — a symptom of the symptom.
3. **Question 3** (10–20 words): Third yes-question. The emotional consequence.
4. **Validation statement** (30–50 words): "If you answered yes to any of those, you're not alone. And more importantly — it's not your fault."
5. **Loop 1 — Reframe** (30–50 words): "The reason [problem persists] isn't [what they think]. The real cause is something that very few people are talking about."
6. **Failed solutions** (50–100 words): "That's why [common approaches] never seem to work. They're treating the wrong thing."
7. **Loop 2 — Solution tease** (20–30 words): "What I'm about to show you addresses the actual cause — something [authority] only recently identified."
8. **Loop 3 — Proof tease** (20–30 words): "And there are [number] people who've already experienced [specific result]."
9. **Transition** (15–25 words): Forward signal.

**Total: 250–450 words for standard page**

### Types 4, 5, 6: See lead type selection document for structure — apply same loop and beat principles.

## Lens Application to Lead Generation

Generate **one complete lead draft** using the primary lens most aligned with the lead type:

| Lead Type | Primary Lens | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Story Lead | Lens 2 (Empath) | Story leads live or die on emotional resonance |
| Promise Lead | Lens 1 (Converter) | Direct promise = DR formula territory |
| Probing Question | Lens 2 (Empath) | Questions must use audience's exact language |
| Revelation | Lens 3 (Skeptic) | Revelation leads need proof anchoring to be credible |
| Agitation | Lens 2 (Empath) | Pain amplification requires audience vocabulary |
| If-Then | Lens 1 (Converter) | Conditional qualifier is a DR formula skill |

**Write the full lead using the primary lens.** Then write a **100-word opening alternative** using a secondary lens. Present both in the output for human consideration during selection.

---

## Loop Implementation Rules

**Rule 1: Open loops with questions or reveals, not conclusions.**
- Closing: "The root cause of fatigue is mitochondrial dysfunction." (closed — explained)
- Opening: "The root cause of fatigue isn't what most doctors tell you." (open — unexplained)

**Rule 2: Reference loops without resolving them.**
- After opening Loop 1 (story), Loop 2 can reference it: "Remember what I said about [X]? That's part of why [Y] doesn't work."
- This keeps Loop 1 active while opening Loop 2.

**Rule 3: The closing sentence of the lead must open or reinforce the final loop.**
- Bad closing: "That's why [Product] was created." (closes loop)
- Good closing: "In just a moment, I'm going to show you the one thing that [authority] discovered that changes everything. But first, I want you to understand why what you've been told about [problem] is costing you [consequence]."

**Rule 4: Layer loops — don't stack them.**
- Bad (stacked): Opens 3 loops in consecutive paragraphs, reader forgets first two
- Good (layered): Open Loop 1 → develop it → reference it → open Loop 2 → link to Loop 1 → open Loop 3

## Quality Criteria
- Full lead written to target length (±10%)
- 3 loops explicitly opened and NOT closed within the lead
- First sentence earns the second (specific, vivid, or surprising)
- At least 2 instances of audience's own vocabulary (from pain quotes or profile)
- No mechanism explained (only teased)
- No root cause revealed (only hinted)
- No product named (only referenced as "what I'm about to show you" or similar)
- Alternative opening provided for human selection comparison

## Failure Modes
- Lead resolves a loop (closes a gap the reader wanted answered) → Revise to withhold resolution
- Lead length falls short of target → Expand emotional beats, don't add padding
- First sentence doesn't earn the second → Rewrite opening — apply "would someone who's about to leave stop at this sentence?" test
- Lead sounds like marketing copy rather than one person speaking to another → Revise to match voice register; reduce abstraction
