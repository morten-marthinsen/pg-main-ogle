# 2.6 — Proof Transition Writer

**Skill:** LP-10-social-proof-writer
**Position:** Layer 2, Microskill 6
**Purpose:** Write the narrative bridge copy that transitions the reader INTO each proof block (from the preceding section) and OUT of each proof block (toward the next section or CTA). Great transitions make proof feel like a natural part of the page's argument. Bad transitions (or no transitions) make proof blocks feel like interruptions.
**Effort Level:** high
**Dependencies:** `transition-plan.md` (1.3), `proof-slot-analysis.md` (1.1), `brief-loaded.md` (0.1), `specimen-proof-copy.md` (0.3)
**Output:** `proof-transitions.md`

## Transition Types

### IN Transitions (Narrative -> Proof)

The IN transition answers: "Why am I about to see testimonials/reviews/proof right now?"

**Type A (Long-Form Sales Page):**
- 1-3 sentences of prose
- Must feel like a natural progression from the preceding section
- Creates anticipation for the proof
- Connects the preceding section's claims to the proof that follows

**Type B (Ecomm/PDP):**
- 0-1 sentences (may be implicit via visual design)
- Section header serves as the transition
- No prose paragraph needed — the layout signals the shift
- If explicit: keep to a single line

### OUT Transitions (Proof -> Next Section)

The OUT transition answers: "Now that I've seen proof, what should I do/think/learn next?"

**Type A:**
- 1-3 sentences that propel the reader forward
- Must bridge FROM the emotional impact of proof TO the logical next step
- Often includes a reader-mirror statement ("If that sounds like you...")
- May include a momentum signal ("And those are just the stories from this month...")

**Type B:**
- 0-1 sentences (often implicit)
- May be replaced by a CTA button placement
- If explicit: bridge to the next product section or purchase CTA

---

## The 6 IN Transition Techniques

Each proof block must use a DIFFERENT technique. No repeats.

| # | Technique | Description | Best For |
|---|-----------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | **Humble Redirect** | Deflect from self-claim to customer proof | After mechanism/product explanation sections |
| 2 | **Evidence Callback** | Reference a specific claim made earlier, now prove it | After scientific/educational sections |
| 3 | **The Challenge** | Acknowledge skepticism, then answer it with proof | Early proof blocks, skeptical audiences |
| 4 | **Volume Reveal** | Lead with a specific number of results | After product intro, near social proof strips |
| 5 | **Story Tee-Up** | Introduce a specific person by name before the testimonial | For featured testimonials and case studies |
| 6 | **Pattern Interrupt** | Stack 3 specific results as a rapid-fire pattern | Before accumulation-zone proof blocks |

## The 5 OUT Transition Techniques

| # | Technique | Description | Best For |
|---|-----------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | **Momentum Builder** | "And that's just a handful..." expand the scope, bridge to next section | Between proof blocks |
| 2 | **Reader Mirror** | Reflect the reader's probable thoughts back at them | Before offer or CTA sections |
| 3 | **Pivot to Action** | Bridge from proof directly to a decision point | Before pricing/offer/CTA |
| 4 | **Proof Summary** | Aggregate the proof into one punchy statement | After the final proof block |
| 5 | **Next Wave Tease** | Tease what comes next to maintain forward momentum | Between education and accumulation zones |

---

## Execution Rules

1. **Every IN transition must reference the PRECEDING section's content.** "But don't just take my word for it" fails because it doesn't connect to anything specific. "I just explained the 7-form mechanism — but what matters is what happens when real people take it" connects.

2. **Every OUT transition must bridge toward the FOLLOWING section.** Dead-end transitions that summarize without propelling forward are failures. The reader must have a reason to keep scrolling.

3. **No two IN transitions use the same technique on the same page.** If you have 3 proof blocks, you need 3 different IN techniques.

4. **No two OUT transitions use the same technique on the same page.**

5. **The BANNED phrases list is absolute:**
   - "But don't just take our word for it" (literal)
   - "See what our customers are saying"
   - "Hear from real people"
   - "Real results from real people"
   - "Success stories that speak for themselves"

6. **Thread to the hero section promise** where possible. At least one transition should callback to the promise statement from the hero section (e.g., "Remember the promise on this page — [promise]. Here's [count] people who've experienced it firsthand.").

---

## Execution Steps

1. **For each proof block,** load the transition plan from 1.3 (technique assignments, context, tone).
2. **Write the IN transition** using the assigned technique. Reference the preceding section's specific content.
3. **Write the OUT transition** using the assigned technique. Bridge to the following section's topic.
4. **Verify uniqueness** — no two IN or OUT transitions use the same technique or similar phrasing.
5. **Verify banned phrases** — zero instances of any banned transition phrase.
6. **Verify threading** — at least one transition callbacks to the hero section promise.

## Quality Criteria
- Every proof block has IN and OUT transitions (or documented as implicit for Type B)
- No repeated techniques across IN transitions or across OUT transitions
- Zero banned phrases
- IN transitions reference the preceding section's specific content
- OUT transitions bridge to the following section's specific topic
- At least one transition callbacks to the hero section promise
- Voice matches page-brief.json direction
- Type A transitions are prose (1-3 sentences); Type B transitions are structural (0-1 sentences)

## Failure Modes
- Generic IN transitions that could go before any proof block -> Each IN must reference the specific preceding section content.
- OUT transitions that summarize but don't propel forward -> The reader must have a reason to keep scrolling after the proof.
- Using banned phrases -> These are absolute. Replace immediately.
- Same technique for multiple blocks -> Diversify. Use the technique lists above.
- Missing hero callback -> At least one transition must thread back to the page's central promise.
- Writing prose transitions for Type B sections -> Type B uses structural transitions. Check page type.
