Summary
Donnie French and Chris McGinley discussed the marketing strategy and product differentiation for the new putter, identifying its key advantage as significant forward weighting—claiming 75% of the weight is in the first 25% of the head—which provides stability and a counteracting force against player-induced rotation or "face drift," a term proposed by Donnie French to replace "toe flow." Chris McGinley confirmed the initial launch will feature only the stock 74-degree lie angle to avoid negatively impacting conversion rates, but revealed an existing custom putter configuration tool that Donnie French plans to use for a later test to gauge customization demand, despite Chris McGinley's caution about the current low-volume custom manufacturing capacity in Oceanside, California. The participants established a narrative framing the RS1 as the next evolutionary step in putter design by actively helping the player, contrasting it with previous designs like Lab Golf, whose main pain point is distance control.

Details
Marketing Strategy and Product Differentiation: Donnie French initiated the discussion by seeking input on the product deck, noting a lack of marketing content and requesting clarification on differentiators to avoid pitfalls similar to past products like SQ. Chris McGinley responded by highlighting that the core argument is authentic, emphasizing that the putter features a significant amount of forward weight, a unique selling proposition that connects to the product's name and performance benefits (00:00:00). Chris McGinley asserted that they could claim the putter is the only one with 75% of its weight in the first 25% of the head, a measurable metric that they plan to confirm with engineering partners (00:02:31).
Forward Weighting as a Primary Advantage: Chris McGinley suggested that forward weighting is the key argument, making the face-down face balance feature secondary, even though the latter is visually unique. The forward weighting enables several performance claims, including stability for a straighter ball roll, the "push-pull" argument related to gravity, and the face-down face balance (00:05:18). Tour players have reacted positively to the concept, expressing surprise that it has not been done before (00:06:15).
The Concept of Face Drift: Donnie French proposed using the term "face drift" instead of "toe flow" to frame a legitimate problem that the new putter solves, noting that toe flow is widely discussed, but face drift is not in modern discourse (00:07:22). Chris McGinley clarified that conventional putters are designed with a tendency to rotate open around the shaft (toe flow), and the new putter is designed to "drift straight". Ben Marcoux suggested that toe flow might be viewed as a "ripple" of face drift (00:08:22).
Counteracting Player-Induced Rotation: Chris McGinley elaborated that face drift or toe flow results from both the putter's mechanical weighting and the player's natural tendency to rotate the club open during the backswing. The putter is designed to incorporate a counteracting force that works against this natural player-induced rotation, helping to keep the face square (00:09:29).
Lie Angle Options and Constraints: The conversation shifted to the lie angle options, which Donnie French found compelling. Chris McGinley confirmed that while there are three potential lie angles (70, 72, and 74 degrees), the product will initially be sold only in the most upright stock specification of 74 degrees for the launch. Chris McGinley noted that the different lie angle options were developed for tour players and custom seeding efforts (00:10:40) (00:12:49).
Customization and Conversion Rate Concerns: Chris McGinley mentioned that different lie angles have not been ordered for the general launch, and customizing these putters at volume would require new operational commitments. Donnie French acknowledged that offering too many choices could negatively affect the conversion rate, siding with the stock configuration for the initial launch, though recognizing the e-commerce landscape is changing (00:12:49).
Launch Options and Shaft Configuration: For the launch, they will offer four options based on shaft and grip combinations (steel/rubber or graphite/polyurethane), creating essentially a heavy putter and a lighter putter, multiplied by right and left-handed versions. Chris McGinley noted that the ambidextrous nature of the putter (flipping the shaft for left-hand use) is a unique and potentially sellable feature (00:13:58).
Lie Angle Customization Challenges: Donnie French inquired about selling the hosel inserts as a separate option for different lie angles, which Chris McGinley demonstrated would require changing the entire shaft assembly, making it too expensive to offer as an upsell for the initial launch (00:14:57). This customization aspect will be reserved for a future custom offering (00:16:01).
Testing Customization Demand: Donnie French proposed a small test to gauge demand for customization by implementing a section on the product page offering a direct call for a custom fitting or a request form (00:16:58). Chris McGinley shared data from Titleist, where over 50% of their golf club business is custom spec, even though stock specs are chosen for the most popular fit (00:19:24).
Custom Putter Configuration Tool: Chris McGinley surprised the team by revealing an existing online tool built by their engineering partners, which allows customers to fully customize the putter's model, length, lie angle, shaft color, alignment line colors, and even include laser engraving (00:21:48). Donnie French was enthusiastic, immediately devising a plan to use the tool as a request form fulfillment mechanism by sending customers a login or a direct link with an integrated payment option (00:23:40).
Custom Manufacturing Capacity: Chris McGinley cautioned that the custom manufacturing cell in Oceanside, California, is currently set up for low volume, capable of assembling only a few putters a day. The plan remains to launch the stock product first, then utilize the custom tool for a later test, potentially after the Founders 500 and the official launch (00:25:29).
Founders 500 Edition and Copy Review: Chris McGinley showed Donnie French the aesthetic design of the limited Founders 500 putter, which will not have sequential numbers due to the high cost (00:27:33). Donnie French confirmed they will send the marketing copy to Chris McGinley for review before page construction to ensure accuracy and to confirm that the claims remain within bounds (00:28:22).
The Hammerhead Design Implication: Chris McGinley suggested using the "hammerhead" appearance as a feature in the copy, implying that the putter's heavy head is less likely to twist off-line, similar to a hammer. Ben Marcoux reinforced the idea that the heavy forward waiting can be positioned as a new, unique product category (00:29:16).
Comparison to Previous Heavy Putters: The team discussed previous "heavy putters," confirming that while heavy putters have existed (like the brand Heavy Putters), none utilized the heavy forward weighting design relative to the shaft that the RS1 employs (00:30:17).
The RS1's Distinction from Lab Golf: Donnie French summarized research indicating that the main pain point for golfers is "face drift" and that Lab Golf's weakness is its unconventional appearance, heavy feel, and high price (00:32:05). Chris McGinley argued that while Lab's zero-torque design is good, it requires the player to completely control the face rotation, whereas the RS1's counteracting force actively helps the human player prevent the face from opening (00:33:19). This positioning—the putter working with the player—was identified as the core, scientifically superior argument (00:34:17).
Evolutionary Narrative and Competitor Framing: Chris McGinley established an evolutionary narrative: old putters flopped open, Lab eliminated the flop (making it entirely player-dependent), and the RS1 not only eliminates the flop but also helps the human player with their natural rotation tendency (00:35:15). This narrative frames the RS1 as the next step in putter design evolution (00:36:48). The plan includes subtly taking a shot at big companies, suggesting that small companies like Performance Golf and Lab are driving innovation because the major manufacturers are "set in their ways" (00:39:25).
Distance Control and Lab Golf's Weakness: The discussion turned to distance control, which analysis found to be the number one technical complaint about Lab putters online. Chris McGinley suggested that the RS1's heavy front may be beneficial for distance control, and that this could be tested using the robot and high-speed cameras (00:41:36) (00:46:17). Chris McGinley also noted that traditional revilers are designed for zero-torque putters like Lab's, and the RS1 may perform better in a face-down balance test on a table (00:47:22).
Forward Weighting Relative to the Shaft: Chris McGinley concluded that the forward weighting is the "special magical sauce" of the RS1, particularly the weight forward of the shaft (00:50:55). This specific design feature is what generates the face-down face balance, the counteracting toe flow, and the higher moment of inertia (MOI) in the face .
