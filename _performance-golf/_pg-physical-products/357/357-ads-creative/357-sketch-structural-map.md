### Section 6 — Feature #1: Dynamic Loft + Analogy (1:10-1:39) | ~120 words

**FEATURE:** Dynamic Loft (from the 3-wood size head)

**TaylorMade does:** First major technical feature (weight distribution / MOI). Uses an x-ray visual. Explains with a real-world analogy (tightrope + pole). Lands the concept.

**TaylorMade says:** "First up is the weight distribution of the classic Spider Tour X clubhead. In this x-ray image, the darker colors represent higher density metals, and you can see how engineers have pushed that heavy material all the way to the edges of the club. This design choice isn't just for looks. It gives the clubhead a high moment of inertia, or MOI. It's kind of like walking on a tightrope. On its own, balancing is nearly impossible. But if someone hands you a long pole that spreads the mass away from your center, it becomes much easier to stay in control. It creates a higher moment of inertia — and we use the same exact concept with these putters."

**Structure:** "First up is [feature]." → Visual explanation → "It's kind of like [analogy]." → "We use the same concept."

**Our parallel argument:** First up is dynamic loft — how the 3-wood size head solves the launch problem. The bigger head pushes the center of gravity lower and further back from the face. Lower CG = higher launch. And the deeper CG wants to line up with the shaft at impact, so the club bends forward — adding loft beyond what's built in. Garden hose analogy: water pressure = swing speed, angle = loft. There's an optimal angle for each pressure. Dynamic loft is like the club adjusting that angle for you at impact.

**Our beat:** Name the feature (dynamic loft). Show it visually (three clubs side view — narrow/medium/wide, CG positions). Lower CG + deep CG = two effects of the big head. Garden hose analogy. Land it.

**Note:** Lower center of gravity (Power Launch Crown) folds into this section naturally — it's the same mechanism. One sentence: "That bigger head pushes the center of gravity lower and further back." Lower = launch. Further back = dynamic loft. Two results, one design choice.

**Structure:** Problem → data/visual → analogy → solution mechanism → proof/comparison → result.

**Our parallel argument:** Dynamic loft gets the ball in the air — but does it go far enough? That's where C-Cup comes in. Problem: senior golfers can't generate enough swing speed on their own. Most people think club speed is what matters — but the number that actually matters is ball speed. "What if you missed the ball? Who cares how fast you swing?" The C-Cup face is a piece of metal shaped like a C, welded to the body. Unlike a flat plate, the C flexes more at impact. More flex = more ball speed = more carry distance — without swinging any harder. That's the Elastic Effect. Then the feature-stack coda: and to make it even easier to hit, we put dual stability rails on the sole — training wheels — so the club glides through the turf instead of digging in. Clean contact, every time.

**Our beat:** Problem (need more speed but can't swing harder). Insight (ball speed > club speed). Mechanism (C-Cup face flexes, creates more ball speed). Result (more carry without more effort). Feature-stack coda: dual rails = training wheels for clean contact. Quick.
