### SECTION 6 — Feature Deep-Dive #1: Dynamic Loft (The Garden Hose + Club Width)

*This mirrors TaylorMade's MOI / weight distribution section with the tightrope analogy.*

| TaylorMade Original | 357 Version (McGinley Voice) |
|---|---|
| "First up is the weight distribution of the classic Spider Tour X clubhead. In this x-ray image, the darker colors represent higher density metals, and you can see how engineers have pushed that heavy material all the way to the edges of the club." | "Now let me show you why that 3-wood head matters so much. Think of it like a garden hose. You turn on the water and you want to squirt it as far as you can. Point the hose down — water hits the ground. Point it too high — water goes straight up and comes right back." |
| "This design choice isn't just for looks. It gives the clubhead a high moment of inertia, or MOI." | "There's an optimal angle — where the water carries as far as it possibly can. The water pressure is your swing speed. The angle of the hose is your loft. And once that angle drops too low for your pressure... the water just hits the ground. That's your critical loft threshold." |
| "It's kind of like walking on a tightrope. On its own, balancing is nearly impossible. But if someone hands you a long pole that spreads the mass away from your center, it becomes much easier to stay in control. It creates a higher moment of inertia — and we use the same exact concept with these putters." | "But here's where it gets interesting. Look at these three clubs from the side. An iron — very narrow front to back. A hybrid — a little wider. And the 357 — much wider. How wide a club head is plays a huge role in how it performs. Because the wider the head, the deeper the center of gravity sits — further back from the face." |
| | "And when you swing it, that center of gravity wants to line up with the shaft. So the club actually bends forward at impact — adding loft that isn't even built into the face. We call that dynamic loft. The wider the head, the more dynamic loft you get. And the 357 has the widest head of any club at this loft." |

**Visual Direction:** Sean draws a garden hose in three positions — aimed down (water splashes at feet), aimed too high (arcs short), and at the optimal angle (water carries far). Labels: "Swing Speed" on the water flow, "Loft" on the hose angle. Then transitions: Sean draws three club heads from a SIDE PROFILE view — iron (thin/narrow), hybrid (medium width), 357 (wide front-to-back). The width difference is dramatic. CG dots on each showing how the wider the head, the deeper the CG. Then draws the 357 at impact — head bending forward, arrows showing dynamic loft. "Dynamic Loft" label.

**Sketch Checkpoint #6:** Garden hose at three angles — down, too high, optimal. Labeled.

**Sketch Checkpoint #7:** Three clubs from SIDE view showing width difference (narrow → medium → wide). CG dots progressively deeper. 357 at impact showing dynamic loft bending. Arrows + label.

A small green with a flag at the end — only the 357's ball is on it. The visual proof is immediate and undeniable.

**Sketch Checkpoint #8:** Three complete trajectory arcs. Iron = low/ballistic, runs past. Hybrid = medium, rolls through. 357 = high, steep, stops on the green. Green with flag — only 357's ball is on it.
