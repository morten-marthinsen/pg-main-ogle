### Section 1: The Problem — Critical Loft Threshold (~0:30-0:55)

| Time | Narration | Visual / Sketch Direction |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 0:30 | Every club in your bag has a certain amount of loft — that's the angle on the face that determines how high the ball goes. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #3]** Sean draws a simple club face with an angle line showing loft. Clean diagram. Label: "Loft." |
| 0:37 | And here's what most golfers don't realize — for your swing speed, every club has a point where the loft gets too low... and the ball just stops going up. | Sean draws a trajectory arc that flattens out. The ball flight gets lower and lower as loft decreases. Visual progression left to right. |
| 0:46 | I call it the critical loft threshold. It's the exact point where a club stops working for you. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #4]** Sean writes "Critical Loft Threshold" and draws a line — above it, ball flights that work. Below it, flights that don't. Clean binary visual. |
| 0:52 | And for most senior golfers, you hit that threshold somewhere around your 4- or 5-iron. | Sean circles the 4-iron and 5-iron zone on the diagram. |

### Section 2: The Proof — Your 4-Iron Is Lying To You (~0:55-1:20)

| Time | Narration | Visual / Sketch Direction |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 0:55 | Here's what that actually looks like. Say you hit your 5-iron — nice, high launch. Good carry. Ball comes down steep and stops on the green. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #5]** Sean draws a golfer hitting a 5-iron. High, arcing ball flight. Ball lands softly. Green with flag. Beautiful trajectory. |
| 1:05 | Now you grab your 4-iron. It's got less loft and a longer shaft — so technically, it should go farther. But watch what happens. | Sean draws the 4-iron trajectory next to the 5-iron. Lower, flatter. |
| 1:12 | The ball doesn't launch high enough. It carries the same distance as your 5-iron... but it runs right over the green. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #6]** The 4-iron ball flight runs past the green. Ball rolling off the back. Sean draws a small "X" where it ends up — past the pin. The 5-iron lands on the green. The 4-iron doesn't. Same carry, totally different result. |
| 1:18 | You've now got two clubs that go the same distance. One of them is no longer playable. That's your critical loft threshold. | Sean draws an equals sign between the two carry distances. Circles the 4-iron and draws a line through it. Dead club. |

### Section 3: The Analogy — The Garden Hose (~1:20-1:45)

| Time | Narration | Visual / Sketch Direction |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 1:20 | Think of it like a garden hose. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #7]** Sean draws a simple garden hose with water coming out. |
| 1:23 | If you point the hose straight down, the water just hits the ground. Point it too high, the water goes up and comes right back. | Sean draws two hose angles — one aimed down (water splashes at feet), one aimed too high (water arcs short). |
| 1:31 | But there's an optimal angle — where the water carries as far as it possibly can. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #8]** Sean draws the middle angle — water arcs perfectly and carries the farthest. Labels it "Optimal." |
| 1:36 | Now — the water pressure is your swing speed. The angle of the hose is your loft. | Sean labels the water flow "Swing Speed" and the hose angle "Loft." Connection made. |
| 1:41 | And if you don't have enough water pressure — meaning enough swing speed — you need more angle. More loft. That's physics. | Sean adjusts the drawing: lower pressure = needs higher angle to carry far. Visual is intuitive and immediate. |

### Section 4: The Mechanism — Not All 21° Clubs Are Equal (~1:45-2:05)

| Time | Narration | Visual / Sketch Direction |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 1:45 | So the question becomes — how do you get more loft AND more distance at the same time? | Transition: Sean moves to a new area of the page. |
| 1:50 | Take three clubs. All 21 degrees of loft. An iron. A hybrid. And a fairway wood. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #9]** Sean draws all three club heads side by side — thin iron, mid-size hybrid, large fairway head. All labeled "21°". |
| 1:57 | Same loft. But totally different performance. The iron barely launches. The hybrid's better — but still not enough for most seniors. | Sean draws trajectory lines above each: iron = low and flat, hybrid = medium. |
| 2:03 | But the fairway wood — with that bigger head — the center of gravity sits deeper, further back from the face. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #10]** Sean draws a dot showing CG position on each club. The fairway wood's CG is noticeably further back. Dotted line from CG to face. |
| 2:07 | And here's the part that changed everything for me as an engineer. When you swing that bigger head, the center of gravity wants to line up with the shaft. So the club actually bends forward at impact — adding loft that isn't even built into the club. | **[SKETCH CHECKPOINT #11]** Sean draws the club head at impact, slightly rotated forward. Arrows showing the bending motion. "Dynamic Loft" label. This is the key visual — the club literally adding loft on its own. |
| 2:15 | We call it dynamic loft. The club doesn't just start at 21 degrees — it adds more at impact. Higher launch. Steeper descent. The ball stops on the green. | Sean completes the trajectory arc above the fairway wood: high launch, steep descent, lands soft. Clearly superior to the other two. |

## PRODUCTION NOTES

- **Total body runtime:** ~2:05 (0:30 to 2:35)
- **Hook runtime:** ~0:30 each
- **Total with hook:** ~2:35
- **One-page constraint:** All sketches live on a single illustrated page. Camera movements (pan, zoom) reveal different areas, but the illustration itself is continuous.
- **Transitions between checkpoints:** AI-assisted mask reveals (looks like drawing is happening live) or simple camera movements across the page.
- **CAD wireframe → photorealistic:** Per McGinley's suggestion, the 357 hero moment (Checkpoint #12) can transition from Sean's sketch → CAD wireframe → photorealistic render.
- **Platform cuts:** Individual sections (especially the garden hose analogy or the 21° comparison) can be extracted as standalone 15-30 second ads for TikTok/Reels.
- **Chris McGinley review needed:** All technical claims (dynamic loft, CG behavior, launch characteristics) should be verified by McGinley before production.
