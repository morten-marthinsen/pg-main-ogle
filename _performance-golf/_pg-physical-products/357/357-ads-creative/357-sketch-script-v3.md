### Section 6 — Feature #1: Dynamic Loft | Target: 118 words

| TaylorMade (118 words) | 357 Version |
|---|---|
| First up is the weight distribution of the classic Spider Tour X clubhead. In this x-ray image, the darker colors represent higher density metals, and you can see how engineers have pushed that heavy material all the way to the edges of the club. This design choice isn't just for looks. It gives the clubhead a high moment of inertia, or MOI. It's kind of like walking on a tightrope. On its own, balancing is nearly impossible. But if someone hands you a long pole that spreads the mass away from your center, it becomes much easier to stay in control. It creates a higher moment of inertia — and we use the same exact concept with these putters. | First up is the dynamic loft that comes from the three-wood size head. Think of this concept like a garden hose. The water pressure is your swing speed. The angle of the hose is your loft. There's an optimal angle for each pressure — and once the angle drops too low, the water just hits the ground. Same thing happens with your approach clubs. But with the 357's bigger head, the club naturally adds loft at impact — beyond what's built into the face. That's dynamic loft. And it's the reason this club launches higher than any iron or hybrid at the same loft. |

### Section 7 — Feature #2: C-Cup Construction | Target: 274 words

| TaylorMade (274 words) | 357 Version |
|---|---|
| Next up is something called the Pure Roll insert. Most golfers don't realize this, but when your ball sits on the green, it's slightly pressed into the surface. It's only millimeters at most, but when you hit the ball, it can pop up, bounce, and skid before coming to what's called true roll. When we line up and chart that spin rate curve on a putt hit with a TaylorMade mallet with the milled face grooves — here in red — you'll see that the ball leaves the face with almost no forward roll. Then each time it bounces and hits the ground, it gains a little spin with friction, until it finally grips the green for the remainder of the putt. The problem is, in this skid zone, there's almost no control on the ball, and you get a lot of randomness in the putt. It's kind of like if an F1 car launched off the line with its tires skidding. There's no traction, no precision, just chaos — until the wheels finally lock in with the track. To fix this, we developed the Pure Roll insert. At a glance, it just looks like grooves on a face, but if you zoom in, you'll see that they're actually angled beams. When they make contact with the ball, they flex, and then as the ball leaves the face, they release — instantly creating topspin. If we overlay that spin chart again, this time with a Spider featuring a Pure Roll insert, the ball starts its forward roll off the face, which minimizes skidding and reaches its true roll sooner, helping it stay on its intended line with more consistency. | Next up is something called C-Cup Construction. Most golfers don't realize this, but the number that matters most in golf isn't club speed — it's ball speed. How fast the ball comes off the face is what determines how far it carries. And most golfers have already hit their limit on swing speed. They can't swing any harder. So the club has to do the work. If you look at the face of most clubs, the face is welded flat — like a plate. A plate is rigid. It doesn't flex much at impact, so it can only return a fixed amount of energy to the ball. The problem is, for senior golfers trying to carry the ball 190 or 200 yards, that's not enough ball speed. It's kind of like bouncing a ball off a brick wall versus a trampoline. The wall is rigid — the ball comes back with limited energy. But a trampoline flexes on contact, stores that energy, and releases it — sending the ball back faster. To solve this, we developed C-Cup Construction. At a glance, it just looks like a normal club face, but if you look closely, there's a piece of metal shaped like a C that's welded to the body. When it makes contact with the ball, the C-shape flexes more than a flat plate — storing more energy at impact. And as the ball leaves the face, that energy releases — generating more ball speed with the exact same swing you're already making. We call that the Elastic Effect. |

**Visual:** Sean draws a close-up of the 357 face. Shows a flat plate vs the C-shaped piece — like a brick wall vs a trampoline. The C flexes, the plate doesn't. Arrows showing energy transfer → ball speed. "Elastic Effect" label.
