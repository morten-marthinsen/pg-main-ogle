## OVERVIEW

The Expectation Schema maps what prospects EXPECT when they encounter slice-fixing driver messaging, based on their accumulated experiences with:
- Previous equipment purchases
- Lessons and instruction
- Marketing claims
- Product performance

Understanding these schemas allows messaging to either confirm positive expectations or deliberately disrupt negative ones.

---

# SECTION 1: EQUIPMENT PURCHASE EXPECTATIONS

## Schema 1.1: "Big Claims, Small Results"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Prospects expect marketing claims to be exaggerated based on repeated disappointment.

> **Evidence:**
> - "I have tried many golf clubs that claim to be longer against my current club and only one of the 8 different drivers really was longer" [ST-001]
> - "The data does not completely support the claims made in the promotional video" [CM-087]
> - "Physics is whispering in my ear and saying, Um, no" [V-100]

**Schema Impact:**
- Default skepticism toward distance/accuracy claims
- Discount marketing numbers by 50%+
- Look for third-party validation

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Lead with third-party data (Golf Digest, MyGolfSpy)
- Underpromise specific numbers, let testimonials overdeliver
- Acknowledge skepticism directly: "You've heard claims before..."

---

## Schema 1.2: "Premium Price ≠ Better Results"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Expensive equipment doesn't guarantee performance for their swing.

> **Evidence:**
> - "For everyone saying the G400 Max is a 'fairway finder'....not for me" [P-120]
> - "I can't imagine paying $220 extra for 8 more yards but missing more fairways" [P-051]
> - "It's $225 more!!!! it would not get that rating from me based on that price" [P-052]

**Schema Impact:**
- Price is not a quality signal
- Expensive failures create extra frustration
- Value proposition must be results-based

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Position against $500-600 flagship drivers
- Emphasize results over brand prestige
- "It's not about what you pay - it's about what works"

---

## Schema 1.3: "One More Try, One More Failure"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Each new equipment purchase carries less hope than the last.

> **Evidence:**
> - "I have many many many...many drivers... Still looking for the 'One'" [P-036]
> - "Over the years I've gone through countless drivers" [P-249]
> - "I've been fitted and taken lessons. But, I've never been consistent" [P-249]

**Schema Impact:**
- Diminishing enthusiasm for new solutions
- Self-protective skepticism
- "Prove it" mentality before purchase

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Acknowledge the pattern: "We know you've tried everything"
- Strong guarantee to reduce risk
- Testimonials from fellow "serial searchers"

# SECTION 2: DRAW-BIAS DRIVER EXPECTATIONS

## Schema 2.1: "Distance Sacrifice Required"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Slice correction REQUIRES giving up distance - it's a tradeoff.

> **Evidence:**
> - "Carry distance for the PING G430 SFT was about eight yards shorter" [CM-030]
> - "This driver hit consistently 15-20 yards shorter than my regular driver" [P-009]
> - "More yards or more fairways? Here's where you must make one of those difficult big-kid golf decisions" [V-019]

**Schema Impact:**
- Accept distance loss as price of accuracy
- Calculate acceptable tradeoff
- Assume ALL draw-bias drivers sacrifice distance

**Messaging Strategy:**
- DISRUPT this schema directly with Golf Digest data
- "5.7 yards FARTHER on average" [H-029]
- Position SF2 as the exception to the rule

---

## Schema 2.2: "Crutch, Not Cure"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Draw-bias drivers are training wheels that prevent real improvement.

> **Evidence:**
> - "Remember, this is JUST AN AID, not the solution" [ST-014]
> - "A driver will not fix you" [ST-015]
> - "I think, long term, it makes it worse by allowing you to get away with it" [RC-013]

**Schema Impact:**
- Guilt about using equipment help
- Worry about becoming dependent
- Concern about "buying your game"

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Reframe: ALL clubs are designed to help
- Game improvement irons are accepted - why not driver?
- "The pros use optimized equipment too"

---

## Schema 2.3: "Overcorrection Risk"

**Expectation Pattern:**
If the draw bias is too strong, you'll trade slice for hook.

> **Evidence:**
> - "I don't step up to a tee box hoping not to slice. So when I put a normal swing on the ball with a driver designed primarily to correct a slice, it should come as no surprise that I saw the ball fly so far in the other direction" [RC-098]
> - "If you straighten out your swing the draw driver will be unhittable, and it will punish 'good' swings" [ST-011]

**Schema Impact:**
- Fear of going from one miss to opposite miss
- Concern about what happens if swing improves
- Worry about versatility

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Show controlled, predictable ball flight
- "Controllable draw, not snap hook"
- Testimonials about consistency, not overcorrection

# SECTION 3: INFOMERCIAL/AS-SEEN-ON-TV EXPECTATIONS

## Schema 3.1: "Gimmick Alert"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Unconventional-looking clubs are gimmicks that don't work.

> **Evidence:**
> - "another 'as seen on TV' gimmick club like the Alien wedge, Hammer driver" [V-007]
> - "I have a friend who's a sucker for those kind of clubs, they rarely stay in his bag" [V-006]
> - "This is the biggest piece of s**t I've seen" [V-099]

**Schema Impact:**
- Immediate dismissal of non-traditional designs
- Association with late-night TV shame
- Quality and durability concerns

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Differentiate from infomercial category
- Emphasize Performance Golf credentials
- Lead with quality: "Forged titanium, not cheap steel"
- Third-party reviews from credible sources

---

## Schema 3.2: "Quality Concerns"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Non-traditional equipment has quality and durability issues.

> **Evidence:**
> - "the heads are made out of a lightweight steel" (not titanium) [V-009]
> - "they have had numerous complaints about faulty Teeless drivers" [V-010]
> - "It's terrible. One of the worst feeling golf clubs I've ever hit, feels like hitting a brick" [P-039]

**Schema Impact:**
- Expect poor feel and feedback
- Expect durability problems
- Expect quality control issues

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Lead with materials: "Forged titanium construction"
- Emphasize feel and feedback in testimonials
- Strong warranty/guarantee

# SECTION 4: LESSON/INSTRUCTION EXPECTATIONS

## Schema 4.1: "Lessons Should Fix Swing, Not Equipment"

**Expectation Pattern:**
The "right" solution is fixing your swing through lessons.

> **Evidence:**
> - "lessons are cheaper than, say, a new driver" [V-080]
> - "If you're having trouble hitting your clubs well, no miracle club is going to help you" [V-102]

**Schema Impact:**
- Guilt about equipment solution
- Feel like they "should" take lessons
- Equipment is giving up on improvement

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Show lessons that DIDN'T work: "I took a lesson... still slicing bad" [P-128]
- Position as complement to lessons, not replacement
- "What lessons couldn't fix, equipment can"

---

## Schema 4.2: "Lessons Don't Work Either"

**Expectation Pattern:**
Lessons promise improvement but often fail to deliver lasting results.

> **Evidence:**
> - "I took a lesson, he corrected my grip... Still, slice/push everything" [P-118]
> - "Felt like I was fed a bunch of nonsense" [P-119]
> - "I took a full swing lesson with cameras at golf tec and they fixed my stance. I'm hitting more consistent but I'm still slicing bad" [P-128]

**Schema Impact:**
- Skepticism toward ALL solutions
- "Already tried that" mentality
- Deeper hopelessness

**Messaging Strategy:**
- Acknowledge lesson failure explicitly
- Position SF2 as "for when lessons didn't work"
- Different mechanism, different result
