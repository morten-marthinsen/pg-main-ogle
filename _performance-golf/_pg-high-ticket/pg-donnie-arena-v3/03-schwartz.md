## SECTION 2: THE PROBLEM

Let me ask you something.

How many times have you gotten better at golf -- and then lost it?

You take a lesson. You go to a school. You find something on YouTube that clicks. And for a couple of weeks, you're playing the best golf of your life. Your swing feels different. Your shots are landing where you aim them. You start thinking, *this is it -- I finally figured it out.*

And then... it fades.

Slowly at first. You can't quite find the feel. Then faster. The old miss comes back. The slice creeps in. The topped irons. The chunks. And within 30 days, maybe 45, you're right back where you started. Same handicap. Same frustration. Same game.

I call it the 30-Day Fade. And if you've been playing golf for more than a few years, you've experienced it. Probably more than once.

And it's not just you. Dennis Clark, a PGA Master Professional, put it like this: *"Most golfers see improvement that lasts about two to four weeks... then they revert right back to their old habits."* That's an instructor saying it -- a man who's spent decades teaching the game. Two to four weeks, and it's gone.

Bobby Clampett -- PGA Tour winner -- was even more blunt. He said 70% of golfers don't improve with traditional instruction. Seven out of ten. Not because they're bad students. Because the model doesn't work.

Maybe you spent $200 on a lesson and faded in two weeks. Maybe you spent $8,000 on a golf school -- played the best golf of your life for a month -- and then watched it all slip away. Back to your old swing. Back to your old scores.

Here's what I need you to understand: that's not your fault.

### The Real Problem

There are a lot of talented instructors out there. Guys who care about your game and genuinely want to help. I'm not going to sit here and trash them -- most of them are dedicated, skilled teachers.

The problem isn't them. The problem is the model they're working inside of.

Think about it. Your instructor gets 30 to 60 minutes a week with you. That's enough time to give you a tip. A feel. Maybe a drill. But it is NOT enough time to find the single root flaw that's causing every problem in your game and build a custom fix around YOUR body, YOUR age, YOUR limitations.

So what happens? They teach you THEIR swing. Their moves. Their feels. But their swing was built for their body. Not yours.

And even if they DO give you something that works -- even if you leave the lesson hitting it pure -- there's nobody there a week later when the old habits start creeping back. There's nobody there a month later when the correction fades and your brain defaults to the patterns it's been running for 20 years.

That's the come-learn-leave model. Show up. Learn great stuff. Get excited. Go home alone. And within 30 days, it's gone.

It fails everyone. It was never designed to make the improvement permanent.

Most golfers blame themselves for this. They think, *"I've invested so much time and money. I've taken so many lessons. Maybe I'm just not a good golfer. Maybe it's too late for me."*

That's not true. You CAN play great golf. Your swing can get dramatically better. But not inside a model that was never built to make it last.
